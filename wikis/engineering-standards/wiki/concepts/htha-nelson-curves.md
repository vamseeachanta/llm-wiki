# High-Temperature Hydrogen Attack (HTHA) and Nelson Curves

> Concept anchor for the high-temperature hydrogen damage family that
> governs materials selection in refinery hydroprocessing, catalytic
> reforming, and any service exposing ferritic steel to elevated
> temperature plus hydrogen partial pressure. Bidirectional with
> [api-rp-941](../standards/api-rp-941.md) (Nelson curves — primary), [api-rp-571](../standards/api-rp-571.md) (damage-
> mechanism catalogue), [api-rp-581](../standards/api-rp-581.md) (RBI HTHA damage factor),
> [hydrogen-embrittlement](hydrogen-embrittlement.md) (low-temperature sibling), and
> [brittle-fracture](brittle-fracture.md) (the failure mode HTHA produces).

## What is HTHA?

**High-temperature hydrogen attack (HTHA)** is a high-temperature
hydrogen damage mechanism that is **distinct** from the low-temperature
hydrogen-embrittlement family ([hydrogen-embrittlement](hydrogen-embrittlement.md) — HE, HISC,
HIC, SOHIC, SSC). HTHA operates above roughly **200 degC (390 degF)**
and at hydrogen partial pressures above roughly **50 psia (0.34 MPa)**;
below this regime, the same alloy systems are governed instead by the
low-temperature HE mechanisms.

The damage chain is:

1. Atomic hydrogen, generated by dissociation at the steel surface in
   the H2-rich process stream, **permeates** into the ferritic lattice
   at a rate set by temperature and pH2 (Sieverts' law).
2. In the steel interior the dissolved hydrogen reacts with **iron and
   alloy carbides** (Fe3C, M3C, M7C3) at grain boundaries:
   `Fe3C + 4 H -> 3 Fe + CH4`. The carbon is consumed as methane.
3. **Methane is too large to diffuse out** of the lattice. It
   accumulates at grain-boundary cavities, generating internal pressure
   that nucleates and grows **intergranular fissures**.
4. Failure is **sudden, brittle, and intergranular**, often
   concentrated in **decarburized HAZ regions** of welds where carbide
   content has already been depleted by thermal exposure during welding
   or PWHT, and where the residual + applied stress state is highest.

The mechanism is mechanistically distinct from HE / HISC: it is
metallurgical (carbide consumption + methane void growth) rather than
embrittlement-by-hydrogen-trapping, and it is irreversible (the carbide
phase is destroyed; baking does not recover the steel).

## Why HTHA cannot be detected by routine inspection

HTHA initiates at the **microstructural scale** — sub-millimeter
grain-boundary voids, decarburized bands, and incipient fissures —
which is **invisible to standard UT thickness gauging and routine
visual / MT / PT inspection**. By the time damage is detectable as
through-wall thinning or a leak, the steel has already lost a
substantial fraction of its load-bearing intergranular cohesion and
brittle catastrophic rupture may be imminent.

Early-stage detection requires **specialised NDT**:

- **AUT (advanced ultrasonic testing)** — phased-array UT (PAUT) and
  time-of-flight diffraction (TOFD) tuned to detect the diffuse
  back-scatter signature of grain-boundary fissure networks before they
  coalesce into through-wall flaws.
- **WFMP (wet fluorescent magnetic particle)** — surface-breaking
  fissure detection on accessible weld and base-metal surfaces,
  particularly at HAZ and decarburized zones.
- **In-situ metallographic replication** — surface-prep, etch, and
  acetate-replica capture of the grain-boundary microstructure for
  optical / SEM examination, used to confirm decarburization and
  incipient methane voids on a sampled population of welds.

The detection asymmetry is the principal reason **materials selection
above the operating envelope is the only reliable mitigation** — the
inspection chain is a backstop, not a substitute, for staying below the
Nelson-curve threshold.

## Nelson curves

The **Nelson curves** are the family of design curves first published
by G.A. Nelson (Shell Development) in the 1949 API research framework
and codified in [api-rp-941](../standards/api-rp-941.md). The curves are plotted on a chart of:

- **x-axis:** operating temperature (degC or degF).
- **y-axis:** hydrogen partial pressure (psia or MPa absolute).
- **One curve per alloy class** (CS, C-Mo, 1Cr-0.5Mo, 1.25Cr-0.5Mo,
  2.25Cr-1Mo, 2.25Cr-1Mo-V, 3Cr-1Mo, 5Cr-0.5Mo, 9Cr-1Mo, austenitic).

Each curve defines the **operating envelope below which the alloy is
HTHA-safe** with no in-service degradation expected over a typical
30-year service life. Operation **above the curve** for that alloy
requires either:

- **Alloy upgrade** to the next Cr-Mo class (the curves stack
  monotonically — higher Cr and Mo content shift the curve up and to
  the right, reflecting the carbide-stability ladder), or
- **Operational derating** (lower T, lower pH2) to bring the duty
  back below the curve, plus **intensive AUT / WFMP / replication
  inspection** on the existing equipment for the remaining service
  life.

The curves are an **explicit design surface**, not a probabilistic
input — a piece of equipment whose duty plots above its alloy's curve
is **non-conforming** under [api-rp-941](../standards/api-rp-941.md) and triggers materials-
upgrade or derating obligations.

## Alloy ladder

Indicative HTHA limits from the Nelson-curve family in [api-rp-941](../standards/api-rp-941.md).
**These approximations are illustrative and must not be used for
design** — for any HTHA screening, plot the actual operating duty
against the publisher-edition curves directly.

| Alloy | Approx HTHA limit (T, pH2) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Carbon steel (CS) | ~200 degC, ~100 psia | First-line low-T + low-pH2 service; hydroprocessing inlet circuits prior to heaters |
| C-0.5Mo | **DISQUALIFIED post-2016** | Was historically credited with a CS-plus margin; CSB Tesoro Anacortes 2010 fatal rupture and subsequent industry reassessment removed C-0.5Mo from the curves in API RP 941 8th ed (2016). Existing C-0.5Mo equipment must be audited / replaced per the 8th-ed transition guidance. |
| 1Cr-0.5Mo | ~290 degC, ~250 psia | Common low-pressure hydrogen-service vessel material |
| 1.25Cr-0.5Mo | ~320 degC, ~400 psia | Hydrofiner reactors; mid-pressure hydroprocessing |
| 2.25Cr-1Mo | ~440 degC, ~3000 psia | Workhorse hydrocracker / hydrotreater reactor and effluent shell material |
| 2.25Cr-1Mo-V | ~480 degC, ~3500 psia | Modern hydrocracker reactor — vanadium micro-alloying improves carbide stability and pushes the HTHA envelope up relative to plain 2.25Cr-1Mo |
| 3Cr-1Mo | similar to 2.25Cr-1Mo | Older alloy, less commonly specified for new build |
| 5Cr-0.5Mo | high-T sulfidation + HTHA | Cracking-tower internals; HTHA + sulfidation joint envelope governs |
| 9Cr-1Mo | high-T sulfidation domain | Furnace tubing; HTHA is generally non-limiting at the operating pH2 of furnace service |

The ladder is **monotonic in Cr + Mo content**: higher alloy content
stabilises the carbide phase against hydrogen attack, shifting the
curve up and to the right. The choice of alloy is therefore a direct
function of the (T, pH2) duty envelope — **specify the alloy whose
curve safely contains the operating point with margin**, then verify
the selection against the [api-rp-571](../standards/api-rp-571.md) sulfidation, creep, and PWHT
constraints that may dominate the choice in adjacent regimes.

## CSB Tesoro Anacortes 2010

On **2 April 2010**, the Tesoro Anacortes refinery (Washington, USA)
suffered a **catastrophic rupture of a hydroprocessing heat-exchanger
shell** during a maintenance startup. **Seven workers were killed**.
The ruptured component was fabricated from **plain C-0.5Mo carbon-
molybdenum steel**.

The U.S. Chemical Safety Board (CSB) investigation concluded that the
failure mechanism was **HTHA in HAZ regions where decarburization
during welding and PWHT had already degraded the carbide content**,
leaving a microstructure that could not retain its intergranular
cohesion against methane-void growth at the operating duty. The duty
plotted **on or near** the C-0.5Mo Nelson curve in the then-current
edition of [api-rp-941](../standards/api-rp-941.md), and the material had been credited with a
margin that the post-incident reassessment found was not robust to
weld decarburization.

The industry response was decisive:

- **API RP 941 8th ed (2016)** removed the C-0.5Mo curve from the
  Nelson-curve family — i.e., **disqualified C-0.5Mo as an HTHA-
  resistant alloy**. C-0.5Mo equipment in HTHA-credible service must
  be assessed against the carbon-steel curve (which it generally
  exceeds) and replaced or derated.
- Every major refiner conducted an **HTHA audit of C-0.5Mo equipment**
  in the **2010–2018 window**. Many vessels and exchangers were
  replaced with 1.25Cr-0.5Mo or higher alloy as a result.
- The CSB report became the precedent case for **inspection-mitigation
  hierarchy** (below) as a regulatory expectation, not just an
  engineering preference.

The Tesoro precedent is the reason HTHA materials selection is now
treated as a **fail-closed** integrity boundary in refinery practice:
the cost of getting it wrong is established at seven lives and a
multi-billion-dollar settlement.

## Inspection-mitigation hierarchy

The control hierarchy for HTHA in operating refineries, in decreasing
order of reliability:

1. **Materials selection above the operating envelope** — specify the
   alloy whose Nelson curve safely contains the (T, pH2) duty with
   margin. This is the only mitigation that does not depend on
   downstream inspection or operational discipline holding indefinitely.
2. **Use 2.25Cr-1Mo-V where margin is needed** — the V-modified grade
   pushes the HTHA envelope up vs. plain 2.25Cr-1Mo and is the modern
   default for new-build hydrocracker reactors.
3. **AUT screening every shutdown for known above-curve service** —
   PAUT + TOFD on the full weld population of any vessel whose duty is
   on or above its alloy's curve, with WFMP on accessible surfaces and
   in-situ replication on a sampled HAZ population.
4. **Replace existing C-0.5Mo equipment per the API RP 941 8th-ed
   audit** — the post-Tesoro disqualification triggered a one-time
   audit-and-replace obligation that is now mostly closed, but residual
   C-0.5Mo equipment in older plants remains a tier-1 inspection target.
5. **RBI-driven inspection prioritisation with HTHA-specific damage
   factor** — [api-rp-581](../standards/api-rp-581.md) Part 2 carries a dedicated **HTHA damage
   factor (DF\_HTHA)** that combines pH2, T, alloy, and exposure time
   into a quantitative POF input. The HTHA-DF is the principal RBI
   knob for prioritising AUT inspection budget across a refinery's
   hydroprocessing fleet.

The hierarchy is **AND, not OR** — a tier-1 mechanical-integrity
programme applies all five levers, with materials selection as the
load-bearing first line and the inspection chain as backstop.

## Standards

Bidirectional links to the standards-page resolvers (each carries
`code_id`, `publisher`, `revision` frontmatter per the wiki schema):

- [api-rp-941](../standards/api-rp-941.md) — *Steels for Hydrogen Service at Elevated
  Temperatures and Pressures in Petroleum Refineries and Petrochemical
  Plants.* **Primary** — holds the Nelson curves themselves and the
  alloy-by-alloy operating-envelope guidance. The 8th-ed (2016)
  disqualification of C-0.5Mo is the post-Tesoro reference point.
- [api-rp-571](../standards/api-rp-571.md) — *Damage Mechanisms Affecting Fixed Equipment in the
  Refining Industry.* The HTHA section catalogues the mechanism,
  affected materials, critical factors, appearance / morphology of
  damage, prevention / mitigation, and inspection / monitoring across
  the refinery damage-mechanism portfolio. Cross-axis with sulfidation,
  creep, and PASCC for the high-temperature regime.
- [api-rp-581](../standards/api-rp-581.md) — *Risk-Based Inspection Methodology.* Part 2 carries
  the HTHA damage factor used as POF input in tier-1 RBI studies.
- [api-510](../standards/api-510.md) — *Pressure Vessel Inspection Code.* In-service
  inspection, repair, alteration, and re-rating of refinery pressure
  vessels, including those in HTHA-credible service.
- [api-std-579](../standards/api-std-579.md) — *Fitness-for-Service.* Parts 4 and 5 (general and
  local metal-loss assessment) and Part 10 (creep + high-temperature
  operation, which includes HTHA) are the FFS routes for HTHA-affected
  equipment.

## Related concepts

- [damage-mechanism-screening](./damage-mechanism-screening.md) — HTHA
  appears in the high-temperature mechanism row of the screening matrix
  and in the hydroprocessing / catalytic-reformer unit-by-unit shortlist;
  screening is the upstream step that flags HTHA as a credible mechanism
  for a given asset.
- [hydrogen-embrittlement](./hydrogen-embrittlement.md) — low-temperature
  sibling family (HE, HISC, HIC, SOHIC, SSC) covering the
  **T < ~200 degC** regime. Mechanistically distinct from HTHA:
  trapping-and-embrittlement vs. carbide-consumption-and-methane-voids,
  and reversible-by-baking vs. irreversible.
- [brittle-fracture](./brittle-fracture.md) — the **failure mode** HTHA
  produces. Once intergranular fissures have networked through a
  carbide-depleted HAZ, the rupture event is a sudden brittle fracture
  indistinguishable on the failure surface from a low-temperature
  DBTT-driven rupture.
- [risk-based-inspection](./risk-based-inspection.md) — HTHA-DF is a
  first-class POF input in tier-1 RBI studies under
  [api-rp-580](../standards/api-rp-580.md) /
  [api-rp-581](../standards/api-rp-581.md).
- [fitness-for-service](./fitness-for-service.md) — assessment route
  for HTHA-affected equipment via [api-std-579](../standards/api-std-579.md)
  Parts 4, 5, and 10.
- [creep-and-stress-rupture](./creep-and-stress-rupture.md) —
  high-temperature damage-mechanism sibling that often coexists with
  HTHA in hydroprocessing reactor service.
