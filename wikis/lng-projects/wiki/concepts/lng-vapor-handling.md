# LNG Vapor Handling

## Scope

This page describes the operational disposition of warm and cold vapor streams in LNG facilities and carriers — vapor displacement during cargo transfer, gas-up + gas-freeing during cooldown/warming, emergency relief, flare-system operation, and vent-mast atmospheric release. It is the **physical-systems companion** to [LNG Boil-Off Gas Management](./lng-boil-off-gas-management.md): BOG management addresses *generation, routing economics, and reliquefaction* of cold-vapor streams; this page addresses the *operational vapor-handling envelope* — including warm-vapor operations, emergency-relief actuation, and atmospheric release.

## Why vapor handling matters

- **Tank-pressure boundary**: typical LNG cargo and storage tanks operate within roughly a 25–300 mbar gauge envelope. Excursion below the lower bound can cause vacuum collapse of the inner barrier; excursion above the upper bound triggers emergency-relief actuation and vent-mast or flare release.
- **Cargo-transfer interlock**: each cubic metre of LNG loaded displaces roughly an equivalent volume of warm vapor that must be returned to the source tank or otherwise disposed of. A vapor-return line and pressure-balance system are loading prerequisites, not optional auxiliaries.
- **Hydrocarbon-air explosion envelope**: methane–air mixtures within roughly the 5%–15% (LFL–UFL) range are flammable. Vapor-handling protocols define inerting, purging, and gas-up sequences specifically to keep tank and piping atmospheres outside this envelope at all times.
- **Atmospheric methane emissions**: methane has a ~80x global warming potential vs. CO₂ over a 20-year horizon. Vent-mast methane release attracts increasing regulatory pressure, pushing operators toward flare combustion or reliquefaction over routine venting.

## Five primary vapor-handling scenarios

- **Cargo loading and unloading** — vapor displaced from the receiving tank is returned to the source tank or shore terminal via a vapor-return arm and pressure-balance line. Failure of the vapor-return path triggers cargo-flow shutdown.
- **Gas-up and gas-freeing** — phase 2 of the cooldown protocol (see [LNG Cooldown + Commissioning Procedures](./lng-cooldown-commissioning.md)). Gas-up displaces inert nitrogen with methane vapor before liquid introduction; gas-freeing displaces methane with inert gas before warm work or tank entry.
- **Boil-off routing** — physical disposition of BOG generated by heat ingress, pump churn, or flash. Routing options (compressor + reliquefaction, fuel-gas, flare, vent) and their economics are covered in the BOG-management sibling; this page covers the physical interfaces.
- **Emergency-relief actuation** — pressure/vacuum relief valves and safety-relief valves provide a fail-safe backup to normal pressure control when control systems, BOG compressors, or vapor-return paths fail.
- **Maintenance and drydock** — pre-warming, tank isolation, and degassing prepare a cargo or storage tank for safe human entry, hot work, or class-survey access.

## Vapor-balance and vapor-return systems

- **Vapor-return arm** — a dedicated marine loading arm coupled with the LNG liquid arm at jetty transfers and ship-to-ship operations. The vapor-return arm carries displaced warm vapor from the receiving tank back to the source.
- **Vapor-return blower** — a shore-side or ship-side blower (or compressor) that maintains vapor-return-line flow against pressure drop and prevents sustained over- or under-pressure in either tank during the transfer.
- **OCIMF and SIGTTO best practice** — ship-shore interface guidance covers vapor-return-line sizing, pressure-management setpoints, condensate-prevention (warm-vapor return into a cold tank can recondense and create pressure swings), and ESD interlock logic.
- **ESD-2 integration** — emergency shut-down level 2 (cargo-flow stop with arm decoupling logic) is normally interlocked with vapor-return availability: loss of the vapor-return path is treated as an immediate transfer-stop condition.

## Flare and vent systems

- **Vent-mast (atmospheric release)** — the traditional disposal route for tank vapor in upset, blowdown, or emergency-relief scenarios. Vent masts are sited and sized for vapor-cloud dispersion under credible weather. Routine venting is increasingly disfavored because the released stream is largely raw methane.
- **Cold flare** — a flare dedicated to LNG vapor. Combustion of low-temperature, low-density methane is technically demanding: the flame envelope can lift off the tip, and pilot reliability and assist-gas (often nitrogen-purged + fuel-gas-assisted) become design-critical.
- **Hot flare** — a shared terminal or refinery flare stack. LNG vapor is mixed with warmer hydrocarbon fuel gas and sometimes with steam to stabilize combustion and reduce smoke.
- **Combined vent + flare** — many terminals vent during normal upset margins and divert to flare for sustained or larger-volume releases. The crossover is set by environmental permit, methane-emission policy, and flare-tip turndown.

## Atmospheric-methane regulatory pressure

- **EU Methane Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2024/1787)** — adopted 2024; mandates leak-detection-and-repair (LDAR) programs, restricts routine venting and flaring, and applies methane-intensity reporting to LNG imports into the EU.
- **US regulation** — EPA Greenhouse Gas Reporting Program Subpart W (petroleum and natural-gas systems) requires methane-emissions reporting; PHMSA and state regulators address LNG-facility venting and flaring under siting and air-permit programs.
- **IMO marine framework** — MARPOL Annex VI air-pollution provisions, with methane-related amendments developed under MEPC, address shipboard methane emissions including methane slip from gas-fueled engines.
- **Industry response** — methane-slip-reduction tank insulation and reliquefaction designs from containment vendors; engine-side mitigation via dual-fuel two-stroke designs (low-pressure ME-GA, high-pressure ME-GI) and four-stroke retrofit programs.

## SIGTTO and IMO IGC Code guidance

- **IGC Code Chapter 8 (Cargo-Tank Venting and Gas-Freeing)** — sets technical requirements for the cargo-tank vent system, pressure/vacuum relief sizing, vent-mast siting, and inert-gas system arrangements on gas carriers. See [IMO IGC Code](../standards/igc-code.md).
- **SIGTTO best-practice guidance** — vent-mast height and dispersion, ignition-source separation, vapor-cloud-dispersion considerations, and ship-shore vapor-return interface practice.
- **Class society procedures** — DNV, ABS, BV, LR, ClassNK and KR each publish vent-system survey, periodic-test, and maintenance procedures aligned with IGC Chapter 8.
- **Onshore counterparts** — for terminal-side venting and emergency relief, see [NFPA 59A](../standards/nfpa-59a.md) (US), CSA Z276 (Canada), and EN 1473 (EU).

## Future trends

- **Methane-slip mitigation** — modern dual-fuel two-stroke engines (high-pressure ME-GI, low-pressure ME-GA) and emerging four-stroke designs reduce engine-side methane slip substantially relative to first-generation dual-fuel propulsion.
- **Cargo-tank reliquefaction** — onboard reliquefaction is now common on FSRUs and large-class LNG carriers (≳174–180k m³), reducing both fuel-gas requirements and methane vented or flared.
- **Carbon-capture and methane-oxidation on vent streams** — pilot-scale technologies for oxidizing or capturing methane from vent streams; not yet commercial at LNG-cargo scale.
- **Cold-energy recovery** — terminal-side regasification cold energy can drive Rankine-cycle power generation, air-separation precooling, or data-center cooling. Cold-energy recovery sits at the boundary of vapor handling and process integration.
