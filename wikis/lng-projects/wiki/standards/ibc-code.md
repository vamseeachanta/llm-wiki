# IMO IBC Code — Construction and Equipment of Ships Carrying Dangerous Chemicals in Bulk

> **Regulatory framing.** The IBC Code is a mandatory IMO instrument adopted under SOLAS Chapter VII Part B and MARPOL Annex II, binding on all SOLAS- and MARPOL-contracting flag states. It is the chemical-tanker counterpart to the IGC Code (liquefied gases) and the construction-side companion to the HNS Convention 2010 (carriage liability). This page is metadata-only; no clause text, formula, table, or figure is reproduced.

## Scope

IMO ship-construction code mandatory under **SOLAS Chapter VII Part B** and **MARPOL Annex II** for chemical tankers. Establishes requirements for ship structure, cargo containment, materials of construction, ventilation, electrical, fire protection, instrumentation, and personnel protection.

Ships are categorized into three types based on the safety and pollution hazard of the cargoes they are certified to carry:

- **Type 1** — most-hazardous chemicals (severe environmental + safety hazards); double-hull plus segregated ballast required
- **Type 2** — intermediate hazard
- **Type 3** — least-hazardous chemicals; minimum-spec arrangements

Applicability:

- Ships > 500 GT carrying liquid chemicals listed in IBC Code Chapter 17 (the cargo list, ~1,500+ products) and Chapter 18 (products to which the Code does not apply)
- Cargo tank arrangements, segregated ballast, double-bottom, and double-hull provisions tied to ship-type designation
- Materials, ventilation, gas-freeing, atmosphere control, electrical area classification, fire-protection, and personnel-protection requirements
- Operational requirements (manuals, procedures, surveys, Certificate of Fitness)

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **Adopted** 1983-12-05 by IMO Resolutions **MEPC.19(22)** and **MSC.4(48)**
- **Entry-into-force** 1986-07-01 (mandatory for new chemical tankers from this date by date of build)
- **MEPC.318(74) + MSC.460(101)** (2019) — most-recent comprehensive amendments updating Chapter 17 cargo list, hazard categorization, and ship-type assignments
- **2024 consolidated edition** — current published reference text incorporating MEPC and MSC amendments through 2023

The BCH Code (Bulk Chemical Code, IMO Resolution A.212(VII), 1971) is the **non-mandatory predecessor** and remains applicable only to chemical tankers built before 1986-07-01.

## Three ship types

- **Type 1 ship** — most-stringent design requirements: double-hull or 760 mm minimum side-clearance from cargo tank, double-bottom, segregated ballast. Required for cargoes with severe environmental and safety hazards (e.g., methyl-isocyanate and similar high-toxicity / high-reactivity products).
- **Type 2 ship** — intermediate: single-hull permitted with 600 mm wing-tank arrangement; double-bottom required. For cargoes with significant but less-severe hazards.
- **Type 3 ship** — least-stringent: minimum-spec hull arrangements. For cargoes with relatively mild hazards.

The cargo list in **Chapter 17** prescribes the ship-type for each chemical based on the product's combined safety + pollution hazard rating. Multi-product carriers must meet the most-stringent ship-type required by any cargo on the certificate.

## Key chapters

- **Ch.1 — General**: definitions, scope, surveys, certification (Certificate of Fitness for the Carriage of Dangerous Chemicals in Bulk)
- **Ch.2 — Ship Survival Capability + Location of Cargo Tanks**: damage-stability, cargo-tank location relative to side and bottom shell, ship-type assignment criteria
- **Ch.3 — Ship Arrangements**: cargo-area boundaries, accommodation/machinery separation, cofferdams, pump-room arrangements
- **Ch.4 — Cargo Containment**: tank construction, secondary barriers where required, integration with hull
- **Ch.5 — Cargo Transfer**: piping, manifolds, cargo pumps, segregation between incompatible cargoes
- **Ch.6 — Materials of Construction**: corrosion compatibility, low-temperature service, lining systems
- **Ch.7 — Cargo Temperature Control**: heating and cooling for products requiring temperature management
- **Ch.8 — Cargo Tank Venting + Gas-Freeing**: pressure/vacuum-relief, vent-mast geometry, inerting and gas-freeing procedures
- **Ch.9 — Environmental Control**: cargo-tank atmosphere (inerting, padding, drying)
- **Ch.10 — Electrical Installations**: hazardous-area classification, certified equipment, intrinsic safety
- **Ch.11 — Fire Protection + Fire Extinction**: structural fire protection, water-spray, deck-foam systems, dry-chemical
- **Ch.12 — Mechanical Ventilation in the Cargo Area**: air-change rates, fan certification, intake/exhaust geometry
- **Ch.14 — Personnel Protection**: PPE, decontamination showers, breathing apparatus, medical first-aid for chemical exposures
- **Ch.15 — Special Requirements**: cargo-specific provisions (toxicity, reactivity, polymerization inhibitors, etc.)
- **Ch.16 — Operational Requirements**: manuals, procedures, training, record-keeping
- **Ch.17 — Summary of Minimum Requirements**: the cargo list (~1,500+ chemicals) — the mandatory companion to MARPOL Annex II categorization; the canonical reference for ship-type, tank-type, vent-type, gauging, and special provisions per product
- **Ch.18 — List of Products to Which the Code Does Not Apply**: out-of-scope products (covered elsewhere or not chemical-tanker cargo)
- **Ch.19 — Index of Products Carried in Bulk**: cross-reference index

(Ch.13 is reserved/skipped in the codified numbering — intentional in the IBC Code structure, not a gap on this page.)

## IBC vs IGC vs BCH

| Code | Cargo | Mandatory? | IMO instrument |
|------|-------|------------|----------------|
| **IBC** | Liquid chemicals (~1,500+ products listed in Ch.17) | **Yes** — SOLAS VII-B + MARPOL II | MEPC.19(22) + MSC.4(48), 1983 |
| **IGC** | Liquefied gases (LNG, LPG, ethylene, ethane, ammonia) | **Yes** — SOLAS VII-C | MSC.5(48), 1983; MSC.370(93) revision, 2014 |
| **BCH** | Pre-IBC liquid chemicals | No (legacy non-mandatory; pre-1986 ships only) | A.212(VII), 1971 |

A vessel certified under IBC carries a **Certificate of Fitness for the Carriage of Dangerous Chemicals in Bulk**. A vessel certified under IGC carries a **Certificate of Fitness for the Carriage of Liquefied Gases in Bulk**. These are distinct certifications — a single vessel typically holds one or the other based on trade.

## IBC + MARPOL Annex II + HNS Convention interaction

- **IBC Code** = ship-construction (build-quality, structural arrangements, materials, equipment) — what the ship must be built to
- **MARPOL Annex II** = pollution-prevention operations — discharge limits, reception facilities, cargo categorization (X, Y, Z, OS), prewash and stripping requirements; what the ship must do operationally
- **HNS Convention 2010** = carriage liability — compensation regime for spill / release damage from hazardous and noxious substances at sea (not yet in force as of 2026; see cross-wiki bridge below)

All three regimes anchor on the **IBC Code Chapter 17 cargo list** as the canonical chemical-categorization reference. A chemical's MARPOL Annex II pollution category and its HNS-Convention coverage status are both keyed to its Chapter 17 entry.

**International / regulatory parallels:**

- **SOLAS Chapter VII Part B** — parent IMO convention; binds IBC Code to flag-state implementation
- **MARPOL Annex II** — pollution-prevention companion; categorizes IBC Chapter 17 cargoes into X/Y/Z/OS pollution categories and sets discharge / reception-facility / prewash requirements
- **HNS Convention 2010** — Hazardous and Noxious Substances liability regime; Protocol of 2010 to the 1996 HNS Convention; not yet in force pending ratification thresholds
- **IMDG Code** — International Maritime Dangerous Goods Code for **packaged** dangerous goods (containers, drums); IBC governs the same chemicals when carried **in bulk** as ship's cargo
- **USCG 46 CFR Part 153** — US-flag implementation of IBC for tank-vessel safety; incorporates IBC Code by reference
- **USCG 33 CFR Parts 154/155/156** — US shore-side and transfer-operation regulations for chemical-tanker calls
- **EPA Vessel General Permit (VGP)** — US discharge regulations for vessel-incidental discharges, including those from IBC-certified tankers
- **STCW Convention** — crew certification including chemical-tanker training endorsements (basic and advanced) for officers and ratings on IBC-certified ships
- **SIGTTO and CDI publications** — industry-best-practice complements (CDI is the chemical-tanker-side analog to OCIMF/SIGTTO for vetting and operational guidance); not mandatory but heavily referenced by class societies and charterers
- **Class society rules** — ABS, DNV, LR, BV, ClassNK, KR, RINA, CCS each issue chemical-tanker rules implementing IBC Code requirements with classification-specific scantling and survey detail
