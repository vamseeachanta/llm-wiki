# Drillship

## Scope

Ship-shaped offshore drilling rig — a self-propelled vessel with a drilling moonpool through the hull and a drilling package on deck. Dynamic positioning (DP) keeps station above the wellhead. Drillships are the rig class of choice for ultra-deepwater (>5,000 ft) and remote-location exploration where mobility, deck load capacity, and DP capability all favor a ship-shaped hull over a semi.

## Class characteristics

- **Hull form** — conventional ship-shape (typically 800-900 ft length, beam 130-150 ft) with internal moonpool
- **Self-propulsion** — azimuth thrusters (typical 6-8 thrusters) provide both propulsion and DP station-keeping
- **Water-depth rating** — modern Gen 6+ drillships rate to 10,000-12,000 ft, with some recent units pushing 12,000+ ft
- **Drilling-depth rating** — 35,000-40,000 ft for the deepest-rated units
- **Variable deck load** — 20,000-25,000 short tons typical for modern units (larger than any semi)
- **Dual-derrick capability** — many Gen 6+ drillships carry two complete drill floors enabling parallel BHA / casing / BOP-handling operations, reducing critical-path time

## Why drillship over semi for ultra-deepwater

- **Higher transit speed** — drillships self-propel at 10-12 knots, vs ~5 knots for a wet-towed semi
- **Larger deck-load capacity** — vital for deepwater BOP stacks (350-500 short tons) and risers
- **Lower mobilization cost** for transoceanic moves
- **Drawback** — higher motion response than semi in heavy seas; harsh-environment work still prefers semis
