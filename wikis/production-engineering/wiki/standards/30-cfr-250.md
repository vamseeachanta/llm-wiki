# 30 CFR 250 — Oil and Gas and Sulphur Operations in the Outer Continental Shelf

## Scope

Title 30 of the Code of Federal Regulations, Part 250 ("30 CFR 250") is the federal regulatory framework that governs oil, gas, and sulphur operations on the United States Outer Continental Shelf (OCS). It is administered by the Bureau of Safety and Environmental Enforcement (BSEE) within the Department of the Interior and operates under the umbrella statute of the Outer Continental Shelf Lands Act (OCSLA). The companion title 30 CFR 550 (administered by the Bureau of Ocean Energy Management, BOEM) covers leasing and resource-evaluation activities; 30 CFR 250 covers the operational lifecycle from exploration drilling through production operations to decommissioning.

This page is the **standards anchor** for federal OCS production reporting, safety-system regulatory framing, and the Notice-to-Lessees (NTL) umbrella in the production-engineering wiki. It does not transcribe regulatory text; the eCFR-published rule text is the canonical reference and is publicly retrievable.

## Why operators read it

- **Operational mandate** — every operator producing on the US OCS operates under 30 CFR 250. The Part covers well design and construction, drilling and completion operations, well-control equipment, production operations, production reporting, safety-and-environmental-management systems (SEMS), and decommissioning. Compliance is not optional.
- **Standards incorporation by reference** — 30 CFR 250 incorporates numerous API standards by reference (including [API RP 14C](api-rp-14c.md), [API Spec 14A](api-spec-14a.md), API Spec 6A, API RP 17F, and others). When the regulation refers to "the latest edition incorporated by reference," operators must check the specific edition that BSEE has incorporated as of the operational date — this is not always the most recent industry-published edition.
- **NTL umbrella** — BSEE issues Notices to Lessees and Operators (NTLs) that interpret, clarify, or extend 30 CFR 250 provisions for specific operational situations. NTLs are not themselves regulations but represent BSEE's operational expectations; operators treat them as effectively binding.
- **Inter-agency interface** — production volumes reported under 30 CFR 250 feed the royalty-calculation interface managed by the Office of Natural Resources Revenue (ONRR) under separate regulations at 30 CFR 1200-1299. The reporting and royalty pathways are operationally coupled even though the regulatory authorities differ.

## Structural overview (subparts relevant to production engineering)

30 CFR 250 is organized into subparts that group requirements by operational phase. Subpart boundaries and content evolve across revisions; operators should consult the current eCFR text rather than relying on summary references for compliance work. The high-level structure as of the time of writing of this page (verify-at-publish-time):

- **Subpart A — General** — definitions, jurisdiction, document submission, lessee/operator obligations
- **Subpart B — Plans and Information** — exploration plans, development and production plans, deepwater operations plans
- **Subpart D — Oil and Gas Drilling Operations** — drilling permit requirements, well-control equipment, fluid-management requirements
- **Subpart E — Oil and Gas Production Requirements** — **central subpart for production-engineering compliance**. Production operations, well-test reporting, production-measurement requirements, flaring and venting authorization, downhole-equipment requirements, sustained-casing-pressure (SCP) reporting and remediation
- **Subpart H — Oil and Gas Production Safety Systems** — incorporates [API RP 14C](api-rp-14c.md) by reference and establishes the offshore production-facility safety-system regulatory framework
- **Subpart Q — Decommissioning Activities** — well plug-and-abandonment, platform removal, site clearance
- **Subpart S — Safety and Environmental Management Systems (SEMS)** — operator-level management-system requirements covering safety culture, hazard analysis, management of change, and operating-procedure discipline

Other subparts cover sulphur operations, geological and geophysical operations, and administrative procedures; the subparts most directly relevant to day-to-day production engineering are E and H.

## Subpart E — production requirements

Subpart E establishes the federal-side production-operations requirements that operators consume daily:

- **Production reporting** — operators report production volumes (oil, gas, water, condensate) on the schedules established by Subpart E and operationalised through BSEE-managed electronic reporting platforms. Production data flows from BSEE-side operational reporting to ONRR-side royalty reporting through coupled interfaces.
- **Production measurement** — the regulatory framework establishes general measurement-accuracy requirements; the technical-methodology details are typically delegated to industry-standard practices (API MPMS, AGA Reports) that operators implement at the meter station.
- **Flaring and venting** — Subpart E provisions cover authorization and reporting requirements for flaring and venting of natural gas. The framework distinguishes routine and non-routine flaring, requires reporting of volumes flared and vented, and establishes the federal-side companion to the state-level flaring rules that operators encounter onshore. See [Gas Flaring Rules](../concepts/gas-flaring-rules.md).
- **Sustained-casing-pressure (SCP) reporting** — operators report SCP exceeding regulatory threshold values and develop remediation plans within specified time intervals. SCP reporting and remediation is one of the most operationally-visible parts of Subpart E and the focus of multiple BSEE NTLs over time.
- **Well-test data submission** — well-test data feeding into the production-allocation framework is reportable to BSEE on the cadence specified in Subpart E. See [Production Allowable Rates](../concepts/production-allowable-rates.md) and the sister sub-issue 1 page on production-allocation methodology.

## Subpart H — production safety systems

Subpart H is the federal regulatory anchor for offshore production-facility safety-system design. It incorporates [API RP 14C](api-rp-14c.md) by reference and operates as the regulatory enforcement layer behind the RP 14C SAFE-chart methodology. Operators specifying offshore safety systems treat RP 14C as the methodology basis and Subpart H as the compliance-verification framework that BSEE inspectors apply.

The incorporation-by-reference mechanism means that when API issues a new edition of RP 14C, operators do not automatically inherit the new edition under Subpart H — the new edition becomes regulatory only after BSEE explicitly updates the incorporation. The operationally-current edition under Subpart H may therefore lag the API-current edition; operators should verify the BSEE-incorporated edition.

## NTL umbrella

BSEE Notices to Lessees and Operators (NTLs) are the operational-guidance mechanism that BSEE uses to interpret or extend 30 CFR 250 provisions. NTLs typically address:

- **Operational interpretations of specific provisions** — clarifying how a regulatory requirement applies to a specific operational situation
- **Reporting-format updates** — establishing new reporting formats or platform changes
- **Incident-response expectations** — establishing expected post-incident reporting and remediation paths after major industry events
- **Enforcement-priority signals** — telegraphing BSEE inspection focus areas for the upcoming operational cycle

NTLs are publicly retrievable from bsee.gov. Operators treat NTLs as effectively binding even though they do not have the legal status of regulations; non-compliance with NTL expectations attracts BSEE enforcement attention even when not specifically tied to a regulatory violation. See [Intervention Triggers](../concepts/intervention-triggers.md) for the integrity-management NTL coverage and [Federal Production Reporting](../concepts/federal-production-reporting.md) for the reporting-side NTL coverage.

## Enforcement framing

30 CFR 250 violations attract BSEE enforcement action ranging from operational corrective-action orders (Incident of Non-Compliance, INC) through civil monetary penalties through suspension of operations through lease cancellation in the most severe cases. The enforcement framework is administrative; criminal-referral cases under OCSLA are handled separately through the Department of Justice. The INC-issuance pattern is publicly visible through BSEE-published enforcement data.

## Historical context

The 30 CFR 250 framework has evolved across multiple regulatory generations:

- **Original framework** — OCSLA was enacted in 1953; the implementing regulations have been issued and revised over the subsequent decades, originally administered by the Minerals Management Service (MMS) within the Department of the Interior
- **Post-Deepwater-Horizon restructuring** — following the April 2010 Macondo well blowout in the US Gulf of Mexico, the Department of the Interior restructured MMS into three successor agencies: BOEM (leasing and resource evaluation), BSEE (operational safety and environmental enforcement), and ONRR (royalty collection). The 30 CFR 250 framework moved to BSEE administration as part of the restructuring
- **Post-Macondo regulatory revisions** — the period 2010-2016 saw substantial revisions to 30 CFR 250 in response to lessons from the Macondo investigation, including the Drilling Safety Rule (2010), the Workplace Safety Rule (SEMS, 2010), the Production Safety Systems Rule (2018), and the Well Control Rule (2016, with subsequent revisions). The post-Macondo revisions have been the most consequential body of 30 CFR 250 revisions in the modern operational period
- **Continuing evolution** — 30 CFR 250 continues to evolve in response to operational experience, technological change, and policy direction; operators should consult current eCFR text rather than relying on historical-context summaries

The historical context is operationally significant because the regulatory framework that an operator is subject to is the framework as currently constituted, not the framework as constituted at any prior point. Operators with long-running operations on the OCS have experienced multiple regulatory-framework generations and have built compliance infrastructure that accommodates the ongoing revision pattern.
