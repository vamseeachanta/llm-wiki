# Gas Flaring Rules

## Scope

Gas flaring is the controlled combustion of natural gas at the point of production or processing as a means of disposing of gas that cannot be (or is not being) captured for commercial use. Venting is the controlled release of natural gas without combustion. Both flaring and venting are regulated by overlapping state, federal, and international regulatory regimes that have evolved substantially over the past two decades in response to climate-and-environment concerns about methane emissions and wasted natural-gas resources.

This page covers the routine-vs-non-routine flaring distinction that anchors most modern flaring regulation, the venting regulation that operates alongside flaring regulation, the ALARP framing that several regulatory regimes apply, the specific Texas RRC and federal BSEE flaring frameworks as practitioner-canonical examples, and the World Bank GGFR Zero Routine Flaring by 2030 initiative as a public-facing international reference framework.

## Routine-vs-non-routine flaring

The most operationally-load-bearing distinction in modern flaring regulation is the routine-vs-non-routine flaring framing:

### Routine flaring

Routine flaring is the steady-state combustion of associated gas from oil-producing wells where gas-capture infrastructure (gathering pipelines, processing capacity, marketing arrangements) is absent, inadequate, or not yet built out. Routine flaring is the regulatory category that climate-and-environment policy has targeted most aggressively over the past decade — the World Bank GGFR Zero Routine Flaring by 2030 initiative, the EU's emerging methane regulation, and the substantial revisions to state-level US flaring rules in the major-producing states have all focused on eliminating or substantially reducing routine flaring.

Routine flaring is typically reported separately from non-routine flaring in the regulatory accounting and is subject to tighter authorisation requirements, time-window limits, and quantitative caps.

### Non-routine flaring

Non-routine flaring is flaring associated with specific operational events:

- **Process upsets** — emergency or unplanned events that require gas-stream depressuring through a flare for safety reasons
- **Maintenance activities** — planned shutdowns or maintenance operations that require gas-stream depressuring
- **Well-testing operations** — flaring associated with well-test flowback to characterise reservoir deliverability before establishing gathering connections
- **Startup and shutdown** — flaring associated with bringing facilities online or taking them offline
- **Equipment failures** — flaring associated with compressor failures, pipeline interruptions, or processing-plant outages that disrupt the normal gas-capture pathway

Non-routine flaring is regulated less stringently than routine flaring on the principle that some level of non-routine flaring is unavoidable in safe operation of oil-and-gas production facilities. The regulatory framing for non-routine flaring focuses on reporting, on minimising duration and volume, and on root-cause analysis of recurring non-routine flaring patterns.

### Operational categorisation

The routine-vs-non-routine categorisation is operator-determined within regulator-defined criteria. The criteria vary across regulatory regimes, and the categorisation can be operationally contested where the boundary cases (e.g., flaring during a multi-week pipeline-construction delay; flaring during a multi-month processing-plant outage; flaring during a deliverability-vs-capacity transient on a new well) do not cleanly fit either category. Several regulatory regimes have tightened the criteria over the past decade specifically to reduce the operator-discretion ambiguity at the boundary.

## Venting

Venting (uncombusted release of natural gas) is regulated alongside flaring but more stringently in most modern regulatory regimes, on the basis that uncombusted methane has substantially higher global-warming potential than combusted methane (the combustion converts methane to CO2, which has lower global-warming potential than methane on a per-molecule basis). Modern regulatory framing in most jurisdictions:

- **Prohibits routine venting** with narrow specific exceptions (e.g. low-pressure equipment where flare combustion is not technically feasible)
- **Requires combustion (flaring) over venting** where the disposal pathway is between flaring and venting and combustion is technically feasible
- **Reports vented volumes separately** from flared volumes for emissions-accounting purposes (different combustion-efficiency assumptions apply)

Some regulatory regimes additionally cover **fugitive emissions** — methane emissions from equipment leaks, valve packings, flange connections, and other unintentional release sources. Fugitive-emission regulation is typically operationally distinct from flaring-and-venting regulation but shares the broader methane-emissions policy framework.

## ALARP framing

Several regulatory regimes anchor flaring authorisation in an ALARP (As Low As Reasonably Practicable) framing. Under ALARP, operators must demonstrate that the flaring volume has been reduced as low as is reasonably practicable given the operational constraints, the available alternatives, and the cost-and-benefit balance.

ALARP framing is procedural rather than prescriptive — the regulator does not set a single quantitative cap that applies uniformly across all operators or all wells, but instead requires operator-by-operator demonstration of the reasonably-practicable reduction. The procedural framing places the burden on the operator to document the reduction effort and to engage with the regulator on the cost-benefit analysis.

ALARP framing is common in jurisdictions with safety-case-based regulation (UK HSE, Norway PSA, Australia NOPSEMA — see [Intervention Triggers](intervention-triggers.md) for the safety-case-regulation framing). US-side regulation is more typically prescriptive than ALARP-based, though some specific provisions (notably some state-level flaring rules) incorporate ALARP-like language.

## Texas RRC Statewide Rule 32

Texas RRC Statewide Rule 32 is the Texas regulatory framework for flaring and venting on oil-and-gas operations in Texas. Rule 32 has been revised multiple times over its operational history, with the most consequential revisions in recent years tightening the routine-flaring authorisation, requiring operator reporting on capture-infrastructure timelines, and adjusting the regulatory enforcement framework. The substantive rule-text is publicly retrievable from rrc.texas.gov.

Operationally, Rule 32 covers:

- **Permit-to-flare authorisation** — operators seeking to flare beyond regulator-defined default authorisations submit applications that the RRC reviews and acts on
- **Time-window limits** — authorised flaring is bounded in time, with extensions requiring additional regulator action
- **Quantitative caps** — some authorisations carry volume caps
- **Reporting requirements** — operators report flared and vented volumes to the RRC on the cadence specified by Rule 32 and the related PR form-class (see [State Production Reporting](state-production-reporting.md))

The Permian-Basin and Eagle Ford-Basin production growth over the past decade has placed Rule 32 under operational stress and driven several of the recent revisions; operators producing in these basins have faced material changes in flaring-authorisation practice over the period.

Other major-producing-state regulators (NDIC for the Bakken Basin, OCC for the SCOOP/STACK and Anadarko Basin, NMOCD for the Permian-Basin New Mexico side) maintain analogous flaring rules with state-specific variations. The NDIC's gas-capture targets that emerged from the Bakken-Basin flaring controversies of the early 2010s are a particularly notable state-side rule revision; operators producing in the Bakken-Basin manage their gas-capture infrastructure with explicit reference to the NDIC-set capture targets.

## Federal BSEE flaring rules

For offshore OCS production, BSEE under 30 CFR 250 Subpart E covers flaring and venting authorisation. The Subpart E provisions parallel the state-side framework with OCS-specific variations:

- **Authorisation requirement** — flaring beyond regulator-defined default authorisations requires BSEE approval
- **Reporting requirement** — flared and vented volumes are reported to BSEE on the cadence specified by Subpart E (see [Federal Production Reporting](federal-production-reporting.md))
- **NTL framing** — BSEE NTLs periodically address flaring expectations, capture-infrastructure timelines, and reporting-format requirements
- **Coupling with safety-system framework** — Subpart H safety-system requirements (see [API RP 14C](../standards/api-rp-14c.md)) include flare-system design and reliability requirements that the Subpart E flaring authorisation interacts with

See [30 CFR 250](../standards/30-cfr-250.md) for the federal regulatory framework.

## World Bank GGFR and Zero Routine Flaring by 2030

The World Bank Global Gas Flaring Reduction Partnership (GGFR) is a multi-stakeholder partnership of governments, oil-and-gas companies, and development institutions that has worked since 2002 to reduce routine gas flaring globally. GGFR publishes regular global-flaring-volume estimates derived from satellite-observation methodologies and supports operator-and-government engagement on flaring-reduction commitments.

The Zero Routine Flaring by 2030 initiative is the central GGFR public-facing commitment framework: governments and operators that endorse the initiative commit to eliminating routine flaring in their operations by 2030, with reporting on progress against the commitment. The initiative does not have direct regulatory force — endorsement is voluntary, and the operational consequence of falling short of the 2030 target is reputational rather than enforcement-based — but it has produced substantial movement in operator-and-government framing over its operational history and serves as the dominant international reference framework for routine-flaring reduction.

GGFR publishes its work on the World Bank's portal at worldbank.org/ggfr. The framework documentation, the annual flaring-volume estimates, and the endorsement-tracking are publicly visible. This wiki cites GGFR publications by URL and paraphrases their substantive content; verbatim text from GGFR publications is not reproduced.

## EPA Subpart W (greenhouse-gas reporting)

Adjacent to the flaring-and-venting authorisation framework is the EPA greenhouse-gas reporting framework under 40 CFR Part 98 Subpart W (Petroleum and Natural Gas Systems). Subpart W requires reporting of methane and CO2 emissions from petroleum-and-natural-gas-system equipment and operations, including emissions associated with flaring, venting, and fugitive sources. Subpart W operates as an emissions-accounting framework that complements the flaring-and-venting authorisation framework operated by BSEE and state regulators.

The Subpart W reporting threshold (the facility-size threshold above which Subpart W reporting applies) and the calculation-methodology specifications are subject to revision; operators reporting under Subpart W should consult the current 40 CFR Part 98 text directly. Subpart W is **adjacent** to this page's scope rather than within it — the methane-emissions-accounting framework is a separate regulatory layer from the flaring-and-venting authorisation framework that this page covers.

## SCADA-and-historian coupling

Flaring volumes are typically tracked through metering at the flare-stack inlet, with the metering data flowing through plant SCADA (see sister sub-issue 3 page `production-scada-architecture.md`) into the operator's data historian (see sister sub-issue 3 page `production-data-historian-patterns.md`). The historian-side data flow then feeds the regulatory-reporting workflow that produces the periodic flaring reports for state and federal regulators.

The SCADA-and-historian coupling is operationally important: data-quality issues in the SCADA-or-historian layer can produce reporting errors that surface as regulatory non-compliance. Operators with material flaring volumes maintain explicit data-quality discipline on the flare-metering data path for this reason.

## Coupling with venting-and-fugitive-emissions surveillance

Modern methane-emissions regulation in several jurisdictions (notably EPA OOOOa/b under the US Clean Air Act, and emerging EU methane regulation) requires operators to perform periodic surveillance of equipment-leak fugitive emissions through Leak Detection and Repair (LDAR) programs. LDAR is operationally adjacent to the flaring-and-venting authorisation framework but is regulated as a separate compliance workflow with its own surveillance, reporting, and enforcement framing.

## Satellite-observation framing

Satellite-observation-based flaring-volume estimation has emerged as an independent verification mechanism for flaring volumes, complementing the operator-reported and regulator-collected data. The dominant satellite-observation methodology uses NOAA's VIIRS (Visible Infrared Imaging Radiometer Suite) instrument data to detect and quantify flare-stack combustion from low-earth orbit; the methodology has been published in peer-reviewed literature and is used by GGFR to produce its global flaring-volume estimates.

Operationally, the satellite-observation methodology produces:

- **Independent verification of reported flaring volumes** — comparing satellite-observed estimates to operator-reported estimates surfaces material discrepancies that flag data-quality issues on either side
- **Detection of unreported or under-reported flaring** — for jurisdictions with weak regulatory-reporting frameworks or for non-cooperating operators, satellite observation produces the only publicly-available flaring data
- **Global-scale tracking of flaring trends** — supporting policy analysis on the effectiveness of flaring-reduction commitments at jurisdiction and operator level

The satellite-observation framing operates as an external-verification layer on top of the operator-side reporting and regulator-side authorisation framework. Some operators integrate satellite-observation data into their internal flaring-volume verification workflows as a quality-assurance mechanism.

## Vendor-software framing

Flare-and-vent reporting software vendors include emissions-management specialists alongside the broader production-accounting vendors (TIBCO, OGsys, Quorum, Enverus). Satellite-observation services that produce independent estimates of flaring volumes (used by GGFR and by some operators for internal-verification purposes) include several specialty providers; this wiki cites them by name and capability class only, without transcribing proprietary methodology.
