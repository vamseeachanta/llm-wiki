# Frac Fluids

## Scope

Frac fluids — the viscosified carrier fluids that transport proppant from surface pumping equipment into a propagating hydraulic fracture — are the load-bearing piece of frac-design hardware between proppant logistics and frac-mechanics performance. Five fluid families cover the practical operator envelope: slickwater (shale workhorse), linear gel (moderate-temp conventional), crosslinked gel (high-temp / high-conductivity), energized (CO2 / N2 charged), and foamed (>50% gas-fraction). This page covers each family's rheology, proppant-transport characteristics, and cleanup logic, plus the selection framework that maps reservoir conditions to fluid choice.

This page is a deeper-dive companion to the [Hydraulic Fracturing](hydraulic-fracturing.md) router. The frac-fluid viscosity testing methodology is captured in [API RP 39](../standards/api-rp-39.md).

## The five frac-fluid families

| Family | Typical viscosity at BHST | Proppant transport mechanism | Cleanup penalty | Workhorse application |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Slickwater** | < 10 cP | Turbulence (rate-dominated) | Minimal | Shale fracs |
| **Linear gel** | 30-100 cP | Carrier viscosity | Moderate | Moderate-temp conventional fracs |
| **Crosslinked gel** | 100-1,000+ cP | Carrier viscosity (high) | Significant | High-temp / high-conductivity conventional fracs |
| **Energized (CO2 / N2)** | Variable | Carrier viscosity + gas-assisted flowback | Variable | Water-sensitive / low-pressure fracs |
| **Foamed** | 100-500+ cP (foam) | Foam viscosity | Variable | Severely water-sensitive / coal-bed-methane fracs |

Each family is built on a different fluid-chemistry backbone and serves a different formation envelope.

## Slickwater

Slickwater is water with a friction-reducer additive at parts-per-million concentration. The friction reducer is typically a high-molecular-weight polyacrylamide-class polymer that suppresses turbulence-induced friction in pipe flow, allowing very high pump rate at modest treating pressure. The fluid is **very low viscosity** at downhole conditions (close to water).

### Rheology
- Near-Newtonian, viscosity close to water (1-3 cP at 70°F surface, < 10 cP at BHST)
- No crosslinker, no significant polymer concentration in the bulk fluid
- Friction-reduction effect is a turbulence-suppression mechanism, not a viscosity-increase mechanism

### Proppant transport
- **Turbulence-driven** — proppant is suspended primarily by the turbulent eddies generated by the high pump rate, not by carrier-fluid viscosity. This is why slickwater fracs require very high rates (often 80-120+ bpm pumping rate).
- **Proppant-concentration limit** — practical limit ~ 2-3 PPA (pounds-of-proppant-added per gallon) because Stokes-law settling outpaces transport above this concentration.
- **Wellbore-proximity settling** — the majority of proppant settles near the wellbore (within tens of feet of the perforation), forming a wedge-shaped propped region rather than a uniform-conductivity propped frac.

### Cleanup
- **Cleanup-friendly** — minimal residual polymer in the proppant pack; flowback is rapid and the post-treatment conductivity penalty is small.
- The cleanup advantage is precisely what makes slickwater the workhorse for shale fracs, where the formation permeability is so low that even modest proppant-pack damage destroys the well's deliverability.

### Application envelope
- **Shale** — Bakken, Eagle Ford, Marcellus, Permian Basin (Wolfcamp, Bone Spring), Haynesville, Niobrara, others.
- **Tight-oil and tight-gas plays** where conductivity demand is moderate and cleanup is critical.
- Not typically used in conventional moderate-to-high-permeability formations where conductivity demand is high.

## Linear gel

Linear gel is water with a linear-polymer thickener — most commonly guar gum or a derivative (hydroxypropyl-guar HPG, hydroxypropyl-cellulose, carboxymethyl-cellulose). No crosslinker is added; the polymer chains are extended in solution but not cross-bonded into a network.

### Rheology
- Power-law non-Newtonian fluid
- Apparent viscosity at downhole shear rate typically 30-100 cP
- Viscosity drops gradually with temperature (linear-polymer chains degrade slowly at typical BHST)
- No crosslinker activation kinetics — viscosity is at maximum from mixing

### Proppant transport
- **Carrier-viscosity-driven** — proppant is suspended by the carrier-fluid viscosity per Stokes-law modified for non-Newtonian behaviour.
- **Proppant-concentration ceiling** — practical limit ~ 4-6 PPA, set by transport capacity and screen-out risk near the wellbore.
- More uniform proppant distribution across the frac length than slickwater.

### Cleanup
- **Moderate cleanup penalty** — residual linear polymer in the proppant pack reduces conductivity. Cleanup is faster than for crosslinked systems because there is no polymer network to degrade.
- Enzyme or oxidative breakers are commonly co-pumped to accelerate polymer degradation post-treatment.

### Application envelope
- Moderate-temperature conventional formations where slickwater's transport capability is insufficient but the cleanup penalty of crosslinked systems is unacceptable.
- Some smaller-job conventional vertical-well fracs.
- Often a fallback when high-temp crosslinker chemistry is unsuitable.

## Crosslinked gel

Crosslinked gel is water + linear-polymer thickener (guar or HPG) + a crosslinker (borate, zirconate, titanate, or aluminate, depending on temperature requirement). The crosslinker bonds the linear polymer chains into a 3D network, generating very high apparent viscosity at downhole shear rate.

### Rheology
- Highly non-Newtonian, viscosity strongly shear-thinning
- Apparent viscosity at downhole shear rate typically 100-1,000+ cP, characterised under [API RP 39](../standards/api-rp-39.md)
- **Crosslinker-activation kinetics** — the crosslink reaction has a temperature-dependent activation time, so the surface mix is initially low-viscosity (pumpable) and crosslinks to full viscosity at downhole temperature. The activation-time window must match the pumping schedule.
- Borate crosslinkers are reversible (pH-dependent); zirconate / titanate crosslinkers are non-reversible

### Crosslinker selection
- **Borate** — workhorse for low-to-moderate BHST (< ~225°F). Reversible chemistry — viscosity can be re-developed in flowback if early breakdown occurs. Cleanup-friendly.
- **Zirconate** — moderate-to-high BHST (200-275°F). Non-reversible. More aggressive thermal-stability than borate.
- **Titanate** — high BHST (250-325°F). Non-reversible. Highest thermal stability.
- **Aluminate** — specialty applications.

### Proppant transport
- **High carrier-viscosity-driven** — proppant transport is excellent; high concentrations (8-12+ PPA) are pumpable.
- The most uniform proppant distribution of any fluid family.

### Cleanup
- **Significant cleanup penalty** — residual crosslinked polymer in the proppant pack significantly reduces conductivity if not fully degraded. The post-treatment propped-pack conductivity can be 20-50% of the lab-measured value if breaker performance is inadequate.
- Aggressive enzyme + oxidative breaker programmes are standard; breaker-mass-balance calculations are part of every crosslinked-gel job design.
- **High-temp tail-out** — at very high BHST (> 275°F), thermal degradation of the polymer can supplement chemical-breaker action; on the other hand, the crosslinker-activation kinetics in the same temperature range can be over-aggressive and screen out the frac before treatment design completes.

### Application envelope
- **Conventional high-permeability, high-rate frac jobs** where high conductivity dominates the productivity gain calculation.
- **Frac-pack** treatments (see [Frac Packing](frac-packing.md)) — TSO designs require the high proppant-concentration ramp that crosslinked carriers enable.
- **High-BHST formations** where slickwater proppant transport is inadequate and linear-gel viscosity is insufficient.

## Energized (CO2 / N2)

Energized frac fluids are otherwise-standard frac fluids (slickwater, linear, crosslinked) with carbon dioxide or nitrogen gas pre-charged in solution or as a dispersed phase. The gas remains in solution during pumping (high pressure) and evolves out as the formation pressure drops during flowback, providing a **flowback drive** independent of formation reservoir energy.

### CO2-energized
- Carbon dioxide is highly soluble in many frac-fluid bases (especially water-based)
- CO2 evolution during flowback provides volumetric expansion that drives water out of the formation
- Particularly effective for **water-sensitive clay formations** where capillary water trapping in the matrix limits cleanup

### N2-energized
- Nitrogen is less soluble than CO2 but provides similar flowback drive
- Inert chemistry — no reaction with formation minerals or fluid additives
- Used where CO2 is unsuitable (e.g. concerns about carbonate-mineral reaction or downstream-pipeline acceptance)

### Operating envelope
- **Water-sensitive formations** where standard water-based cleanup leaves trapped water (water blocks) that destroy the well's deliverability
- **Low-pressure formations** where reservoir energy is insufficient to drive flowback unaided
- Cost premium relative to standard water-based: CO2 and N2 supply, pumping-equipment surface capacity, additional surface-handling complexity

## Foamed

Foamed frac fluids are frac-fluid base + gas (N2 or CO2) blended at high gas fraction (often 60-80% by volume, sometimes higher) to form a stable foam at the pumping conditions. The foam has high apparent viscosity from the bubble-bubble interaction, providing proppant transport with very low liquid content.

### Rheology
- Foam viscosity scales with gas fraction and bubble-size distribution
- Apparent viscosity 100-500+ cP depending on foam quality
- Stability is the key engineering challenge — the foam must remain stable through surface mixing, downhole pumping, and frac propagation

### Operating envelope
- **Severely water-sensitive formations** (some coal-bed methane plays, some tight-gas formations with extreme clay-swelling sensitivity)
- **Low-pressure formations** where flowback drive is the gating problem
- Highest cost and most surface-equipment complexity of any frac-fluid family — foam mixing requires specialised surface units and the gas supply is the major cost driver.

## Additives that cut across all families

Each frac-fluid family is built on a base + additive package. Common additive classes:

- **Friction reducer** — slickwater bulk additive; trace in other families
- **Polymer thickener** — guar, HPG, cellulose derivatives; sets viscosity in linear and crosslinked systems
- **Crosslinker** — borate, zirconate, titanate, aluminate; activates the polymer network in crosslinked gel
- **Buffer** — pH control for crosslinker activation
- **Clay control** — KCl substitute (often choline chloride or tetramethylammonium chloride) to prevent clay swelling in formation
- **Iron control** — chelants (citric acid, EDTA) to prevent precipitation of iron-bearing scale
- **Biocide** — to prevent bacterial growth in the polymer-laden fluid system
- **Enzyme breaker** — degrades polymer post-treatment (low-temp systems)
- **Oxidative breaker** — degrades polymer post-treatment (high-temp systems)
- **Scale inhibitor** — to prevent post-treatment scale formation in the producing well
- **Surfactant** — to assist flowback through capillary-water-block mitigation; tested per API RP 42

The specific recipes are vendor-proprietary and **not reproduced** in this wiki — vendors (Halliburton, Schlumberger, Baker Hughes, Liberty, ProFrac, and independents) market frac-fluid systems as integrated chemical packages with proprietary additive composition and proprietary on-the-fly mixing workflows.

## Rheology testing — API RP 39 framework

The viscosity-vs-time-vs-temperature characterisation that feeds frac-design simulators is performed under [API RP 39](../standards/api-rp-39.md) for crosslinked water-based systems. The methodology is also widely adapted (with documented modifications) for linear-gel, energized, and foamed systems where formal RP coverage is less developed.

Key test outputs that feed the frac-design simulator:

- **Viscosity-vs-time at BHST and fixed shear rate** — primary input for proppant-transport calculation and pump-schedule duration design
- **Viscosity-vs-shear-rate sweep at BHST** — characterises the non-Newtonian model parameters (power-law indices, Herschel-Bulkley parameters)
- **Viscosity-vs-temperature ramp** — characterises the crosslinker-activation kinetics for crosslinked systems
- **Viscosity decay after breaker addition** — characterises post-treatment cleanup time

## Selection framework — matching fluid to formation

A simplified operator decision tree for fluid selection:

1. **Permeability first.** Shale / tight (sub-mD) → slickwater + sand. Moderate-perm conventional → linear or crosslinked + sand/RCS. High-perm conventional → crosslinked + sand/RCS/ceramic.
2. **BHST second.** < 225°F → borate crosslinker (or linear gel). 225-275°F → zirconate. > 275°F → titanate or specialty.
3. **Water sensitivity third.** Mild → standard water-based. Moderate → energized (CO2 / N2). Severe → foamed.
4. **Conductivity target fourth.** Low (slickwater-acceptable) → slickwater. Moderate → linear gel. High → crosslinked gel.
5. **Cleanup tolerance fifth.** Cleanup-critical → slickwater or linear-gel. Cleanup-tolerable → crosslinked.

## Common operator mistakes

1. **Crosslinker-activation timing mismatch.** A crosslinker designed for a different BHST activates either too early (screens out before frac geometry is established) or too late (insufficient viscosity during proppant transport). The pre-job RP 39 temperature-ramp test is the operator's primary defence.
2. **Underestimated cleanup penalty in crosslinked systems.** Lab-measured propped-pack conductivity often overstates field conductivity by 2-5x if breaker performance is inadequate. Real-time flowback-rate monitoring is the only practical post-treatment cleanup-quality measure.
3. **Slickwater proppant-concentration over-design.** Pushing slickwater above 2-3 PPA risks screen-out at the wellbore. The historical impulse to "load more proppant per gallon" must be tempered by the carrier-fluid's transport limit.
4. **Foamed-fluid stability failure.** Foam stability is sensitive to surface mixing quality, gas-supply pressure stability, and downhole shear conditions. A foam that becomes a slug-flow mixture downhole loses proppant transport and screens out.
