# PCP Heavy-Oil Application

## Scope

PCP dominates heavy-oil and bitumen production where ESP and rod pump struggle. Heavy oil (API gravity 10-22°) and bitumen (< 10° API) have high viscosity at downhole temperature — kinematic-friction losses in centrifugal-pump impeller passages reduce ESP head dramatically, and rod-string drag in heavy fluid limits rod-pump rate. PCP's positive-displacement design + low-shear cavity geometry handles viscous fluid efficiently.

## Major heavy-oil production basins and PCP use

- **Western Canada (Alberta heavy oil + Saskatchewan medium-heavy)** — PCP standard for most cold-flow heavy-oil wells; SAGD-injected wells also use PCP on the production side
- **Venezuelan Faja (extra-heavy)** — historic PCP use; production challenges due to political/economic factors
- **California heavy oil** (Kern River, Belridge, San Joaquin Valley) — PCP standard for thermal EOR (steam-flood) production
- **Colombia / Brazil heavy-oil onshore** — PCP common

## Sand control by design

PCP cavities have **smooth low-shear flow paths** compared with ESP impeller passages. Sand passes through without abrading rotor or stator significantly (within sand-load limits — extreme sand still wears the elastomer eventually).

Operators in sand-producing reservoirs often deploy PCP **without** dedicated sand-control completions (gravel pack / screens) and accept faster pump replacement intervals as the trade-off.

## Cold-flow vs thermal applications

- **Cold-flow heavy oil** — produces by reservoir pressure + PCP lift; works for API gravities above ~12°
- **Steam-assisted gravity drainage (SAGD)** — bitumen heated by steam injection; PCP lifts the heated, lower-viscosity production. Elastomer must handle elevated temperature (HNBR or specialty grade).
- **Cyclic steam injection (CSI / huff-and-puff)** — alternating injection / production cycles; PCP handles the production cycle

## Design considerations

- **Elastomer selection** — HNBR or specialty grade for thermal applications; NBR for cold-flow if no aromatic content
- **Higher torque rating** — viscous fluid requires more rotational power
- **Lower RPM** — slow rotation gives elastomer time to recover from cavity deformation
- **Rod-string design** — heavier-grade rods needed for surface-driven; ESPCP avoids the rod-string limit
- **Stuffing-box sealing** — viscous fluid tends to seal better naturally but generates more frictional heat
