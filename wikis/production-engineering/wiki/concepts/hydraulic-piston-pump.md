# Hydraulic Piston Pump

## Scope

A downhole reciprocating piston pump driven by **power fluid** pumped from surface via a parallel string. Distinct from jet pump (no moving parts) — the hydraulic piston pump HAS moving downhole parts (piston + valves) but uses hydraulic energy rather than electric or rotary mechanical energy to drive them.

## How it works

1. High-pressure power fluid is pumped down a power-fluid string from surface
2. Downhole, the power fluid drives a "motor" piston back and forth
3. The motor piston is mechanically connected to a "production" piston in the parallel production string
4. The production piston, on each stroke, lifts produced fluid up the tubing
5. Power fluid + produced fluid return to surface (commingled mode) or separately (closed-loop)

## Two configurations

### Open / commingled

- Power fluid + produced fluid commingle in the return string
- Simpler surface handling — single separator handles the combined stream
- Lower CAPEX
- Most common configuration

### Closed-loop (concentric or parallel)

- Power fluid stays separate from produced fluid via concentric tubing arrangement
- Power fluid recirculated cleanly at surface; tighter quality control
- Higher CAPEX; specialty applications (sour service, asphaltene-bearing crude)

## Operating envelope

- **Rate**: 200-12,000 bbl/d
- **Depth**: works in deep wells; deeper than ESP envelope
- **Free-gas tolerance**: low to moderate (the production piston cavitates if intake gas is high)
- **Wellbore deviation**: works in deviated wells; horizontal wells more challenging

## Strengths and weaknesses

| Strength | Weakness |
|---|---|
| Tolerant of deep wells where ESP fails | Power-fluid system at surface adds complexity |
| Moving parts are at depth but no rotation (less wear than rod-pump rod-string) | Lower efficiency than ESP at design point |
| Tolerates moderate solids | Piston-and-valve wear in solids-bearing service |
| Works in deviated wells | Higher CAPEX than rod pump |
