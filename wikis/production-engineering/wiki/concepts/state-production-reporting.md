# State Production Reporting

## Scope

State-level production reporting is the operator-facing regulatory workflow that satisfies state oil-and-gas conservation regulators' requirements for permit-tracking, completion-tracking, and ongoing production-volume reporting on onshore wells. Unlike federal OCS reporting (which operates under a single regulatory framework — see [Federal Production Reporting](federal-production-reporting.md)), state-side reporting operates under jurisdiction-specific frameworks that differ across producing states.

This page covers the Texas Railroad Commission (RRC) framework as the practitioner-canonical primary, with brief analog framing for Louisiana, North Dakota, Oklahoma, and the major-producing-state framework class. Operators with multi-state portfolios manage cross-state rule differences as part of their production-accounting infrastructure; this page covers the framework concept and the form-class taxonomy rather than transcribing form-field-level detail.

## Texas RRC framework

The Texas Railroad Commission has regulatory authority over oil-and-gas operations in Texas, including production reporting, conservation-regulation rate-control (see [Production Allowable Rates](production-allowable-rates.md)), well-permit issuance, well-completion tracking, and orphan-well prevention. The RRC framework is the longest-established and most-imitated state-side oil-and-gas regulatory framework in the United States; many other state regulators model their forms and workflows on the RRC pattern with state-specific variations.

The RRC reporting workflow operates through a series of forms that operators file at different points in the well lifecycle:

### Form W-1 — Application for Permit to Drill, Recomplete, or Re-Enter

The Form W-1 (and the related W-1H for horizontal drilling) is filed before drilling operations commence and serves as the operator's application for permit-to-drill. The form captures lease information, well location, target formation, planned operations, and the operator's compliance history. Once the W-1 is approved, the operator may commence drilling under the permit. Re-completions to a different target formation and re-entries of plugged wells use the same form-class with operational-history attachments.

### Form W-2 — Oil Well Completion Report

The Form W-2 is filed after well completion and captures the as-completed well configuration: total depth, casing strings and depths, completion interval, perforation density, completion method (cased-hole vs open-hole, fracture-stimulated vs natural-completion), and the initial production-test results. The W-2 is the regulatory record that establishes the well's compliance baseline; subsequent reporting tracks the well against the W-2-established baseline.

A companion G-1 form (Gas Well Completion Report) serves the same role for gas wells. The form-class distinction between oil and gas wells follows the state's well-classification framework — wells are classified as oil-wells, gas-wells, or casinghead-gas-wells based on the producing GOR and other criteria, and the form-class follows the classification.

### Form P-4 — Producer's Transportation Authority

The Form P-4 establishes the operator-of-record for the well's production for purposes of routing produced volumes to gathering and transportation. The form captures the operator-of-record identity, the gathering-system connection, and the production-stream routing. The P-4 is the regulatory mechanism that links the well-side production-tracking to the downstream gathering-side volume-tracking; both sides must agree on the operator-of-record and the routing for the regulatory accounting to reconcile.

### Form PR — Producer's Production Report

The Form PR is the monthly production-volume report that each operator submits for each producing lease. The PR captures the monthly produced volumes of oil, gas, and water by lease, the dispositions (sold to purchaser, used as lease fuel, flared, vented, injected back into the formation), and the inventory balance at month-end. The PR is the regulatory record of monthly production against which allowable-rate compliance is assessed (see [Production Allowable Rates](production-allowable-rates.md)) and is the data source for state-level production statistics.

### Other RRC forms

The RRC framework includes additional forms covering specific operations: H-15 for SCP (sustained-casing-pressure) reporting and remediation, H-1 for casing-cementing reports, W-3 for plug-and-abandonment reports, W-12 for inspection reports, P-5 for organizational reports identifying the operator entity itself. The form catalogue is extensive; operators consume the forms relevant to their operational mix.

### Statewide Rules

The Texas RRC operates under a body of Statewide Rules that establish the regulatory requirements that the forms operationalise. Several Statewide Rules of particular relevance to production reporting:

- **Statewide Rule 13** — casing, cementing, drilling, well-control, and completion requirements
- **Statewide Rule 14** — plugging requirements for abandoned wells
- **Statewide Rule 16** — lease automatic custody-transfer (LACT) measurement requirements
- **Statewide Rule 32** — flaring and venting authorization; see [Gas Flaring Rules](gas-flaring-rules.md)
- **Statewide Rule 36** — hydrogen-sulfide-containing operations
- **Statewide Rule 79** — definitions and classifications

The Statewide Rules are publicly retrievable from rrc.texas.gov. Operators preparing compliance documentation should consult the current rule-text rather than secondary summaries; the rules are amended periodically and the current version takes precedence.

### Electronic submission

The RRC operates electronic-submission platforms for most form-classes. Operators submit forms through web-based interfaces and (for high-volume reporters) through bulk-upload pathways with format specifications published by the RRC. The electronic-submission platforms operate alongside the regulatory framework — the form-content requirements are set by Statewide Rules, and the platform-side details (file formats, submission cadences, error-handling) are set by the RRC's IT infrastructure. Both layers are subject to revision; operators should consult the current RRC technical documentation for platform-side detail.

## State-level analog framing

Other major producing states maintain regulatory frameworks that parallel the RRC pattern with state-specific variations. The Texas RRC framework is the operationally-dominant model for several historical reasons (Texas is the largest US oil producer, the RRC framework predates most other state frameworks, and operators with multi-state portfolios typically build accounting infrastructure around the RRC pattern first). Other-state frameworks are summarised here at form-class level only; operators producing in each state should consult the state regulator directly for current form-content requirements.

### Louisiana — Office of Conservation (OOC)

The Louisiana Department of Natural Resources Office of Conservation maintains the regulatory framework for oil-and-gas operations in Louisiana. The OOC framework includes a Form DM-1R (Monthly Oil and Gas Production Report) that parallels the RRC PR form, plus permit and completion forms that parallel the RRC W-1 and W-2 forms. The substantive regulatory requirements differ in detail from the Texas framework; the form-class structure is similar.

### North Dakota — Industrial Commission (IC)

The North Dakota Industrial Commission Department of Mineral Resources Oil and Gas Division maintains the regulatory framework for North Dakota oil-and-gas operations. The Bakken-play production boom that ramped from approximately 2008 onward placed the NDIC framework under operational stress and drove a substantial expansion of the regulator's electronic-submission infrastructure. Production-reporting forms in the NDIC framework parallel the RRC pattern with North Dakota-specific variations (e.g. the Bakken-play-specific gas-capture targets that emerged from the flaring-rule revisions).

### Oklahoma — Corporation Commission (OCC)

The Oklahoma Corporation Commission Oil and Gas Conservation Division maintains the regulatory framework for Oklahoma oil-and-gas operations. The OCC framework follows the broad form-class pattern with Oklahoma-specific variations; the SCOOP/STACK-play production growth has driven OCC framework revisions over the past decade similar to the NDIC pattern.

### Other major-producing-state regulators

- **Pennsylvania DEP** — Marcellus and Utica wet-gas and dry-gas production reporting
- **Colorado COGCC** (now Energy and Carbon Management Commission, ECMC) — Wattenberg, Piceance, and other Colorado-basin reporting
- **New Mexico OCD** — Permian-basin (New Mexico side) reporting
- **California CalGEM** — California-basin reporting under the state's stringent environmental-regulation framework
- **Wyoming WOGCC**, **Alaska AOGCC**, **Kansas KCC**, **West Virginia DEP** — other major-producing-state frameworks

The cross-state rule-and-form variation is substantial. Operators with multi-state portfolios typically build production-accounting infrastructure that includes state-specific reporting pipelines and consume vendor software (see Vendor framing below) that abstracts cross-state differences behind a unified operator-facing interface.

## Operator-of-record discipline

A recurring operational pattern across state regulatory frameworks is the operator-of-record discipline: the regulator tracks each well against a single operator-of-record (captured on the RRC P-4 form in Texas and on analog forms in other states), and the operator-of-record is the entity responsible for compliance with production-reporting, allowable-rate-tracking, and remediation obligations. Transfers of operatorship (sales of well-operating interests between operators) trigger regulator-side update workflows that must be completed for the new operator to take over the compliance obligations.

The operator-of-record discipline is operationally important for:

- **Well-portfolio sales and acquisitions** — the buying-and-selling operators must coordinate the operator-of-record update through the regulator to ensure continuity of compliance reporting
- **Orphan-well prevention** — when an operator becomes insolvent or otherwise unable to maintain its operator-of-record obligations, the regulator's operator-of-record tracking is the mechanism through which the regulator identifies orphan wells and pursues plug-and-abandonment under the state's orphan-well program
- **Cross-regulatory coordination** — for wells with mixed federal-and-state obligations (BLM-onshore wells, particularly), the operator-of-record at state level must align with the operator-of-record at federal level for the dual-reporting workflow to reconcile

## Filing cadence and electronic-submission framing

Filing cadences across state regulators converge on a monthly pattern for production-volume reports (the PR equivalent in each state) and on event-triggered cadences for permit-and-completion forms (filed at the operational event rather than on a periodic schedule). Specific cadences and deadlines differ across states; some states impose late-filing penalties on monthly production reports filed after the regulatory deadline.

Electronic-submission platform maturity varies across state regulators. The major-producing-state regulators (Texas RRC, NDIC, OCC, OOC) operate mature electronic-submission platforms with bulk-upload pathways. Smaller-volume state regulators may operate paper-or-PDF-driven workflows that high-volume operators find operationally limiting.

## Coupling with federal reporting

For wells producing on federally-administered lands (BLM-administered onshore), operators may face dual reporting requirements: state-side reporting under the state's framework and federal-side reporting under the BLM/ONRR framework. The dual-reporting pattern is operationally distinct from the federal-OCS pattern (where federal-only reporting applies) and from the all-fee-lands onshore pattern (where state-only reporting applies). See [Federal Production Reporting](federal-production-reporting.md) for the federal side.

## Coupling with allowable-rate compliance

State production reporting is the data source against which allowable-rate compliance is assessed. Monthly PR filings establish the produced volumes for each lease; the allowable-rate framework (see [Production Allowable Rates](production-allowable-rates.md)) consumes the PR data and tracks over-and-under-production carryover against the regulatory allowable.

## Severance-tax interface

Adjacent to the conservation-regulator production-reporting workflow is the state-side severance-tax workflow that consumes production-volume data and produces state-tax obligations on produced hydrocarbons. The severance-tax framework is operationally distinct from the conservation-regulation framework — typically administered by the state's revenue department (e.g., Texas Comptroller of Public Accounts for the Texas severance tax) rather than by the oil-and-gas conservation regulator — but is coupled through the underlying production-volume data.

Operators typically maintain production-accounting infrastructure that produces both the conservation-regulator monthly production reports (PR-form-class) and the severance-tax monthly returns from the same underlying production-allocation source. The two workflows have different deadline structures, different form-content requirements, and different audit-and-reconciliation regimes, but they consume the same primary production data.

The severance-tax framework is operationally substantive but is outside this wiki's scope; this page references the interface to flag the operational coupling.

## Vendor-software framing

Production-accounting software vendors that handle multi-state regulatory-reporting workflows include TIBCO, OGsys, Quorum, Enverus (formerly Drillinginfo), and several specialty independents. These vendors abstract cross-state rule differences behind unified operator-facing interfaces; they are cited by name and general capability only. Proprietary form-encoding internals, regulator-platform-integration details, and state-rule-specific encoding pipelines are not transcribed in this wiki.

## Notes

- Form-content requirements, Statewide Rules, and platform-side details are subject to revision. Operators preparing compliance documentation should consult the current rule-text and current form-content specifications from the relevant state regulator directly; this page summarises the framework at concept level only.
- The Texas RRC framework is treated as the practitioner-canonical primary not because Texas is operationally dominant in all senses, but because the RRC framework is the longest-established and most-imitated state-side oil-and-gas regulatory framework; operators with multi-state portfolios typically build accounting infrastructure around the RRC pattern first and extend to other states with state-specific variations.
- State-side regulatory text (Statewide Rules, form-content specifications) is publicly retrievable but, per the wiki's discipline, verbatim text greater than 30 words is not transcribed — operators should consult the regulator's published rule-text directly.
- Cross-state rule variation is substantial enough that operators producing in a new state typically face several months of operational ramp-up to align the state-side reporting infrastructure with the new regulator's framework; the operational lift is not trivial.
