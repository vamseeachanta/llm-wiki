# Plunger Lift

## Scope

Plunger lift uses a free-piston that cycles between bottomhole and surface, sweeping a fluid slug up the tubing on each cycle. Energy comes from **reservoir gas alone** — no external power, no surface pump. The method is the lowest-cost artificial-lift technique but is rate-limited and works only for liquid-loaded gas wells where reservoir gas pressure can lift the plunger and a fluid slug to surface.

## Cycle anatomy

A plunger-lift cycle has four phases:

1. **Afterflow** — well produces gas through the surface choke; liquids accumulate in the tubing-string bottom; gas-injection-tubing pressure rises
2. **Shut-in** — surface motor valve closes; tubing-pressure equalizes (or rises); plunger drops to the bottom-hole bumper spring
3. **Plunger arrival / production** — motor valve opens; gas pressure differential propels plunger to surface, lifting the liquid slug above it
4. **Plunger return** — slug arrives at surface, separated into surface storage; plunger held at lubricator (briefly); next afterflow begins

Cycle time per well typically 10-90 minutes.

## Operating envelope

- **Liquid rate**: < 200 bbl/d (this is the rate-limiting factor — plunger lift is for *low-rate* wells)
- **Gas rate**: typically 200-2,000 Mcf/d (provides the lift energy)
- **Depth**: < 12,000 ft typical
- **Gas-liquid ratio (GLR)**: must exceed minimum for plunger to reach surface; rule-of-thumb 300+ scf/bbl per 1000 ft depth
- **Wellbore deviation**: works in vertical and moderately-deviated wells (< 30-45°); not for horizontal

## Strengths and weaknesses

| Strength | Weakness |
|---|---|
| No surface pump, lowest equipment cost | Rate-limited (< 200 bbl/d liquid) |
| Energy from reservoir gas (no external power) | Requires sufficient GLR for plunger to reach surface |
| Minimal operating complexity | Cycle-time tuning is sensitive |
| Long mean-time-to-failure | Wellbore deviation limit |
