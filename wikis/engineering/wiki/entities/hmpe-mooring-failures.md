# HMPE Mooring Line Failures

Industry-wide failure pattern of High Modulus Polyethylene (HMPE) mooring ropes, primarily affecting LNG carriers during 2007-2011 and continuing through 2023.

## 2007-2011 Industry Pattern

Approximately 3% of HMPE ropes in LNG service failed during this period. Key findings from TTI/DSM Dyneema Users Group investigation:

| Finding | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Failure position | Aft breast lines failed 2x as often as other positions |
| Failure tension | Only 10-60% of MBL — premature degradation, not overload |
| Vessel type | Q-flex (216,000m3) and Q-max (266,000m3) disproportionate |
| Ports affected | Three ports with elevated rates (likely Ras Laffan, South Hook, Withnell Bay) |

### Root Causes Identified

- **Low HMPE content**: Manufacturers reduced content to cut costs
- **Increased lay lengths**: Created internal distortion
- **Axial compression fatigue (ACF)**: Primary degradation mechanism in jacketed HMPE

### Failure Modes Ruled Out

Bending fatigue, tension fatigue, creep rupture, hysteresis heating, chemical degradation, oxidation, UV degradation.

## Notable Incidents

- **Zarga at Milford Haven (2015)**: HMPE rope parted during berthing, severe crew head injury. MAIB found rope was not fit for purpose — jacketed construction increased ACF likelihood. Snap-back over 15m in less than 1 second.
- **BW Fleet in Spain**: 7 HMPE lines failed simultaneously. Jacketed construction hid internal core damage. Fleet-wide replacement with non-jacketed Dyneema SK78 eliminated all further failures.
- **MV Asterix at Busan (2023)**: 30mm HMPE (Samson Quantum 12, Dyneema SK78/polyester blend) parted at pedestal roller. Bosun sustained serious injuries. Crew falsely believed HMPE does not snap back.

## Mitigation: Non-Jacketed Dyneema SK78

BW Fleet Management's fleet-wide switch from jacketed HMPE to non-jacketed Samson AmSteel-Blue (Dyneema SK78) resulted in zero incidents after 1,500+ operating hours per vessel across all ports worldwide.
