# Pipeline Walking

## Scope

Cumulative axial displacement ("walking") of high-temperature subsea pipelines under repeated startup-and-shutdown thermal cycling. This page covers walking mechanisms, end-expansion budget interaction, and anchor-design mitigation. It does NOT cover lateral buckling (see [[pipeline-lateral-buckling]]), upheaval buckling (see [[pipeline-upheaval-buckling]]), or corroded fitness-for-service (see [[pipeline-integrity-assessment]]).

## Key Concepts

- **Asymmetric thermal cycling** — uneven warm-up between hot and cold ends, a tieback-temperature gradient along the pipeline, or seabed-slope-induced gravity bias drives directional displacement per cycle.
- **Three classical mechanisms** — (1) thermal-transient asymmetry, (2) seabed-slope gravity bias, (3) tension at the riser-tieback end transferring axial pull into the flowline.
- **Per-cycle creep** — displacement accumulates millimetre-to-centimetre per cycle; over a 25-year design life this consumes available end-expansion budget at PLETs and spool tie-ins.
- **End-expansion interaction** — walking reduces or exhausts the cold-end expansion budget designed into the [[pipeline-end-expansion-spool-design]] spool/jumper.
- **Mitigation** — anchor structures (suction-pile or driven-pile mid-line anchors), increased axial soil resistance, larger spool envelopes, or deliberate pre-set offset of the spool.
- **Reference literature** — Carr/Bruton walking-mechanism literature (NAME only).
