# Pipeline Route Selection

## Scope

Route engineering for subsea and onshore pipelines: corridor selection, geohazard avoidance, third-party-asset crossings, and free-span vs trench economics. This page does NOT cover corroded pipeline fitness-for-service (see [[pipeline-integrity-assessment]]) or free-span fatigue mechanics (see [[free-span-viv-fatigue]]).

## Key Concepts

- **Corridor selection** — balance shortest-distance against geohazards, fishing-trawl density, and existing-asset proximity; iterate against bathymetry and geophysical survey passes.
- **Geohazard avoidance** — submarine landslides, mud volcanoes, gas seeps, faults, ice gouging, and steep-slope crossings push the route off the geodesic line.
- **Third-party crossings** — over/under existing pipelines, subsea cables, and ship-anchorage corridors require crossing-agreements and engineered protection (mattresses, rock dump, sleepers).
- **Free-span budget** — span height/length limits inherited from the seabed roughness drive trenching, rock-dump, or route-deviation choices upstream of [[free-span-viv-fatigue]].
- **Onshore HDD entry/exit envelope** — horizontal directional drilling at landfalls and infrastructure crossings sets entry/exit pits, drilling-fluid containment, and pull-back tension.
- **Pipelay-vessel weather window** — chosen route influences pipelay duration which feeds the [[pipeline-installation-methods]] vessel selection.
