# Fink (2026) — Cruise-Ship Stability Myths

Raw URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/mario-alexander-fink-78533116_navalarchitecture-marineengineering-shipdesign-share-7458534034877460480--J4N

Author: Mario Alexander Fink (practitioner account, 1,305 followers at time of capture). Format: LinkedIn post (myth-debunk practitioner essay). Date observed: 2026-05-13.

## Relevance

A myth-debunk essay framed around five common public misconceptions about modern cruise-ship stability. Distinguished from popular-explainer posts by quantitative references (form-stability and waterplane-area mechanism, transverse metacentric height GM, righting-arm GZ curve, 40–50° positive-righting heel range, SOLAS damage-stability survivability with multiple flooded compartments). Useful as a citation-quality trigger source pointing at the existing in-wiki stability pages; the quantitative values named are consistent with textbook treatments on those pages.

## Key teachings (as presented by the author)

- **Myth 1 — "Cruise ships need a deep keel for stability."** Reality: modern passenger ships are form-stability vessels. A wide hull with a large waterplane area produces a high transverse metacentric height (GM); the keel is not the load-bearing stability feature.
- **Myth 2 — "Hurricane wind can tip a cruise ship."** Reality: a 150,000 t vessel's inertia and large righting moment dominate the wind heeling moment; the heeling-moment-to-righting-moment ratio at hurricane wind speeds is small compared with the GZ-curve capacity.
- **Myth 3 — "Cruise ships have a top-heavy center of gravity."** Reality: heavy machinery, fuel, and water tanks live deep in the hull; the volumetric centroid of those tanks lowers G and raises GM relative to a naïve "tall superstructure" intuition.
- **Myth 4 — "Cruise ships capsize easily."** Reality: the righting-arm (GZ) curve of a modern cruise ship typically remains positive out to 40–50° of heel before downflooding angle effects bring the curve to zero.
- **Myth 5 — "Flooding a compartment will sink the ship."** Reality: SOLAS damage-stability criteria require survivability with multiple compartments flooded; subdivision and damage-control architecture make single-compartment flooding a designed-survivable case.

## Limitations of this source

The post asserts the conclusions but does not derive them — GM is named without the BM − BG decomposition, the GZ curve is named without the integrated-area energy criterion, and the SOLAS damage-stability framing does not distinguish deterministic from probabilistic (SOLAS Ch II-1 Part B-1) treatments. A skeptical reader cannot reproduce the numbers from the post alone; reproduction requires the textbook and IMO references on the concept pages.
