# Marine Propulsors

## Scope

This page catalogues the principal propulsor types installed on commercial and naval ships and summarizes their selection drivers. It complements `concepts/propeller-theory.md` (which covers the open-water performance of an isolated propeller) and `concepts/ship-resistance-components.md` (which covers what the propulsor must overcome). Detailed thrust/torque coefficient theory lives on the propeller-theory page; this is a comparative-survey page.

## Key Concepts

- **Fixed-pitch propeller (FPP)** — single-piece blades cast or bolted to the hub; simplest and most efficient at design point; speed control is via shaft RPM.
- **Controllable-pitch propeller (CPP)** — blade angle adjustable in service; trades peak efficiency for off-design flexibility, reverse without shaft reversal, and fine maneuvering control.
- **Podded propulsion** — electrically driven motor housed in an external pod that swivels through 360 degrees; combines propulsion and steering; common on cruise ships.
- **Azimuth thruster** — Z-drive or L-drive train transmits engine power to a steerable lower unit; widely used on tugs, OSVs, and dynamic-positioning vessels.
- **Waterjet** — internal pump accelerates water through a steerable nozzle; favored at high speeds (>30 kn) where cavitation limits propellers.
- **Contra-rotating propellers** — two coaxial propellers turning in opposite directions; recovers swirl energy at the cost of mechanical complexity.
- **Cycloidal (Voith-Schneider) propeller** — vertical-axis blades with cyclic pitch; instantaneous thrust vectoring; specialized harbor-tug and ferry use.
- **Ducted (Kort) propeller** — accelerating or decelerating duct around the blade tips; raises bollard thrust at low advance speed.
- **Pre-swirl and post-swirl ducts / fins** — energy-saving devices that condition the inflow or recover swirl downstream.
