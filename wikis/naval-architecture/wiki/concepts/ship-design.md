# Ship Design Process

Systematic approach to naval architecture vessel design from concept to construction.

## Design Phases

1. **Conceptual design** — preliminary dimensions, hull form selection, power estimate
2. **Preliminary design** — refined hull form, general arrangement, weight estimate
3. **Contract design** — specification development, cost estimation, tender preparation
4. **Detailed design** — structural drawings, systems design, class approval support
5. **Production design** — construction drawings, assembly sequence, outfitting

## Design Spirals and Methods

- **Parametric design** — systematic variation of key dimensions and coefficients
- **Design spiral** — iterative process converging on optimal solution
- **Weight estimation** — empirical formulas and lightship weight prediction
- **Power estimation** — Holtrop-Mennen, series-based, and CFD methods
- **Cost estimation** — capital and operating cost modeling

## Disciplines and adjacent expertise

Per Sherwood (2026) — see [source page](../sources/sherwood-2026-naval-architect-listening.md) — the naval architect's scope of discipline can be split into **core** technical domains the architect owns directly and **adjacent / supporting** expertise the architect must integrate from other specialists during conceptual through detailed design.

**Core domains owned by the naval architect:**

- Arrangements and capacities
- Hydrodynamics
- Propulsion
- Stability
- Structural design

**Adjacent expertise the naval architect must integrate:**

- Electrical systems
- Mechanical systems
- HVAC
- Fitout
- Vessel operations — crewing, maintenance, operating costs
- Regulations and compliance

**Practitioner observation:** missing critical input from adjacent disciplines at the start of, and during, execution forfeits opportunities to improve the product. The architect's technical expertise is necessary but not sufficient; stakeholder-listening discipline determines whether that expertise translates into a better vessel.

### Public references for the scope-of-discipline framing

The Sherwood essay is a compact practitioner-level enumeration; the textbook references below cover the same scope-of-discipline territory in depth and are the canonical citables a wiki page should anchor on.

- **Lamb, T. (Ed.) (2003).** *Ship Design and Construction*, 2 vols. Society of Naval Architects and Marine Engineers (SNAME). ISBN 0-939773-40-6 (Vol. I), 0-939773-41-4 (Vol. II). 55-chapter SNAME compendium covering the full design-and-construction span — design phases, structures, machinery, electrical, outfitting, regulations, and shipyard production — i.e. the union of Sherwood's "core" and "adjacent" lists.
- **Watson, D.G.M. (1998).** *Practical Ship Design*. Elsevier Ocean Engineering Series, Vol. 1. Elsevier Science. ISBN 978-0-08-042999-1. Single-author treatment spanning concept design, detail design, structural design, hydrodynamics, regulations, specifications, and economics; treats both merchant and naval vessels.
