# Mark Prentice: R5 Mooring Chain Hardness Limit Explained

Source captured from the public LinkedIn post page:
- `https://www.linkedin.com/posts/mark-prentice-185722115_offshoremooring-mooringchain-r5chain-share-7445394222393810945-Gq9n`

## Summary

The post explains the engineering reason behind limiting offshore R5 mooring chain surface hardness to approximately 340 HB. The central claim is that very high-strength mooring chain operates in a coupled environment of:
- high tensile loading
- seawater exposure
- cathodic protection
- hydrogen generation at the steel surface

Under those conditions, excessive surface hardness and local hard spots increase susceptibility to hydrogen-induced stress cracking (HISC) / hydrogen embrittlement.

The post frames the hardness limit as a practical manufacturing and integrity control rather than an arbitrary specification value.

## Key Technical Claims Captured from the Post

- R5 chain is attractive because of high tensile strength and weight/cost advantages in deepwater mooring systems.
- Cathodic protection can generate atomic hydrogen that diffuses into high-strength steels.
- Local hard spots created by poor heat treatment, grinding, or handling damage can act as crack initiation sites.
- Surface hardness limits are used to maintain a tempered microstructure with better resistance to embrittlement.
- The hardness limit is only one part of a wider control system that also includes heat-treatment qualification and broader material-property controls.

## What This Source Does Not Prove on Its Own

This post should not be treated as a design or acceptance basis by itself. It does not provide:
- project-specific qualification evidence
- hardness mapping methodology for a specific procurement lot
- fracture-toughness or fatigue data
- weld/HAZ-specific criteria for a given manufacturing route
- numerical CP potential limits for a particular chain/environment combination

Those need standards, manufacturer qualification records, and project-specific engineering evidence.
