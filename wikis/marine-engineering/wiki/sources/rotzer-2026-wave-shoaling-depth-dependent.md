# Rötzer (2026) — Why a 3 m Wave Doesn't Mean the Same Everywhere

Raw URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/daniel-r%C3%B6tzer-807a1577_why-a-3-m-wave-doesnt-mean-the-same-everywhere-share-7454827288501624832-om52

Author: Daniel Rötzer. Format: LinkedIn post (industry-practitioner essay). Date observed: 2026-05-07.

## Relevance

A practitioner-level explanation of why identical significant-wave-height (Hs) thresholds describe materially different operational risk in shallow versus deep water. Useful as a teaching anchor for the wave-shoaling → breaking-wave-loading causal chain and as a counterweight to the assumption that a single Hs-based operability cutoff transfers across regions or water depths.

## Core teaching points (per Rötzer)

1. **Shoaling kinematics.** As a wave train enters shallower water (the post uses the German Bight versus deeper Atlantic as the contrast), its celerity decreases, wavelength shortens, height can increase, and the profile becomes steeper.
2. **Breaking threshold.** At a depth-and-steepness threshold the wave becomes unstable and breaks. The transition is not a smooth scaling — it is a regime change.
3. **Load-character regime change.** Pre-break loads on a fixed structure or vessel are characterised by relatively smooth, rolling forces; post-break loads are short-duration impact loads that can be several times higher in magnitude than non-breaking waves of the same height.
4. **Operational-limit critique.** A 2.5–3.0 m Hs operational limit calibrated for deeper water represents a very different level of risk in shallower parts of the same regional sea (the post calls out the shallower parts of the North Sea, with structures, foundations, and vessels in the 20–50 m water-depth band as the practical zone).
5. **Decision question.** Rötzer asks the audience whether this misunderstanding (uniform Hs cutoffs across depths) shows up in offshore planning or operational-limit-setting in their experience.
