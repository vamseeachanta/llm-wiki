# Lloyd's Maritime Institute (2026) — Tug Working-End Fender Height (LinkedIn discussion prompt)

Raw URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/maritimeindustry-shipping-marinetechnology-ugcPost-7459920110288560129-LKS1

Publisher account: Lloyd's Maritime Institute. Format: LinkedIn post (training-derived industry discussion prompt). Date observed: 2026-05-12.

## Relevance

A short discussion-prompt post raising the question of whether there is an "ideal" fender height for a tug's working end when assisting vessels of different types in port. The post frames the question rather than answering it, identifying four factors the responder should weigh: **vessel freeboard, hull geometry, fender configuration, and tug design**. Useful as a teaching anchor / question prompt for the [Tug Working-End Fendering](../concepts/tug-working-end-fendering.md) concept page; the concept-page treatment supplies the substantive synthesis (regime spectrum across vessel types, working-end definition by tug-propulsion type, stability and hull-curvature constraints) grounded in public references rather than in the LinkedIn post.

## What the post asserts vs. raises

The post does **not** state a recommended height or a sizing methodology. It asks readers:

- whether a single setup "consistently delivers the best performance and safety", and
- (if a measurement is offered) approximately what value, or which tug design has been seen to perform well.

Treat the substantive design content elsewhere in the wiki, not on this source page.

## Factors named by the post

- **Vessel freeboard.** The vertical distance from the waterline to the deck edge of the assisted vessel; varies widely between laden tankers / bulkers and ballasted container ships / RoRo / cruise.
- **Hull geometry.** Bow flare, parallel midbody, shoulder transitions, and stern overhang all change where the tug actually contacts the ship.
- **Fender configuration.** Face geometry (cylindrical, wing, push-knee, tube), face stiffness, face height range, single-step versus multi-step arrangements.
- **Tug design.** Conventional bow-fender tug versus ASD / Z-drive tug versus tractor (Voith) tug — the "working end" is not the same for all three.
