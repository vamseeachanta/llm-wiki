# Salvage Convention 1989 — Doctrinal Synthesis

This page is the practitioner-impact companion to the [standards-tier metadata resolver](../standards/salvage-convention-1989.md). It synthesizes what the **International Convention on Salvage 1989** means for salvors, shipowners, P&I clubs, and coastal-state regulators — without reproducing convention text. The broader cross-cutting salvor-claim doctrine (LOF, SCOPIC, "no cure, no pay" maxim, three classical elements) sits at [Salvage](./salvage.md); this page is the **Convention-specific synthesis** nested below it, scoped to the treaty itself and its Article 14 environmental innovation.

## Why Salvage Convention 1989 matters

- **Modernization of the 1910 Brussels regime.** The Convention replaces — between states-parties — the centuries-old "no cure, no pay" pure-form codified in the 1910 Brussels Salvage Convention with a partial-compensation hybrid. Article 13 retains the classical reward-on-success calculus; Article 14 superimposes a fallback environmental-protection compensation that runs even where success is incomplete. This is the doctrinal pivot that defines the modern salvage industry.
- **Post–Amoco Cadiz innovation.** The 1978 *Amoco Cadiz* casualty exposed a structural defect in pure "no cure, no pay": salvors had a financial incentive to refuse engagement with environmentally-risky casualties whose cure prospects looked marginal, because failure meant zero recovery. Article 14 corrects that incentive by making environmental-protection efforts compensable independent of cure success — a deliberate post-incident policy choice traceable in the IMO Diplomatic Conference travaux.
- **Underpins the LOF + SCOPIC industry framework.** **Lloyd's Open Form (LOF)** — the dominant global salvage contract — has incorporated the Convention by reference since LOF 95. The **Special Compensation P&I Club Clause (SCOPIC)**, introduced in 2000 by the International Salvage Union and the International Group of P&I Clubs, is a contractual elaboration of (and practical substitute for) Article 14. Most major salvages today operate on LOF + SCOPIC, with Article 14 as the treaty-layer fallback when SCOPIC is not invoked.

## Doctrinal evolution

- **1910 Brussels Salvage Convention.** First international codification of salvage law. Anchored on pure "no cure, no pay" — reward payable only on a useful result, calculated against salved value plus the classical danger / skill / time / expense factors. Adequate for the early-20th-century freighter casualty mix; structurally inadequate for the post-1960s tanker era.
- **1978-03-16 — Amoco Cadiz (Brittany, France).** VLCC grounding off Portsall releasing approximately **220,000 tonnes** of crude oil along the Brittany coast. The salvage timeline — protracted negotiation between *Amoco Cadiz*'s master, the German tug *Pacific*, and the casualty's insurers, against a backdrop of pure "no cure, no pay" exposure for the salvor — became the textbook driver for a treaty-level fix. The case is the proximate political cause of Article 14 a decade later.
- **1980 CMI Lisbon Draft.** The Comité Maritime International produced an academic and industry draft directly addressing the Amoco Cadiz lesson. The Lisbon Draft introduced the **environmental-effort compensation** concept that survives, in revised form, as Article 14 of the 1989 Convention.
- **1989-04-28 — Convention adopted.** IMO Diplomatic Conference, London. The CMI Lisbon Draft's environmental-compensation concept was operationalized as Article 14, with reward-fixing criteria modernized at Article 13.
- **1996-07-14 — Convention enters into force.** Threshold of 15 ratifications met under Article 29. State-party count has since grown past 70 states; the regime is now near-universal among major flag and coastal states.
- **1995 — LOF 95.** Lloyd's Open Form revised to incorporate the 1989 Convention by reference. From this point forward, LOF salvages are operating on the treaty regime even in jurisdictions that have not yet ratified.
- **1997 — *The Nagasaki Spirit* (UK House of Lords).** Held that "expenses fairly attributable" under Article 14 did not include a profit element. The ruling rendered Article 14 commercially unattractive for salvors and triggered immediate industry work on a contractual alternative.
- **1999 — SCOPIC clause drafted.** International Salvage Union + International Group of P&I Clubs jointly developed a contractual replacement: a fixed-tariff-rate compensation regime invocable by the salvor at LOF execution, with property-underwriter P&I-club guarantee posted up front.
- **2000 — LOF 2000.** Incorporates SCOPIC as an optional clause. SCOPIC begins displacing Article 14 in operational practice almost immediately.
- **2011 — LOF 2011.** Current standard form (with subsequent SCOPIC revisions including SCOPIC 2014). The LOF + SCOPIC + Salvage 1989 triad is now the operating framework for an estimated 70–90 % of global commercial salvages.

## Article 14 — Special Compensation

Article 14 is the convention's defining innovation and the doctrinal heart of the modernization. The mechanic operates in two stages:

- **Article 14(1) — expenses-only fallback.** Where the salvor has carried out a salvage operation in respect of a vessel which **by itself or its cargo threatened damage to the environment**, and where the salvor has **failed to earn a reward under Article 13** sufficient to cover its expenses (or has earned no reward at all because the operation failed in part or in whole), the salvor is entitled to special compensation from the **owner of the vessel** equivalent to the salvor's **expenses**. This breaks "no cure, no pay" — recovery now flows even on failed cures, provided environmental threat existed.
- **Article 14(2) — environmental-protection uplift.** Where the salvor by its own effort has **prevented or minimised damage to the environment**, the special compensation may be **increased by up to 30 %** of expenses (the standard uplift), or **up to 100 %** of expenses in exceptional cases of skilful salvage (the discretionary uplift). The uplift is at the tribunal's or arbitrator's discretion based on the actual environmental benefit achieved.
- **Effect on salvor incentive.** The combined Article 14(1) + (2) mechanic shifts the salvor's economic calculus at the engagement decision: even a marginal-cure casualty becomes attractive provided environmental threat exists, because expenses-plus-uplift is recoverable independent of cure outcome. This is the targeted Amoco-Cadiz-driven incentive-correction.
- **Funding source.** Article 14 special compensation is paid by the **vessel owner** (Article 14(1)), with subrogation flowing through the owner's hull-and-machinery and P&I cover. The compensation is **not** a charge against salved property the way an Article 13 reward is — a structural difference that matters for limitation-of-liability and pro-rata-distribution analysis under LLMC 1996.

## SCOPIC — industry compromise

The Article 14 mechanic, as interpreted in *The Nagasaki Spirit* (1997 UK HL), embedded a structural problem: "expenses" was held to mean **out-of-pocket expenses without a profit element or commercial-rate-of-return component**. For a salvage industry whose capital intensity (specialized tugs, rapid-deployment crew, specialist equipment) demands a commercial return, expenses-only recovery was commercially unattractive. The industry response was SCOPIC.

- **The Nagasaki Spirit problem (1997).** UK House of Lords, *Semco Salvage & Marine Pte Ltd v. Lancer Navigation Co Ltd* (sub nom. *The Nagasaki Spirit*) [1997] AC 455. Held that "fair rate" for purposes of Article 14 expenses means actual cost without a profit margin. Salvors were left recovering bare expenses for environmentally-driven engagements — economically irrational at the engagement-decision margin.
- **SCOPIC clause (drafted 1999, in force from LOF 2000).** Salvor-invocable contractual alternative. Triggered by the salvor at any point during a LOF salvage; once invoked, the **property underwriters' P&I club** posts a guarantee at LOF execution, and the salvor's compensation flips from Article 14 to a **published daily-tariff rate plus a uniform 25 % bonus**. The tariff covers personnel, tugs, equipment, and overhead at industry-published rates with periodic refresh.
- **Pros and cons.** SCOPIC delivers **predictability** — the salvor knows the tariff at engagement; the owner / P&I club knows the daily-burn exposure. The trade-off is **cost**: SCOPIC tariff-rate compensation is materially more expensive for owners than Article 14 expenses-only recovery during prolonged operations (multi-week salvages can run to several million dollars in SCOPIC compensation). Article 14 remains cheaper for owners but commercially uncertain for salvors.
- **Operational reality.** Industry practice since LOF 2000 has been: **salvor invokes SCOPIC at LOF execution as a near-default**, particularly for any environmentally-meaningful casualty. **Article 14 has become the fallback** for the residual cases where SCOPIC is not invoked or is withdrawn. This means that the treaty-layer Article 14 mechanic is in operation, but the typical compensation calculation is now governed by the contractual SCOPIC tariff rather than the *Nagasaki Spirit* expenses-only reading. The treaty obligation remains; the commercial interface has moved onto the contract.

## Convention scope

Beyond Article 14, the convention sets out the broader operating framework for salvage operations. Key operative provisions:

- **Article 1 — Definitions.** Defines `salvage operation` as **any act or activity undertaken to assist a vessel or any other property in danger** in navigable waters or in any other waters whatsoever. The definition is intentionally broad — it captures both classical hire-of-services salvage and modern environmental-protection salvage. `Damage to the environment` is also defined here, with explicit reference to substantial physical damage to human health or to marine life or resources.
- **Article 8 — Duties of the salvor and the owner.** Mutual cooperation duties. The **salvor** must exercise due care, exercise due care to prevent or minimise environmental damage, accept reasonable assistance from other salvors when reasonably required by the owner or master, and accept the intervention of other salvors when reasonably required. The **owner and master** must cooperate fully with the salvor, exercise due care to prevent or minimise environmental damage, and accept redelivery of the vessel when reasonably requested. These duties are the foundation of the salvor's claim to a reward and a vector for cap-loss-style defenses where breach is shown.
- **Article 13 — Criteria for fixing the reward.** Ten enumerated factors that govern Article 13 reward calculations: salved value of vessel and other property; skill and efforts of salvors in **preventing or minimising environmental damage**; measure of success; nature and degree of danger; salvors' skill and efforts in salving the vessel, other property, and life; time used and expenses + losses incurred; risk of liability and other risks run by salvors or their equipment; promptness of services; availability of vessels intended for salvage operations; and state of readiness, efficiency, and value of the salvor's equipment. Note that environmental-prevention skill is **embedded in the Article 13 reward calculus itself**, not just in Article 14 — a deliberate redundancy that makes environmental performance compensable on both the success and the failure track.
- **Article 14 — Special Compensation.** The defining innovation; covered above.
- **Article 18 — Effect of misconduct.** The salvor may **forfeit reward** in whole or in part where the salvage operations have become necessary or more difficult because of fault or neglect on the salvor's part, or where the salvor has been guilty of fraud or other dishonest conduct. The provision is a counterweight to the broad cooperation duties of Article 8 — it gives owners a discount lever where salvor misconduct is shown.

## Practitioner impact

### For salvors

- **Dual-track compensation system.** Modern salvors operate a dual-track economic model: Article 13 reward on success (the upside), with Article 14 / SCOPIC as the downside-protection floor. Engagement-decision economics are driven by the SCOPIC-tariff exposure profile rather than by raw Article 13 reward potential, particularly for environmentally-meaningful casualties.
- **LOF + SCOPIC standard contracting.** Master service agreements with International Salvage Union (ISU) members default to LOF + SCOPIC. SCOPIC invocation is salvor-controlled and is typically exercised at engagement.
- **Article 14 as fallback.** Where SCOPIC is not invoked (rare, but happens — particularly for non-environmentally-driven engagements), Article 14 remains the treaty-layer fallback. Salvors must therefore preserve evidence of expenses and environmental-protection effort even when operating under SCOPIC, in case the SCOPIC invocation is later challenged.

### For shipowners

- **SCOPIC is owner-funded but salvor-invoked.** This asymmetry is structural: SCOPIC compensation is paid by the property underwriters' P&I club (i.e. ultimately by the owner) but invoked by the salvor at the salvor's commercial discretion. Owners cannot block SCOPIC invocation; they can only manage SCOPIC duration through cooperation that minimises operational time.
- **Significant cost during prolonged operations.** SCOPIC tariff compensation can run to several million dollars on multi-week salvages. Cost-management discipline (rapid decision-making on places of refuge, avoidance of secondary damage that prolongs operations, prompt payment of guarantees) is operationally and financially load-bearing.
- **Article 8 cooperation duties.** Owner-side breach of Article 8 cooperation duties (e.g. refusal of redelivery, refusal to accept reasonable salvor instructions) can ground a higher Article 13 award or extended SCOPIC duration; pre-incident master-and-crew training on cooperation duties is a P&I-driven loss-prevention investment.

### For P&I clubs

- **SCOPIC obligations covered by P&I cover.** The International Group of P&I Clubs' Pooling Agreement and General Excess of Loss reinsurance contract are calibrated for SCOPIC tariff exposure. SCOPIC guarantees are issued by individual member clubs at LOF execution.
- **Article 14 expenses indemnified separately.** Where Article 14 (rather than SCOPIC) is the operative mechanism, the expenses-only compensation flows through the owner's H&M and P&I cover under the standard salvage-and-collision indemnity heads. The two tracks are accounted for distinctly.
- **Place-of-refuge coordination.** P&I clubs increasingly coordinate with coastal-state authorities on place-of-refuge decisions during environmental-threat salvages — a soft-law extension of the convention's environmental-protection objective into the regulatory interface.

### For environmental governance

- **Incentive structure for early salvor engagement.** The Convention's most under-appreciated effect is at the casualty-engagement decision-margin: by removing the "no cure, no pay" disincentive for environmentally-risky casualties, Article 14 (and SCOPIC) make early salvor engagement the rational commercial choice. This has measurably reduced post-1996 casualty escalation timelines compared with the pre-Convention pattern visible in *Amoco Cadiz* and *Torrey Canyon*.
- **Coastal-state cooperation.** Articles 8 and 17 (cooperation duties) provide the doctrinal hook for coastal-state intervention during environmentally-driven salvages. The interface with UNCLOS Articles 221 and 225 (coastal-state intervention to prevent pollution) is the broader public-international-law backdrop.

## Notable case law

- ***The Nagasaki Spirit* (1997) AC 455 (UK House of Lords).** *Semco Salvage & Marine Pte Ltd v. Lancer Navigation Co Ltd*. Leading interpretation of Article 14 — held that "fair rate" for purposes of Article 14(2) special compensation refers to actual expenses incurred, not a commercial-profit-margin element. The decision exposed the structural commercial defect in Article 14 and was the **immediate driver of the SCOPIC 2000 reform**.
- ***The Tojo Maru* (1972) AC 242 (UK House of Lords).** Pre-Convention salvage-doctrine foundational case; the leading 20th-century House of Lords analysis of salvor duties of care and the boundary between salvage services and tort. Still cited as the doctrinal anchor for the Article 8 cooperation-duty framework, even though the specific holding was on negligence-of-salvor liability under the older regime.
- ***The Cape Bari* (2016) UKPC 20.** Privy Council on the application of Article 13 reward criteria in modern salvages and the interaction of LOF arbitration with limitation-of-liability clauses in side contracts. Useful modern reference for the Article 13 / LOF-arbitration interface.
- ***The Solomon Trader* (2019, Solomon Islands).** Bauxite-carrier grounding releasing approximately **300 tonnes** of heavy fuel oil off Rennell Island. Multi-month salvage operation triggered both the Salvage Convention 1989 (operational salvage) and the Nairobi WRC 2007 (post-incident wreck-removal), illustrating the modern interaction between the two regimes when a salvage operation transitions into a wreck-removal operation. Coastal-state liability interface against an under-resourced flag administration.
- ***The MV Stena Polaris*.** Environmental-protection-rate Article 14 application; cited for the practical operation of the Article 14(2) up-to-30 % uplift in cases of demonstrable environmental-damage minimisation.
