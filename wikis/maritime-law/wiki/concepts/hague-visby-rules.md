# Hague-Visby Rules — Doctrinal Synthesis

> Practitioner-facing companion to the standards-tier metadata resolver at [`standards/hague-visby-rules.md`](../standards/hague-visby-rules.md). This page synthesizes what the Hague-Visby regime means in operational practice for shippers, carriers, freight forwarders, NVOCCs, and P&I underwriters — what changed between Hague 1924 and Visby 1968, why the US sat out the Visby Protocol, and how the regime sits in relation to Hamburg and the still-pending Rotterdam Rules. No convention text is reproduced.

## Why Hague-Visby matters

- **Most-invoked private-law instrument in international shipping.** Where SOLAS / MARPOL / STCW govern the public-law surface, Hague-Visby governs the private contract: the carrier's liability to the cargo interest under sea-going bills of lading. It is the bedrock of the cargo-claim docket in every major admiralty jurisdiction, and the regime that any practitioner drafting a paramount clause, evaluating a notice of claim, or pricing P&I cover must speak fluently.
- **Three-jurisdictional reality.** International ocean carriage operates under three live regimes simultaneously — the original **Hague Rules (1924)**, the modernized **Hague-Visby Rules (1968 Visby Protocol + 1979 SDR Protocol)**, and the UN-sponsored alternative **Hamburg Rules (1978)** — with a fourth, the **Rotterdam Rules (2009)**, signed but not in force. Forum-selection and choice-of-law contests turn on which regime applies, with materially different package limits and burden-of-proof structures across them.
- **Practical-effect ubiquity.** Even states that are not party to Hague-Visby have typically enacted COGSA-style domestic legislation modelled on Hague or Hague-Visby. Combined with paramount-clause incorporation in bills of lading, the *practical* reach of the Hague / Hague-Visby framework substantially exceeds the formal ratification list.

## Doctrinal evolution

- **1893 US Harter Act.** First national legislation to constrain carrier-liability disclaimer clauses in bills of lading — the prototype for treaty-level codification a generation later. The Harter Act introduced the doctrinal split between management-of-vessel (carrier excused for navigational fault) and care-of-cargo (carrier liable) that Hague 1924 then internationalized.
- **1924 Hague Rules (Brussels Convention).** First international carrier-liability treaty, drafted under the auspices of the Comité Maritime International (CMI). Set the basic three-pillar architecture that survives to this day: (1) mandatory carrier obligations under Article III; (2) enumerated carrier defenses under Article IV.2; (3) package-limit cap under Article IV.5. Original cap denominated in £100 sterling per package (gold value).
- **1968 Visby Protocol.** Modernized the package-limit; introduced the container clause at Article IV.5(c) (clarifying when the container itself or its contents count as "the package"); added jurisdictional-clause discipline; and tightened the one-year time-bar with extension provisions. This is the cycle that took Hague from a 1920s-era regime to one capable of handling containerized trade.
- **1979 SDR Protocol.** Replaced the gold-franc / Poincaré-franc unit of account with the IMF Special Drawing Right. Practical effect: the package limit ceased drifting with gold-market fluctuation and became a stable, periodically-reset value (currently 666.67 SDR per package or 2 SDR per kilogram, whichever is higher).
- **1978 Hamburg Rules.** UN-sponsored alternative regime, more cargo-friendly in its allocation of risk: abolishes the navigational-fault defense, extends the period of carrier responsibility from tackle-to-tackle to port-to-port, raises package and per-kg caps. Adopted by approximately 35 mostly-developing states; not adopted by any of the major shipping flag states or major cargo-trading economies. Hamburg is doctrinally important but commercially marginal.
- **2009 Rotterdam Rules.** Comprehensive modernization attempt under UNCITRAL: door-to-door multimodal scope, abolition of the navigational-fault defense, raised package limits (875 SDR / 3 SDR per kg), modernized electronic-bill-of-lading provisions. Signed by approximately 25 states (including the US in 2009), ratified by approximately 5; **not in force**. Rotterdam remains the on-the-shelf successor that, in the absence of further ratifications, is unlikely to displace Hague-Visby in the foreseeable horizon.

## Carrier obligations and defenses

The architecture of Hague-Visby is a tightly coupled obligation–exception–limit triad. The carrier owes a defined set of affirmative duties; if breach causes cargo loss the carrier is *prima facie* liable; the carrier may discharge that liability by invoking one of the enumerated defenses; and even where liability survives the defenses, it is capped at a package-limit unit-of-account that the SDR Protocol re-anchored to IMF SDRs.

### Mandatory obligations (Article III)

- **Seaworthiness — Article III(1).** The carrier must, *before and at the beginning of the voyage*, exercise **due diligence** to: (a) make the ship seaworthy; (b) properly man, equip, and supply the ship; (c) make the holds, refrigerating chambers, and all other parts of the ship in which goods are carried fit and safe for their reception, carriage, and preservation. The duty is **due-diligence-not-strict-liability** and is **non-delegable** (per *The Muncaster Castle*).
- **Cargo care — Article III(2).** The carrier must "properly and carefully load, handle, stow, carry, keep, care for, and discharge the goods carried." This is the day-to-day operational duty that anchors most cargo-claim litigation.
- **Bill of lading issuance — Article III(3)–(8).** On the shipper's demand the carrier must issue a bill of lading reflecting the leading marks, number / quantity / weight, and apparent order and condition of the goods. Article III(8) renders any clause attempting to relieve the carrier from this regime null and void — the regime is **one-way mandatory** in favor of the cargo interest.
- **One-year suit-or-bar — Article III(6).** Cargo claims must be brought within one year of delivery (or the date when the goods should have been delivered); thereafter the carrier and the ship are discharged from all liability. This time-bar is a hard limitation that drives forum-selection urgency in cargo-claim docket management.

### The 17 carrier defenses (Article IV.2)

The exceptions are the operational core of the carrier's defensive position. Representative heads include: act of God; act of war and acts of public enemies; arrest or restraint of princes; quarantine restrictions; act or omission of the shipper; strikes or lockouts; riots and civil commotions; saving or attempting to save life or property at sea; perils, dangers, and accidents of the sea; defects in the vessel hidden by reasonable diligence (latent defects); inherent vice of the goods; insufficient packing; insufficient marks; the famous **navigational-fault defense** under Article IV(2)(a) — "act, neglect, or default of the master, mariner, pilot, or servants of the carrier in the navigation or in the management of the ship"; **fire** (without actual fault or privity of the carrier) under Article IV(2)(b); and the residual catch-all at Article IV(2)(q) — "any other cause arising without the actual fault or privity of the carrier", on which the carrier bears the burden of proof.

The navigational-fault defense is the most commercially significant and the most criticized; its abolition is the headline doctrinal shift in both Hamburg and Rotterdam.

### Package limit (Article IV.5)

- **Pre-Visby (Hague 1924).** £100 gold sterling per package or unit. Time-eroded by gold-market drift to a level that ceased to be a credible cap by mid-century.
- **Post-Visby (Visby 1968 + SDR 1979).** **666.67 SDR per package or unit, OR 2 SDR per kilogram of gross weight of the goods lost or damaged, whichever is higher.** The dual-axis structure (per-package OR per-kg) addressed the long-standing tension between high-value low-weight cargo and bulk commodities. Container clause at Article IV.5(c) governs whether the container itself or its enumerated contents count as the relevant "package or unit."
- **Loss of limit — Article IV.5(e).** The package-limit is unavailable if the damage resulted from the carrier's act or omission "done with intent to cause damage, or recklessly and with knowledge that damage would probably result." This is the gateway equivalent in the cargo-claim regime to the LLMC global-cap-breaking gateway.

## Practitioner impact

### For shippers and cargo interests

- **Inspection on receipt + written notice.** Article III(6) requires written notice of damage at the time of delivery (apparent damage) or **within 3 days of delivery** (concealed damage); failing notice raises a presumption that the carrier delivered the goods as described in the bill of lading. The 3-day window is unforgiving and is the most common procedural trap for inexperienced cargo interests.
- **One-year time-bar.** Suit must be commenced within one year of delivery or the date the goods should have been delivered. Settlement-track negotiation must run with the litigation clock visibly counting down; counsel routinely files protective writs to preserve the position.
- **Choice-of-law and forum.** Where a US port is involved, expect a Hague-vs-Hague-Visby contest; counsel screens for paramount clauses and Himalaya clauses early and decides whether to litigate in the US (Hague + COGSA $500 cap) or in a Hague-Visby jurisdiction (666.67 SDR / 2 SDR per kg cap).
- **Burden of proof.** *Volcafe v CSAV* [2018] UKSC 61 realigned English law: where the carrier invokes inherent vice, the **carrier** bears the legal burden of proving both that loss arose from inherent vice and that the carrier exercised reasonable care under Article III(2). Cargo-claim pleading is structured around this burden allocation.

### For carriers

- **Standardized defenses + package limit.** The 17 enumerated defenses and the SDR-denominated package-limit together cap extreme-risk exposure to a level underwriters can price. Without Hague-Visby the carrier would face full common-law negligence exposure, which the freight market cannot bear at competitive rates.
- **Due-diligence-not-strict-liability standard.** The Article III(1) seaworthiness obligation is due-diligence, which means systemic preventative-maintenance and crew-competency programs — properly documented — are the carrier's primary defense. Strict liability would require the carrier to underwrite latent defects it could not detect.
- **Non-delegable seaworthiness duty.** *The Muncaster Castle* fixed that due-diligence cannot be discharged by hiring a competent independent contractor: the carrier remains liable for the contractor's failures. Practical consequence: yard-period oversight is part of the seaworthiness obligation, not separable from it.

### For freight forwarders and NVOCCs

- **Hague-Visby applies to bills of lading they issue.** A forwarder or NVOCC that issues its own house bill of lading is treated as the contracting carrier under that bill, and Hague-Visby applies whether or not the forwarder operates the vessel. This is the doctrinal basis for NVOCC liability in container shipping.
- **Chain-of-custody record-keeping.** When loss occurs in a leg of carriage involving multiple carriers and connecting bills of lading, allocating liability requires documentary evidence of where in the chain the goods were when the loss occurred. Tally records, port-handling reports, and EIRs (equipment interchange receipts) are the operational records that decide the contest.
- **Himalaya clause practice.** Bills of lading routinely incorporate Himalaya clauses extending the carrier's Hague-Visby defenses and limits to subcontractors (stevedores, terminal operators, inland-leg carriers). The forwarder's drafting choice on Himalaya clause scope is the practical mechanism for distributing the regime's protective shell across the chain.

### For P&I clubs and cargo underwriters

- **P&I covers carrier liability under Hague-Visby.** International Group P&I clubs underwrite the carrier's contractual and tortious liability for cargo, with the Hague-Visby regime forming the standard basis for cover (deviations from Hague-Visby may trigger cover restriction or call for separate evaluation). Standardized treaty terms reduce underwriting variance and are the precondition for the pooling/reinsurance structure of the International Group.
- **Cargo underwriters subrogate to the cargo interest.** When cargo insurers pay a claim they take subrogation rights against the carrier; the resulting recovery action runs as a Hague-Visby cargo claim with the underwriter standing in the assured's shoes. The regime's burden-of-proof and package-limit provisions therefore set the recoverable ceiling for cargo-side underwriting.

## US carve-out: COGSA

- **US Carriage of Goods by Sea Act 1936.** US implementation of the Hague Rules 1924, modeled closely on the Brussels text but **never updated** to incorporate the Visby Protocol or the SDR Protocol. The US is the principal major-trading-nation outlier — it ratified Hague but neither Visby nor SDR.
- **US package limit = $500/package.** Statutory, not SDR-indexed. Substantially lower than Hague-Visby's 666.67 SDR (~US$886 at recent SDR exchange rates), and the gap has widened over time as SDR values have drifted while the US$500 figure has remained statutorily fixed since 1936.
- **Paramount-clause opt-in.** Many US-trade contracts of carriage opt-in to Hague-Visby (or even Hamburg) via a paramount clause selecting a non-US governing law and forum. Whether that opt-in is enforceable in US federal admiralty jurisdiction is a recurring contest, with the *Vimar Seguros* (1995) and subsequent SCOTUS line generally favoring enforceability of choice-of-foreign-law and choice-of-foreign-forum clauses.
- **US ratification of Rotterdam.** The US signed Rotterdam in 2009 but has not ratified; ratification was politically tied to a broader maritime-law modernization package that has not advanced. As a result the Hague-vs-Hague-Visby split is the live and persistent reality for any cargo-claim involving a US port.

## Hague-Visby vs Rotterdam Rules

- **Geographic scope.** Hague-Visby is **port-to-port** (tackle-to-tackle, more strictly): the regime governs from loading on the ship to discharge from the ship, with pre-loading and post-discharge legs outside the regime. Rotterdam is **door-to-door**: it covers the multimodal carriage including the inland legs, with carrier liability extending to incidents occurring during the inland leg under specified conditions.
- **Liability cap.** Rotterdam **raises** the package limit to **875 SDR per package** or **3 SDR per kilogram**, whichever is higher (vs Hague-Visby's 666.67 SDR / 2 SDR per kg). The increase reflects three decades of inflation since the SDR Protocol re-anchoring and a deliberate policy choice to raise carrier exposure modestly.
- **Navigational-fault defense — abolition.** Rotterdam **eliminates** the Article IV(2)(a) navigational-fault defense. This is the single most contested change: it shifts a meaningful slice of casualty liability from cargo-side to carrier-side and is the principal reason carrier interests have not pressed for ratification.
- **Electronic transport records.** Rotterdam expressly contemplates negotiable and non-negotiable electronic transport records, providing the regime infrastructure for electronic bills of lading that Hague-Visby pre-dates and does not address.
- **Net effect on practice.** Until Rotterdam enters into force, Hague-Visby remains the operative framework. Practitioners track Rotterdam ratification state-by-state but draft on the assumption that Hague-Visby (with COGSA in US trade) is the applicable regime for the duration of the current contract horizon.

## Notable case law

- **The Muncaster Castle [1961] AC 807.** UK House of Lords. Pre-Visby. Established that the Article III(1) due-diligence obligation to make the ship seaworthy is **non-delegable**: the carrier remains liable for unseaworthiness caused by independent contractors (here, ship-repair-yard fitters). The leading authority on the scope and character of the due-diligence duty.
- **The Eurasian Dream [2002] 1 Lloyd's Rep 719.** UK Commercial Court. Carrier identity and the application of Hague-Visby to charterer-issued bills of lading; treats the interaction between the demise clause, the identity-of-carrier clause, and the underlying charterparty. See [`entities/eurasian-dream-2002.md`](../entities/eurasian-dream-2002.md).
- **The Eurus [1996] 2 Lloyd's Rep 408.** UK Commercial Court. Interpretation of Article IV(2)(a) — the navigational-fault defense — in a context involving the master's deviation decisions; clarifies the boundary between "navigation or management of the ship" (excused) and care-of-cargo (not excused).
- **Effort Shipping Co Ltd v Linden Management SA ("The Giannis NK") [1998] AC 605.** UK House of Lords. Interpretation of Article IV.6 (carriage of dangerous goods); established that the carrier's right to land or destroy dangerous goods is not conditional on the shipper's knowledge of the dangerous nature; the shipper's strict liability for dangerous goods runs in parallel with Article IV(2)(p) carrier-defense.
- **Volcafe Ltd v Compania Sud Americana de Vapores SA [2018] UKSC 61.** UK Supreme Court. Burden of proof under the Article IV(2)(m) inherent-vice exception: carrier bears the legal burden of proving both that the loss arose from inherent vice and that the carrier exercised reasonable care under Article III(2). Realigned the English law position with the structure of Article III/IV and is now the leading inherent-vice burden-of-proof authority.
