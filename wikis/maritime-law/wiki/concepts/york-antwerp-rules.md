# York-Antwerp Rules — Doctrinal Synthesis

This page is the practitioner-impact companion to the [standards-tier metadata resolver](../standards/york-antwerp-rules.md). It is **instrument-specific** — focused on the York-Antwerp Rules (YAR) as a CMI-maintained private codification with a distinctive version-evolution dynamic — and is the narrower sibling of the cross-cutting [general-average doctrine page](./general-average.md), which covers the underlying loss-sharing institution. Where that page treats the doctrine, this page treats the rule-set: who maintains it, how it has evolved, why one edition rather than another ends up controlling a given adjustment, and how the version-selection question shows up in charter-party paramount clauses and average-adjuster practice.

## Why York-Antwerp Rules matter

- **Private-codification, not treaty.** The YAR are maintained by the Comité Maritime International (CMI), not by any state, IMO, or UN body. They have no entry-into-force date and no ratification register. They bind only by **contractual incorporation** through paramount clauses in bills of lading, voyage charters, and time charters — yet the practical reach of that incorporation is so close to universal in international shipping that the YAR function as a *de facto* harmonized regime alongside (and despite) their formal private-law status.
- **General-average framework.** The YAR specify how an extraordinary sacrifice or expenditure made for the common safety of the maritime adventure (ship + cargo + freight at risk) is **apportioned among the contributing interests** in proportion to the values saved at the time and place the adventure ends. The doctrinal foundation sits in [general-average](./general-average.md); the YAR provide the operational rule-set that the average adjuster applies to allocate loss.
- **Continuous version-evolution.** YAR has been revised at irregular intervals — 1890, 1924, 1950, 1974, 1994, 2004, 2016 — driven by industry-practice shifts, insurance-market evolution, and recurring policy disputes (most prominently around the treatment of salvage). This makes **version-selection** itself a first-order practitioner question: an adjustment under YAR 1994 can yield a materially different distribution from the same casualty under YAR 2004 or YAR 2016.

## Doctrinal evolution

- **Pre-1890.** General-average doctrine traceable to *Lex Rhodia* (~700 BC) and Justinian's *Digest* (533 AD); by the 19th century each major maritime nation and the Hanseatic ports applied locally-divergent rules on what counted as a recoverable sacrifice or expenditure. The fragmentation made cross-jurisdictional adjustments unpredictable and was the operating problem the early CMI conferences set out to solve.
- **1864 York and 1877 Antwerp conferences.** First multilateral attempts at a common rule-set; produced precursor codifications that fed into the 1890 consolidation.
- **1890 York-Antwerp Rules.** First comprehensive private codification under the now-permanent name. Established the lettered Rules of Interpretation (A, B, C) and numbered operational Rules (I onward) that all subsequent editions retained as the structural skeleton.
- **1924 + 1950 + 1974.** Incremental refinements responding to changes in trading practice — the rise of motor ships, the post-WWII reconstruction of insurance markets, the early containerization era. The 1974 edition was the long-tenure incumbent and remained dominant in older charter forms well after 1994 was published.
- **1994 York-Antwerp Rules.** Comprehensive overhaul. Tightened the **non-separation agreement** rule, reworked the **salvage interaction** so that salvor rewards under the Salvage Convention 1989 sat outside GA expenditure, and made several insurance-market-driven adjustments to the lettered Rules. YAR 1994 became — and largely remains — the market incumbent.
- **2004 York-Antwerp Rules.** Removed time-charterer and commission-agent rules; reclassified salvage in a way that effectively brought salvor rewards back inside GA in many circumstances. Controversial: the 2004 edition diverged from market expectations on salvage treatment and was rejected by significant segments of the charter-form market, with many bills of lading deliberately retaining "York-Antwerp Rules 1994" rather than upgrading.
- **2016 York-Antwerp Rules** (current edition). Reverted the contentious 2004 salvage changes and substantially restored the 1994 framework. Broader market acceptance than 2004; gradually displacing 1994 in newer charter forms but with slow uptake — many incumbent paramount clauses still cite 1994 by reference or default.

## Rule structure

YAR is built on a deliberately layered architecture that the average adjuster applies in order:

- **Rules of Interpretation (lettered: A, B, C).** Define the **GA act** (Rule A — extraordinary sacrifice or expenditure voluntarily and reasonably made in time of peril for the common safety of the adventure), the **scope of the common adventure** (Rule B), and the boundary between GA and **particular average** (Rule C — loss falling on a single interest is particular average, not GA). These set the doctrinal foundation.
- **Numbered Rules (I–XXIII).** Specify the allowance and apportionment of particular sacrifice and expenditure types — jettison of cargo, damage by water used to extinguish fire, voluntary stranding, port-of-refuge expenses, wages and maintenance during refuge, temporary repairs, contribution values, provision of funds, and so on. Each numbered Rule operationalizes the lettered principles for a recurring fact pattern.
- **Rule of Construction.** Where a numbered Rule and a lettered Rule conflict, the lettered (interpretive) Rule prevails on fundamental principles. Practically this means an adjuster cannot use a numbered-Rule allowance to circumvent the GA-act definition in Rule A.
- **Practical effect.** The adjuster applies whichever **edition** is incorporated by the paramount clause — YAR 1994, YAR 2004, or YAR 2016 — and produces an adjustment statement that allocates loss across the contributing interests in proportion to saved values.

## Key Rules

The numbered Rules operationalize the recurring GA fact patterns. The most frequently invoked include:

- **Rule X — Expenses at port of refuge.** Extra costs arising from voluntary deviation to a refuge port for common safety: port charges, pilotage, towage, and the cost of unloading, storing, and reloading cargo where necessary for repairs or to enable the voyage to continue.
- **Rule XI — Wages and maintenance of crew.** Compensation for the ship-side cost of crew wages and provisions during the period the vessel is detained at a port of refuge as a consequence of the GA act. Notably narrower in scope than Rule X — covers only the detention period attributable to GA-allowable repairs or operations.
- **Rule XI(d) (2016 amendment).** Targeted treatment of certain salvage-related crew and operational costs that had been inconsistently allocated under earlier editions; introduced to clarify the boundary between Rule XI compensation and salvage-reward apportionment after the 2004→2016 reversion.
- **Rule XIV — Temporary repairs.** Categories of temporary repair that are GA-allowable when undertaken to enable the common adventure to continue, including cost-comparison principles where temporary repairs would have been needed anyway as particular average.
- **Rule XVII — Contribution values.** Methodology for valuing the **ship**, **cargo**, and **freight at risk** for purposes of computing each interest's pro-rata share of the GA contribution. Valuations are pinned to the time and place the adventure ends, which makes Rule XVII the operational core of the adjustment.
- **Rule XX — Provision of funds and cash advances.** Short-term financing arrangements for GA expenditure incurred at the port of refuge or en route — agency commissions, interest, and the costs of raising funds against GA security.

## Practitioner impact

### For shipowners

- **GA declaration as standard incident-response.** For major casualties — machinery breakdown, fire, grounding, heavy-weather jettison, salvage operations — a GA declaration is the standard early-stage operational decision. The declaration triggers the contribution-bond and average-bond mechanism that secures cargo's share before cargo is released at the discharge port.
- **YAR version specification through the paramount clause.** The shipowner's drafting choice in the charter party determines which YAR edition will govern the adjustment. The choice is consequential — not merely formal — and is typically aligned with hull-insurance policy expectations.

### For cargo interests

- **Contribution-bond posting at the discharge port.** Cargo cannot leave the port of discharge until the cargo interest posts a **GA bond** plus an underwriter's **cash deposit** or **average guarantee** securing its prospective contribution. The mechanic is procedurally onerous but is the lever by which the shipowner secures contribution before the adjustment is finalized.
- **Cargo-insurance interface.** Most marine cargo policies indemnify the assured for GA contributions paid; the cargo-insurer typically posts the average guarantee on the cargo interest's behalf. The cargo-claim docket therefore runs the GA contribution claim through the cargo-insurer rather than the shipper directly.

### For Average Adjusters

- **Professional discipline.** GA adjustments are produced by **certified average adjusters** — in the UK, members of the **Association of Average Adjusters (AAA)**; comparable bodies operate in the US (AAA US), Europe, and Asia. The adjuster's statement is the authoritative allocation document on which the contributions are demanded and paid.
- **Multi-year adjustment cycles.** Major casualties produce adjustments running for years — *Maersk Honam* and *Ever Given* are the modern reference examples, each involving thousands of cargo interests and overlapping insurance recoveries. The professional infrastructure (AAA-certified adjusters, P&I-club coordination, hull-and-machinery underwriter participation) is what makes this scalable.

### For insurance markets

- **Hull-and-Machinery + cargo + P&I.** GA contribution claims are typically paid directly by the underwriter of the contributing interest — H&M for the ship, cargo policy for the cargo, freight insurance for freight at risk — under standard policy wording. P&I cover sits adjacent rather than primary on GA contributions but engages on the salvage-interaction question and on adjuster cost.
- **YAR-edition pricing.** Underwriters price hull and cargo cover with the YAR edition referenced in the assured's typical paramount-clause practice as a baseline assumption; significant divergence from that baseline (e.g. routinely chartering vessels under YAR 2004 in a 1994-priced book) is a disclosure surface at renewal.

## York-Antwerp version selection

The version-selection question is recurring and consequential because the editions are not minor refinements — the 2004→2016 reversion in particular materially changes salvage treatment.

- **Charter-party paramount clauses** typically cite either **"York-Antwerp Rules 1994"** (the long-tenure market incumbent — observed to remain dominant in a substantial share of charter forms post-2016) or **"York-Antwerp Rules 2016"** (the modern preference for newer drafting). Some legacy clauses still reference 1974 or simply "York-Antwerp Rules" without a year — a drafting risk.
- **Why 1994 persists.** Market familiarity, settled adjuster practice, and insurance-market pricing all calibrated around the 1994 framework. The 2004 edition's salvage-treatment changes were rejected by significant segments of the market, and the 2016 reversion has not fully displaced 1994 in incumbent forms.
- **Voyage-specific negotiation.** The version is itself a negotiable charter-party term. Where one party prefers a particular edition (e.g. cargo-side preference for 1994 salvage treatment, shipowner preference for 2016 allowances under specific Rules), the choice is bargained explicitly rather than left to the form default.

## York-Antwerp + Salvage Convention 1989 interaction

How **salvage** is allocated through the GA framework has been the most contested question in the YAR version-evolution sequence and remains the most consequential interface between YAR and the [Salvage Convention 1989](../standards/salvage-convention-1989.md).

- **The underlying problem.** Modern salvage operations — particularly those engaging Article 14 special compensation for environmentally-threatening casualties — are increasingly costly and complex. Whether the salvor's reward (fixed under Article 13 of the Salvage Convention) is a GA-allowable expenditure, or instead falls on the salved interests proportionally outside the GA mechanism, drives a materially different distribution.
- **2004 Rules.** Treated salvage substantially as GA expenditure — bringing the salvor's reward inside the GA mechanism and making it apportionable across ship, cargo, and freight in proportion to saved values rather than apportioned by interest-by-interest direct settlement with the salvor.
- **2016 Rules.** Reverted to the 1994 framework — the salvor's reward is treated primarily as **particular average** on each saved interest, allocated directly between the salvor and that interest, with only certain *salvage-related expenses* (e.g. specified port-of-refuge costs incurred during the salvage operation) brought inside the GA computation. This restored the pre-2004 market expectation and is one of the principal reasons YAR 2016 has been more readily adopted than YAR 2004.

## Notable case law

- ***The Aastra* (early 1980s).** Pre-1994-framework treatment of salvage in a GA adjustment; cited as an illustration of the doctrinal-unsettlement that drove the 1994 salvage-interaction reforms.
- ***The Star Sirius* (1991).** Interpretation of Rule X (port-of-refuge) — addressed the boundary of "voluntary deviation for common safety" against ordinary commercial deviations.
- ***General Insurance Co of Trieste v Cory* (1893).** Foundational late-19th-century English authority on GA principles — the "common danger / voluntary act / successful preservation" three-part test that the lettered Rules later codified.
- ***The Roanoke* (1908).** Common-safety-versus-common-benefit distinction under the 1924 / 1950 framework. Cited in early-20th-century adjustments for the proposition that GA reaches sacrifices in time of peril for common safety, not commercial benefit-sharing post-peril.
- ***Maersk Honam* (2018) and *Ever Given* (2021).** Modern reference cases — large container fire and Suez grounding respectively — both stress-tested YAR 2016 cargo-handling and salvage-interaction Rules against multi-thousand-cargo-interest adjustments. See [[entities/mv-ever-given-2021]](../entities/mv-ever-given-2021.md).
