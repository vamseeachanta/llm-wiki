# Charterparties — Voyage, Time, and Bareboat

## Scope

A charterparty is the contract by which a shipowner places vessel capacity at the disposal of a charterer for either a defined voyage, a defined period, or full possession. This page covers the three primary charter types and the dominant standard forms; it does NOT cover the bill-of-lading regime (Hague-Visby / Hamburg / Rotterdam Rules) or marine insurance (both deferred).

## Doctrine

- **Voyage charter** — vessel hired for one or more named voyages; freight paid per tonne, lump-sum, or worldscale-flat; owner retains operational control. Standard forms: **GENCON 1994** (BIMCO general dry cargo), **Shellvoy 6** (oil tanker), **Asbatankvoy** (US oil). Doctrine surface: laytime and demurrage, deviation, cargoworthiness.
- **Time charter** — vessel hired for a stated period; charterer pays hire in advance, gives employment orders within trading and cargo limits, and pays voyage costs (bunkers, port charges, canal dues). Owner retains technical management. Standard forms: **NYPE 1946 / 93 / 2015**, **Baltime 1939 / 2001**, **Shelltime 4**. Doctrine surface: off-hire clauses, employment-and-indemnity, safe-port warranty, withdrawal for non-payment.
- **Bareboat (demise) charter** — full possession transferred; charterer mans and operates the vessel; owner retains only the reversionary interest. Standard form: **BARECON 2017**. Doctrine surface: insurance and class maintenance, redelivery, conversion to ownership (BARECON Part IV).
- **Incorporation**: voyage and time charters routinely incorporate the Hague-Visby Rules into bills of lading issued under the charter, plus arbitration clauses (LMAA, SMA, ICC).
- **Safe-port warranty** (e.g., NYPE 93 cl. 5) is a strict guarantee that the nominated port is prospectively safe; breach gives rise to damages (see *The Eurasian Dream*).
- **Carrier identity** under bills of lading issued by the master is determined by the demise clause and the bill's conditions; see *The Eurasian Dream*.
- Civil-law jurisdictions follow equivalent contract types under their commercial codes, but English law dominates the standard-form market via choice-of-law clauses.

## Cases

- **The Eurasian Dream (2002) 1 Lloyd's Rep 719** — UK LMAA on carrier identity and Hague-Visby duties; see `../entities/eurasian-dream-2002.md`.
- **MSC Flaminia (2012)** — shipper-charterer indemnity and the dangerous-cargo regime; see `../entities/msc-flaminia-2012.md`.
- **The Achilleas (2009) 1 AC 61** — UK House of Lords on remoteness for late redelivery.
