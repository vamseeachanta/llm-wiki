# Charterparties — Doctrinal Synthesis

> Practitioner-impact companion to the bill-of-lading regime concept page at [`concepts/hague-visby-rules.md`](./hague-visby-rules.md). This page synthesizes the **three primary charter types** — voyage, time, and bareboat (demise) — the **dominant standard forms** (NYPE, GENCON, BARECON, Shellvoy, Asbatankvoy, Baltime, Shelltime), and the **load-bearing clauses** that drive the bulk of LMAA and SMA arbitration disputes: safe-port warranty, off-hire, paramount clause, employment-and-indemnity, and dispute-resolution. Bills of lading issued under charterparties carry their own regime (Hague-Visby / Hamburg / Rotterdam) and are covered separately on [`concepts/hague-visby-rules.md`](./hague-visby-rules.md); marine insurance is deferred to its own page.

## Why this concept matters

- **Charterparties are the dominant commercial contract of international shipping.** The vast majority of world tonnage moves under voyage or time charterparties; bareboat charters underpin most ship-finance leasing and a substantial share of bulk operating fleets. The doctrinal substrate is therefore the operating contract law of the entire industry.
- **English law and standard-form supremacy.** Despite the global, multi-jurisdictional character of shipping, **English law and London arbitration (LMAA)** dominate charter dispute resolution through choice-of-law and arbitration clauses in nearly every standard form. New York (SMA), Singapore (SCMA), and Hong Kong (HKIAC) are the principal alternatives. The result is a globally coherent body of authority on standard charter terms.
- **Standard forms are negotiated infrastructure.** **BIMCO** (the Baltic and International Maritime Council), the **AMC** (Asociación Mexicana de Cabotaje), the **Japan Shipping Exchange**, and individual oil-major and grain-trader bodies maintain the standard forms. Updates (e.g., **NYPE 2015**, **BARECON 2017**, **GENCON 2022**, **Shellvoy 7**) are negotiated multi-year processes between owners' and charterers' representative bodies; the resulting forms become the de-facto contract architecture of the segment.
- **Load-bearing clauses with extensive case law.** The safe-port warranty (*The Eastern City*, *The Saga Cob*, *The Ocean Victory*), off-hire (*The Aquacharm*, *The Doric Pride*), employment-and-indemnity (*The Island Archon*, *The Kos*), paramount clause (incorporating Hague-Visby), and remoteness on late redelivery (*The Achilleas*) are each anchored by Supreme Court / House of Lords / Privy Council authority that the standard forms are drafted around.

## Doctrinal evolution

- **17th–18th c. — origin in the bottomry bond and contract of affreightment.** The charterparty (literally "*carta partita*", a divided document) emerged as a standalone bilateral contract for vessel hire, distinct from the bill of lading (which evidenced the carriage of specific goods).
- **19th c. — UK statutory backdrop.** *Common Carriers Act 1830* and *Bills of Lading Act 1855* shaped the cargo-claim side; the charterparty side developed largely in common law through the major Victorian shipping cases. Standard-form drafting begins with the early Baltic Exchange forms.
- **1909 — first GENCON.** The Baltic and White Sea Conference adopts the GENCON form; the modern dry-cargo voyage-charter standard form lineage begins.
- **1913 — Baltime.** Standard time-charter form; the precursor to the modern Baltime 1939 / 2001 lineage.
- **1946 — NYPE 1946.** New York Produce Exchange time-charter form; revised 1981, **1993** (the modern reference), and **2015**. Now the dominant time-charter form globally for dry-bulk and general-cargo trades.
- **1973 — BARECON A.** First standard-form bareboat charter; revised 1989, 2001, **2017** (current). BARECON 2017 introduced clearer financial-leasing and purchase-option drafting (Part IV).
- **1989 — Shellvoy 5; 1994 — Shellvoy 6; 2022 — Shellvoy 7.** Oil-tanker voyage-charter form lineage; Shellvoy 7 modernised laytime, demurrage, and emissions clauses.
- **1994 — GENCON 1994.** Modernised general dry-cargo voyage charter; remained the dominant form until **GENCON 2022**.
- **2001 — Baltime 2001.** Modernised time-charter alternative to NYPE; preferred in some European trades.
- **2015 — NYPE 2015.** Modern reference time-charter form; expanded BIMCO clauses (sanctions, cyber, war-risk, ISPS, Polar Code); unified demurrage / off-hire / employment-and-indemnity drafting.
- **2017 — BARECON 2017.** Current bareboat form; cleaned-up Part IV purchase-option and financial-lease provisions.
- **2022 — GENCON 2022.** Modernised dry-cargo voyage form; expanded BIMCO sanctions, electronic bill-of-lading, and emissions clauses.

## Three charter types: voyage / time / bareboat

The three types differ in **who bears voyage costs**, **who exercises operational control**, **how hire/freight is computed**, and **who is the carrier under bills of lading issued by the master**. The distinctions are foundational; mis-classification is a recurring source of disputes.

- **Voyage charter (vessel hired for one or more named voyages).**
  - *Freight*: paid per tonne, lump-sum, or **worldscale-flat** (oil tankers); typically due on completion of loading or on signing bills of lading.
  - *Voyage costs*: borne by the **owner** (bunkers, port charges, canal dues, agency fees).
  - *Operational control*: retained by the **owner**; the charterer specifies the cargo and the loading/discharging ports.
  - *Carrier under bills of lading*: typically the **owner** (master signs on behalf of the owner; demise clause typically inapplicable).
  - *Doctrine surface*: laytime and demurrage, deviation, cargoworthiness, freight payment.
  - *Standard forms*: **GENCON 1994 / 2022** (dry cargo), **Shellvoy 6 / 7** (oil), **Asbatankvoy** (US oil), **Norgrain**, **Synacomex**, **BPVoy**, **Tankervoy**.
- **Time charter (vessel hired for a stated period).**
  - *Hire*: paid in advance, typically every 15 days; expressed as $/day or $/MT/day.
  - *Voyage costs*: borne by the **charterer** (bunkers, port charges, canal dues — though "owner pays bunkers" variants exist for special trades).
  - *Operational control*: shared — **charterer** gives employment orders within trading and cargo limits; **owner** retains technical management.
  - *Carrier under bills of lading*: turns on the demise clause and the bill's conditions; the *Eurasian Dream* line of authority is the modern English-law anchor.
  - *Doctrine surface*: off-hire clauses, employment-and-indemnity, safe-port warranty, withdrawal for non-payment, late-redelivery remoteness.
  - *Standard forms*: **NYPE 1946 / 1993 / 2015**, **Baltime 1939 / 2001**, **Shelltime 4**, **Linertime**, **STB Time**.
- **Bareboat (demise) charter (full possession transferred to the charterer).**
  - *Hire*: paid as a charter rental, typically monthly; often structured as a financial-lease equivalent with a purchase option.
  - *Voyage costs*: all borne by the **charterer**.
  - *Operational control*: **charterer** mans, navigates, and operates the vessel; the owner retains only the reversionary interest in the hull.
  - *Carrier under bills of lading*: the **bareboat charterer** is the disponent owner / carrier; the registered owner is typically not a contracting party with cargo interests.
  - *Doctrine surface*: insurance and class maintenance, redelivery condition, conversion to ownership (Part IV of BARECON), flag-state and registry questions.
  - *Standard form*: **BARECON 2017**.

## Specific clauses of practitioner interest

- **Safe-port warranty (e.g., NYPE 1993 cl. 5; Shelltime 4 cl. 4).** A **strict guarantee** that the nominated port is prospectively safe for the vessel's call. The classical formulation in *The Eastern City* [1958] 2 Lloyd's Rep 127: a port is safe where the vessel can reach it, use it, and return from it without, in the absence of some abnormal occurrence, being exposed to danger that cannot be avoided by good navigation and seamanship. The warranty has been narrowed and refined by *The Saga Cob* (1992), *The Ocean Victory* (2017, UK Supreme Court), and a substantial body of LMAA awards.
- **Off-hire clauses (e.g., NYPE 1993 cl. 17; NYPE 1946 cl. 15).** Define the events that suspend hire — engineering breakdown, dry-docking, master/crew default, deficiency of crew, drydocking, requisition, etc. The classical authority is *The Aquacharm* [1980] 2 Lloyd's Rep 237 (CA) on the requirement of a "full working" vessel; modern authority includes *The Doric Pride* [2006] 2 Lloyd's Rep 175 on the interface with charterer's orders.
- **Employment-and-indemnity (NYPE 1993 cl. 8; NYPE 1946 cl. 8).** Owner-charterer indemnity for consequences of charterer's orders. The modern English-law anchor is *The Island Archon* [1995] 1 All ER 233 (CA), refined in *The Kos* [2012] UKSC 17.
- **Paramount clause.** Standard charterparty mechanism for incorporating the Hague-Visby Rules (or, in some trades, the Hague Rules or Hamburg Rules) into the contract by reference. The clause typically applies the convention to bills of lading issued under the charter and may apply a modified version to the charterparty itself. See [`concepts/hague-visby-rules.md`](./hague-visby-rules.md) for the cargo-side regime.
- **Dispute-resolution clause.** Almost universally an **arbitration clause** designating LMAA, SMA, SCMA, or HKIAC seat with English, US (federal admiralty), Singaporean, or Hong Kong governing law. **LMAA** runs the largest commercial-arbitration volume globally for charter disputes; standard-form arbitration clauses (BIMCO Arbitration Clause, Centrocon clause) are reproduced in nearly every modern form.
- **Bunker clauses (e.g., NYPE 1993 cl. 7).** Quantity, quality, and price for bunkers on delivery and redelivery; modern forms increasingly cross-reference IMO 2020 / MARPOL Annex VI sulphur-content limits.
- **War-risk, sanctions, and cyber clauses.** Modern BIMCO clauses (**BIMCO War Risks**, **BIMCO Sanctions Clause for Time / Voyage Charters 2020**, **BIMCO Cyber Security Clause 2019**) are routinely incorporated; the post-2014 sanctions regime and post-2017 *Maersk* / *NotPetya* cyber awareness drove rapid clause-development.
- **BARECON Part IV — purchase option.** Bareboat-charter conversion to ownership at the end of the charter period, typically structured as a financial lease for tax and accounting purposes; the principal mechanism behind much of the modern KS, KG, and Japanese operating-lease ship-finance market.

## Practitioner impact

### For shipowners

The choice between voyage, time, and bareboat chartering is a fundamental commercial-strategy decision: voyage charter exposes the owner to bunker, port-charge, and laytime risk but captures the spot-market premium; time charter shifts voyage cost to the charterer and produces predictable hire revenue; bareboat charter strips the owner of operational involvement entirely and produces pure capital-rental revenue. Standard-form selection (NYPE 1993 vs 2015; GENCON 1994 vs 2022) is itself a negotiation lever. Owners maintain in-house chartering desks plus broker relationships through the Baltic Exchange (London), Howe Robinson, Clarksons, BRS, and SSY.

### For charterers (oil majors, dry-bulk traders, container lines)

Voyage and time charters are the operating vehicles for oil majors (BP, Shell, ExxonMobil, TotalEnergies, Chevron — through their respective trading desks), dry-bulk grain and ore traders (Cargill, ADM, Bunge, Trafigura, Glencore, Vitol), and container lines (using time-chartered tonnage to flex capacity). Charterer-side standard forms (Shellvoy, BPVoy, ExxonMobil's voyage form) reflect oil-major-specific risk allocations. Charterers' due-diligence on tonnage is increasingly automated through OCIMF SIRE 2.0 (oil) and RightShip (dry bulk).

### For LMAA / SMA / SCMA tribunals

LMAA is the dominant charter-arbitration venue globally; the LMAA Terms (current edition: LMAA Terms 2017) define the procedural framework. Tribunals issue ~600+ charter awards annually, of which a substantial fraction proceed on appeal to the English Commercial Court under the Arbitration Act 1996 §§ 67–69 (jurisdiction, serious irregularity, point of law). The resulting body of Commercial Court and Court of Appeal authority is the principal modern law-development engine on standard-form clauses.

### For brokers and BIMCO

Charter brokers facilitate the matching of tonnage to cargo demand on the Baltic Exchange and equivalent platforms; their fixture recap forms the operational summary that crystallizes into the formal charterparty. BIMCO's role as the principal industry body — drafting standard forms, sanctions clauses, war-risk clauses, and emerging cyber and emissions clauses — makes it a quasi-regulatory voice for shipowners across the entire commercial-contract surface.

### For ship financiers

Bareboat charters with purchase options (BARECON Part IV) underpin a substantial share of ship finance — Japanese operating leases, Norwegian KS / KG structures, German shipping funds, and modern Chinese leasing-house structures all rely on the bareboat-charter form as the operating contract layer of a structured-finance transaction. The legal robustness of BARECON's purchase-option drafting is therefore a load-bearing assumption of the global ship-finance market.

## Notable case law

- ***The Eurasian Dream* [2002] 1 Lloyd's Rep 719 (Comm).** UK LMAA / Commercial Court on **carrier identity** under bills of lading issued by the master under a time charter. Held the time-chartered shipowner was the carrier; demise-clause analysis turned on the bill's conditions. Modern reference for the time-charter-vs-bareboat-vs-voyage carrier-identity question; and on the **seaworthiness duty** under Hague-Visby Article III r. 1. See [`entities/eurasian-dream-2002.md`](../entities/eurasian-dream-2002.md).
- ***The Achilleas* [2008] UKHL 48.** UK House of Lords on **remoteness** for late redelivery under a time charter. Held that recoverable damages do not extend to the lost premium on a follow-on charter where that loss is outside the type contemplated by the parties. The modern leading authority on remoteness in commercial contracts; cited far beyond the charter context.
- ***The Kos* [2012] UKSC 17.** UK Supreme Court on **employment-and-indemnity** under NYPE; reaffirmed the broad scope of the indemnity for consequences of charterer's orders, and clarified the relationship between off-hire and the indemnity.
- ***The Ocean Victory* [2017] UKSC 35.** UK Supreme Court on the **safe-port warranty** under a time charter; held that an "abnormal occurrence" — the rare combination of swell and gusting wind that produced the *Ocean Victory* casualty at Kashima — was not a breach of the safe-port warranty even though the casualty was foreseeable in retrospect. Substantially narrowed the reach of the safe-port warranty.
- ***The Eastern City* [1958] 2 Lloyd's Rep 127.** UK Court of Appeal; the **classical formulation of safe-port warranty** ("a port is safe where the vessel can reach it, use it, and return from it without...") that subsequent authority refined but never displaced.
- ***MSC Flaminia* (2012)** — fire and explosion in the Atlantic following misdeclared dangerous cargo. **Shipper-charterer indemnity** under the dangerous-cargo regime (Hague-Visby Article IV r. 6 and equivalent charter clauses); the case ran in parallel UK and US proceedings and intersected with general-average distribution. See [`entities/msc-flaminia-2012.md`](../entities/msc-flaminia-2012.md).
