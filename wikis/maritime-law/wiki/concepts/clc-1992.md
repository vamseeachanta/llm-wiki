# CLC 1992 — Civil Liability Convention for Oil Tanker Spills

## Summary

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Scope | Persistent-oil cargo spills from laden or unladen tankers |
| Key Feature | Strict liability + compulsory insurance + tonnage-indexed cap |
| Layered with | 1992 Fund Protocol + 2003 Supplementary Fund Protocol |
| Reference | IMO LEG/CONF.8/4 (CLC) + LEG/CONF.9/15 (Fund) as amended |
| Standards-resolver | [standards/clc-1992](../standards/clc-1992.md) |

## Details

CLC 1992 is the doctrinal core of the international civil-liability regime for oil pollution from tankers. It channels strict liability for pollution damage to the registered shipowner, requires compulsory insurance evidenced by a "Blue Card" certificate, and caps recovery at a tonnage-indexed limit denominated in IMF Special Drawing Rights (SDR). It is the first of three superimposed compensation tiers — the second being the [1992 IOPC Fund](../standards/clc-1992.md) and the third the optional 2003 Supplementary Fund — that together form the modern oil-spill compensation architecture.

## Three-Tier Compensation Architecture

The regime is best understood as three superimposed layers, each with its own SDR cap, addressee, and trigger condition. The caps below reflect the post-2000 amendments (effective 2003) for tier 1+2 and the 2003 Supplementary Fund Protocol (effective 2005) for tier 3.

| Tier | Instrument | Liable party | Cap (per incident, post-2003) | Trigger |
|------|------------|--------------|-------------------------------|---------|
| 1 | CLC 1992 | Registered shipowner | ≈ 4.51M SDR floor → 89.77M SDR ceiling (tonnage-scaled) | Strict, channeled |
| 2 | 1992 Fund Protocol | IOPC Fund 1992 | 203M SDR aggregate (incl. tier 1) | Tier 1 inadequate |
| 3 | 2003 Supplementary Fund | Supplementary Fund | 750M SDR aggregate (incl. tiers 1+2) | Tier 2 inadequate, opt-in states |

### SDR-cap evolution

| Year | Event | Tier-1 ceiling | Tier-1+2 aggregate |
|------|-------|----------------|--------------------|
| 1992 | CLC + Fund 1992 Protocols adopted | 59.7M SDR | 135M SDR |
| 1996 | First tacit-amendment review | (no cap change) | (no cap change) |
| 2000 | LEG.1(82) + LEG.2(82) raised caps ~50.37% | 89.77M SDR | 203M SDR |
| 2003 | Supplementary Fund Protocol opens optional tier 3 | (no change) | 750M SDR (incl. tier 3) |

Practical maximums per incident class — useful when sizing exposure for a P&I club or a coastal-state response budget — fall roughly into: small coastal tanker (under 5,000 GT) capped near the floor; mid-size product tanker around 25M-45M SDR; VLCC at full cap. Tier 2 and tier 3 then layer on top for incidents where shipowner liability is exhausted or the shipowner can invoke the Article V limitation gateway.

## Article-by-Article Doctrinal Notes

The following are doctrinal-synthesis summaries — convention text is not reproduced. Refer to the IMO LEG depositary publication for the operative wording.

- **Article I (Definitions)** — defines "ship" narrowly as a sea-going vessel constructed or adapted to carry oil in bulk as cargo, and "oil" as persistent hydrocarbon mineral oil. Bunker-only spills from non-tankers fall outside CLC and are governed by [bunkers-convention-2001](./bunkers-convention-2001.md).
- **Article III (Channeling of liability)** — strict liability is channeled exclusively to the registered shipowner, with narrow defences (act of war, third-party sabotage with intent to cause damage, negligence of a public authority maintaining navigational aids). Claims against managers, charterers, pilots, salvors, and crew are barred unless they acted with intent or recklessly with knowledge — the "channeling shield" that distinguishes CLC from open-tort regimes.
- **Article V (Limitation gateway)** — the shipowner may limit liability to the tonnage-indexed cap unless the spill resulted from the shipowner's "personal act or omission, committed with the intent to cause such damage, or recklessly and with knowledge that such damage would probably result." This is a deliberately higher bar than the [llmc-1996](./llmc-1996.md) Article 4 break-of-limitation test, and few CLC limitations have ever been broken in reported practice.
- **Article VII (Compulsory insurance)** — vessels carrying more than 2,000 tons of persistent oil cargo must maintain insurance or other financial security up to the Article V cap, evidenced by a state-issued certificate ("Blue Card"). Port states routinely deny entry to non-certificated tankers; certification is the operational chokepoint that makes CLC self-enforcing at the dock.
- **Article IX/XI (Jurisdiction and recognition)** — claims must be brought in the courts of the contracting state(s) in whose territory or EEZ pollution damage occurred, and judgments are mutually recognized among parties. This forum-allocation rule is what made the [mv-prestige-2002](../entities/mv-prestige-2002.md) Spanish criminal-court route a doctrinally contested move (see worked example below).

## Worked Examples — Named Incidents

The four incidents below are the canonical doctrinal-synthesis examples used to teach CLC 1992 in practice. Each is cross-linked to its dedicated entity page.

### Erika (1999) — first major test of post-2000 regime
The [mv-erika-1999](../entities/mv-erika-1999.md) sinking off Brittany released ~20,000 tonnes of heavy fuel oil and produced the first major deployment of the 1992 IOPC Fund. Total admitted claims exceeded the tier-1+2 cap available at the time, which directly motivated the 2000 cap amendments and the 2003 Supplementary Fund Protocol. Parallel French criminal proceedings against the classification society and charterer (Total) ran outside CLC's channeling shield, on the theory that channeling does not bar criminal liability — a doctrinal carve-out still actively litigated.

### Prestige (2002) — jurisdictional stress test
The [mv-prestige-2002](../entities/mv-prestige-2002.md) breakup off Galicia tested CLC's Article IX/XI forum rules when Spain pursued the master (Mangouras) through criminal proceedings and sought damages well above the CLC + Fund cap. The European Court of Human Rights' 2010 *Mangouras v. Spain* ruling on the €3M bail allowed CLC's compulsory-insurance architecture to be used as a benchmark for proportionality — an unintended doctrinal spillover that linked CLC to human-rights review of detention measures. The case also triggered the 2003 Supplementary Fund's first major political momentum.

### Sea Empress (1996) — CLC + Fund interaction baseline
The [sea-empress-1996](../entities/sea-empress-1996.md) grounding at Milford Haven generated UK claims that exhausted tier 1 and pulled the 1992 Fund into a coordinated payout. The subsequent UK Court of Appeal review of "pure economic loss" claims (fishery-dependent businesses without direct property damage) established interpretive guidance on Article I "pollution damage" that the IOPC Fund's claims manual continues to cite.

### Aegean Sea / Amoco Cadiz — pre-1992 baselines
The [aegean-sea-1992](../entities/aegean-sea-1992.md) (La Coruña, 1992) and earlier [amoco-cadiz-1978](../entities/amoco-cadiz-1978.md) (Brittany, 1978) incidents predate or sit at the boundary of the 1992 Protocol regime. Amoco Cadiz in particular drove the 1984 Protocol effort (which never entered force) and ultimately the 1992 Protocols, which is why both are routinely cited as the "before" cases when teaching the modern three-tier architecture. The earlier [torrey-canyon-1967](../entities/torrey-canyon-1967.md) incident is the original political driver of the 1969 CLC, which the 1992 Protocols superseded.

## US Carve-Out — CLC vs OPA 90

The United States is not a party to CLC 1992 and operates the parallel domestic regime under the Oil Pollution Act of 1990. The two regimes diverge across four practitioner-relevant axes:

| Criterion | CLC 1992 | [opa-90](./opa-90.md) |
|-----------|----------|------------------------|
| Geographic scope | Territorial sea + EEZ of contracting states | US navigable waters + 200-nm EEZ + adjoining shorelines |
| Liability standard | Strict, channeled to registered shipowner | Strict, joint-and-several across "responsible parties" (owner, operator, demise charterer) |
| Cap | Tonnage-indexed SDR cap, raised by tacit amendment | Statutory dollar cap, indexed; cap forfeited on gross negligence, willful misconduct, or specified safety violations |
| Cap-loss / break of limitation | High bar — "intent or recklessly with knowledge" (Article V) | Lower bar — gross negligence or regulatory-violation triggers cap loss |
| Compensation tier-up | 1992 Fund + 2003 Supplementary Fund | Domestic Oil Spill Liability Trust Fund (per-incident cap) |
| Forum | CLC contracting-state courts (Articles IX/XI) | US federal courts |

Tankers calling US ports therefore carry dual financial-responsibility evidence: the CLC Article VII Blue Card for non-US calls plus a US Certificate of Financial Responsibility (COFR). P&I clubs typically issue both.

## Practitioner Impact — Stakeholder Map

- **Shipowners and operators** — must hold tonnage-scaled insurance up to the Article V cap, accept channeled strict liability, and maintain Article VII certificates accepted by every port state visited. The Article V high break-of-limitation bar is the principal commercial benefit.
- **P&I clubs (International Group)** — are the de facto issuers of CLC Blue Cards and provide the financial security that makes Article VII enforceable. The cap structure aligns with the International Group's pooling and reinsurance attachment points.
- **Coastal states** — recover clean-up costs and pure-economic-loss claims through CLC tier 1, escalate to the 1992 Fund for tier 2, and (if a Supplementary Fund party) tier 3. Forum allocation under Article IX makes the affected coastal state the natural plaintiff venue.
- **IOPC Fund administrators (London)** — operate the 1992 Fund and Supplementary Fund as inter-state mutual-compensation pools funded by levies on oil receivers in contracting states; their claims manual is the de facto interpretive supplement to CLC Article I "pollution damage."
- **Flag states** — issue Article VII certificates and bear the [flag-state-jurisdiction](./flag-state-jurisdiction.md) responsibilities for verifying insurance, with port-state denial of entry as the operational backstop.
- **Cargo interests / oil receivers** — fund tiers 2 and 3 indirectly through per-tonne contributions; this is the structural reason CLC + Fund is described as a "shared liability" regime rather than a pure shipowner-pays regime.
