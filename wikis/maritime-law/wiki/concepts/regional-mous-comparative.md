# Regional Port-State-Control MoUs — Comparative Synthesis

> Cross-cutting comparative companion to the per-MoU standards-tier metadata resolvers under [`standards/`](../../standards/) and to the Paris-specific doctrinal page at [`concepts/paris-mou.md`](./paris-mou.md). This page is the **navigation hub** for comparative analysis across all nine regional PSC MoUs — the structural-template, NIR-adoption, joint-CIC, dual-membership, and geographic-specialization patterns that the per-MoU pages would otherwise duplicate. The cross-cutting concept-level treatment of port-state control as a regime sits in [`concepts/port-state-control.md`](./port-state-control.md).

## Why a comparative synthesis matters

- **Nine regional MoUs operate globally** with overlapping methodologies and largely non-overlapping geographies. A per-MoU concept page for each of the eight non-Paris MoUs would duplicate ~80% of the Paris-MoU doctrinal content; a single comparative synthesis is the higher-leverage shape.
- **Comparative analysis surfaces patterns** that no single-MoU page can: NIR-adoption pace, joint-CIC participation, dual-membership patterns, geographic specialization, and the post-2022 Black Sea geopolitical disruption.
- **Practitioner navigation.** Most ships transit multiple MoU regions in a single voyage cycle; understanding inter-MoU interactions matters for compliance planning, port-call sequencing, flag-state choice, and insurance-rating inputs.

## NIR (New Inspection Regime) adoption pattern

- **Paris MoU pioneered NIR in 2011** — the comprehensive shift from White/Grey/Black-list-driven inspection cadence to risk-based selection (HRS / SRS / LRS, multi-factor Ship Risk Profile). See [`concepts/paris-mou.md`](./paris-mou.md) for the practitioner translation of the risk-tier mechanics.
- **Tokyo MoU adopted in 2017** — six-year delay from Paris; substantial structural alignment, with regional adaptation for the Asia-Pacific flag-mix.
- **Mediterranean and Indian Ocean adopted in 2014** — earliest non-Paris adopters; both are geographically adjacent to the Paris MoU sphere and share many EU-flag and EU-RO inspection counterparts.
- **Caribbean, Black Sea, Riyadh, Abuja, and Viña del Mar — adoption pending or partial.** Varying levels of NIR-equivalent risk-based selection are in place, but the full Paris-style multi-factor Ship Risk Profile has not been formally adopted.
- **Adoption-pace pattern.** Economic size of the member-authority bloc and EU-affiliation correlate with adoption-speed: Paris and Mediterranean / Indian Ocean (2011 / 2014 / 2014) are at the front; resource-constrained regional secretariats are at the back. The pattern is consistent with a structural-diffusion reading of the Paris-MoU template.

## Joint Concentrated Inspection Campaigns (CICs)

- **Paris and Tokyo MoUs have run joint CICs since the mid-1990s** — annual themed inspection cycles in which all inspections in the participating MoU regions include an expanded scope on the campaign theme during a defined window (typically three months).
- **Caribbean MoU joined joint-CIC coordination since 2009**; Indian Ocean MoU since 2010. Mediterranean, Black Sea, and Riyadh MoUs run aligned-but-not-fully-joint campaigns.
- **Recent themes (illustrative):** STCW hours-of-rest (2024), fire-safety on cargo and passenger ships (2023), stability and cargo-securing (2022), Ballast Water Management Convention compliance (2021), ISM Code refresher (2020).
- **Coordination mechanisms.** Shared CIC themes globally; inspection-data sharing through PSC databases (THETIS-EU on the Paris MoU side, APCIS on the Tokyo MoU side, regional equivalents elsewhere); harmonized reporting forms for the campaign window.

## Member-overlap and dual-membership patterns

- **EU member states.** Full Paris MoU members and observers in Mediterranean MoU (geographically adjacent). The EU coastline is wholly covered by Paris on the Atlantic / North Sea / Baltic and by Mediterranean on the southern frontier.
- **Australia.** Full Tokyo MoU member and participating member of Indian Ocean MoU — reflecting Australia's two-ocean coastline.
- **Oman and UAE.** Full Riyadh MoU members and Indian Ocean MoU members — overlapping inspection-regime focus on Strait-of-Hormuz tanker traffic.
- **Russia and Turkey.** Paris MoU and Black Sea MoU members; post-2022 Russia operational-suspension affects coordination across both regimes (see Geopolitical disruption below).
- **Implications.** Ships visiting overlapping-region ports may be inspected under different MoU NIR variants on consecutive port calls; coordination through the shared PSC databases prevents duplicate-inspection burden and inspection-shopping.

## Geographic specialization patterns

- **Cruise-ship density** drives Caribbean and Mediterranean MoU inspection priorities — expanded passenger-ship inspection scope, life-saving-appliance focus, and SOLAS Chapter II-2 fire-safety emphasis.
- **Tanker traffic** drives Indian Ocean, Riyadh, and Black Sea MoU oil-tanker focus — MARPOL Annex I compliance, cargo-tank inert-gas-system inspection, and ship-to-ship-transfer-area enforcement.
- **Container and bulk-carrier emphasis** is structural to Paris and Tokyo MoUs given the EU-cargo and Asia-Pacific-cargo traffic profiles.
- **Chokepoint coverage.** Strait of Hormuz (Riyadh and Indian Ocean MoUs jointly); Suez (Mediterranean and Indian Ocean MoUs); Bosphorus (Black Sea and Mediterranean MoUs); Cape of Good Hope (Indian Ocean MoU). Chokepoint geography drives inter-MoU coordination demand independently of the formal joint-CIC programme.

## Flag-state ranking and commercial impact

- **Paris MoU's annual White / Grey / Black flag-state performance index** drives global flag-of-convenience economics and insurance markets. The ranking is one of the most-watched outputs of the global PSC regime.
- **Tokyo MoU and Indian Ocean MoU maintain parallel flag-performance rankings** with substantially similar methodology. Caribbean, Mediterranean, Black Sea, Riyadh, Abuja, and Viña del Mar MoUs have lighter ranking-publication practice — some publish annual reports without a formal White/Grey/Black tiering.
- **Effect.** P&I and H&M underwriters consult MoU rankings as inputs to premium calculation; charterers consult rankings in tonnage-selection vetting; competing flag-state administrations consult rankings in their registry-marketing strategy. The commercial-impact channel is independent of any individual detention or banning event.

## Geopolitical disruption — Black Sea MoU 2022+

- **Russia–Ukraine war (2022 onward).** Russian and Ukrainian member-authority coordination within the Black Sea MoU has been effectively suspended; routine information-exchange and shared-inspection-targeting operations across this membership pair have not functioned at pre-2022 cadence.
- **Bulgaria, Romania, Turkey, and Georgia continue operations** under the Black Sea MoU framework, with adaptations to the disrupted regional context.
- **Black Sea Grain Initiative (2022–2023).** A parallel inspection-regime coordinated outside the formal MoU framework, under UN auspices, demonstrated that ad-hoc PSC-adjacent arrangements can function during MoU disruption — but the structural fragility of the formal Black Sea MoU regime in a wartime regional context is now part of its risk profile.
- **Geographically-adjacent MoUs** (Paris on the EU side via the Bosphorus-transit fleet, Mediterranean MoU on the southern Black Sea entry) absorb some of the coordination overflow.

## Practitioner impact

- **For shipowners and chartering operators.** Multi-MoU compliance planning is required for any voyage cycle that crosses MoU boundaries. Flag-state choice and Recognized-Organization relationship affect the Ship Risk Profile (or NIR-equivalent ranking) consistently across all MoUs that have adopted the NIR model — improvement on Paris MoU rankings tends to propagate to Tokyo, Mediterranean, and Indian Ocean MoU outcomes within one to two reporting cycles.
- **For port-state authorities.** Inspection-target generation under THETIS-EU (Paris), APCIS (Tokyo), or regional equivalents; multi-MoU coordination through the database-sharing layer; coordinated CIC-period operations.
- **For insurance markets.** MoU rankings are inputs to P&I and H&M premium calculations and to vetting-program coverage decisions. The Paris MoU annual ranking is the most-consulted single input; the Tokyo and Indian Ocean MoU rankings provide regional confirmation.

## Future trends

- **NIR adoption** is trending across all MoUs. The pending-adoption MoUs (Caribbean, Black Sea, Riyadh, Abuja, Viña del Mar) face structural pressure to converge on the Paris-style risk-based model as the de-facto global standard.
- **Cyber-PSC.** Post-NotPetya (2017) shipping IT incidents drove cyber-risk inspection inclusion. The IMO MSC.428(98) ISM-Code-cyber resolution effective 2021 is now part of the inspection-scope expansion under multiple MoUs.
- **Decarbonization PSC.** Post-IMO MEPC.328(76) GHG strategy (2021, revised 2023): bunker-quality, EEXI (Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index), and CII (Carbon Intensity Indicator) compliance are becoming PSC inspection topics under the Paris and Tokyo MoUs and propagating outward.
- **Digital PSC tools.** AI-assisted inspection-target generation pilots are emerging at the Paris and Tokyo MoU secretariats; broader regional adoption is the structural follow-on.
