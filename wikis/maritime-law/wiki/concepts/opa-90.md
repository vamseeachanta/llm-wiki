# OPA 90 — Doctrinal Synthesis

> Practitioner doctrinal companion to the standards-tier metadata resolver at [`standards/opa-90.md`](../standards/opa-90.md). This page synthesizes what the **Oil Pollution Act of 1990 (OPA 90, 33 U.S.C. §§ 2701–2762)** means in operational practice for tanker operators, oil majors, P&I clubs, coastal-state regulators, and federal trustees — what changed after Exxon Valdez, why the United States built a domestic regime in parallel to (and stricter than) the international CLC/Bunkers framework, and how the *cap-loss* mechanic for negligence and regulatory violations has shaped litigation exposure since Deepwater Horizon. Cross-cutting era treatment lives at [`concepts/environmental-liability.md`](./environmental-liability.md); CLC-vs-OPA-90 multi-criteria comparison anchored at [`standards/clc-1992.md`](../standards/clc-1992.md).

## Why OPA 90 matters

- **US carve-out from the international regime.** The United States is *not* a party to the 1992 CLC, the 2001 Bunkers Convention, or the 2010 HNS Convention. OPA 90 is therefore the **exclusive domestic framework** for oil-spill liability in US navigable waters and the US Exclusive Economic Zone — there is no IOPC fallback layer for incidents in US waters. See [`standards/clc-1992.md`](../standards/clc-1992.md), [`standards/bunkers-convention-2001.md`](../standards/bunkers-convention-2001.md), and [`concepts/hns-convention-2010.md`](./hns-convention-2010.md) for the international parallels the US declined.
- **Higher exposure than international regimes.** Tier-1 vessel-class caps under §2704 are quantitatively lower than CLC's $89.77M ceiling, but OPA 90's **cap-loss** trigger is materially broader: gross negligence, willful misconduct, *or* a violation of an applicable federal safety, construction, or operating regulation will void the cap and expose the responsible party to unlimited liability. Many US incidents have therefore exceeded what would have been the equivalent CLC ceiling.
- **Driver of preventive design measures.** OPA 90 § 4115 imposed a phased single-hull tank-vessel **phase-out** (1990–2015) on a schedule deliberately coordinated with **MARPOL Annex I Regulation 13F (now Reg. 19)** ([`standards/marpol-73-78.md`](../standards/marpol-73-78.md)). The US schedule effectively front-ran the international one and forced a fleet-wide newbuild and conversion cycle that reshaped tanker economics for two decades.
- **Precedent for state-law preservation and extraterritorial reach.** §2718 explicitly **preserves state-law authority** to impose additional liability and additional requirements; OPA 90 does *not* preempt state oil-spill law. Alaska, California, Washington, and several Atlantic states maintain regimes that are stricter on either liability quantum, financial-responsibility evidence, or operational requirements (e.g. tug escorts, vessel traffic management).

## Doctrinal evolution

- **1989-03-24 — Exxon Valdez (Prince William Sound, Alaska).** Approximately **11 million gallons (~257,000 bbl)** of Alaska North Slope crude released; the proximate political driver for OPA 90. The pre-OPA-90 regime — primarily the Federal Water Pollution Control Act (Clean Water Act) §311 and a patchwork of state laws — was widely judged inadequate, and the *Exxon Shipping Co. v. Baker* litigation that followed remained unresolved for nearly two decades.
- **1990-08-18 — OPA 90 enacted.** Public Law 101-380; codified at Title 33 U.S.C. §§ 2701 *et seq.* Signed by President George H. W. Bush. Created a unified domestic oil-spill liability and compensation framework, the **Oil Spill Liability Trust Fund (OSLTF)**, the **Certificate of Financial Responsibility (COFR)** regime, and the §4115 single-hull phase-out.
- **1990–2015 — single-hull phase-out.** §4115's schedule retired single-hull tank vessels operating in US waters on a tonnage- and age-graduated calendar; the 2015 backstop date coincided with the IMO's accelerated MARPOL Annex I phase-out (advanced by MEPC.111(50) following the [*MV Erika*](../entities/mv-erika-1999.md) and [*MV Prestige*](../entities/mv-prestige-2002.md) incidents).
- **2010-04-20 — Deepwater Horizon (Macondo, US Gulf of Mexico).** First major OPA 90 application of the post-Exxon-Valdez generation. Approximately **3.19 million bbl** released over 87 days (US v. BP, EDLA findings). The incident operationalized OPA 90's gross-negligence cap-loss mechanic against an oil major and demonstrated the cumulative reach of OPA 90 stacked with Clean Water Act §311 civil penalties and state-law claims. See [`entities/deepwater-horizon-2010.md`](../entities/deepwater-horizon-2010.md).
- **2010–2014 — BP settlement and aggregate exposure.** Aggregate BP exposure across OPA 90 removal-cost and damages claims, Clean Water Act §311 civil penalties, Natural Resource Damage Assessment (NRDA), state-law claims, and class-action settlements approached **~US$65 billion**. The matter is the modern reference case for "what cap-loss looks like when it lands."

## Statutory architecture — Title 33 USC §§ 2701 *et seq.*

OPA 90 is codified as **Subchapter I of Chapter 40 of Title 33** (33 U.S.C. §§ 2701–2762) but cross-references and amends multiple other titles (Title 26 IRC for OSLTF funding, Title 46 for vessel inspection, Title 43 for OCSLA / offshore facilities). Practitioners must navigate at least five subchapter clusters:

| Subchapter / cluster | Sections | Subject matter |
|---|---|---|
| **I — Oil Pollution Liability and Compensation** | §§ 2701–2720 | Definitions, strict liability, defenses, recoverable damages, §2704 caps, §2704(c) cap-loss, claims procedure, COFR (§2716), state-law preservation (§2718) |
| **II — Prince William Sound** | §§ 2731–2738 | Alaska-specific provisions: navigation safety study, Prince William Sound Citizens' Advisory Council, federal funding for tanker escort and contingency planning |
| **III — Title III provisions and conforming amendments** | §§ 2751–2762 | Tax-related funding mechanics, conforming amendments to other federal statutes, Outer Continental Shelf provisions, Trans-Alaska Pipeline Liability Fund consolidation into OSLTF |
| **§4115 (Title IV — Prevention and Removal)** | OPA 90 § 4115; codified at 46 U.S.C. § 3703a | Single-hull tank-vessel phase-out schedule; bracketed by Coast Guard navigation, vessel-inspection, and personnel rules |
| **§9001 et seq. cross-statutes** | 26 U.S.C. § 9509; 33 U.S.C. § 1321 (CWA §311); 43 U.S.C. § 1813 (Trans-Alaska) | OSLTF funding mechanics; Clean Water Act §311 civil-penalty stacking; pre-OPA-90 Trans-Alaska Pipeline Liability Fund rolled into OSLTF |

The §2701 definitions clause is operationally load-bearing: the breadth of "responsible party" in §2701(32) — vessel owner *or* operator *or* demise charterer for a vessel; lessee *or* permittee for an offshore facility; licensee for a deepwater port; right-of-way holder for a pipeline — is what makes OPA 90's strict-liability reach broader than the CLC's owner-only channeling under [`concepts/clc-1992.md`](./clc-1992.md).

## §2702 — Strict liability scope and damage categories

§2702(a) imposes strict liability on every "responsible party" for a "vessel or facility from which oil is discharged, or which poses the substantial threat of a discharge of oil" into the navigable waters or adjoining shorelines of the United States, or into the US EEZ. The **trigger is broader than the CLC's "incident" formulation**: a *substantial threat* of discharge is itself a covered event, allowing federal removal-cost recovery before any oil reaches the water. The §2703 defenses (act of God; act of war; act of a third party with no contractual relationship to the responsible party) are narrow, fact-specific, and conditioned on the responsible party reporting the incident, providing reasonable cooperation, and complying with removal orders.

§2702(b) defines two parallel exposure heads:

- **§2702(b)(1) — Removal costs.** All federal, state, tribal, and qualifying private removal costs incurred consistent with the National Contingency Plan (40 CFR Part 300). The federal removal-cost head is recoverable by the United States via subrogated OSLTF claims and direct cost-recovery actions.
- **§2702(b)(2) — Damages.** Six enumerated categories:
  1. **Natural-resource damages** — assessed and recovered by federal, state, and tribal trustees under §2706 and the NRDA regulations at 15 CFR Part 990. This is the long-tail head: NRDA assessments routinely run 5–15 years post-incident.
  2. **Real and personal property damage** — physical injury to or destruction of property.
  3. **Loss of subsistence use of natural resources** — subsistence-fishing, subsistence-hunting, and tribal subsistence harvest losses.
  4. **Loss of revenues to government** — lost taxes, royalties, rents, fees, or net profit shares to federal, state, tribal, or local governments.
  5. **Loss of profits and earning capacity** (the "B(2)(E)" head) — economic loss without physical injury, broader than common-law tort recovery, and the head most aggressively litigated in post-Deepwater-Horizon claims.
  6. **Costs of providing increased public services** during a removal — overtime, additional staffing, additional equipment, and additional public-safety services.

## §2704 — Tier-1 caps and the §2704(c) cap-loss gateway

§2704(a) limits the responsible party's aggregate liability for removal costs *and* damages to a tier-1 cap that depends on vessel class or facility type. The Coast Guard indexes these caps periodically (most recently in 2019 and 2023 cycles, codified at **33 CFR Part 138**); do not cite §2704 amounts without checking the current CFR.

| Responsible-party class | §2704(a) cap (illustrative; check 33 CFR Part 138 for current values) |
|---|---|
| Single-hull tank vessel > 3,000 GT | Historically the greater of **$3,500/GT or $25.85M** (post-2019 indexing) |
| Tank vessels (other than single-hull) > 3,000 GT | The greater of **$2,300/GT or $19.43M** |
| Tank vessels ≤ 3,000 GT | The greater of **$2,300/GT or $4.99M** |
| Other vessels (non-tank) | The greater of **$1,200/GT or $997,100** |
| Offshore facilities (other than deepwater ports) | **$137.66M** plus all removal costs (Coast Guard 2019 indexing of the original $75M cap) |
| Onshore facilities and deepwater ports | **$350M plus removal costs** |
| Mobile offshore drilling units (MODU) | Treated as a tank vessel up to its tank-vessel cap, then any excess as an offshore facility |

§2704(c) is the **cap-loss gateway**. The §2704(a) limits **do not apply** if the incident was proximately caused by:

1. **Gross negligence or willful misconduct** of the responsible party, or of an agent / employee acting within the scope of employment, or of a person acting pursuant to a contractual relationship with the responsible party;
2. **Violation of an applicable federal safety, construction, or operating regulation** — the trigger that operationalized cap-loss in *Deepwater Horizon* via 30 CFR Part 250 (BSEE / formerly MMS) findings; or
3. **Failure to report the incident, refusal to cooperate with removal, or refusal to comply with a removal order** — a procedural-cooperation failure post-incident.

Once the cap is broken, exposure is **unlimited** and the responsible party is liable for the full §2702(b) bundle. The §2704(c) cap-loss is materially broader than the CLC Art. V(2) cap-loss (intentional or recklessly-with-knowledge) — see comparison table below. Federal courts have applied §2704(c) on a preponderance-of-evidence civil standard and have not required scienter for the "violation of regulation" prong.

## §2716 COFR and §2718 state-law preservation

**§2716 — Certificates of Financial Responsibility (COFR).** Compulsory financial-responsibility evidence for vessels over 300 GT operating in US waters and for offshore facilities. The COFR is the operational analog to the CLC blue card and is administered by the **National Pollution Funds Center (NPFC)** of the US Coast Guard. Acceptable evidence under 33 CFR Part 138 includes:

- Insurance issued by an authorized COFR-guarantor;
- Surety bond from an authorized surety;
- Qualification as self-insurer (financial-statement test);
- Financial guaranty from a parent or affiliate; or
- Other acceptable evidence approved by NPFC.

Several traditional P&I clubs decline to act as COFR-guarantor of first instance (the COFR sue-and-be-sued clause exposes the guarantor to direct-action suit by claimants, which conflicts with the P&I "pay-to-be-paid" rule). This produced a market in **dedicated COFR-guarantor entities** (Shoreline Mutual, First Line, SCB&A) that interpose between the underlying P&I cover and the COFR certificate.

**§2718 — Preservation of state authority.** OPA 90 explicitly does **not** preempt state oil-spill liability or financial-responsibility law. States may impose **additional** liability, additional requirements, additional fund mechanisms, and additional operational rules. Upheld in *United States v. Locke*, 529 U.S. 89 (2000), which preserved most of Washington State's tanker-operating regime against an OPA-90 preemption challenge while striking certain rules that conflicted with the federal vessel-design regime under [`standards/marpol-73-78.md`](../standards/marpol-73-78.md) and Title 46 vessel-inspection law. *Locke* is the doctrinal anchor for §2718 boundary disputes; it preserves state operational authority but not state vessel-design authority.

## OPA 90 vs CLC / Bunkers / HNS — multi-criteria comparison

| Feature | [CLC 1992](../standards/clc-1992.md) | [Bunkers 2001](../standards/bunkers-convention-2001.md) | [HNS 2010](../concepts/hns-convention-2010.md) | **OPA 90** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Geographic scope | EEZ + territorial sea of party states | EEZ + territorial sea of party states | EEZ + territorial sea of party states | US navigable waters + US EEZ + adjoining shorelines |
| Trigger | "Incident" — discharge or threat of discharge of persistent oil | Discharge of bunker oil from non-tankers | Incident involving carriage of HNS cargo | Discharge **or substantial threat of discharge** of oil |
| Liability standard | Strict, channeled to registered owner | Strict, channeled to registered owner | Strict, channeled to registered owner | Strict, applied to **broader "responsible party"** (owner, operator, demise charterer, lessee, permittee, licensee, ROW holder) |
| Cap-loss trigger | CLC Art. V(2) — *intentional or recklessly with knowledge* | LLMC 1996 Art. 4 — same standard via cross-reference | HNS Art. 9(2) — same standard | **§2704(c) — gross negligence OR willful misconduct OR violation of federal regulation OR failure to cooperate** |
| Tier-1 cap | Up to ~SDR 89.77M (~US$120M+) tonnage-graduated | Defers to LLMC 1996 cap | Up to SDR 100M tonnage-graduated | $4.99M–$25.85M vessel-dependent; $137.66M offshore facility |
| Compulsory financial responsibility | CLC blue card | Bunkers blue card | HNS blue card | **§2716 COFR** (NPFC-administered) |
| Compensation fund | IOPC 1992 Fund + Supplementary Fund (SDR 750M ceiling) | None — LLMC fallback only | HNS Fund (planned, not yet operational) | **OSLTF** (per-incident $1B ceiling) |
| State / sub-federal preemption | International instruments preempt inconsistent state law in party states | Same | Same | **No federal preemption** — §2718 preserves state law |
| Treaty status (US) | Not a party | Not a party | Not a party | US domestic statute |

## OSLTF — Oil Spill Liability Trust Fund

- **Federal trust fund**, administered by the US Coast Guard's **National Pollution Funds Center (NPFC)**, Treasury-held, established under §9509 of the Internal Revenue Code (1986).
- **Funded by a per-barrel tax** on crude oil received at US refineries and on petroleum products imported into the US (the rate has been adjusted by Congress several times; the tax has lapsed and been reinstated repeatedly, most recently restored by the **Inflation Reduction Act of 2022** at $0.09/bbl, scheduled to step up).
- **Pays for**: federal removal costs, costs and damages above the §2704 cap (where the cap is *not* broken), uncompensated damages where the responsible party is unknown or insolvent, and federal NRDA assessment costs.
- **Statutory ceiling**: per-incident expenditure cap of **US$1 billion** (with a $500M sub-cap on natural-resource damage claims). Over US$3–5 billion in cumulative claims have been processed since 1990; OSLTF balances and obligations are tracked in the NPFC's annual reports to Congress.
- **Claims process under §2713.** Claimants must first present a claim to the responsible party (or its COFR-guarantor); only after 90 days, or after denial, may the claimant proceed against OSLTF. NPFC adjudicates claims on a documentary record and pays valid claims by Treasury draft. Denied claims can be appealed to NPFC's appellate process and ultimately to federal district court.
- **Subrogation**: when OSLTF pays a claim, it acquires the claimant's rights against the responsible party and may pursue cost recovery; the Fund is a major civil-litigation actor in post-incident recovery.

## Worked-example mini-case-studies

- **[Exxon Valdez](../entities/torrey-canyon-1967.md) (1989-03-24) — pre-OPA-90 trigger.** ~257,000 bbl Alaska North Slope crude into Prince William Sound. The litigation predated OPA 90 and ran on Clean Water Act §311 and federal-maritime-common-law tracks. *Exxon Shipping Co. v. Baker*, 554 U.S. 471 (2008), capped punitive damages at a 1:1 ratio to compensatory damages under federal maritime common law — a holding that signals the Supreme Court's posture on punitive multipliers in marine-pollution litigation but that does not bind OPA 90 cases directly. Aggregate Exxon exposure exceeded **US$1 billion** in compensatory and punitive damages. The case is the *political driver* for OPA 90 but not itself an OPA 90 case.
- **[Deepwater Horizon](../entities/deepwater-horizon-2010.md) (2010-04-20) — gross-negligence cap-loss.** *In re Oil Spill by the Oil Rig "Deepwater Horizon"*, MDL 2179 (EDLA) produced findings of gross negligence and willful misconduct against BP under §2704(c)(1) **and** regulatory-violation findings under 30 CFR Part 250 (§2704(c)(2)). Aggregate civil penalties under Clean Water Act §311(b)(7) of **US$5.5 billion** (consent decree); OPA 90 / NRDA settlement of **US$8.8 billion**; total estimated cross-head exposure ~**US$65 billion**. The doctrinal lesson: regulatory-compliance record is now inseparable from post-incident liability ceiling, and individual MMS / BSEE incidents-of-noncompliance can become evidentiary anchors for cap-loss findings years later.
- **Cosco Busan (2007-11-07) — bunker spill in San Francisco Bay.** Container ship struck the Bay Bridge in fog; 53,569 gallons of heavy fuel oil (IFO 380) released. OPA 90 covered the bunker discharge (US is not a party to the [Bunkers Convention](../standards/bunkers-convention-2001.md), so domestic OPA 90 liability applied). The pilot was criminally convicted under the Migratory Bird Treaty Act and the Clean Water Act. Aggregate settlement ~**US$44.4M** across NRDA, removal costs, and private claims. Doctrinal anchor: OPA 90 covers non-tanker bunker spills with the same §2702 bundle as tanker spills, illustrating the breadth of US-domestic coverage relative to the international tanker-oil/non-tanker-bunker bifurcation.
- **Refugio Beach Pipeline (2015-05-19) — onshore facility, Plains All American.** Plains All American Pipeline corrosion-induced rupture released ~2,934 bbl of crude near Refugio State Beach, California. OPA 90 onshore-facility liability under §2702 plus California state-law liability under [California's OSPRA / Lempert-Keene-Seastrand Act] (Government Code §§ 8670.1 *et seq.*). Plains was criminally convicted of nine counts including a felony Clean Water Act violation; aggregate exposure across federal civil penalties, state penalties, NRDA, and class-action settlements exceeded **US$335M**. Doctrinal anchor: §2718 state-law overlay materially expands recovery and operational obligations beyond the OPA 90 federal floor.

## State-law parallels — §2718 overlay

§2718 is the gateway through which state regimes operate **on top of** OPA 90, not in lieu of it. The principal regimes:

- **California — OSPRA / Lempert-Keene-Seastrand Oil Spill Prevention and Response Act of 1990** (Government Code §§ 8670.1 *et seq.*; Public Resources Code §§ 8750 *et seq.*). Imposes additional financial-responsibility evidence (up to $1 billion for tank vessels), additional NRDA recovery via the California Office of Spill Prevention and Response (OSPR), and additional contingency-planning requirements. Operates statewide for state waters and through OSPR's role as state on-scene coordinator.
- **Alaska — Alaska Stat. § 46.04** (Oil and Hazardous Substances Pollution Control). Post-Exxon-Valdez Prince William Sound tug-escort regime, contingency-planning requirements (the "C-plan" process), and a separate state oil-spill response fund. Strict liability for cleanup costs and damages with very narrow defenses.
- **Washington — RCW 90.56** (Oil and Hazardous Substance Spill Prevention and Response). Tanker-operating, contingency-planning, and financial-responsibility regime adopted after the 1991 *Tenyo Maru* incident. The regime tested in *United States v. Locke*, 529 U.S. 89 (2000); preserved authorities include design-neutral operating rules, training requirements above STCW, and financial-responsibility quanta exceeding §2716.
- **New Jersey — Spill Compensation and Control Act** (N.J.S.A. 58:10-23.11 *et seq.*). Strict, joint-and-several liability for cleanup and damages, with the New Jersey Spill Compensation Fund as a state analog to OSLTF. Notable for its use in non-petroleum hazardous-substance recoveries beyond the federal OPA 90 oil-only scope.
- **Louisiana — Direct Action statute** (La. R.S. 22:1269). Permits direct action by claimants against the liability insurer of the responsible party, materially altering OPA 90 settlement dynamics for incidents in Louisiana waters and the Louisiana-anchored Gulf of Mexico tort docket. Major operator in *Deepwater Horizon* claim-aggregation strategy.

A practitioner compliance map for any incident in US waters therefore overlays four layers: §2702 federal floor + §2716 COFR + applicable state-law liability + applicable state contingency-planning / operating regime.

| **EPA** | FOSC for inland-zone incidents under National Contingency Plan | Joint investigation authority; CWA §311 civil-penalty referrals |
| **NOAA / DOI / state trustees / tribal trustees** | Natural-resource trustees under §2706; conduct NRDA under 15 CFR Part 990 | Multi-year scientific assessment; recovery channels into restoration projects |

## Practitioner impact

### For tanker operators

The COFR regime under §2716 is operationally heavier than the CLC blue-card regime: NPFC accepts a narrower list of insurance evidence, several P&I clubs do not directly underwrite COFR (which spawned a market in dedicated COFR-guarantor entities), and the 2010–2014 indexing cycle pushed financial-responsibility quanta upward. The §4115 single-hull phase-out drove a fleet-wide newbuild cycle 1990–2015 that reshaped tanker economics. Post-Deepwater-Horizon, charterers' due-diligence questionnaires routinely probe the operator's OPA-90 compliance history, including any §2704(c) regulatory-violation exposure that could be re-weaponized in a future incident.

### For coastal states

§2718's preservation of state authority is the practical foundation for the stricter regimes in **Alaska**, **California (OSPRA / Lempert-Keene-Seastrand)**, **Washington**, **Oregon**, and several Atlantic states. Alaska's regime, in particular, was reinforced post-Exxon-Valdez with the Prince William Sound tug-escort and contingency-planning regime under Alaska Stat. 46.04. California's regime imposes additional financial-responsibility evidence and additional NRDA recovery. Practitioners must perform a layered compliance map: OPA 90 federal floor + applicable state-law overlay.

### For oil majors and offshore operators

The §2704(c) cap-loss for *regulatory violation* is the headline litigation risk. In the Deepwater Horizon litigation, the District Court (EDLA) findings on gross negligence and willful misconduct, combined with regulatory-violation findings under 30 CFR Part 250 (BSEE / formerly MMS), drove the cap-loss outcome. The doctrinal lesson: an offshore project's regulatory-compliance record is now inseparable from its post-incident liability ceiling, and individual MMS / BSEE incidents-of-noncompliance can become evidentiary anchors for cap-loss findings years later.

### For P&I clubs

US-trading vessels require **OPA-90-specific cover** beyond the standard P&I pollution lines: COFR-guarantor underwriting, indexed financial-responsibility evidence, and litigation-cost reserves shaped by US class-action and NRDA exposure. The International Group of P&I Clubs' Pooling Agreement and the General Excess of Loss reinsurance contract are calibrated with a US-domestic exposure tail in mind. The interaction with [`concepts/limitation-of-liability.md`](./limitation-of-liability.md) and [`concepts/llmc-1996.md`](./llmc-1996.md) is asymmetric: OPA 90 displaces LLMC-style global limitation for OPA 90 claims in US waters, but LLMC 1996 (where applicable in admiralty proceedings) may still cap non-OPA-90 heads (e.g. wreck removal, cargo, third-party property loss outside the §2702(b)(2) bundle).

### For federal trustees and NRDA

NOAA, Department of the Interior (DOI), the affected states, and federally-recognized tribes act as natural-resource trustees under §2706 and the NRDA regulations (15 CFR Part 990). NRDA practice is its own discipline: it is the dominant source of long-tail liability after the immediate removal phase, and it operates on multi-year scientific assessment timelines that outlast the criminal and civil-penalty tracks.

## Notable application

- **Exxon Valdez (1989-03-24)** — the pre-OPA-90 driver. The civil and criminal aftermath ran on the prior CWA §311 / common-law track; the *Exxon Shipping Co. v. Baker*, 554 U.S. 471 (2008) ruling capped punitive damages at a 1:1 ratio to compensatory damages under federal maritime common law (a holding that does not bind OPA 90 cases directly but signals the Supreme Court's posture on punitive multipliers in marine-pollution litigation). Aggregate Exxon exposure exceeded **US$1 billion** in compensatory and punitive damages.
- **Deepwater Horizon / Macondo (2010-04-20)** — the modern reference case. *In re Oil Spill by the Oil Rig "Deepwater Horizon"*, MDL 2179 (EDLA), produced findings of gross negligence and willful misconduct against BP under §2704(c), aggregate civil penalties under Clean Water Act §311(b)(7) of **US$5.5 billion** (consent decree), an OPA 90 / NRDA settlement of **US$8.8 billion**, and total estimated exposure across all liability heads (federal, state, private class-action, and shareholder derivative) of approximately **US$65 billion**. See [`entities/deepwater-horizon-2010.md`](../entities/deepwater-horizon-2010.md).
- ***United States v. Locke*, 529 U.S. 89 (2000)** — the Supreme Court's principal §2718 preemption-boundary case, arising from Washington State's tanker-operating regime adopted after the *Tenyo Maru* spill. The Court preserved most state authority but struck rules that conflicted with the federal vessel-design and crew-training scheme; the case remains the doctrinal anchor for OPA-90/state-law boundary disputes.
