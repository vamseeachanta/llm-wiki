# OPA 90 — Doctrinal Synthesis

> Practitioner doctrinal companion to the standards-tier metadata resolver at [`standards/opa-90.md`](../standards/opa-90.md). This page synthesizes what the **Oil Pollution Act of 1990 (OPA 90, 33 U.S.C. §§ 2701–2762)** means in operational practice for tanker operators, oil majors, P&I clubs, coastal-state regulators, and federal trustees — what changed after Exxon Valdez, why the United States built a domestic regime in parallel to (and stricter than) the international CLC/Bunkers framework, and how the *cap-loss* mechanic for negligence and regulatory violations has shaped litigation exposure since Deepwater Horizon.

## Why OPA 90 matters

- **US carve-out from the international regime.** The United States is *not* a party to the 1992 CLC, the 2001 Bunkers Convention, or the 2010 HNS Convention. OPA 90 is therefore the **exclusive domestic framework** for oil-spill liability in US navigable waters and the US Exclusive Economic Zone — there is no IOPC fallback layer for incidents in US waters.
- **Higher exposure than international regimes.** Tier-1 vessel-class caps under §2704 are quantitatively lower than CLC's $89.77M ceiling, but OPA 90's **cap-loss** trigger is materially broader: gross negligence, willful misconduct, *or* a violation of an applicable federal safety, construction, or operating regulation will void the cap and expose the responsible party to unlimited liability. Many US incidents have therefore exceeded what would have been the equivalent CLC ceiling.
- **Driver of preventive design measures.** OPA 90 § 4115 imposed a phased single-hull tank-vessel **phase-out** (1990–2015) on a schedule deliberately coordinated with **MARPOL Annex I Regulation 13F (now Reg. 19)**. The US schedule effectively front-ran the international one and forced a fleet-wide newbuild and conversion cycle that reshaped tanker economics for two decades.
- **Precedent for state-law preservation and extraterritorial reach.** §2718 explicitly **preserves state-law authority** to impose additional liability and additional requirements; OPA 90 does *not* preempt state oil-spill law. Alaska, California, Washington, and several Atlantic states maintain regimes that are stricter on either liability quantum, financial-responsibility evidence, or operational requirements (e.g. tug escorts, vessel traffic management).

## Doctrinal evolution

- **1989-03-24 — Exxon Valdez (Prince William Sound, Alaska).** Approximately **11 million gallons (~257,000 bbl)** of Alaska North Slope crude released; the proximate political driver for OPA 90. The pre-OPA-90 regime — primarily the Federal Water Pollution Control Act (Clean Water Act) §311 and a patchwork of state laws — was widely judged inadequate, and the *Exxon Shipping Co. v. Baker* litigation that followed remained unresolved for nearly two decades.
- **1990-08-18 — OPA 90 enacted.** Public Law 101-380; codified at Title 33 U.S.C. §§ 2701 *et seq.* Signed by President George H. W. Bush. Created a unified domestic oil-spill liability and compensation framework, the **Oil Spill Liability Trust Fund (OSLTF)**, the **Certificate of Financial Responsibility (COFR)** regime, and the §4115 single-hull phase-out.
- **1990–2015 — single-hull phase-out.** §4115's schedule retired single-hull tank vessels operating in US waters on a tonnage- and age-graduated calendar; the 2015 backstop date coincided with the IMO's accelerated MARPOL Annex I phase-out (advanced by MEPC.111(50) following the *MV Erika* and *MV Prestige* incidents).
- **2010-04-20 — Deepwater Horizon (Macondo, US Gulf of Mexico).** First major OPA 90 application of the post-Exxon-Valdez generation. Approximately **3.19 million bbl** released over 87 days (US v. BP, EDLA findings). The incident operationalized OPA 90's gross-negligence cap-loss mechanic against an oil major and demonstrated the cumulative reach of OPA 90 stacked with Clean Water Act §311 civil penalties and state-law claims.
- **2010–2014 — BP settlement and aggregate exposure.** Aggregate BP exposure across OPA 90 removal-cost and damages claims, Clean Water Act §311 civil penalties, Natural Resource Damage Assessment (NRDA), state-law claims, and class-action settlements approached **~US$65 billion**. The matter is the modern reference case for "what cap-loss looks like when it lands."

## Key statutory provisions

- **§2702 — strict liability.** A "responsible party" is strictly liable for removal costs and damages from any **discharge, or substantial threat of discharge, of oil** into the navigable waters or adjoining shorelines of the United States, or into the US EEZ. No fault element; the defenses are narrow (act of God, act of war, act of a third party, with notification and cooperation duties attached).
- **§2702(b)(2) — categories of recoverable damages.**
  - Removal costs (federal, state, tribal, and private).
  - Natural-resource damages (assessed and recovered by federal, state, and tribal trustees under the NRDA process).
  - Real and personal property damage.
  - Loss of subsistence use of natural resources.
  - Loss of revenues to government.
  - Loss of profits and earning capacity (the "B(2)(E)" head — economic loss without physical injury, broader than common-law tort recovery).
  - Costs of providing increased public services during a removal.
- **§2704 — Tier-1 liability caps (vessel-class, indexed for inflation; 2023 limits shown).**
  - Single-hull tank vessel > 3,000 GT — historically **US$1,200/GT or $10M**, whichever is greater (pre-2010 base; the OPA 90 amendments raised these and the Coast Guard indexes them periodically).
  - Tank vessels (other than single-hull) — **the greater of $2,200/GT or $18.7M**.
  - Other vessels — **the greater of $1,100/GT or $939,800**.
  - Offshore facilities — historically $75M plus removal costs; the cap on offshore-facility damages was revisited after Deepwater Horizon but remains far below realistic worst-case exposure when the cap is preserved.
  - The Coast Guard's Final Rule cycle (most recently 2019, codified at 33 CFR Part 138) is the operative source for current dollar values; do not cite §2704 amounts without checking the current CFR.
- **§2704(c) — cap-loss (the "break the limit" gateway).** The §2704 limits **do not apply** if the incident was proximately caused by:
  - gross negligence or willful misconduct of the responsible party (or an agent / employee / contractor),
  - violation of an applicable federal safety, construction, or operating regulation, or
  - failure to report the incident, refusal to cooperate with removal, or refusal to comply with a removal order.
  Once the cap is broken, exposure is **unlimited** and the responsible party is liable for the full §2702(b)(2) bundle.
- **§2716 — Certificates of Financial Responsibility (COFR).** Compulsory financial-responsibility evidence for vessels over 300 GT operating in US waters and for offshore facilities. The COFR is the operational analog to the CLC blue card and is administered by the **National Pollution Funds Center (NPFC)**. Acceptable evidence includes insurance, surety bond, qualification as self-insurer, or financial guaranty.
- **§2718 — preservation of state authority.** OPA 90 explicitly does **not** preempt state oil-spill liability or financial-responsibility law. States may impose **additional** liability, additional requirements, additional fund mechanisms, and additional operational rules. This was upheld in *United States v. Locke*, 529 U.S. 89 (2000), which preserved most of Washington State's tanker-operating regime against OPA-90 preemption challenge while striking certain rules that conflicted with the federal vessel-design regime.

## OPA 90 vs CLC / Bunkers — comparative summary

| Feature | CLC 1992 + Bunkers 2001 | OPA 90 |
|---|---|---|
| Geographic scope | EEZ + territorial sea of party states | US navigable waters + US EEZ + adjoining shorelines |
| Strict liability | Yes — channeled to the registered owner | Yes — broader "responsible party" entity scope (vessel owner, operator, demise charterer, offshore-facility lessee/permittee, pipeline operator, deepwater port licensee) |
| Compulsory insurance / financial responsibility | Yes — CLC blue card / Bunkers blue card | Yes — Certificate of Financial Responsibility (COFR) under §2716 |
| Cap-loss for negligence | Limited — only intentional or recklessly-with-knowledge conduct (CLC Art. V(2)) | **Broad** — gross negligence, willful misconduct, *or* federal regulation violation (§2704(c)) |
| Tier-1 cap (illustrative) | CLC: up to ~SDR 89.77M (~US$120M+); Bunkers: defers to LLMC 1996 | $18.7M–$939k vessel-dependent under §2704; $75M offshore-facility cap |
| Compensation funds | IOPC 1992 Fund + Supplementary Fund (treaty-based, levy-financed) | OSLTF (federal trust fund, financed by per-barrel tax) |
| State-law preemption | International instruments preempt inconsistent state law in party states | **No federal preemption** — §2718 preserves state law |
| Treaty status (US) | US not a party | US domestic statute |

## OSLTF — Oil Spill Liability Trust Fund

- **Federal trust fund**, administered by the US Coast Guard's **National Pollution Funds Center (NPFC)**, Treasury-held, established under §9509 of the Internal Revenue Code (1986).
- **Funded by a per-barrel tax** on crude oil received at US refineries and on petroleum products imported into the US (the rate has been adjusted by Congress several times; the tax has lapsed and been reinstated repeatedly, most recently restored by the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022).
- **Pays for**: federal removal costs, costs and damages above the §2704 cap (where the cap is *not* broken), uncompensated damages where the responsible party is unknown or insolvent, and federal NRDA assessment costs.
- **Statutory ceiling**: per-incident expenditure cap of **US$1 billion** (with a $500M sub-cap on natural-resource damage claims). Over US$3–5 billion in cumulative claims have been processed since 1990; OSLTF balances and obligations are tracked in the NPFC's annual reports to Congress.
- **Subrogation**: when OSLTF pays a claim, it acquires the claimant's rights against the responsible party and may pursue cost recovery; the Fund is a major civil-litigation actor in post-incident recovery.

## Practitioner impact

### For tanker operators

The COFR regime under §2716 is operationally heavier than the CLC blue-card regime: NPFC accepts a narrower list of insurance evidence, several P&I clubs do not directly underwrite COFR (which spawned a market in dedicated COFR-guarantor entities), and the 2010–2014 indexing cycle pushed financial-responsibility quanta upward. The §4115 single-hull phase-out drove a fleet-wide newbuild cycle 1990–2015 that reshaped tanker economics. Post-Deepwater-Horizon, charterers' due-diligence questionnaires routinely probe the operator's OPA-90 compliance history, including any §2704(c) regulatory-violation exposure that could be re-weaponized in a future incident.

### For coastal states

§2718's preservation of state authority is the practical foundation for the stricter regimes in **Alaska**, **California (OSPRA / Lempert-Keene-Seastrand)**, **Washington**, **Oregon**, and several Atlantic states. Alaska's regime, in particular, was reinforced post-Exxon-Valdez with the Prince William Sound tug-escort and contingency-planning regime under Alaska Stat. 46.04. California's regime imposes additional financial-responsibility evidence and additional NRDA recovery. Practitioners must perform a layered compliance map: OPA 90 federal floor + applicable state-law overlay.

### For oil majors and offshore operators

The §2704(c) cap-loss for *regulatory violation* is the headline litigation risk. In the Deepwater Horizon litigation, the District Court (EDLA) findings on gross negligence and willful misconduct, combined with regulatory-violation findings under 30 CFR Part 250 (BSEE / formerly MMS), drove the cap-loss outcome. The doctrinal lesson: an offshore project's regulatory-compliance record is now inseparable from its post-incident liability ceiling, and individual MMS / BSEE incidents-of-noncompliance can become evidentiary anchors for cap-loss findings years later.

### For P&I clubs

US-trading vessels require **OPA-90-specific cover** beyond the standard P&I pollution lines: COFR-guarantor underwriting, indexed financial-responsibility evidence, and litigation-cost reserves shaped by US class-action and NRDA exposure. The International Group of P&I Clubs' Pooling Agreement and the General Excess of Loss reinsurance contract are calibrated with a US-domestic exposure tail in mind.

### For federal trustees and NRDA

NOAA, Department of the Interior (DOI), the affected states, and federally-recognized tribes act as natural-resource trustees under §2706 and the NRDA regulations (15 CFR Part 990). NRDA practice is its own discipline: it is the dominant source of long-tail liability after the immediate removal phase, and it operates on multi-year scientific assessment timelines that outlast the criminal and civil-penalty tracks.

## Notable application

- **Exxon Valdez (1989-03-24)** — the pre-OPA-90 driver. The civil and criminal aftermath ran on the prior CWA §311 / common-law track; the *Exxon Shipping Co. v. Baker*, 554 U.S. 471 (2008) ruling capped punitive damages at a 1:1 ratio to compensatory damages under federal maritime common law (a holding that does not bind OPA 90 cases directly but signals the Supreme Court's posture on punitive multipliers in marine-pollution litigation). Aggregate Exxon exposure exceeded **US$1 billion** in compensatory and punitive damages.
- **Deepwater Horizon / Macondo (2010-04-20)** — the modern reference case. *In re Oil Spill by the Oil Rig "Deepwater Horizon"*, MDL 2179 (EDLA), produced findings of gross negligence and willful misconduct against BP under §2704(c), aggregate civil penalties under Clean Water Act §311(b)(7) of **US$5.5 billion** (consent decree), an OPA 90 / NRDA settlement of **US$8.8 billion**, and total estimated exposure across all liability heads (federal, state, private class-action, and shareholder derivative) of approximately **US$65 billion**. See [`entities/deepwater-horizon-2010.md`](../entities/deepwater-horizon-2010.md).
- ***United States v. Locke*, 529 U.S. 89 (2000)** — the Supreme Court's principal §2718 preemption-boundary case, arising from Washington State's tanker-operating regime adopted after the *Tenyo Maru* spill. The Court preserved most state authority but struck rules that conflicted with the federal vessel-design and crew-training scheme; the case remains the doctrinal anchor for OPA-90/state-law boundary disputes.
