# Limitation of Liability — Doctrine and Statutory Regimes

## Scope

Limitation of liability is the doctrine under which a shipowner (and certain related parties) may cap aggregate exposure for maritime claims arising out of a single incident at a tonnage-based ceiling. This page is the doctrinal layer above the convention surface; the LLMC 1976/1996 page (`./llmc-1996.md`) carries the convention identity, and the CLC/Bunker/HNS pages cover regime-specific oil-pollution caps.

## Doctrine

- Historical rationale (Mandaraka-Sheppard Vol 2 Ch. 17): risk-allocation between shipowners and claimants in a high-capital, high-loss industry; corollary to compulsory insurance.
- Modern global regime: **Convention on Limitation of Liability for Maritime Claims (LLMC) 1976** as amended by the **1996 Protocol** and the **2012 amendments** (in force 2015); see `./llmc-1996.md`.
- Tonnage-based limits are calculated in **Special Drawing Rights (SDR)** with a stepped function on gross tonnage; LLMC limits are periodically uplifted by IMO Legal Committee tacit-amendment procedure.
- The right to limit is **lost** where the claimant proves the loss resulted from the limiting party's personal act or omission committed with intent to cause loss, or recklessly with knowledge that loss would probably result (LLMC Art. 4 — the "wilful misconduct" gateway, deliberately set higher than gross negligence).
- US regime: **Limitation of Vessel Owner's Liability Act 1851** (46 U.S.C. §§ 30501-30512) — caps owner exposure at post-casualty vessel value plus pending freight; routinely invoked in admiralty as a procedural concursus device. The US is NOT party to LLMC.
- Regime-specific caps run in parallel: CLC 1992 for tanker oil pollution, Bunker 2001 for non-tanker bunker spills, HNS 2010 for hazardous and noxious substances, Athens 2002 for passenger claims, MLC 2006 for seafarer compensation.

## Cases

- **MV Prestige (2002)** — Spanish prosecution and the related civil claims interrogated the LLMC limitation defence and the gateway for breaking the cap.
- **Deepwater Horizon (2010)** — operator BP and rig owner Transocean made distinct limitation claims; Transocean's Limitation Act petition was heavily contested under the gross-negligence and OPA-90 unlimited-liability framework.
- **The Bowbelle (1996)** — UK case on whether dredger owner could rely on LLMC limitation after the *Marchioness* disaster; the cap was held available.
