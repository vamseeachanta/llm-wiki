# Salvage — Doctrinal Synthesis

> Practitioner doctrinal companion to the standards-tier metadata resolver at [`standards/salvage-convention-1989.md`](../standards/salvage-convention-1989.md). This page synthesizes salvage as a **cross-cutting doctrine** — voluntary rescue of property at peril for reward — across its statutory, contractual, and economic surfaces. It covers the salvor-claim doctrine (LOF, SCOPIC, "no cure no pay", Article 14 environmental compensation), the salvage-vs-wreck-removal boundary, and the modern economics of professional salvage. It is doctrinally distinct from [`concepts/salvage-convention-1989.md`](./salvage-convention-1989.md), which is an instrument-specific synthesis of the IMO 1989 convention.

## Why salvage matters

- **The voluntary-rescue economic engine.** Modern professional salvage (SMIT, Boskalis, Resolve Marine, Nippon Salvage, Donjon-SMIT) underwrites a critical-incident response capability that no flag state, port state, or coastal state maintains independently. The salvage award is the price the international system pays to keep that capacity standing-by.
- **"No cure, no pay" as risk allocation.** The salvor risks its own equipment, crew, and operating capital; the property owner pays only on success. The maxim — codified at Article 12 of the **1989 Salvage Convention** — is the load-bearing economic principle of the entire industry. Without it, salvors would have to bill on time-and-materials, which would shift the casualty risk back to the property owner and collapse the standby-readiness model.
- **Article 14 environmental-protection compensation.** The 1989 convention's headline doctrinal innovation: salvors who confront an **environmentally threatening** casualty earn special compensation (expenses + up to a 30 % uplift, or 100 % in exceptional cases) **even when the cure fails**. This was the regime's response to the *Amoco Cadiz* (1978) and *Exxon Valdez* (1989) lessons that "no cure, no pay" had become a perverse incentive for salvors to walk away from oil-spill casualties where success was uncertain.
- **Salvage-vs-wreck-removal boundary.** Once a casualty crosses from "property in peril" into "wreck", the regime shifts from salvage (Lloyd's Open Form / 1989 convention) to wreck removal (Nairobi WRC 2007 / national wreck-removal acts). The boundary is not a bright line and frequently involves the same contractor under successive contracts — but it triggers different liability ceilings, different funding sources, and different regulatory authorities.

## Doctrinal evolution

- **Pre-modern — Lex Rhodia (~3rd century BCE) and medieval admiralty.** The proto-salvage doctrine sits inside the same maritime-loss-sharing tradition as general average. Roman and Byzantine maritime law recognized rewards for voluntary rescue at sea; medieval admiralty courts (Oléron, Wisby, Hanse) carried the doctrine into European mercantile law.
- **1910 Brussels Convention for the Unification of Certain Rules of Law respecting Assistance and Salvage at Sea.** First modern multilateral codification. Established "no cure, no pay" as the controlling rule and a six-factor reward analysis. Ratified widely; survived as the international substrate for most of the 20th century.
- **1967 *Torrey Canyon* + 1978 *Amoco Cadiz* + 1989 *Exxon Valdez*.** Three cumulative casualties exposed the same defect: under pure "no cure, no pay", salvors had a rational incentive to abandon environmentally threatening tanker casualties where cure looked unlikely. The 1989 convention's Article 14 was the deliberate doctrinal correction.
- **1989-04-28 — International Convention on Salvage adopted (IMO London).** In force **14 July 1996**. Replaced the 1910 Brussels Convention between states-parties. Article 13 lists the ten reward criteria; Article 14 introduces special compensation for environmentally threatening casualties.
- **1999 — SCOPIC clause.** Special Compensation P&I Club Clause; jointly developed by the International Salvage Union and the International Group of P&I Clubs in response to *The Nagasaki Spirit* (1997, UK House of Lords) which had ruled that "expenses" under Article 14 did not include profit. SCOPIC is a **contract substitute** that the salvor can invoke unilaterally, replacing Article 14's special compensation with a fixed daily-rate tariff plus a 25 % uplift, backed directly by the property underwriters' P&I club. Latest revision: **SCOPIC 2020**.
- **2000s–2020s — LOF revisions.** Lloyd's Open Form has evolved through LOF 80, LOF 90, LOF 95, LOF 2000, and **LOF 2020**, each iteration tightening the SCOPIC-invocation mechanics and the publication regime for arbitral awards. The London Salvage Arbitration Branch publishes anonymized award summaries that establish the de facto doctrinal benchmarks for reward-quantum analysis.

## Three-tier salvage analytical framework

Practitioners working a modern salvage case structure their analysis on three doctrinal tiers, each with distinct success conditions and reward mechanics:

- **Tier 1 — Pure cure (Article 13).** Classical salvage: the salvor saves the property, earns a reward proportionate to salved value and the danger averted. "No cure, no pay" applies. Article 13's ten criteria — salved value, skill, danger, time, expenses, success — drive the arbitral quantum. This is the dominant case in benign-environment casualties (groundings without spill, machinery failures, fires extinguished before structural loss).
- **Tier 2 — Environmental salvage (Article 14 special compensation).** The casualty threatens damage to the environment. The salvor recovers expenses (defined post-*Nagasaki Spirit* as cost-without-profit) plus up to 30 % uplift (or up to 100 % in exceptional cases at the tribunal's discretion) where the salvor prevented or minimized environmental damage. Available **even on partial or complete failure of cure** — the regime's deliberate departure from "no cure, no pay" in pollution casualties. In contemporary practice, Article 14 has been largely supplanted in operational salvage by **SCOPIC** (see Tier 3).
- **Tier 3 — SCOPIC-substitute (contractual).** Where the salvor invokes SCOPIC under an LOF, the Article 14 mechanism is **suspended** and replaced by a tariff-based daily-rate calculation plus 25 % uplift, secured directly by the property underwriters' P&I club issuing a SCOPIC security. SCOPIC may be invoked from the outset or after operations begin. Tier-1 reward (pure cure under Article 13) remains available in parallel; the salvor takes the higher of (Article 13 reward) or (SCOPIC-tariff payable).

The three tiers are layered, not exclusive: a single casualty can move between them as the operational reality evolves.

## LOF + SCOPIC mechanics

- **Lloyd's Open Form 2020 (LOF 2020).** Single-page master agreement; binds salvor and property in a salvage contract on standard terms with an English-law / London-arbitration governing surface. The LOF allows the salvor to take "best endeavours" without negotiating quantum upfront — quantum is determined post-operation by the **Lloyd's Salvage Arbitration Branch**.
- **SCOPIC invocation.** The salvor delivers written SCOPIC notice to the owner / underwriters. From the moment of invocation, the SCOPIC daily tariff begins to run, the P&I club must issue a SCOPIC security (typically US$3 million as a starting figure, increased on request), and the salvor may not later withdraw SCOPIC retrospectively.
- **Award structure.** The arbitrator first determines the Article 13 reward (pure cure, against salved value). If SCOPIC has been invoked, the arbitrator separately calculates the SCOPIC-tariff payable. The salvor recovers **the higher of the two**, plus the unrecovered SCOPIC daily-rate accrued before SCOPIC invocation. Where the Article 13 reward exceeds the SCOPIC-tariff, the property pays the Article 13 reward and SCOPIC is "off the table"; where SCOPIC exceeds Article 13, the P&I-club-secured SCOPIC payment is made and Article 13 is "off the table".
- **Discount mechanism.** Where the salvor invokes SCOPIC and the Article 13 reward later proves to be the higher figure, the SCOPIC tariff is **discounted by 25 %** of the SCOPIC-tariff value. This is the mechanism that disincentivizes salvors from invoking SCOPIC in clearly-curable casualties.

## Practitioner impact

### For salvors

The standby-readiness business model depends on the convention-and-LOF surface holding firm. Modern salvors maintain forward-deployed assets (anchor-handling tugs, oil-spill response equipment, deep-water diving and ROV capability) priced on the assumption that LOF / SCOPIC awards will recover the operating-cost-plus-margin tail across a portfolio of casualties. Erosion of the cap-loss gateway in subject-matter conventions (HNS, Bunkers, OPA 90) has progressively expanded environmental-salvage demand, but Article 14 / SCOPIC-tariff economics have not kept pace with operating-cost inflation — the 2020 SCOPIC revision tariffs are widely regarded by ISU members as below break-even on the largest-class deepwater casualties.

### For shipowners and P&I clubs

The LOF surface is the operationally efficient choice in 80–90 % of casualties: no contract negotiation, no quantum dispute pre-operation, fast salvor commitment. The P&I club's SCOPIC-security obligation is part of the standard cover. Underwriters track salvor-performance-history at a portfolio level and increasingly run their own technical advisors (the "SCR" — Special Casualty Representative) on major casualties to oversee SCOPIC-tariff accrual.

### For coastal states

Article 14 / SCOPIC operationalize the public-policy interest in environmentally clean casualty resolution. Coastal-state regulators (US Coast Guard, MAIB, BSH, AMSA, MMC Mauritius) increasingly assert directive authority over salvage operations under places-of-refuge regimes (IMO Resolution A.949(23)), national wreck-removal acts, and oil-pollution-emergency-response plans. The salvor must coordinate with the regulator from the outset; "salvor's choice of method" is no longer purely a commercial decision.

### For courts and arbitrators

The Lloyd's Salvage Arbitration Branch and its appellate tier are the dominant doctrinal forum for Article 13 / Article 14 quantum analysis in the English-speaking world. Civil-law jurisdictions reach similar outcomes through national maritime-code provisions (French Code des transports L5132-1 *et seq.*, German HGB §§ 740–753, Japanese Commercial Code Book IV Chapter 7). National courts handle salvage-as-defence to property-damage claims and salvage-vs-wreck-removal boundary disputes.

## Salvage-vs-wreck-removal interface

- **The doctrinal boundary.** Salvage applies to property *in peril at sea*; wreck removal applies to property *that has come to rest as a wreck* and creates a navigational, pollution, or safety hazard. The regimes have different funding sources, different liability ceilings, and different regulatory drivers.
- **Operational continuity.** In practice, the same contractor often operates under successive contracts — first an LOF for the salvage attempt, then a wreck-removal contract (often Wreckhire 2010 or Wreckstage 2010, BIMCO-published) for the post-loss removal. The transition typically occurs when the casualty has become structurally unsalvageable and the regulatory authority has issued a wreck-removal directive.
- **Liability cap interaction.** Salvor claims (including Article 14 special compensation) are **excluded from LLMC** under Article 3(b) — the salvor's claim ranks against the property owner's general estate, not against the limitation fund. Wreck-removal claims are excluded from LLMC by default but may be opted-in by a state-party under Article 18(1); states that have done so (e.g. Australia, Canada, UK for certain claim categories) fold wreck-removal into the LLMC fund.
- **Funding distinction.** Salvage rewards are paid by **property and cargo interests** (proportionate to salved value). Wreck-removal costs are paid by the **vessel owner** under Nairobi WRC 2007 compulsory-insurance evidence, and where the owner is uncovered or insolvent, by the **coastal state** acting as last-resort.

## Notable case law

- ***The Tojo Maru* (1971) AC 242 (UK House of Lords).** Foundational modern authority on salvor liability for negligence during salvage operations. A diver employed by the salvor fired a bolt-gun into a tank that had not been fully gas-freed, causing an explosion. House of Lords held the salvor liable for negligent salvage, rejecting the argument that the salvor's status immunized them from negligence claims. Doctrinally important for the "professional salvor's standard of care" baseline.
- ***The Nagasaki Spirit* (1997) AC 455 (UK House of Lords).** The defining case on Article 14 "expenses". Held that "expenses" are the salvor's actual cost without an embedded profit element — i.e. Article 14 special compensation does not, by itself, deliver a positive return on capital. The ruling was the proximate doctrinal driver for the 1999 SCOPIC clause.
- ***The Rhone Maru* (1980) AC 423 (UK).** Earlier authority on salvor liability and the "professional standard" applicable to specialist salvage operations. Cited alongside *Tojo Maru* in the salvor-negligence doctrine line.
- ***MV Wakashio* (2020, Mauritius).** Capesize bulk carrier grounded on Pointe d'Esny coral reef, 25 July 2020. SMIT Salvage and Nippon Salvage operated under an LOF; salvage operations were complicated by environmental sensitivity (Blue Bay Marine Park) and by the eventual structural failure of the vessel that converted the casualty from salvage into wreck removal. Modern reference case for the salvage-to-wreck-removal transition under coastal-state regulatory pressure. See [`entities/mv-wakashio-2020.md`](../entities/mv-wakashio-2020.md).
- ***Solomon Trader* (2019, Solomon Islands).** Bulk carrier grounded on Rennell Island reef, 5 February 2019. Australian-flagged salvage operation under coastal-state regulatory direction; the operation was prolonged by access constraints and by the eventual decision to remove rather than refloat. Cited in the Pacific-region wreck-removal literature for the financial-responsibility-evidence gap (the vessel's hull-and-machinery and P&I cover proved insufficient against the actual wreck-removal cost).
- ***Stena Polaris* (2009, UK Salvage Arbitration).** Recent published Article 13 reward analysis; salvor refloated a chemical tanker off the UK coast under LOF. Award discussed the interplay of pure-cure reward against environmental-protection contribution under Article 13(1)(b).
- ***MV Ever Given* (2021, Suez).** SMIT Salvage / Nippon Salvage refloated the vessel after the six-day Suez blockage. The salvage award was negotiated separately from the Suez Canal Authority's detention and damages claims. See [`entities/mv-ever-given-2021.md`](../entities/mv-ever-given-2021.md).
