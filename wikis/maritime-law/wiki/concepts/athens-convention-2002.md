# Athens Convention 2002 — Passenger Death and Injury

## Summary

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Scope | Passenger death, injury, and luggage claims arising on international sea voyages |
| Key Feature | Two-tier liability (250,000 SDR strict / 400,000 SDR fault) + compulsory insurance + direct action |
| Layered with | 1976 Protocol (war-risk reservation), 2002 Protocol (compulsory insurance), EU Regulation 392/2009 |
| Reference | IMO LEG/CONF.13/20 (2002 Protocol), entry-into-force 2014-04-23 |
| Standards-resolver | [standards/athens-convention-2002](../standards/athens-convention-2002.md) |

## Details

The Athens Convention 2002 — strictly the 1974 Athens Convention Relating to the Carriage of Passengers and their Luggage by Sea, as amended by the 2002 Protocol — is the doctrinal core of the international civil-liability regime for passenger death, personal injury, and luggage loss on international sea voyages. It channels a two-tier liability framework to the carrier (with separate "performing carrier" exposure), requires compulsory insurance for vessels licensed to carry more than twelve passengers, and grants claimants a direct right of action against the insurer up to the compulsory-insurance ceiling.

Unlike [clc-1992](./clc-1992.md), [bunkers-convention-2001](./bunkers-convention-2001.md), and [hns-convention-2010](./hns-convention-2010.md) — which together regulate cargo-side and bunker-side pollution liability — Athens governs the passenger side of the maritime-liability map. It interlocks with [llmc-1996](./llmc-1996.md) at Article 7(2) (separate aggregate cap for passenger claims) and is incorporated across the European Union by Regulation 392/2009.

## Protocol Architecture — 1974 → 1976 → 2002

The instrument practitioners now call "Athens 2002" is the third generation of a treaty whose original 1974 form is no longer the governing text in any modern jurisdiction. The architecture below is the indispensable orientation for reading any pre-2014 case law.

| Year | Instrument | Headline change | Status |
|------|------------|-----------------|--------|
| 1974 | Athens Convention (original) | Fault-based liability, 46,666 SDR per passenger cap (originally Poincaré francs, later SDR-converted) | Superseded — only relevant for historic claims |
| 1976 | 1976 Protocol | SDR-denomination of caps, war-risk reservation framework | Superseded by 2002 Protocol |
| 1990 | 1990 Protocol (never in force) | First attempt at higher caps + compulsory insurance — failed to attract ratifications | Abandoned in favour of 2002 Protocol |
| 2002 | 2002 Protocol | Two-tier liability (strict + fault), compulsory insurance, direct action against insurer, IMO Reservation on war risks | **In force 2014-04-23**; consolidated text is "Athens Convention 2002" |
| 2009 | EU Regulation 392/2009 | Incorporates Athens 2002 across EU member states ahead of IMO entry-into-force; extends to Class A and B domestic voyages | In force in EU since 2012-12-31 |

The 2002 Protocol's protracted ratification timeline — adopted 2002, in force 2014 — is itself a doctrinal datum: it tracks the difficulty of marrying compulsory war-risk insurance to a market that effectively withdrew terrorism cover after 9/11. The IMO Reservation adopted 2006 by the IMO Legal Committee, which permits states to limit war-risk exposure to 250,000 SDR per passenger / 340,000,000 SDR per ship per incident, was the political precondition that unblocked ratifications.

## Two-Tier Liability Caps

The 2002 Protocol's defining innovation is a split between strict liability for "shipping incidents" and fault-based liability for non-shipping incidents, each with its own per-passenger ceiling.

| Tier | Trigger | Liability standard | Per-passenger cap | Aggregate per-incident cap (per ship) |
|------|---------|---------------------|-------------------|----------------------------------------|
| 1 | Shipping incident (shipwreck, capsizing, collision, stranding, explosion, fire, defect) | Strict, with narrow defences | 250,000 SDR | 340,000,000 SDR (war-risk reserved); otherwise no aggregate cap on Tier 1 alone |
| 2 | Same shipping incident, additional fault-based recovery above Tier 1 | Fault-based (carrier's fault or neglect) | Up to 400,000 SDR | Subject to LLMC 1996 Article 7(2) passenger aggregate (175,000 SDR × number of passengers ship is licensed to carry) |
| Non-shipping incident | All other on-board injury (slip, fall, food, assault, etc.) | Fault-based (claimant must prove carrier fault) | Up to 400,000 SDR | Same LLMC interlock |
| Luggage / cabin luggage | Damage to luggage | Fault-based with rebuttable presumption | 2,250 SDR (cabin luggage) → 12,700 SDR (vehicles) → 3,375 SDR (other) | n/a |

The "shipping-incident" definition (Article 3.5) is what makes Tier 1 enforceable: it is the trigger that flips the burden of proof. For non-shipping incidents the passenger must prove fault; for shipping incidents the carrier is strictly liable up to the Tier 1 cap and faces a rebuttable presumption of fault on Tier 2.

### Interaction with LLMC 1996

[llmc-1996](./llmc-1996.md) Article 7(2) sets a separate aggregate cap for passenger claims of 175,000 SDR × the number of passengers the ship is licensed to carry, with no overall ceiling. Athens caps run **per passenger**; LLMC caps run **per ship per incident**. The two regimes operate in parallel rather than as alternatives: Athens decides whether the carrier is liable and at what per-head ceiling; LLMC decides whether the carrier can globally limit aggregate exposure across all passenger claims arising from the same incident. A ship licensed for 3,000 passengers therefore faces a notional LLMC passenger aggregate of 525M SDR even before Athens per-head caps are applied. For claims numbers below the LLMC ceiling, Athens controls; above it, LLMC controls.

## Article-by-Article Doctrinal Notes

The following are doctrinal-synthesis summaries — convention text is not reproduced. Refer to the IMO LEG depositary publication for the operative wording.

- **Article 1 (Definitions)** — defines "carrier" (party that concludes the carriage contract) and "performing carrier" (party that actually performs the carriage, e.g. the operator of a ship chartered to a tour operator). Both are jointly and severally liable, which is the doctrinal hook for cruise-line liability when the contracting tour operator differs from the operating shipowner.
- **Article 3 (Liability of the carrier)** — Article 3.1 establishes Tier 1 strict liability for shipping-incident loss up to 250,000 SDR per passenger, subject to defences in Article 3.1(a)–(b) (act of war / hostilities / terrorism; act of a third party with intent to cause the incident). Article 3.5 defines "shipping incident" exhaustively. Article 3.5(a) presumes carrier fault for Tier 2 above 250,000 SDR, with the burden on the carrier to prove the incident occurred "without the fault or neglect of the carrier."
- **Article 4bis (Compulsory insurance)** — vessels licensed to carry more than twelve passengers must maintain insurance or other financial security up to 250,000 SDR per passenger times the licensed passenger capacity, evidenced by a state-issued "Athens Certificate." Direct action against the insurer is granted to claimants up to the compulsory-insurance ceiling, and the insurer's defences are limited (the insurer may not invoke defences personal to the carrier other than wilful misconduct of the carrier itself). This is the structural innovation that distinguishes Athens 2002 from Athens 1974/1976 and is operationally analogous to the [clc-1992](./clc-1992.md) Article VII "Blue Card."
- **Article 7 (Limit of liability for death and personal injury)** — sets the Tier 2 ceiling at 400,000 SDR per passenger and prescribes the limitation gateway: the carrier loses the Article 7 limit if the loss "resulted from an act or omission of the carrier done with the intent to cause such loss, or recklessly and with knowledge that such loss would probably result." This high-bar test mirrors [clc-1992](./clc-1992.md) Article V and is deliberately stricter than the [llmc-1996](./llmc-1996.md) Article 4 test.
- **Article 16 (Time-bar)** — two-year limitation period for actions, with extension and tolling rules favourable to the claimant where the carrier or insurer has not given clear notice.
- **Article 17 (Jurisdiction / forum)** — the claimant may sue in the courts of (a) the defendant's habitual residence or principal place of business, (b) the place of departure or destination under the contract, (c) the claimant's domicile or habitual residence if the defendant has a place of business there, or (d) the place where the contract was made. Forum-shopping latitude is therefore wider than under [clc-1992](./clc-1992.md) Article IX, which restricts plaintiffs to the place of damage.
- **Article 17bis (Recognition and enforcement)** — judgments rendered by a competent Athens-2002 forum are recognized and enforced in all other contracting states, subject to narrow public-policy and natural-justice exceptions.
- **Article 22 (War-risk reservation)** — permits states, under the IMO Reservation framework, to limit war-risk exposure to 250,000 SDR per passenger / 340M SDR per ship per incident. This reservation is what made the compulsory-insurance market viable post-9/11; absence of the reservation would have left underwriters exposed to uncapped terrorism liability.

## Worked Examples — Named Incidents

The following are doctrinal-synthesis examples used to teach Athens 2002 in practice. None of these incidents currently has a dedicated entity page in this wiki; they are referenced descriptively here pending future entity-page work.

### Costa Concordia (2012) — multi-jurisdictional Athens stress test
The Costa Concordia capsizing off Isola del Giglio (32 fatalities; ~4,200 persons on board) is the canonical post-2002-Protocol cruise-liability case, even though the incident occurred before the Protocol's 2014 entry-into-force. EU Regulation 392/2009 had already incorporated Athens 2002 across EU member states from 2012-12-31, so Italian-law claims proceeded effectively under the Athens 2002 framework. Aggregate passenger claims exceeded €1B across multiple jurisdictions (Italy, Germany, France, US-resident passengers in US courts), illustrating the practical force of Article 17 forum latitude. The case also tested the carrier / performing-carrier distinction (Article 1) where Carnival Corporation as ultimate parent and Costa Crociere as operator faced parallel claims.

### Norman Atlantic (2014) — EU 392/2009 in operation
The Norman Atlantic ferry fire in the Adriatic (28 fatalities; ~478 persons on board) is the cleanest worked example of EU Regulation 392/2009 operating as the legal substrate. The vessel was on an Italy-Greece intra-EU voyage, both flag (Italy) and EU jurisdiction applied, and claims proceeded under Athens 2002 as incorporated by 392/2009 with direct action against the insurer (Article 4bis equivalent in 392/2009). The incident also exercised the joint-and-several "carrier / performing carrier" rule: the contracting carrier (ANEK Lines as charterer) and the operating carrier (Visemar di Navigazione as owner) faced parallel claims.

### Sewol (2014) — comparison case, not Athens-applicable
The Sewol sinking off South Korea (304 fatalities, predominantly secondary-school students on a class trip) is doctrinally important precisely because Athens 2002 **does not apply**: the voyage was a domestic coastal route (Incheon to Jeju), not an international voyage within the meaning of Athens Article 2. South Korean domestic compensation law applied instead. Sewol is the standard comparative example used to illustrate the "international voyage" gateway in Athens Article 2, and it motivates EU Regulation 392/2009's extension of Athens-equivalent protection to Class A and B domestic voyages within the EU.

### Marchioness (1989) — pre-Athens historic precedent
The Marchioness collision on the River Thames (51 fatalities) predates Athens by decades and was governed by the Merchant Shipping Acts and English common-law negligence. Routinely cited in UK practitioner literature as the "before" case when teaching the modern Athens framework: domestic-only voyage, pre-compulsory-insurance regime, common-law fault-based recovery without per-passenger SDR caps. Marchioness directly motivated the UK's 1995 Merchant Shipping Act passenger-claim updates and (more distally) UK support for the 1990/2002 Protocol effort.

## Athens vs CLC vs Bunkers — Multi-Criteria Comparison

Athens sits on the passenger-liability axis of the maritime-liability map; CLC/Bunkers/HNS sit on the cargo / bunker / hazardous-substances axis. Practitioners routinely confuse which regime applies because all four use a similar architectural template (compulsory insurance + cap + direct action), so the discriminating axes below are the practitioner-relevant ones.

| Criterion | Athens 2002 | [clc-1992](./clc-1992.md) | [bunkers-convention-2001](./bunkers-convention-2001.md) |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------|
| Loss type | Passenger death / injury / luggage | Persistent oil cargo pollution | Bunker-fuel pollution from non-tankers |
| Liable party | Carrier + performing carrier (joint/several) | Registered shipowner (channeled) | Registered shipowner + bareboat charterer + manager + operator |
| Cap basis | Per passenger (250k Tier 1 / 400k Tier 2 SDR) | Per incident, tonnage-indexed SDR ceiling | LLMC cap (no separate Bunkers cap) |
| Cap-loss test | Article 7 — intent or recklessness with knowledge | Article V — same high-bar test | LLMC Article 4 — same high-bar test |
| Compulsory insurance evidence | Athens Certificate (Article 4bis) | Blue Card (Article VII) | Bunkers Certificate (Article 7) |
| Direct action against insurer | Yes (Article 4bis) | Yes (Article VII) | Yes (Article 7) |
| Forum | Article 17 — claimant's choice among four fora | Article IX — place of damage only | Article 9 — place of damage |
| LLMC interlock | Article 7(2) passenger aggregate | LLMC Article 3(b) excludes CLC claims | LLMC governs cap |
| EU regional supplement | Regulation 392/2009 | Directive 2004/35 (Environmental Liability) | Directive 2004/35 |

## EU Regulation 392/2009 — Regional Supplement

EU Regulation 392/2009 ("the Passenger Liability Regulation" or PLR) incorporates Athens 2002 into EU law and extends the framework in three operationally significant ways:

1. **Domestic-voyage extension** — applies to Class A and Class B domestic voyages within EU waters, closing the "international voyage" gap in Athens Article 2. (The Sewol comparison above illustrates the gap PLR fills.)
2. **Pre-IMO entry-into-force** — became binding within the EU on 2012-12-31, ahead of the IMO 2014-04-23 entry-into-force, so EU member states applied the Athens 2002 framework two years earlier than the global treaty regime.
3. **Mandatory advance payment** — Article 6 PLR requires the carrier to make an advance payment of at least €21,000 within fifteen days of identification of the person entitled to damages following a shipping incident causing death or personal injury. This is a procedural innovation not present in Athens 2002 itself.

A separate Regulation 1177/2010 ("the Passenger Rights Regulation") covers passenger rights for delay and cancellation and sits adjacent to but outside the Athens / 392/2009 liability framework.

## Practitioner Impact — Stakeholder Map

- **Cruise lines and ferry operators** — must hold per-passenger insurance up to 250,000 SDR × licensed passenger capacity, accept Tier 1 strict liability for shipping incidents, and maintain Article 4bis Athens Certificates accepted by every port state visited. The Article 7 high break-of-limitation bar is the principal commercial benefit. Performing-carrier joint-and-several exposure (Article 1) requires careful contract drafting in cruise / ferry charter chains.
- **P&I clubs (International Group)** — are the de facto issuers of Athens Certificates and provide the financial security that makes Article 4bis enforceable. The compulsory-insurance ceiling at 250,000 SDR per passenger × passenger capacity drives reinsurance attachment-point pricing for cruise tonnage.
- **Plaintiffs' bar (passenger-side personal-injury counsel)** — Article 17 forum latitude is the most plaintiff-favourable element of the regime, allowing US-resident passengers on European cruises to sue in US-jurisdiction fora where any defendant has a place of business. Direct-action rights against the insurer (Article 4bis) bypass carrier-insolvency risk.
- **Coastal states** — host competent fora under Article 17 when the place of departure or destination falls within their territory. Port-state denial of entry to non-certificated vessels is the operational backstop for Article 4bis enforcement.
- **Flag states** — issue Athens Certificates and bear the [flag-state-jurisdiction](./flag-state-jurisdiction.md) responsibilities for verifying insurance.
- **EU regulators (DG MOVE)** — administer Regulation 392/2009, monitor Article 6 PLR advance-payment compliance, and coordinate cross-border enforcement among EU member states.
- **War-risk underwriters** — operate under the IMO Reservation framework (Article 22) which caps war-risk exposure at 250,000 SDR per passenger / 340M SDR per ship per incident, allowing the compulsory-insurance market to function post-9/11.
