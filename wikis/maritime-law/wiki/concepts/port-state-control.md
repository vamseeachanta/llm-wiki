# Port-State Control — Paris MoU, Tokyo MoU, and Regional Regimes

## Scope

Port-state control (PSC) is the regime under which a sovereign port state inspects foreign-flag vessels in its ports for compliance with international maritime conventions (SOLAS, MARPOL, MLC, COLREGs, STCW, Load Lines). This page covers the inspection-and-detention regime; flag-state primary jurisdiction is covered separately in `./flag-state-jurisdiction.md`.

## Doctrine

- Legal anchor: **UNCLOS Article 218** (port-state enforcement against discharge violations) and **Article 219** (detention of unseaworthy vessels), supplemented by the inspection mandates of SOLAS, MARPOL, and MLC themselves.
- Operational anchor: **regional Memoranda of Understanding** harmonising inspection criteria, target rates, and detention reporting across signatory port states. The two oldest and largest are the **Paris MoU** (1982, 28 European and North-Atlantic states) and the **Tokyo MoU** (1993, Asia-Pacific). Other MoUs cover the Caribbean, Mediterranean, Indian Ocean, Black Sea, West and Central Africa, Latin America, Persian Gulf.
- Inspection priority is risk-based: each MoU runs a vessel-risk classification (Paris MoU: New Inspection Regime / Ship Risk Profile) that combines flag performance, recognised-organisation performance, company performance, ship type, and age to set inspection frequency.
- Detention authority: PSC officers may detain vessels exhibiting deficiencies that present a clear hazard to safety, health, or the environment; detention triggers public listing and contributes to the flag-state and company performance-tier calculations.
- **United States operates outside the MoU framework** — the US Coast Guard runs its own PSC programme (the "Qualship 21" inverse-recognition tier) anchored in the Ports and Waterways Safety Act and 33 CFR Subchapter P.
- Outcome: PSC has become the de-facto global enforcement mechanism for IMO conventions where flag-state enforcement has historically been weak (open-registry "flags of convenience").

## Cases

- **MV Erika (1999)** — French detention proceedings and subsequent prosecution illustrated the PSC-to-prosecution pipeline; influenced the Paris MoU's switch to the New Inspection Regime in 2011.
- **MV Wakashio (2020)** — Mauritian PSC and post-grounding criminal proceedings under MARPOL Annex I.
