# Bunkers Convention 2001 — Bunker Fuel Spill Liability

## Summary

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Scope | Bunker (fuel) oil spills from non-tankers ≥1,000 GT |
| Key Feature | Strict liability + compulsory insurance + LLMC-referenced cap |
| Liability circle | Registered owner OR bareboat charterer OR manager OR operator |
| Compensation tiering | None standalone — caps via [llmc-1996](./llmc-1996.md) |
| Reference | IMO LEG/CONF.12/19 (adopted 2001-03-23; in force 2008-11-21) |
| Standards-resolver | [standards/bunkers-convention-2001](../standards/bunkers-convention-2001.md) |

## Details

The Bunkers Convention 2001 is the international civil-liability instrument for **fuel-oil pollution from non-tanker vessels** — the gap-fill counterpart to [clc-1992](./clc-1992.md), which covers persistent oil cargo from tankers but leaves bunker spills from container ships, bulk carriers, ferries, RoRo, and cruise ships outside its scope. The convention was deliberately modelled on CLC 1992's doctrinal core — strict liability, compulsory insurance, direct action against the insurer — but with three architectural divergences: (i) a **wider liability circle** that channels strict liability jointly and severally to the registered shipowner, bareboat charterer, manager, **and** operator; (ii) **no standalone liability cap**, with limitation routed through [llmc-1996](./llmc-1996.md) instead; and (iii) **no second-tier compensation fund** equivalent to the IOPC Funds. Adopted at IMO London on 2001-03-23 and entered into force 2008-11-21, the convention now binds 95+ states covering the substantial majority of world non-tanker tonnage by flag — with the United States the principal non-party (gap covered domestically by [opa-90](./opa-90.md)).

## Three-Tier Liability/Coverage Architecture

Unlike CLC 1992's three superimposed *compensation* tiers (shipowner / IOPC Fund / Supplementary Fund), the Bunkers Convention exposes a three-tier *liability and coverage* stack: the substantive liability rule (Article 3), the compulsory-insurance and certificate machinery (Article 7), and the limitation interface (Article 6, routing to LLMC). Each layer is necessary and none is sufficient on its own — together they make the regime self-enforcing at the dock.

| Tier | Instrument layer | Mechanism | Operational chokepoint |
|------|------------------|-----------|------------------------|
| 1 | Article 3 — Strict liability | Channeled to broad liability circle (owner, bareboat charterer, manager, operator); narrow defences only | Pollution damage in territorial sea or EEZ of party state |
| 2 | Article 7 — Compulsory insurance + BOPIC | Insurance up to LLMC limit; **Bunker Oil Pollution Insurance Certificate (BOPIC)** issued by flag state; direct action against insurer permitted | Port-state denial of entry to non-certificated ships ≥1,000 GT |
| 3 | Article 6 — LLMC interface | Cap aggregates by reference to LLMC 1976 / 1996 Protocol applicable in forum state | Limitation fund constituted under LLMC procedures |

### Coverage gap relative to CLC

The architectural omission of a Bunkers fund is a recognized compensation gap. Where a CLC casualty exhausts the shipowner's tier-1 cap, the [1992 IOPC Fund](../standards/clc-1992.md) tops up to roughly 203M SDR aggregate, and a 2003 Supplementary Fund party can escalate to 750M SDR. A Bunkers casualty that exhausts the LLMC cap has **no further international route** — the claimant exits the convention's machinery and falls back on whatever domestic-law residual exists. Some IMO Legal Committee delegations have proposed a Bunkers fund expansion; as of 2024 no instrument has been adopted.

## Article-by-Article Doctrinal Notes

The following are doctrinal-synthesis summaries — convention text is not reproduced. Refer to the IMO LEG depositary publication for the operative wording.

- **Article 1 (Definitions)** — defines "**bunker oil**" as *any hydrocarbon mineral oil, including lubricating oil, used or intended to be used for the operation or propulsion of the ship, and any residues of such oil*. Critically, "**ship**" is expanded beyond CLC's tanker-only scope to *any seagoing vessel and seaborne craft, of any type whatsoever*. This single definitional move is the convention's whole purpose: it pulls container ships, bulk carriers, ferries, RoRo, fishing vessels, cruise ships, and warships-under-commercial-charter into the regime that CLC's "tanker carrying persistent oil cargo" trigger excluded. "Pollution damage" mirrors the CLC formulation, including reasonable measures of reinstatement.
- **Article 3 (Strict liability)** — strict liability for pollution damage caused by bunker oil from the ship at the time of the incident, channeled to the **registered shipowner OR bareboat charterer OR manager OR operator** jointly and severally. Defences are CLC-style narrow: act of war or hostilities, exceptional natural phenomenon of inevitable and irresistible character, third-party act done with intent to cause damage, and negligence of public authorities maintaining navigational aids. The wider liability circle is the principal divergence from CLC Article III's owner-only channeling — reflecting the more diffuse commercial structure of non-tanker operation, where the registered owner is often a single-vessel SPV with thin assets.
- **Article 6 (Limitation interface)** — Bunkers liability is **subject to limitation under the LLMC 1976 or 1996 Protocol**, whichever the forum state is party to. The convention does **not** create a standalone liability cap — this is the principal architectural divergence from CLC Article V's tonnage-indexed SDR cap. The practical effect is that the cap rises automatically with future LLMC tacit-amendment increases without re-amendment of Bunkers itself; the 2012 LLMC amendments raising property-claim limits flowed through to Bunkers exposure without convention-level action. See [limitation-of-liability](./limitation-of-liability.md) for the limitation-doctrine framing.
- **Article 7 (Compulsory insurance + BOPIC)** — ships **above 1,000 GT** registered in a state party must maintain insurance or other financial security covering the LLMC limit, certified by a **Bunker Oil Pollution Insurance Certificate ("BOPIC" — the Bunkers analogue to the CLC "Blue Card")**. The certificate is issued by the flag state (or, for ships flagged in non-parties calling at party ports, by a party state). **Direct action against the insurer** is permitted (Article 7(10)), preserving the claimant's recovery route when the operator is insolvent, sanctioned, or jurisdictionally elusive. Port states routinely deny entry to non-certificated ships ≥1,000 GT — this is the operational chokepoint that makes the convention self-enforcing.
- **Article 9 (Jurisdiction)** — actions for compensation may be brought only in the courts of the state party in whose territory (including territorial sea or EEZ) the pollution damage was caused or reasonable preventive measures were taken. Mirrors CLC Article IX forum-allocation; recognized judgments enforce among parties.
- **Article 10 (Time-bar)** — claims are extinguished unless action is brought within **three years from the date when the damage occurred**, with an absolute longstop of **six years from the date of the incident**. The dual-trigger structure (damage-discovery and incident-occurrence) is identical to CLC Article VIII and is the standard public-international-law convention pattern for pollution-claim time-bar.

## Worked Examples — Named Incidents

The four incidents below are the canonical doctrinal-synthesis examples used to teach the Bunkers Convention in practice. Each is cross-linked to its dedicated entity page where one exists.

### Cosco Busan (2007) — pre-Bunkers-in-force, US gap-illustrator
The container ship *Cosco Busan* struck the San Francisco–Oakland Bay Bridge in fog on 2007-11-07 and released approximately 200 m³ (~58,000 US gallons) of IFO-380 bunker fuel into San Francisco Bay. The incident occurred **before the Bunkers Convention's 2008-11-21 entry-into-force**, and the United States is in any event not a party. Litigation proceeded under [opa-90](./opa-90.md) — the master pleaded guilty to misdemeanor MARPOL and Migratory Bird Treaty Act counts, and the operator paid US$44.4M to settle natural-resource damage claims. The case is taught as the canonical illustration of the gap that Bunkers fills internationally: a non-tanker fuel-oil release with no available CLC route, requiring resort to a domestic statutory regime. It also demonstrates why the United States chose to maintain OPA 90 rather than accede.

### MV Wakashio (2020) — first major Bunkers Convention application at scale
The [mv-wakashio-2020](../entities/mv-wakashio-2020.md) Capesize bulk carrier grounded on the reef off Pointe d'Esny, Mauritius, on 2020-07-25 and breached on 2020-08-06, releasing approximately 1,000 tonnes of Very-Low-Sulfur Fuel Oil (VLSFO) into a UNESCO biosphere reserve and Ramsar wetland. Mauritius is a Bunkers Convention party, and the casualty became the **first high-visibility application of the convention's full machinery at scale**. Britannia P&I Club's BOPIC enabled direct claims against the club; aggregate disclosed wreck-removal expenditure (under the parallel [nairobi-wrc-2007](./nairobi-wrc-2007.md) framework) reached approximately US$36M, with total response and compensation costs in the higher tens of millions. Operator Mitsui OSK Lines accepted civil-liability exposure under the Bunkers framework alongside ¥1B (~US$9M) of ex gratia commitments. The case validated the post-Erika compensation stack outside CLC: Bunkers 2001 + Nairobi WRC 2007 + LLMC 1996 operating in concert for non-tanker bulker incidents.

### Sanchi (2018) — bunker-fuel sub-incident of a multi-pollutant casualty
The [sanchi-2018](../entities/sanchi-2018.md) condensate-tanker collision in the East China Sea on 2018-01-06 released approximately 113,000 tonnes of South Pars condensate (CLC marginal — condensate sits at the boundary of "persistent oil") *and* approximately 1,000 tonnes of bunker IFO/MGO from the hull. The bunker-fuel release fell unambiguously within the **Bunkers Convention 2001 strict-liability machinery** — illustrating the doctrinally important point that on a multi-pollutant casualty involving both cargo oil and bunker fuel, the two regimes operate in parallel on the same incident: CLC for the cargo-derived component (where persistent), Bunkers for the bunker-fuel component. Sanctions-impacted insurance complicated practical recovery, but the doctrinal allocation is clean.

### Rena (2011) — pre-accession driver
The Liberian-flagged container ship *Rena* grounded on Astrolabe Reef off Tauranga, New Zealand, on 2011-10-05 and ultimately released roughly 350 tonnes of fuel oil. New Zealand was not yet a Bunkers Convention party at the time (acceded 2013), but the casualty's response and litigation already reflected Bunkers principles, and the incident **drove NZ accession**. Practitioners cite Rena alongside Wakashio as paired references for non-tanker bunker-spill response — Rena as the late pre-accession reference and Wakashio as the first full in-force application.

## Bunkers vs CLC vs OPA-90 — Multi-Criteria Comparison

The three regimes exhaust the international and major-domestic civil-liability landscape for ship-source oil pollution. Practitioners must routinely allocate a single casualty across them — particularly on multi-pollutant scenarios (Sanchi-class) or multi-jurisdictional voyages (any tanker calling US ports).

| Criterion | Bunkers Convention 2001 | [clc-1992](./clc-1992.md) | [opa-90](./opa-90.md) |
|-----------|-------------------------|---------------------------|------------------------|
| Pollutant scope | Bunker (fuel) oil — including lubricating oil and residues | Persistent oil **cargo** | All oil (cargo + bunker), persistent and non-persistent |
| Vessel scope | Any seagoing vessel ≥1,000 GT (compulsory insurance threshold); strict liability applies regardless of GT | Tanker carrying persistent oil cargo in bulk | Any vessel in US navigable waters or EEZ |
| Geographic scope | Territorial sea + EEZ of contracting state | Territorial sea + EEZ of contracting state | US navigable waters + 200-nm EEZ + adjoining shorelines |
| Liability standard | Strict, channeled to broad circle (owner OR bareboat charterer OR manager OR operator, joint and several) | Strict, channeled exclusively to **registered shipowner** | Strict, joint-and-several across "responsible parties" (owner, operator, demise charterer) |
| Cap | **None standalone** — cap routed through [llmc-1996](./llmc-1996.md) | Tonnage-indexed SDR cap (Article V); raised by tacit amendment | Statutory dollar cap, indexed; cap forfeited on gross negligence, willful misconduct, or specified safety violations |
| Compulsory insurance | **BOPIC** — Bunker Oil Pollution Insurance Certificate (Article 7); ships ≥1,000 GT | Article VII certificate ("Blue Card"); tankers carrying ≥2,000 tonnes of persistent oil cargo | US Certificate of Financial Responsibility (COFR) |
| Direct action against insurer | Yes (Article 7(10)) | Yes (Article VII(8)) | Yes (33 USC §2716(f)) |
| Compensation tier-up beyond shipowner | **None** — no Bunkers fund | 1992 IOPC Fund + 2003 Supplementary Fund | Domestic Oil Spill Liability Trust Fund |
| Forum | Contracting state where pollution damage occurred (Article 9) | Contracting state(s) where pollution damage occurred (Articles IX/XI) | US federal courts |
| US status | Non-party | Non-party | US-domestic statute |

The practical implication is that **non-tankers calling US ports carry dual financial-responsibility evidence**: the BOPIC for non-US calls plus a US COFR for the OPA 90 exposure. P&I clubs (International Group) typically issue both. **Tankers carrying persistent oil cargo carry triple cover**: CLC Blue Card + BOPIC (for the bunker-oil component) + COFR. The Bunkers Convention's BOPIC is required for tankers too, since the cargo-vs-bunker pollutant scope distinction means CLC does not cover the tanker's own fuel-oil bunkers.

## Practitioner Impact — Stakeholder Map

- **Shipowners and operators** — must hold insurance up to the LLMC limit for any seagoing vessel ≥1,000 GT, accept channeled strict liability across the broader Article 3 circle, and maintain Article 7 BOPIC certificates accepted by every port state visited. The wider liability circle is a structural commercial risk relative to CLC: a manager or operator can be held jointly liable even where the registered owner is the contracting party with the cargo interest.
- **P&I clubs (International Group)** — are the de facto issuers of BOPIC certificates and provide the financial security that makes Article 7 enforceable. The convention's reliance on LLMC for the cap rather than a standalone Bunkers cap aligns the regime with the International Group's pooling and reinsurance attachment points (which are in turn LLMC-calibrated). Britannia's role in [mv-wakashio-2020](../entities/mv-wakashio-2020.md) is the canonical practitioner illustration.
- **Coastal states** — recover clean-up costs and pure-economic-loss claims through the Article 3 strict-liability route, escalate to the LLMC limitation fund where applicable, and have **no further international tier** if claims exceed the LLMC cap. Forum allocation under Article 9 makes the affected coastal state the natural plaintiff venue. SIDS coastal states (Mauritius being the worked example) face a structural compensation-capacity gap relative to large-economy coastal states.
- **Insurers (P&I + war-risk underwriters)** — are exposed to direct action under Article 7(10), which structurally disintermediates the shipowner from the recovery path. The post-Sanchi sanctions-and-war-risk-exclusion landscape has complicated this where flag-state operations carry sanctions exposure; underwriters now treat sanctions-listed-flag operational risk as a discrete coverage category.
- **Flag states** — issue BOPIC certificates and bear [flag-state-jurisdiction](./flag-state-jurisdiction.md) responsibilities for verifying insurance, with port-state denial of entry as the operational backstop. Non-party flag states (Iran, Venezuela, sanctions-impacted registries) face market exclusion via party-state port denial.
- **Masters and operators** — are not personally liable under the Article 3 channeling shield (channeled to the corporate liability circle), but criminal-law and master's-personal-liability routes remain available outside the convention. Wakashio's master pleaded guilty to Mauritian merchant-shipping offences (endangering safe navigation) carrying a 20-month suspended sentence — illustrating the criminal-law parallel track that runs outside Bunkers' civil-liability machinery.
- **IMO Legal Committee** — the convention's stewarding body; has discussed Bunkers fund-tier expansion and remains the forum for any future amendments. As of 2024 no formal amendments to the convention have been adopted since its 2001 conclusion.
