# Rotterdam Rules — Doctrinal Synthesis

> Practitioner doctrinal companion to the standards-tier metadata resolver at [`standards/rotterdam-rules.md`](../standards/rotterdam-rules.md). This page synthesizes what the UN Convention on Contracts for the International Carriage of Goods Wholly or Partly by Sea (2009) would mean for carriers, shippers, freight forwarders, and P&I clubs if it entered force — and why, after fifteen years on the table, it has not. The page does not reproduce convention text.

## Why Rotterdam Rules matter (despite not being in force)

- **Comprehensive carrier-liability modernization.** Rotterdam was drafted to address the container-era multimodal reality that **Hague-Visby (1924/1968)** was never designed for. Hague-Visby pre-dates intermodal containerization, electronic transport documents, just-in-time global supply chains, and the operational reality that most cargo damage in modern trade is not localized to the sea leg.
- **Door-to-door scope.** Rotterdam covers inland-leg incidents under sea-carriage contracts, eliminating the long-running "where did the damage occur" liability-gap puzzle that splits cargo claims between sea-carriage law (Hague-Visby), road-carriage law (CMR), and rail-carriage law (CIM-COTIF). For containerized cargo moving under a single through-bill of lading, the gap is the practitioner's daily problem.
- **Failed-launch case study.** Even setting aside the merits, Rotterdam is the textbook illustration of why convention-modernization-by-ratification is hard in shipping: aligned industry resistance, asymmetric switching costs, and a competing-incumbent regime with a century of jurisprudence behind it. The sociology of the failure is as instructive as the text would have been operationally.

## Doctrinal evolution

- **2002** — UNCITRAL Working Group III on Transport Law begins drafting, with significant input from the **CMI** (Comité Maritime International) and the maritime industry.
- **2008** — UN General Assembly resolution **A/RES/63/122** adopts the convention text on 2008-12-11; opens for signature in **Rotterdam** in 2009.
- **2009** — convention named "Rotterdam Rules" upon the Rotterdam signature ceremony of 2009-09-23. Twenty-five states sign, including the United States, France, the Netherlands, Norway, Denmark, Greece, Poland, and Switzerland.
- **As of 2024** — 5 ratifications: **Spain, Togo, Republic of the Congo, Cameroon, Benin**. Article 94 requires 20 ratifications before entry into force.
- **Major maritime nations have NOT ratified.** US, UK, Germany, France, Netherlands, Japan, China, Korea, Singapore, and all major flag states of registry have not advanced ratification through their legislatures since the 2009 signature wave. The convention is effectively stalled.

## Why ratification stalled

- **P&I clubs and carrier-industry resistance.** Rotterdam abolishes the navigational-fault defence (a long-standing carrier-favouring exception under Hague-Visby Article IV.2(a)) and raises the carrier liability profile across the regime. The International Group of P&I Clubs and the major liner-industry trade associations advised against ratification on cost-of-insurance grounds.
- **Shipper community ambivalence.** Rotterdam includes shipper-friendly multimodal coverage, but it also imposes shipper-side burdens such as the higher proof-of-loss standard at Article 17(2) and the explicit volume-contracts derogation regime. Shipper trade associations (BIFA, FIATA, ESC) split internally on net effect.
- **Hague-Visby comfort.** The existing Hague-Visby regime is well-understood, with roughly a century of decided case law from the major commercial-court jurisdictions (English, US 2nd Cir., Singapore, Hong Kong). Switching to a new instrument with no body of jurisprudence is a high-friction proposition; the value of certainty is large.
- **Volume contracts loophole.** **Article 80** allows bilateral derogation from many provisions for "volume contracts" (defined as contracts providing for the carriage of a specified quantity of goods in a series of shipments). Cargo-claimant interests perceive this as a carrier-favouring escape hatch that erodes the shipper-protective baseline of the rest of the convention.
- **Domestic-law uncertainty.** Ratification requires substantial COGSA-style implementation legislation (in the US sense — a new Carriage of Goods by Sea Act tracking Rotterdam in place of the existing Hague-aligned COGSA). Ratifying states would need to update domestic carriage law, conflict-of-laws rules, and procedural law extensively. The legislative-bandwidth cost is non-trivial.

## Carrier obligations and defences

- **Article 17 — basis of liability.** Presumed-fault-with-defences standard. The carrier is liable for loss, damage, or delay during the period of responsibility unless the carrier proves a listed cause and that the cause is not attributable to its fault. Conceptually similar to Hague-Visby Article IV but with one decisive difference: the **navigational-fault defence is abolished**.
- **Article 17(3) — listed defences.** 17 enumerated defences (act of war, act of public enemy, act of authority, quarantine, strike, riot, perils of the sea, latent defect not discoverable by due diligence, and similar). The list is reorganized and tightened relative to Hague-Visby Article IV.2 but the structural concept of an exhaustive defence catalogue is preserved.
- **Article 59 — package limit.** **875 SDR per package or 3 SDR per kg, whichever higher.** Materially higher than Hague-Visby's **666.67 SDR / 2 SDR per kg** SDR Protocol limits, and modestly higher than the Hamburg Rules' **835 SDR / 2.5 SDR per kg**.
- **Article 17(2) — initial proof.** The shipper bears the initial burden to prove that loss, damage, or delay occurred during the period of responsibility. The structure is conceptually similar to Hague-Visby's burden-shifting framework, but the explicit codification of the shipper's initial step is read by some commentators as a higher practical burden than the case-law-developed Hague-Visby position.

## Door-to-door scope

- **Article 12 — period of responsibility.** From receipt of the goods by the carrier to delivery, including any inland leg performed by the maritime carrier or its sub-contractors under the same contract.
- **Article 26 — inland-leg interaction.** Special provisions limit the application of overlapping inland-mode-specific conventions where damage is localized to the inland leg and another mandatory regime (such as **CMR** for international road carriage) would otherwise govern. The article is one of the most-negotiated provisions in the convention and is the structural compromise that allowed maritime-plus scope without colliding with the existing road / rail / air conventions.
- **Practical effect.** Rotterdam covers inland-leg damage under a sea-carriage contract; Hague-Visby does not. For container cargo moving under a through-bill of lading, this is the change that most directly affects the operational claim handling.

## Practitioner impact (if entered into force)

### For carriers

Loss of the navigational-fault defence; higher package-limit exposure; multimodal scope means inland-leg insurance review across the carrier's sub-contractor network. Liner operators would re-paper their bills of lading and standard terms; tramp operators would face the volume-contract derogation question on every long-term contract of affreightment.

### For shippers

Better inland-leg coverage; standardized multimodal-claim handling under one regime; reduced "where did the damage occur" gateway disputes. Offsetting these gains: the Article 17(2) initial-burden codification and the volume-contract derogation regime are negative shifts for cargo claimants in some scenarios.

### For freight forwarders and NVOCCs

Clarified multimodal-contract regime; potential reduction in chain-of-custody disputes between road-carrier, rail-carrier, terminal-operator, and ocean-carrier. NVOCC documentation practice would converge on the Rotterdam transport-document framework, including the explicit electronic-transport-record provisions at Articles 35–58.

### For P&I clubs

Re-underwriting required across the liner book and across owner's-side mutual entries. Industry-side analyses circulated during the ratification debate estimated **10–30% premium increase** for similar risk exposure under Rotterdam compared with Hague-Visby, driven by the abolished navigational-fault defence and the higher package-limit ceiling. The figure is a sensitivity-band estimate, not a published actuarial result, and was contested by cargo-side commentators.

## Rotterdam vs Hague-Visby vs Hamburg

| Feature                  | Hague-Visby (1968 / 1979 SDR Protocol) | Hamburg Rules 1978                | Rotterdam Rules 2009                |
|--------------------------|----------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Scope                    | Port-to-port (tackle-to-tackle)        | Port-to-port                      | Door-to-door (maritime-plus)        |
| Navigational-fault defence | Yes (Art. IV.2(a))                  | No                                | No                                  |
| Package limit            | 666.67 SDR                              | 835 SDR                           | 875 SDR                             |
| Per-kg limit             | 2 SDR                                   | 2.5 SDR                           | 3 SDR                               |
| Suit-or-bar period       | 1 year                                  | 2 years                           | 2 years                             |
| In force                 | Yes (since 1968 / SDR Protocol 1984)   | Yes (since 1992; ~30 states)      | No (5 ratifications; 20 needed)     |

## Outlook

- **Stalled but not formally abandoned.** UNCITRAL and the UN Treaty Collection keep the convention text active and the ratification register open. There is no expressed political intent at IMO or UNCITRAL to withdraw or supersede the instrument.
- **Possible future revival.** Changing trade-flow patterns (increased multimodal containerization, e-commerce parcel volumes, digital bill-of-lading adoption) may eventually shift the political-economy calculus. A revival would more plausibly come through a wave of mid-tier maritime states ratifying together than through any single major-flag-state ratification.
- **Practical workaround.** Bilateral contracts of carriage can opt-in to Rotterdam-flavoured terms via paramount-clause references, replicating much of the Rotterdam liability and document framework as a matter of contract rather than treaty. BIMCO standard forms have been quietly absorbing some Rotterdam-aligned drafting choices through ordinary form-revision cycles.
