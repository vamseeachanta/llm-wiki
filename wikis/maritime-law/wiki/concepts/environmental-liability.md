# Environmental Liability in Maritime Law — Doctrinal Synthesis

> Cross-cutting concept page that ties together every IMO compensation-stack instrument and the parallel US-domestic regime. The legal framework for holding parties accountable for marine pollution incidents — oil spills, chemical and HNS releases, bunker fuel discharges, wreck-removal cost recovery, and ecological damage — has evolved through **six discernible eras** spanning the *Torrey Canyon* (1967) through *Wakashio* (2020) and the EU Maritime Regulation cycle now in flight. This page synthesizes that evolution and the unifying mechanisms (strict liability, channeling, compulsory insurance, tiered compensation funds, cap-loss gateways) without reproducing convention text.

## Why environmental liability matters

- **Defines the outer ceiling on shipowner pollution exposure.** The compensation-stack architecture (CLC + IOPC Funds + Bunkers + HNS + Nairobi WRC, layered against LLMC for residual claims) is the substrate on which the international P&I-and-reinsurance market prices catastrophic-spill exposure. Without these instruments, the long-tail of pollution liability would be effectively un-underwritable.
- **Aligns operational regulation (MARPOL) with civil-liability allocation.** MARPOL 73/78 controls *operational discharge*; the CLC / Bunkers / HNS / Nairobi WRC stack allocates *casualty-driven civil liability*. Practitioners must distinguish: an Annex I MARPOL violation may evidence regulatory non-compliance for cap-loss purposes (under OPA 90 §2704(c) or analogous regimes), but does not itself create the civil-claim cause of action.
- **Drives preventive design and crewing standards.** OPA 90 §4115's single-hull phase-out (1990–2015) and the parallel MARPOL Annex I Reg. 19 schedule reshaped tanker newbuild economics for two decades. Post-*Erika* and *Prestige*, accelerated phase-out under MEPC.111(50) demonstrated how civil-liability incidents feed back into IMO operational rule-making.
- **Surfaces the international vs domestic divide.** The US is *not* a party to CLC 1992, Bunkers 2001, HNS 2010, or Nairobi WRC 2007. OPA 90 is the exclusive US-domestic regime; coastal states (Alaska, California, Washington) layer additional state-law obligations under §2718 preservation. Counsel mapping a global tanker portfolio routinely runs two parallel analyses — one international, one US-domestic — and reconciles them through the LLMC-residual / OSLTF-coverage interface.

## The 6 eras of maritime environmental liability

### Era 1 — Pre-1967: nascent state-by-state regimes

Before *Torrey Canyon*, marine pollution liability was a patchwork of common-law nuisance and trespass, flag-state criminal-discharge offenses (e.g. the Oil in Navigable Waters Acts in the UK and the early US Federal Water Pollution Control Act), and bilateral diplomatic protests. There was no international civil-liability framework; cross-border casualties produced ad hoc claim presentations to flag-states with no compulsory financial-responsibility evidence and no reliable cap. The 1954 OILPOL Convention addressed *operational* discharge but said nothing about casualty civil liability. The era's defining feature is the **absence of a casualty-driven compensation architecture** — an absence the *Torrey Canyon* loss made politically untenable overnight.

### Era 2 — 1967–1989: CLC 1969 + Fund 1971 framework

The 1967 [[Torrey Canyon]] grounding off Cornwall (~119,000 tons of crude into the Channel) directly drove the IMO Legal Committee's 1969 Brussels diplomatic conference, producing the **Civil Liability Convention 1969 (CLC 1969)**: strict liability channelled to the registered tanker owner, compulsory insurance, and a tonnage-banded cap. The 1971 IOPC Fund Convention added a second-tier compensation layer financed by levies on oil receivers in importing states. Mid-era casualties tested the regime: the 1976 *Argo Merchant* off Nantucket (~7.7M gallons of No.6 fuel oil) catalyzed US Coast Guard tanker-inspection acceleration; the 1978 [[Amoco Cadiz]] casualty (Brittany, ~220,000 tons) tested the international regime's adequacy and produced US litigation (under the prior US regime) that ultimately yielded ~US$200M+ in Brittany / French claimant recoveries — but exposed the cap as inadequate for major casualties; the 1979 *Atlantic Empress* / *Aegean Captain* Caribbean collision (~287,000 tons combined) remained the largest tanker spill on record but produced minimal claimant recovery owing to the deep-water dispersal pattern. The 1984 CLC/Fund Protocols sought to raise tier-1 caps but failed to attract sufficient ratifications. The era closes with the regime in place but visibly under-quantum'd, and with the US visibly losing patience with international amendment cycles.

### Era 3 — 1989–1990: Exxon Valdez and the OPA 90 US carve-out

The 1989-03-24 *Exxon Valdez* grounding in Prince William Sound (~257,000 bbl) was the political driver for the United States to walk away from the international CLC framework and build a parallel domestic regime. **OPA 90** (Public Law 101-380, signed 1990-08-18, codified at 33 U.S.C. §§ 2701 *et seq.*) created the **Oil Spill Liability Trust Fund (OSLTF)**, the **Certificate of Financial Responsibility (COFR)** regime, the §2704(c) **cap-loss gateway** (gross negligence, willful misconduct, *or* regulatory violation), and the §4115 single-hull phase-out. The era's signature legacy: a **structural divergence** between the international stack and the US-domestic regime that persists to this day. The same 1989–1990 window also produced the **Alaska Oil Spill Prevention and Response** (Stat. 46.04, the toughest US state regime); the *American Trader* (Huntington Beach, 1990, ~417,000 gallons) became the proximate driver for **California's OSPRA / Lempert-Keene-Seastrand Act** the same year, anchoring §2718 state-law parallelism from day one. See [[OPA 90 — Doctrinal Synthesis]](./opa-90.md).

### Era 4 — 1992–2001: CLC 1992 + IOPC 1992 + Supplementary Fund preparation

The IMO responded to *Exxon Valdez* and *Amoco Cadiz* with the **1992 Protocols to CLC and the IOPC Fund Convention**, raising tier-1 caps, broadening geographic scope to the EEZ, and tightening the cap-break gateway (now requiring intent or reckless conduct with knowledge — the same standard later adopted in LLMC 1996 Article 4). Mid-era casualties stress-tested the protocols: the 1993 [[Braer]] grounding off Shetland (~85,000 tons, dispersed by storm without major shoreline impact), the 1996 [[Sea Empress]] casualty off Milford Haven (~72,000 tons; produced UK port-authority criminal liability), and the 1997 *Nakhodka* casualty in the Sea of Japan (~6,200 tons; produced Japan's IOPC Fund claim and accelerated Asian ratifications). The 1999 [[MV Erika]] casualty off Brittany (~20,000 tons) and the 2002 [[MV Prestige]] casualty off Galicia (~63,000 tons) revealed that even CLC 1992 + IOPC 1992 ceilings (~SDR 89.77M + ~SDR 203M = ~US$400M combined at the time) were inadequate for major spills with EU-coast clean-up cost. The 2000 *United States v. Locke* (529 U.S. 89) Supreme Court decision parallel-tracked: state authority preserved on operations and contingency planning, but state vessel-design standards preempted by federal regime. This era prepared the ground for the **2003 Supplementary Fund Protocol** and the EU's **Erika I/II/III** legislative packages that closed Era 5.

### Era 5 — 2001–2015: Bunkers 2001 + HNS 2010 + Supplementary Fund 2003

Three IMO instruments closed major scope-gaps in this era. The **Bunkers Convention 2001** (in force 2008-11-21) extended strict-liability + compulsory-insurance architecture to *non-tanker* bunker-fuel spills, which had previously fallen through the CLC scope crack. The **2003 Supplementary Fund Protocol** added an optional third-tier compensation layer (~SDR 750M) for states-parties willing to absorb the additional levy. The **HNS Convention 2010** (2010 Protocol; not yet in force as of 2026) extended the architecture to hazardous and noxious substances — bulk and packaged chemicals, LNG, LPG. EU member states moved faster than the IMO consensus could absorb: the **Erika III package** (Directives 2009/15/EC through 2009/21/EC, plus Regulation 391/2009 on classification societies, finalized 2009) introduced port-state-control acceleration, classification-society civil liability, mandatory accident investigation, and master/seafarer fair-treatment guarantees. National prosecutions ran ahead of the IMO civil-liability framework: the French Cour de cassation (2012) recognized *préjudice écologique* against Total in *Erika*, and the Spanish Tribunal Supremo (2016) imposed criminal sanction on the *Prestige* master and owner — both reflecting Member-State frustration with cap-bounded civil recovery. The era also saw the **2008 IMO Annex VI Tier III NOx** and **2020 Sulfur Cap (0.5% global; 0.1% ECA)** rules sharpen MARPOL's operational layer in parallel with civil-liability evolution. See [[CLC 1992]](./clc-1992.md), [[Bunkers Convention 2001]](./bunkers-convention-2001.md), [[HNS Convention 2010]](./hns-convention-2010.md).

### Era 6 — 2015+: Nairobi WRC + emerging carbon-intensity + EU MR 2024

The 2015-04-14 entry-into-force of the **Nairobi WRC 2007** closed the final gap in the IMO compensation stack: post-incident wreck-removal cost allocation, with strict liability on the registered owner, default EEZ scope (with territorial-sea opt-in), and compulsory insurance plus a Wreck Removal Certificate for vessels ≥300 GT. The 2020 [[MV Wakashio]] casualty in Mauritius (~1,000 tons of VLSFO) was the first major Nairobi WRC application and the first to combine Bunkers 2001 + Nairobi WRC + MLC 2006 (crew-welfare) liability heads in a single proceeding. The 2018 *Sanchi* condensate-tanker casualty in the East China Sea (~136,000 tons of condensate, no IOPC recovery owing to non-persistent cargo type) reinforced the analytical importance of the CLC's "persistent oil" cargo definition. In parallel, the **EU MRV Regulation 2015/757 (extended 2024 to non-EU calls)** and the **EU ETS extension to maritime CO2 (2024–2027 phase-in)** introduce a *carbon-intensity* liability layer that operates on operational emissions rather than casualty events — an analytically distinct regime, but one that practitioners must integrate into the same compliance architecture. The **IMO 2023 GHG Strategy** (revised at MEPC 80) targets net-zero "by or around" 2050 with mid-term economic measures (a fuel standard plus a GHG pricing mechanism) under negotiation. The era's open question is whether a future IMO Greenhouse Gas Fund (under MEPC discussion) will fold into the compensation-stack idiom or remain a standalone pricing mechanism.

**Cross-era doctrinal threads.** Three threads run end-to-end through the six eras:

1. **Strict liability with cap-and-break** has been the constant architectural bone since CLC 1969 — every subject-matter regime added since (Bunkers, HNS, Nairobi WRC) imports it intact, with subject-matter-specific tier and quantum adjustments.
2. **The cap quantum has had to be raised in every era** (1969 → 1984-failed → 1992 → 2000 amendments → 2003 Supplementary Fund) because casualty experience consistently exceeded the prior ceiling; the inflation-and-experience adjustment cycle is now an expected feature of the regime, not an exception.
3. **The international vs national jurisdictional fault-line** has widened, not closed — national criminal prosecution (France post-*Erika*, Spain post-*Prestige*), national operational layering (US states under §2718; EU member states under Erika III), and EU regulatory acceleration each routinely run ahead of the IMO Legal Committee amendment cycle.

## Tier-of-liability framework

The compensation-stack architecture operates on a **tiered-and-channelled** model. For oil-tanker spills (CLC 1992 + IOPC), the tiers are:

- **Tier 1 — Registered owner under CLC 1992.** Strict liability; cap of ~SDR 89.77M for the largest tankers; channeled to the registered owner with limited defenses (act of God, war, third-party act). Insurance is compulsory; the **CLC blue card** is the practical evidence.
- **Tier 2 — IOPC 1992 Fund.** Treaty-based fund financed by levies on oil receivers in member states; ceiling ~SDR 203M (cumulative with Tier 1). Pays where the Tier 1 cap is exceeded, the owner is insolvent, or the owner is exonerated.
- **Tier 3 — IOPC Supplementary Fund (2003 Protocol).** Optional third tier; states-parties opt in. Combined ceiling with Tiers 1+2 reaches ~SDR 750M (~US$1B). Adopted by EU member states post-*Erika* / *Prestige*.

Parallel structures apply for the other subject-matter regimes:

- **Bunkers 2001** — single-tier, channelled to registered owner; defers to LLMC 1996 for cap quantum.
- **HNS 2010** — two-tier: owner + HNS Fund (when in force; not yet ratified as of 2026).
- **Nairobi WRC 2007** — single-tier with LLMC interaction for cap.
- **OPA 90 (US-domestic)** — structurally similar but not treaty-based: §2702 strict liability + §2704 caps + OSLTF as the trust-fund analog of IOPC + §2704(c) cap-loss gateway. State funds (CA, AK, WA, TX, LA) operate as additional fund layers on top.

See [[LLMC 1996 — Doctrinal Synthesis]](./llmc-1996.md) for how the residual aggregate cap interacts with the subject-matter regimes.

**National-law-vs-international-treaty interaction.** The tiered architecture lives inside a more granular interaction layer that practitioners must map deal-by-deal:

- **US OPA-90 carve-out.** The US is *not* a CLC/IOPC party; OPA 90 §2702 stands alone as the federal civil liability head. §2718 explicitly preserves state-law liability heads (no preemption ceiling), so a single incident routinely produces stacked federal-OPA-90 + state-OSPRA + state-natural-resource-trust claims with separate quantum and separate cap-break tests.
- **EU Erika III package interaction with CLC.** Directives 2009/15-21/EC and Regulation 391/2009 do not displace CLC tier-1 channeling but add classification-society civil liability, an EMSA-led port-state-control acceleration regime, and Member-State accident-investigation obligations that operate *alongside* the CLC compulsory-insurance evidence layer. The Member State recovers under CLC + IOPC; criminal and administrative sanction proceeds under national law.
- **French and Spanish national prosecutions.** Both *Erika* (Cour de cassation 2012) and *Prestige* (Tribunal Supremo 2016) demonstrated that national criminal prosecution of master, owner, and classification society proceeds *in parallel* to the CLC civil cap, and that *préjudice écologique* (now codified in French Civil Code Art. 1247 since the 2016 Loi Biodiversité) provides a domestic ecological-damage head that exceeds the CLC compensable-damage definition.
- **State funds and direct-action statutes.** US state funds (CA's OSPRA-administered fund, AK's Oil & Hazardous Substance Release Prevention & Response Fund, WA's Spill Prevention Account) operate as state-level OSLTF analogs; direct-action statutes (Louisiana Direct Action Statute, R.S. 22:1269 — applies to P&I in OPA-90 contexts) allow claimant suit against the insurer without owner joinder, mirroring the CLC Article VII(8) direct-action mechanic.

## Strict liability + compulsory insurance — the unifying mechanism

Across every era from CLC 1969 forward, two design features unify the regime:

- **Strict liability** — no fault element; defenses are narrow and exhaustively enumerated (act of God, war, third-party act, government negligence in maintaining navigation aids).
- **Compulsory insurance with direct action** — the claimant may sue the insurer directly, without the owner's participation; the blue-card / COFR mechanism makes financial responsibility a port-entry condition rather than an after-the-fact recovery problem.

These two features together solve the practical problem the pre-1967 era could not: a coastal state facing a major spill can recover without proving negligence, without joining the owner in a foreign forum, and without waiting on flag-state cooperation. The cost is borne by the shipping industry through P&I and reinsurance markets that price the cap-bounded exposure into freight rates.

**Cap-loss gateways** operate as the doctrinal pressure-relief valve — in egregious cases, the cap evaporates and exposure becomes unlimited:

- **CLC 1992 Art. V(2) and LLMC 1996 Art. 4** — intent or reckless-with-knowledge; high subjective-knowledge bar rarely cleared.
- **OPA 90 §2704(c)** — gross negligence, willful misconduct, *or* regulatory-violation alone; materially lower bar, cleared multiple times in modern US practice (most prominently in *Deepwater Horizon*).
- **Bunkers 2001 and Nairobi WRC 2007** — defer to LLMC Art. 4 standard via the residual-cap interface; same high subjective-knowledge bar applies.

The asymmetry between the international and US cap-break standards is the practitioner's most operationally consequential divergence in the entire compensation-stack architecture.

## Practitioner impact

The compensation-stack architecture engages a broad set of stakeholders, each with materially different interests and operational obligations:

**Shipowners.** Compulsory insurance and blue-card / COFR evidence are operational gating items for port entry. The cost of the compensation-stack tiers is internalized through P&I calls. Cap-loss exposure under §2704(c) drives compliance-record management — every MMS / BSEE incident-of-noncompliance is a potential evidentiary anchor for a future cap-loss finding.

**Oil majors / charterers.** Vetting (OCIMF SIRE 2.0, RightShip), chartering exclusion clauses (single-hull, age-based), and indemnity flow-down to time charterers under non-CLC heads (e.g. OPA 90 "responsible party" definition reaches the operator/demise charterer, not just the registered owner) drive the upstream-of-casualty risk transfer. Post-*Deepwater Horizon*, oil majors price spill-class incidents into project NPVs explicitly rather than as residual.

**P&I underwriters.** Underwrite the Tier-1 layer (CLC blue card, Bunkers blue card, COFR) and the LLMC residual; the International Group's Pooling Agreement and General Excess of Loss reinsurance contract are calibrated to the layered cap structure. Different jurisdictions' cap-break rates feed pricing — the OPA 90 §2704(c) "regulatory-violation" gateway materially raises US-call premium relative to international-only deployment, and individual clubs (Skuld, Britannia, North) maintain separate US-COFR product lines.

**Coastal-state regulators.** Recover Tier-1 + Tier-2 + (if opted in) Tier-3 compensation under treaty-based mechanisms; in the US, recover under OPA 90 §2702(b)(2) plus OSLTF subrogation. Maintain state contingency plans (CA OSPR, AK ADEC, WA Ecology, LA OSCRD), pre-position oil-spill-response-organization (OSRO) coverage, and run state-level NRDA trustee processes alongside federal NOAA/DOI trustees. Receive levy-financed contributions from oil-importing economies under IOPC.

**Flag states.** Issue CLC, Bunkers, and Wreck Removal blue-card endorsements; perform compulsory-insurance verification; subject to IMO audit (IMSAS) and EU "grey/black list" port-state-control aggregation. White-list flag-state status materially affects port-state-control inspection burden and charterer acceptance.

**Environmental NGOs and ecological-damage claimants.** Recover natural-resource damages through trustee-led NRDA (US, under §2706) or the *préjudice écologique* / equivalent ecological-damage doctrines (France post-*Erika*, codified in Civil Code Art. 1247; EU member states under the Environmental Liability Directive 2004/35/EC). Standing varies sharply: French *associations de protection de l'environnement agréées* may bring civil action; US NGOs typically intervene through citizen-suit mechanisms (CWA §505), not direct OPA 90 claim heads.

**Regulators (IMO, EU, US Coast Guard, EMSA).** Enforce the operational layer (MARPOL, ISPS, ISM) and supervise the financial-responsibility layer (CLC blue card endorsement, COFR issuance, Wreck Removal Certificate). EMSA additionally aggregates EU-wide casualty intelligence under Directive 2009/18/EC and feeds it into Erika III iteration. The US Coast Guard administers the OSLTF and §4115 phase-out compliance. Feed casualty experience back into IMO Legal Committee amendment cycles.

## US vs international divergence

The structural divide is the practitioner's first analytical fork in any pollution incident with US contact:

- **OPA 90 §2718** preserves state-law liability and additional financial-responsibility requirements. *United States v. Locke*, 529 U.S. 89 (2000), is the doctrinal anchor: state authority preserved on operational, contingency-planning, and financial-responsibility heads, but rules conflicting with the federal vessel-design regime (manning, equipment, construction) are preempted.
- **OPA 90 §2704(c) cap-loss** is materially broader than the international Article 4 / Article V(2) standard — gross negligence, willful misconduct, *or* regulatory-violation alone breaks the cap, vs the international "intent or reckless-with-knowledge" gateway that requires the limiting party's actual subjective knowledge. *Deepwater Horizon* (2010) is the leading case where §2704(c) operated against an oil major.
- **OSLTF vs IOPC.** The OSLTF is funded by a per-barrel tax on US-received crude and imported petroleum (lapsed and reinstated multiple times; most recently restored by the Inflation Reduction Act 2022). The IOPC Fund is funded by levies on contributing oil receivers in member states. Both pay where the Tier-1 cap is exceeded or the owner is insolvent; neither pays cumulatively (a US-waters incident has no IOPC fallback).
- **State-law parallel evolution (US §2718 layer).** The state regimes that operate alongside OPA 90 are not interchangeable; each was driven by a specific casualty and codifies a distinct quantum and operational layer:
  - **Alaska — Stat. 46.04** (post-*Exxon Valdez* 1989): unlimited liability for cleanup and damages with no statutory cap; tug-escort requirement in Prince William Sound; the OHSRPRF state fund administered by ADEC; Cook Inlet RCAC and PWS RCAC citizen-oversight bodies.
  - **California — OSPRA / Lempert-Keene-Seastrand 1990** (post-*American Trader*): Office of Spill Prevention and Response (OSPR) within DFW; cents-per-barrel fund levy; mandatory contingency plans for tankers and non-tank vessels ≥300 GT; strict NRDA recovery via state trustees in addition to federal trustees.
  - **Washington — Stat. 88.46 / 90.56** (post-*Nestucca* 1988 and *Tenyo Maru* 1991): Department of Ecology administers the Spill Prevention Account; advanced rescue-tug stationing in Neah Bay; SERP / GRP geographic-response-plan inventory.
  - **New Jersey — Spill Compensation and Control Act (N.J.S.A. 58:10-23.11)** (post-*Olympic Glory* 1990 and a chain of NY/NJ harbor incidents): broad strict-liability head extending beyond OPA 90 to non-petroleum hazardous substances; integrates with the federal CERCLA regime for industrial-source spills.
  - **Louisiana — R.S. 30:2451 et seq. + Direct Action Statute R.S. 22:1269**: state Oil Spill Coordinator's Office; broad direct-action regime that allows claimants to sue P&I insurers directly without owner participation, materially altering OPA 90 litigation strategy in Gulf incidents (heavily litigated post-*Deepwater Horizon*).
  - **Texas — Oil Spill Prevention and Response Act (Nat. Res. Code §40)** (post-*Mega Borg* 1990 and *Apex Houston* 1986): GLO administers; cents-per-barrel fund; Texas General Land Office oil-spill response strategically positioned for Houston / Galveston / Corpus Christi traffic.
- **EU directive evolution.** Parallel to US §2718 layering, EU member states layer civil-liability and operational rules through the **Erika I/II/III packages (2002–2009)** plus the **Environmental Liability Directive 2004/35/EC** (which applies to maritime ecological damage in EU waters in addition to the CLC tier-1 quantum). The 2024 EU MRV/ETS extension introduces a separate carbon-intensity layer that does not displace civil liability but adds an operational-emissions monetization head.
- **Net practitioner consequence.** A globally-deployed tanker carries layered exposure: international CLC + IOPC + (often) Supplementary Fund + LLMC residual, *plus* (when calling US ports) OPA 90 + state-law overlays *plus* (when calling EU ports) EU Erika III + ELD + MRV/ETS. The compliance and insurance architecture must service all three stacks simultaneously.

## Notable case law spanning eras

- **[[Torrey Canyon]] (1967, Cornwall, UK)** — pre-CLC casualty that drove the entire IMO compensation-stack project. ~119,000 tons of crude into the Channel; recovery was diplomatic and ad hoc; the political shock produced CLC 1969 and Fund 1971 within four years.
- **[[Amoco Cadiz]] (1978, Brittany, France)** — first major casualty under CLC 1969; ~220,000 tons; US litigation in the Northern District of Illinois ultimately produced ~US$200M+ in Brittany / French claimant recoveries. Demonstrated cap inadequacy and drove the 1984 → 1992 protocol cycle.
- **[[MV Erika]] (1999, Brittany, France)** — ~20,000 tons under CLC 1992; produced the 2003 Supplementary Fund Protocol and the EU's *Erika I/II/III* legislative packages (port-state inspection acceleration, single-hull phase-out advance, classification-society liability). The French Cour de cassation (2012) recognized *préjudice écologique* and held Total criminally and civilly liable.
- **[[MV Prestige]] (2002, Galicia, Spain)** — ~63,000 tons; produced extended litigation over CLC 1992 cap-break (eventually denied) and IOPC Fund recovery; Spain's Supreme Court (2016) held the master and owner liable with penal sanction. Combined with *Erika* drove MARPOL Annex I single-hull phase-out advance under MEPC.111(50).
- **[[Sea Empress]] (1996, Wales, UK)** — port authority criminal liability for the pollution loss; demonstrated the CLC / criminal-law interface and broadened the doctrinal frame for non-owner liability.
- **[[Deepwater Horizon]] (2010, US Gulf of Mexico)** — modern OPA 90 reference case; ~3.19 million bbl over 87 days; aggregate exposure ~US$65 billion across §2702(b)(2) damages, Clean Water Act §311 civil penalties (~US$5.5B consent decree), NRDA settlement (~US$8.8B), state-law claims, and class-action recoveries. §2704(c) cap-loss operated on findings of gross negligence and 30 CFR Part 250 regulatory violations.
- **[[MV Wakashio]] (2020, Mauritius)** — first major application of the Nairobi WRC 2007 compulsory-insurance and Wreck Removal Certificate regime; ~1,000 tons of VLSFO bunker fuel; combined Bunkers 2001 + Nairobi WRC + MLC 2006 (crew welfare) liability heads. Demonstrated the Era-6 multi-instrument compensation architecture in operation.

Bunker-fuel spill in Sydney Harbour; established the **reasonable-foreseeability** test for tort remoteness, displacing the *Re Polemis* "direct consequence" test. The pre-CLC common-law substrate that the IMO compensation-stack later overlaid for tanker-and-bunker pollution; still the controlling authority on remoteness for non-CLC / non-OPA bunker-spill consequential-loss claims and a frequently-cited tort-doctrine anchor for environmental claims that fall outside the Tier-1 strict-liability conventions.
