# Nairobi WRC 2007 — Doctrinal Synthesis

This page is the practitioner-impact companion to the [standards-tier metadata resolver](../standards/nairobi-wrc-2007.md). It synthesizes what the Nairobi International Convention on the Removal of Wrecks 2007 (in force 14 April 2015) means for shipowners, P&I clubs, coastal states, salvors, and maritime insurers — without reproducing convention text. The cross-cutting doctrinal anchor for the wreck-removal/salvage interface sits at [Salvage](./salvage.md); the LLMC-interaction analysis sits at [Limitation of Liability](./limitation-of-liability.md). This page is the WRC-specific synthesis nested below them.

## Why Nairobi WRC matters

- **Closes the IMO compensation-stack gap.** The pre-2015 IMO liability stack — CLC 1992 (tanker oil), Bunkers 2001 (non-tanker oil), HNS 2010 (hazardous and noxious substances), Athens 2002 (passenger personal injury) — covered specific damage *types* but not the cost of physically removing the wreck itself. LLMC 1996 caps overall shipowner liability but treats wreck-removal claims as a default *opt-out exclusion* under Article 2(1)(d). WRC 2007 fills the wreck-removal-specific cost-allocation gap with a strict-liability + compulsory-insurance regime.
- **Post-incident cost-allocation, not pollution liability.** WRC is conceptually distinct from the CLC/Bunkers/HNS pollution-liability framework. Those regimes channel compensation for *damage* caused by escaped substances; WRC channels payment for *physical removal* of the wreck (and its cargo and detached parts) from coastal-state waters once the casualty has occurred.
- **Costa Concordia driver.** Italy bore €1.5 billion-plus in direct wreck-removal cost after the 2012 grounding off Isola del Giglio — at the time the largest single-vessel wreck-removal operation in history. The case crystallized coastal-state political momentum behind WRC ratification and is widely cited as the practical impetus for the 2015 entry-into-force.

## Doctrinal evolution

- **1989 Salvage Convention.** Addressed *in-progress* salvage (Article 13 reward for salved value; Article 14 special compensation for environmentally threatening operations). It does *not* allocate the cost of removing what is left after salvage fails or is never attempted.
- **1996 LLMC Protocol — Article 2(1)(d).** Wreck-removal and cargo-removal claims default *outside* the LLMC limitation cap, with a state-by-state opt-in mechanism under Article 18(1) for inclusion. This created a fragmented pre-WRC landscape: in non-opt-in states, wreck-removal was uncapped against the owner but unsupported by compulsory insurance; in opt-in states it was capped under LLMC but still had no dedicated direct-action machinery.
- **Pre-WRC (pre-2015) gap.** Coastal states routinely bore the wreck-removal cost themselves and chased the registered owner through whatever domestic-law mechanism existed. Without compulsory insurance and a certified financial-guarantee instrument, coastal-state recovery was contingent on owner solvency and amenability to local jurisdiction.
- **2007 Nairobi adoption.** IMO Diplomatic Conference, Nairobi, 23 May 2007.
- **2015-04-14 entry-into-force.** Achieved on the 90th day after the 10th instrument of ratification was deposited (Article 18 entry-into-force trigger), per the convention's standard one-year delay mechanism.
- **60+ states-parties as of 2024.** Steady accession trajectory, with major flag and coastal-state participation across Europe, Africa, Asia-Pacific, and the Caribbean.

## Three-tier scope

WRC's geographic application is structured in three layers — a default scope, a territorial-sea opt-in, and a wreck-classification gate.

- **Article 1 — default geographical scope: the EEZ.** WRC applies by default within the Exclusive Economic Zone (out to 200 nautical miles from baselines) of a state-party. This aligns with UNCLOS Article 56 EEZ jurisdiction and was the political compromise that allowed broad ratification: states gained a wreck-removal cost-allocation tool for the EEZ without needing to extend the convention into their territorial sea.
- **Article 3(2) — territorial-sea opt-in.** State-parties may by declaration extend WRC to their internal and territorial waters (out to 12 nautical miles). The United Kingdom, Germany, the Netherlands, and several other major maritime states have made the Article 3(2) declaration; others have retained domestic wreck-removal regimes for their territorial waters and applied WRC only in the EEZ.
- **Article 5 — wreck classification.** A "wreck" under WRC means a ship that has sunk, stranded, broken up, or otherwise become incapable of providing maritime transport service following a maritime casualty — together with any cargo aboard at the time of the casualty and any object lost at sea from the ship. The definition is broad enough to capture cargo and detached parts, which is significant for container-ship and bulk-carrier casualties where the wreck-removal cost is dominated by cargo recovery rather than hull removal.

## Compulsory insurance and the Wreck Removal Certificate

WRC's enforcement architecture mirrors the CLC 1992 pattern of *compulsory insurance + certificate-on-board + direct action against the insurer*.

- **Article 12 — compulsory insurance.** Ships of 300 GT or above registered in a state-party must maintain insurance or other financial security sufficient to cover the limits of liability under WRC, up to the applicable LLMC ceiling.
- **Wreck Removal Certificate (WRC-cert).** The state-issued certificate evidencing the Article 12 insurance — analogous to the CLC certificate — must be carried aboard. Port-state authorities may inspect the certificate as part of routine PSC under the regional MoUs and may detain a ship found to be sailing without one.
- **Direct action against the insurer.** A claimant with a WRC-covered claim may sue the insurer directly for liability covered by the certificate, with the insurer permitted only the limited defenses available to the assured under the certificate plus the wilful-misconduct defense personal to the assured. This eliminates the owner-solvency contingency that hampered pre-WRC coastal-state recovery.
- **Coverage triggers.** The financial guarantee covers the cost of locating, marking, and removing the wreck — the three operational stages typically separately tendered by coastal-state authorities.

## Liability framework

- **Article 10 — strict liability of the registered owner.** Liability attaches to the registered shipowner for the costs of locating, marking, and removing the wreck, subject to the narrow Article 10(1) defenses (act of war, act of god, third-party intentional act, negligence of a coastal-state authority in maintaining navigational aids).
- **Article 11 — limitation subject to LLMC.** Owner liability under WRC is "subject to" any applicable limitation regime, in practice meaning the LLMC 1996 cap where the state of jurisdiction is a 1996-Protocol party. The interaction depends on whether that state has made the LLMC Article 18(1) opt-in to fold wreck-removal claims back inside the LLMC fund (see *WRC vs LLMC 1996* below).
- **Article 13 — time limits.** Claims must be brought within three years of the date on which the hazard has been determined and within six years of the date of the maritime casualty. The dual-clock structure protects owners from indefinite latent-claim exposure while giving coastal-state authorities adequate time to investigate and tender removal operations.
- **Article 15 — dispute settlement.** Disputes between state-parties are routed through the UNCLOS Part XV dispute-settlement framework, with ITLOS, Annex VII arbitration, ICJ, and conciliation as the available venues.

## Practitioner impact

### For shipowners and P&I clubs
- **WRC-cert is mandatory for ≥300 GT.** The certificate is now part of the standard pre-departure documentation set, on a par with the CLC and Bunkers certificates. Underwriting the underlying cover is overwhelmingly handled by the International Group of P&I Clubs as part of standard mutual cover.
- **Insurance pricing reflects wreck-removal exposure.** Wreck-removal cost is a dominant tail-risk variable in P&I underwriting — Costa Concordia, Wakashio, and the Hoegh Osaka-class casualties have all set premium-pricing reference points. The compulsory-insurance regime does not change the underlying risk but does standardize the product and the claim-handling channel.

### For coastal states
- **Materially improved cost-recovery for wreck-removal operations.** The combination of strict liability on the registered owner, compulsory insurance, and direct action against the insurer means coastal-state recovery is no longer hostage to owner solvency or to the procedural friction of cross-border enforcement against a foreign-flagged owner.
- **Reduced direct-state burden.** The regime substantially shifts the wreck-removal funding burden from coastal-state treasuries to shipowner-side P&I cover, particularly for the EEZ scope where prior recovery mechanisms were thin.

### For salvors and wreck-removal contractors
- **Clarified payment framework.** With insurance backing the owner's liability under a certified financial guarantee, contractor risk on long-tail wreck-removal operations is materially reduced. This has supported the growth of dedicated wreck-removal subcontracting arms within major salvage groups.
- **Salvage-vs-wreck transition.** Where a prolonged salvage operation transitions into a wreck-removal operation, contracting practice now anticipates the LOF/SCOPIC-to-WRC handoff explicitly.

### For maritime insurers
- **WRC + CLC + Bunkers + HNS + Athens exposure-mapping.** A modern P&I underwriting submission must map the assured's exposure across all five regimes plus the LLMC umbrella. The product mix is more complex than under any single prior regime, and certificate-management is itself a meaningful operational workstream.

## WRC vs Salvage Convention 1989

- **Salvage 1989 — in-progress operations.** Applies while the vessel is still at peril and potentially salvageable. Reward is fixed under Article 13 against salved value (the "no cure, no pay" baseline), with Article 14 special compensation as a partial environmental-protection exception.
- **WRC 2007 — post-incident operations.** Applies once the vessel has sunk, stranded, broken up, or otherwise become incapable of providing transport service. Cost-allocation is against the registered owner under strict liability, backed by compulsory insurance.
- **Boundary cases.** A prolonged salvage operation that fails to recover the vessel may transition into a WRC wreck-removal operation; contracting practice routinely anticipates the handoff. The factual transition point — vessel still salvageable vs vessel a wreck within Article 5 — is where the regime selection turns.

## WRC vs LLMC 1996

- **LLMC Article 2(1)(d) default exclusion.** Wreck-removal and cargo-removal claims sit *outside* the LLMC limitation cap by default. Without the WRC, coastal-state wreck-removal claims against the owner are uncapped under LLMC.
- **LLMC Article 18(1) opt-in.** A state-party to LLMC may by reservation include wreck-removal claims *inside* the LLMC cap. Roughly half of LLMC 1996 parties have made the opt-in; the other half have not.
- **WRC interaction — Article 11.** WRC liability is "subject to" LLMC limitation. The practical effect varies by state:
  - In an *opt-in* state (wreck-removal *inside* the LLMC cap), the WRC owner-liability is capped at the LLMC fund's pro-rata share for the wreck-removal claim.
  - In a *non-opt-in* state (wreck-removal *outside* the LLMC cap), the WRC owner-liability runs up to the full WRC-cert insurance limit, uncapped by LLMC.
  This makes state-of-jurisdiction LLMC opt-in status a first-order litigation variable in major wreck-removal disputes, on a par with the WRC territorial-sea-opt-in question.

## Notable application

- **Costa Concordia (2012, Italy).** Pre-WRC-in-force, but the dominant case-study driver of the convention. €1.5 billion-plus Italy-borne direct wreck-removal cost; primary ratification driver for the 2007–2015 momentum that produced the convention's entry-into-force.
- **MV Wakashio (2020, Mauritius).** First major WRC application post-entry-into-force. Mitsui OSK Lines (registered owner) and Britannia P&I (insurer) covered approximately US$36 million in wreck-removal cost under the WRC framework, demonstrating the direct-action mechanism in operation.
- **Solomon Trader (2019, Solomon Islands).** Bauxite-carrier grounding off Rennell Island; the WRC framework applied for the wreck-removal cost-allocation, with the registered owner and insurer engaged through the certificate's direct-action channel.
- **Ever Given (2021, Suez).** Refloated rather than abandoned; the vessel was not formally a wreck within Article 5, and the dispute resolved under the salvage and Suez-Canal-Authority detention regimes. Often cited in textbook treatments to illustrate the salvage-vs-wreck classification boundary.
