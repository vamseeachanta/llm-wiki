# Flag-State Jurisdiction — UNCLOS Articles 91 and 94

## Scope

Flag-state jurisdiction is the doctrine that every merchant ship has the nationality of the state whose flag it is entitled to fly, and that this state is the primary regulator of the ship's construction, manning, operation, and discipline on the high seas. Port-state control is the secondary enforcement layer covered in `./port-state-control.md`.

## Doctrine

- **UNCLOS Article 91 (Nationality of ships)**: every state fixes the conditions for granting nationality, registering ships, and flying its flag. A **genuine link** must exist between the state and the ship.
- **UNCLOS Article 92 (Status of ships)**: ships sail under one flag only and are subject to that state's exclusive jurisdiction on the high seas, subject to treaty exceptions and the right of visit.
- **UNCLOS Article 94 (Duties of the flag state)**: register maintenance, jurisdiction under internal law over master and crew, and obligations on construction, equipment, seaworthiness, manning, signalling, collision prevention, and pollution prevention.
- Open registries (Panama, Liberia, Marshall Islands collectively flag the majority of world tonnage) are lawful under UNCLOS where the genuine-link test and Article 94 duties are met. The IMO Flag State Implementation Code (Resolution A.1158) sets auditable performance criteria; audits are mandatory under the IMO Member State Audit Scheme (IMSAS, in force 2016).
- **Delegation**: flag states routinely delegate statutory survey and certification to classification societies acting as Recognised Organisations (RO Code, MSC.349).
- **Limits**: port-state control, coastal-state jurisdiction in the territorial sea and EEZ (UNCLOS Parts II and V), and universal jurisdiction over piracy (Article 105) all carve into flag-state exclusivity.

## Cases

- **MV Prestige (2002)** — Spanish prosecution exposed enforcement gaps when the flag state declines to act; informed the EU's third maritime safety package and the Bunker Convention's compulsory-insurance regime.
- **The MV Saiga (No 2) (1999)** — ITLOS held that the genuine-link requirement does not authorise other states to refuse to recognise the flag.
