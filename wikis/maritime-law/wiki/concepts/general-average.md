# General Average — Doctrinal Synthesis

> Practitioner-impact companion to the standards-tier metadata resolver at [`standards/york-antwerp-rules.md`](../standards/york-antwerp-rules.md). General average is the broader doctrine; the York-Antwerp Rules are the modern instrument that codifies it. This page synthesizes the sacrifice-and-contribution mechanic that has reallocated maritime casualty losses for ~3,000 years and that, in modern container-shipping practice, still governs how owners, cargo interests, and underwriters split the bill after a fire, grounding, or general-purpose salvage operation.

## Why this concept matters

- **The pre-insurance loss-sharing institution.** General average predates marine insurance by roughly two-and-a-half millennia and remains the doctrinal substrate on which modern hull, cargo, and freight insurance allocate post-casualty contributions. Without GA, a shipowner who jettisons a portion of cargo to save the adventure would bear the entire economic burden — the doctrine reallocates that burden across all interests *whose property was saved*.
- **Universal incorporation by reference.** Practically every voyage charter, time charter, and bill of lading on the global market incorporates a version of the **York-Antwerp Rules (YAR)** by name. The choice between **YAR 1994 / 2004 / 2016** is a negotiated contract term with material consequences for salvor remuneration, time-bar lengths, and crew-wage treatment.
- **Active in modern container-shipping practice.** The 2010s–2020s saw repeated GA declarations on major box ships — *MV Maersk Honam* (2018 fire), *MV Yantian Express* (2019 fire), *MV Ever Given* (2021 Suez grounding), *MV Felicity Ace* (2022 fire). Each adjustment ran for years and required cargo interests to post **GA bonds** before release of cargo.
- **Interface with salvage and limitation.** GA does not stand alone — a salvage award under the Salvage Convention 1989 may be allowed in average (Rule VI), and the shipowner's GA contribution is itself capped under LLMC 1996 if the cap-break standard is not met. Practitioners coordinate the three doctrines together in the post-casualty workflow.

## Doctrinal evolution

- **~800 BCE — Lex Rhodia de iactu.** The Rhodian Sea Law's *de iactu* ("on jettison") principle is the ancestral source: cargo jettisoned to save the ship is contributed for by all whose property is saved. Adopted into Justinian's Digest (D.14.2) and from there into the European *ius commune*.
- **Medieval sea codes (12th–14th c.).** *Rolls of Oléron*, the *Consolato del Mare*, the *Laws of Wisby*, and the *Hanseatic Sea Code* all carry recognisable GA provisions. The doctrine is one of the oldest continuously-applied rules in commercial law.
- **1864 — York Rules.** First international codification attempt at a Glasgow conference of underwriters and average adjusters; eleven rules adopted to harmonise national divergence in adjustment practice.
- **1877 — York-Antwerp Rules.** Antwerp conference expanded and reissued the Rules under their modern name. Adopted by the **Comité Maritime International (CMI)** as its standard set; subsequent revisions in 1890, 1924, 1950, 1974, **1994, 2004, and 2016**. The Rules are non-statutory — they apply only by contractual incorporation.
- **1994 / 2004 / 2016 revisions.** YAR 1994 added a "paramount rule" (Rule of Interpretation) requiring conformity with the lettered and numbered rules. **YAR 2004** introduced controversial changes (excluded salvage from Rule VI, capped time-bar at one year, restricted crew-wage allowances) that the market largely rejected; many bills of lading reverted to YAR 1994. **YAR 2016** is the CMI's reconciliation: salvage allowable in average again, modified time-bar, restored allowance scope.
- **2010s–2020s — container era.** Mega-box-ship fires and the Suez 2021 grounding pushed GA into general business-press visibility. Cargo interests and freight forwarders became routine GA participants; **GA bond + insurance guarantee** workflows were standardized by IUMI and BIMCO.

## Sacrifice + contribution mechanic

The doctrine operates through three sequential analytical steps; an independent **average adjuster** performs each step and issues a written *adjustment statement*.

- **Step 1 — Identification of the GA act.** Per **Rule A** (YAR 2016): an extraordinary sacrifice or expenditure must be (a) intentionally and reasonably made, (b) in time of peril, (c) for the common safety of the maritime adventure (ship + cargo + freight at risk).
  - *Sacrifices* — jettison of cargo (Rule I), damage by water used to extinguish fire (Rule III), cutting away of damaged spars or rigging (Rule IV), voluntary stranding (Rule V), engine damage from forcing the ship aground or off (Rule VII).
  - *Expenditures* — port-of-refuge costs (Rule X), salvage payments (Rule VI, restored under YAR 2016), wages and maintenance during port-of-refuge stays (Rule XI), temporary repairs (Rule XIV).
- **Step 2 — Valuation of saved interests.** Contributing values are determined as of the *time and place* the adventure ends (typically the port of destination): ship value (sound less damage), cargo value (CIF or invoice), freight at risk. Each interest contributes in proportion to its saved value relative to total saved value.
- **Step 3 — Allowance and apportionment.** The adjuster allows recoverable losses (the GA expenditures and sacrifices, valued under the lettered and numbered rules) and apportions the total across the contributing interests. The shipowner typically carries the GA expenditure forward and recovers from cargo and freight via the adjustment statement.
- **GA bond and guarantee.** Cargo cannot be released to consignees until they post (a) a **GA bond** (consignee's contractual undertaking to pay the assessed contribution) and (b) a **GA guarantee** (typically from the cargo underwriter, backstopping the bond). The bond-and-guarantee workflow is the operational chokepoint of every modern GA case.

## Specific York-Antwerp Rules of practitioner interest

- **Rule of Interpretation (YAR 1994 onward).** The lettered and numbered rules together define what is allowable in average; the numbered rules prevail over the lettered rules where they conflict, but the Rule of Interpretation provides that nothing not explicitly allowed by the rules is recoverable in average.
- **Rule A — definition of GA act.** Cited above; the gateway test for any allowance.
- **Rule VI — salvage payments.** Under YAR 1994, salvage paid to third-party salvors is allowable in GA. **YAR 2004 excluded it** (treating salvage as a separate distribution). **YAR 2016 restored it** with refinements: salvage is allowable in average, subject to specific carve-outs for SCOPIC remuneration and certain Article 14 special-compensation elements.
- **Rule X — port of refuge expenses.** The largest single category in most modern GA cases — extraordinary entry costs, agency fees, towage in and out, and port dues at a port of refuge entered for common safety.
- **Rule XI — wages and maintenance at port of refuge.** Crew wages, provisions, and bunker costs during the period of additional detention at a port of refuge. YAR 2004 narrowed this allowance; YAR 2016 partially restored it.
- **Rule XIV — temporary repairs.** Temporary repairs at a port of refuge to allow the adventure to proceed are allowable as GA where they enable the common-safety completion of the voyage.
- **Rule XX — provision of funds.** Adjuster's commission, interest on disbursements, and the cost of providing the adjustment.

## Practitioner impact

### For shipowners

The shipowner's office (typically the master + the technical and operations teams + the GA-claims department) decides whether to **declare general average**. Modern practice is to instruct an average adjuster within hours of a casualty — *Average Adjusters' Association* members in the UK, *Association of Average Adjusters of the United States*, the *AMD* (France), the *DAVAA* (Germany). The shipowner advances the GA expenditure (port-of-refuge costs, salvage payments, temporary repairs), then recovers via the adjustment statement, which can take 2–5 years to finalize for a complex container-ship case. Hull underwriters cover the owner's GA contribution and reimburse the GA disbursement directly.

### For cargo interests

Consignees discover the GA workflow when their cargo cannot be released without posting a GA bond and guarantee. The assessed contribution is typically a small percentage of cargo value — but for high-value cargo (electronics, automobiles, project equipment), absolute amounts can be substantial. Cargo underwriters (operating under *Institute Cargo Clauses* A, B, or C, or the corresponding national equivalents) backstop the guarantee in nearly all cases. Self-insured shippers and uninsured shippers face direct exposure and are the most-stressed counterparty in any GA case.

### For average adjusters

The adjuster acts as a quasi-judicial finder of fact and law: identifying the GA act under Rule A, classifying allowances under the numbered rules, valuing the contributing interests, and apportioning the total. Modern adjustments are issued as written *statements* with supporting workpapers and run from a few months for a simple jettison case to several years for a complex multi-modal box-ship fire. The adjuster's fee is itself allowable in average under Rule XX.

### For P&I clubs and hull underwriters

Hull underwriters cover the owner's GA contribution. P&I clubs cover certain GA-adjacent exposures — collision liability, wreck removal, environmental — but not GA contribution itself. The interface between hull and P&I in the post-casualty workflow is governed by the *International Hull Clauses* and the IG Pooling Agreement.

### For salvors

Where the salvage award is allowable in average (YAR 1994 / 2016, not YAR 2004), the salvor is paid by the salvage contract and the cost is then redistributed across ship + cargo + freight via the GA adjustment. SCOPIC remuneration under the *Lloyd's Open Form* with the SCOPIC clause is *not* allowable in GA — it sits in a separate distribution lane.

## Comparison with salvage

| Feature | General average | Salvage |
|---|---|---|
| Loss-sharing direction | Reallocates loss *among the saved interests* | Compensates a third party who *performs the rescue* |
| Trigger | Voluntary sacrifice or expenditure for common safety | Voluntary services rendered to vessel/cargo in peril |
| Codification | York-Antwerp Rules (CMI, contractual incorporation) | Salvage Convention 1989 (treaty law, mostly enacted into national law) |
| Counterparty | Shipowner ↔ cargo interests ↔ freight | Salvor ↔ all benefiting interests |
| Rule for "no benefit, no claim" | Rule C — only loss directly caused by the GA act is allowable | Article 12 of Salvage Convention 1989 — "no cure, no pay" |
| Environmental dimension | Indirect (Rule G — basis of contribution at the place the adventure ends) | Direct (Article 14 special compensation; SCOPIC) |

The two doctrines run in parallel after most modern casualties: salvage governs payment to the rescuer, GA governs how that payment is distributed across the saved interests. **YAR 2016 Rule VI** is the bridge.

## Notable case law and incidents

- ***MV Ever Given* (2021)** — owner Shoei Kisen declared general average within days of the Suez Canal grounding. The Egyptian SCA's separate canal-claim (initial $916M, reduced through negotiation) was settled outside GA. The case became a textbook example of GA bond-and-guarantee workflow at scale: thousands of cargo interests across the box-ship's slot bookings posted bonds before release. See [`entities/mv-ever-given-2021.md`](../entities/mv-ever-given-2021.md).
- ***MSC Flaminia* (2012)** — fire and explosion in the Atlantic following misdeclared dangerous cargo. GA declared; adjustment ran for over a decade and intersected with the carrier's indemnity claim against the shipper of the misdeclared cargo. The *Stolt Tankers* line of US authority on dangerous-cargo indemnity cross-references the GA distribution. See [`entities/msc-flaminia-2012.md`](../entities/msc-flaminia-2012.md).
- ***Maersk Honam* (2018)** — 5th largest box ship at the time; fire near Oman; GA declared; cargo bond rate reportedly exceeded 50% of cargo value at one stage of the adjustment, an unusually high figure that drew industry attention to YAR 1994 vs 2016 selection in bills of lading.
- ***The Makis Agreement / Makis* (1929)** — historical UK authority on the meaning of "extraordinary" expenditure under Rule A; still cited for the proposition that ordinary running costs are not allowable in average.
- ***Goulandris Bros v Goldman & Sons* [1958] 1 QB 74** — UK Commercial Court; the Astypalaea / *Cretan Star* dispute established the modern English-law approach to time-bar for GA contribution claims and remains a teaching case on the contractual incorporation of YAR.
