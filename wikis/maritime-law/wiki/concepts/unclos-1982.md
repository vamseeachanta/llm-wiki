# UNCLOS 1982 — Doctrinal Synthesis

> **Companion page.** This page is the practitioner-facing doctrinal synthesis. The metadata-resolver companion is [standards/unclos-1982](../../standards/unclos-1982.md), which carries the `code_id`, publisher, consolidated edition, and amendment-status fields used by downstream calc citations.

## Why UNCLOS matters

- **The umbrella treaty.** UNCLOS is the source-of-authority instrument under which most IMO and ILO maritime instruments operate; it is widely called the **"constitution of the oceans"** and is the explicit or implicit jurisdictional foundation for SOLAS, MARPOL, MLC, STCW, ISPS, the Salvage Convention, and the civil-liability conventions (CLC, Bunkers, HNS, LLMC, Athens).
- **Codified customary international law.** Many UNCLOS provisions reflect pre-existing customary practice — most prominently the 1945 Truman Proclamation on the continental shelf and the 1958 Geneva Conventions on the Law of the Sea. Ratifying states accept these provisions in treaty form; non-ratifying states (notably the United States) generally accept the customary version of most provisions, with deep-seabed mining as the historic carve-out.
- **Maritime zones taxonomy.** UNCLOS fixes the now-canonical zones: **Internal Waters** (full sovereignty) → **Territorial Sea** (12 nm, sovereign with right of innocent passage) → **Contiguous Zone** (24 nm, limited enforcement) → **Exclusive Economic Zone** (200 nm, sovereign rights over resources) → **Continental Shelf** (geophysical, may extend beyond 200 nm) → **High Seas** (freedom-of-navigation regime) → **The Area** (deep seabed, common heritage of mankind).
- **Scope of disputes.** UNCLOS Part XV provides four jurisdictional venues — **ITLOS** (International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea, Hamburg), **Annex VII arbitration**, the **ICJ**, and **Annex VIII special arbitration**. ITLOS has handled roughly thirty cases since its 1996 inauguration; most EEZ-fishing and resource-boundary disputes have gone to Annex VII arbitral tribunals.

## Doctrinal evolution

- **1958 Geneva Conventions on the Law of the Sea** — first multilateral codification of the territorial sea, contiguous zone, high seas, continental shelf, and fishing/conservation regimes; conspicuously omitted the EEZ and the deep-seabed mining regime that would dominate the next round.
- **1973–1982 Third UN Conference on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS III)** — nine years of negotiations producing 320 articles and 9 annexes; took place against the decolonization wave and the New International Economic Order, which is why the deep-seabed regime emerged with strong distributive-equity language.
- **1982 adoption (Montego Bay) → 1994 entry-into-force** after the 60th ratification (Guyana). State-party count is now 168+.
- **1994 Implementation Agreement (Part XI deep-seabed regime)** — addressed US/UK/Western-industrialized objections to the original Part XI by replacing the production-control and mandatory-technology-transfer provisions with a market-oriented regime; treated as integral to UNCLOS.
- **1995 UN Fish Stocks Agreement** — extends UNCLOS Articles 63–64 for **straddling** and **highly-migratory** fish stocks, using regional fisheries management organizations (RFMOs) as the operational layer.
- **2023 BBNJ Agreement (Biodiversity Beyond National Jurisdiction)** — extends UNCLOS for high-seas biodiversity, marine genetic resources, area-based management tools (including marine protected areas in ABNJ), environmental impact assessment, and capacity-building / technology transfer.

## Practitioner impact

### For coastal states

Coastal states hold a **layered bundle of rights**, each with its own legal character:

- **Internal Waters** — full sovereignty (no right of innocent passage except where a state newly enclosed waters by drawing straight baselines).
- **Territorial Sea (12 nm)** — sovereignty subject to innocent passage (Articles 17–26).
- **Contiguous Zone (24 nm)** — enforcement-only jurisdiction over customs, fiscal, immigration, and sanitary infringements (Article 33).
- **EEZ (200 nm)** — **sovereign rights** (not sovereignty) over living and non-living resources, plus jurisdiction over artificial islands, marine scientific research, and environmental protection (Articles 56, 73).
- **Continental Shelf** — sovereign rights for non-living resources of seabed and subsoil; the Continental Shelf may extend **beyond 200 nm** under Article 76 if the geological criteria are met, in which case the state must submit data to the **Commission on the Limits of the Continental Shelf (CLCS)** and pay revenue-sharing under **Article 82** for non-living resource exploitation beyond 200 nm.

### For ship operators

Ship operators rely on three layered navigation regimes:

- **Innocent passage** through the Territorial Sea (Articles 17–26) — passage that is "not prejudicial to the peace, good order, or security of the coastal state"; specific activities listed in Article 19(2) make passage non-innocent.
- **Transit passage** through international straits used for international navigation (Articles 37–44) — a stronger regime than innocent passage; cannot be suspended; applies to both surface and submerged transit.
- **Archipelagic sea-lanes passage** through designated routes in archipelagic waters (Article 53) — substantively similar to transit passage.

### For environmental governance

- **Article 192** — general obligation to **protect and preserve the marine environment**; applies to all marine areas including the EEZ and high seas.
- **Articles 207–212** — source-specific pollution obligations: land-based, seabed activities, dumping, vessel-source, atmospheric — these are the **parent provisions** for MARPOL (vessel-source), the London Convention/Protocol (dumping), and OPRC.
- **Articles 217–220** — the enforcement architecture: flag-state, port-state, and coastal-state competences. Article 218 is the textual basis for port-state-control authority over violations occurring outside the port state's own waters.

### For dispute-resolution bodies

UNCLOS Article 287 lets each state declare a preferred forum at ratification; if the parties have not chosen the same forum, the default is **Annex VII arbitration**. In practice:

- **ITLOS** receives prompt-release cases (Article 292) and provisional-measures applications by default; merits cases by election.
- **Annex VII tribunals** handle most EEZ, continental-shelf, and resource-boundary merits cases (e.g., South China Sea, Bay of Bengal *Bangladesh v. India*, Chagos MPA).
- **ICJ** hears UNCLOS-flavored disputes when the parties prefer it (e.g., Costa Rica v. Nicaragua maritime boundary).
- **Annex VIII special arbitration** for fisheries, marine environment, marine scientific research, and navigation disputes — rarely invoked.

### For indigenous and small-island states

- **Article 121(3)** — "rocks which cannot sustain human habitation or economic life of their own shall have no exclusive economic zone or continental shelf." This single sentence is doctrinally explosive: applied at scale by the 2016 South China Sea PCA, it constrains how much ocean a state can claim from a small feature, which materially affects small-island and indigenous-territory claims.
- **Part X (Articles 124–132)** gives **land-locked states** rights of access to and from the sea and freedom of transit through neighboring states, mediated by bilateral agreements.

## Umbrella relationship to IMO/ILO instruments

UNCLOS does not itself prescribe ship construction, crew certification, pollution standards, or labour rights; instead it confers authority on the IMO and ILO instruments that do, anchoring each by Article:

- **Articles 207–212** → **MARPOL** (pollution-prevention, all sources).
- **Article 211** → **MARPOL Annexes I–VI** (vessel-source pollution specifically).
- **Article 94** → **SOLAS** and **STCW** (flag-state duties for safety and crew competence).
- **Article 235** → **CLC**, **Bunkers**, **HNS** (state and operator liability for environmental damage).
- **Articles 217–220** → **Paris MoU**, **Tokyo MoU**, **ISPS Code** (port-state-control authority and security).
- **Articles 91–94** → **MLC** (nationality of ships, flag-state duties as basis for labour-rights enforcement).
- **Articles 98–99** → **Salvage Convention 1989** and **MLC** (duty to render assistance; suppression of slavery and trafficking).

This is what "constitution of the oceans" means in practice: every operational maritime instrument can be traced upward to a UNCLOS Article.

## Notable cases / events

- **South China Sea Arbitration (Philippines v. China), 2016** — Annex VII tribunal under Article 287; rejected China's nine-dash-line claim as incompatible with UNCLOS, applied Article 121(3) at scale to deny EEZ entitlement to most Spratly features.
- **M/V "Saiga" cases (1997 and 1999)** — first major ITLOS cases; Saiga (No. 1) established the **prompt-release procedure** under Article 292; Saiga (No. 2) limited hot-pursuit and bunker-supply enforcement and held that the **genuine-link** requirement does not authorize other states to refuse to recognize the flag.
- **Chagos Marine Protected Area Arbitration (Mauritius v. UK), 2015** — Annex VII tribunal; UK's declaration of a no-take MPA around the Chagos archipelago violated UNCLOS Articles 2(3), 56(2), and 194(4) by ignoring Mauritian rights; reinforced by the ICJ Chagos Advisory Opinion (2019).
- **Bay of Bengal cases — *Bangladesh v. Myanmar* (ITLOS, 2012)** and ***Bangladesh v. India* (Annex VII, 2014)** — first ITLOS continental-shelf delimitation, including the outer continental shelf beyond 200 nm; introduced the equidistance/relevant-circumstances method in a Bay-of-Bengal-specific configuration.
- **Truman Proclamation, 1945** — pre-UNCLOS unilateral US claim over the continental shelf; crystallized within roughly a decade into customary international law and was directly codified at the 1958 Geneva Conference and again in UNCLOS Part VI. The reference example for how state practice accelerates into custom.

## US position and customary international law

- The United States has **signed but not ratified** UNCLOS (the Senate has not advised and consented). The US did sign the 1994 Implementation Agreement.
- US administrations from Reagan onward have stated that the United States accepts most UNCLOS provisions as **customary international law**, with the deep-seabed regime as the historic carve-out (now addressed by the 1994 Implementation Agreement).
- **Practical consequence:** the US Navy and Coast Guard operate consistently with the UNCLOS navigation regime (innocent passage, transit passage, freedom-of-navigation operations), but the United States cannot invoke ITLOS or Annex VII arbitration as a party. US disputes proceed bilaterally, through ICJ where consent exists, or through diplomatic channels.
