# Black Sea MoU on Port State Control

## Scope

The Black Sea MoU is an **administrative arrangement among 6 maritime authorities** — Bulgaria, Georgia, Romania, Russia, Turkey, and Ukraine — coordinating port-state-control (PSC) inspections of foreign-flag vessels visiting Black Sea ports. It is an administrative agreement among port-state authorities, not a treaty and not a private codification; the controlled-vocabulary `instrument_type` for this page is therefore `mou`.

The geographic scope is geographically tighter than most of its sibling MoUs and covers the **Black Sea basin proper** — the riparian coastlines of the six member authorities — together with the **Bosphorus and Dardanelles entry corridor** that connects the basin to the Mediterranean and the wider world. Approximately **50,000 ships per year** transit the Turkish Straits, making the Black Sea MoU's port-state mandate disproportionately consequential for the size of its membership.

The operational goals are to:

- **Detect and correct sub-standard ships** through a risk-based regional inspection regime;
- **Address chokepoint-specific safety concerns** unique to the Bosphorus/Dardanelles transit and the basin's heavy oil-tanker traffic, with attendant focus on MARPOL Annex I cargo-system integrity, COLREGs compliance for narrow-channel transits, and STCW manning on long-haul tanker voyages from Russian and Caspian-export terminals;
- **Harmonize regional inspection priorities** with the adjacent Paris MoU (the principal partner and methodology source — Bulgaria and Romania are EU member states and Paris MoU full members) and Mediterranean MoU (geographic adjacent on the southern side of the Turkish Straits) so that owners cannot port-shop their way around member-state enforcement.

The Black Sea MoU is the structural sibling of the Paris MoU and Tokyo MoU and operationalises the same underlying source-authority stack — SOLAS Chapter XI, MARPOL Article 5(2), MLC 2006 Title 5, ILO C147, COLREGs, and the Load Lines Convention — across the Black Sea basin.

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **Founded**: Istanbul, Turkey, **7 April 2000**, at a ministerial conference of Black Sea rim maritime authorities.
- **Entry into force**: **7 April 2000**, when the inspection regime began operating among the original signatories.
- **Secretariat**: Istanbul, Turkey.
- **Current parties**: 6 member authorities as of 2024.
- **Methodology track**: the Black Sea MoU has been a Paris MoU follower in inspection methodology — NIR-equivalent risk-based selection has been adopted gradually post-2014, with prior White/Grey/Black-list ranking informing flag-state targeting decisions during the transition. The two EU members (Bulgaria, Romania) operate as Paris MoU full members in parallel, which functionally pulls Black Sea MoU practice toward Paris MoU equivalence on the western half of the basin.

For non-treaty MoU instruments such as this, the equivalent of "adoption resolution + entry-into-force threshold" is the ministerial-conference signature plus the date the inspection regime began operating among the original signatories.

## Member authorities + flag-state context

- **Members (6)**: Bulgaria, Georgia, Romania, Russia, Turkey, Ukraine.
- **Critical chokepoint coverage**: the Black Sea MoU member states ring the **Bosphorus and Dardanelles** entry corridor (the Turkish Straits) — one of the world's most important maritime chokepoints, with ~50,000 transits annually and a long-running navigational-safety record dominated by tanker incidents in narrow waters.
- **Heavy oil-tanker traffic**: significant **Russian crude and product** export through Novorossiysk and Tuapse, **Caspian-origin oil** transiting Russian and Georgian terminals, and routine tanker traffic between Black Sea load ports and Mediterranean/Atlantic discharge ports drives an Annex I-focused inspection emphasis — cargo-system integrity, oil-record-book completeness, MARPOL discharge controls, and tanker-specific structural surveys are weighted heavily in Black Sea MoU CICs.
- **Geopolitical context (post-2022)**: the **Russia–Ukraine war from February 2022** has produced significant operational disruption to the regime. Russian and Ukrainian member coordination has been effectively suspended; Bulgaria, Romania, Turkey, and Georgia continue to operate the regime among themselves and with adjacent MoUs. Sanctions-driven traffic-pattern changes (shadow-fleet tanker movements, off-AIS transits, and STS transfers) have created PSC priorities that no other regional MoU faces at this intensity.
- **EU overlap**: Bulgaria and Romania are Paris MoU full members and apply Paris MoU NIR criteria at their Black Sea ports; Turkey is a Paris MoU full member as well, applying NIR throughout its Black Sea, Marmara, Aegean, and Mediterranean coastlines.

## Inspection priorities

- **Tanker-focused Concentrated Inspection Campaigns (CICs)** are run regularly, reflecting the basin's tanker-heavy traffic mix and the Bosphorus/Dardanelles incident history.
- **Narrow-channel and chokepoint navigation**: COLREGs compliance, bridge-resource-management procedures, and pilot-master exchange records receive disproportionate attention given the Turkish Straits transit profile and historical groundings/collisions in the channel.
- **Sanctions-era shadow-fleet inspection (post-2022)**: aged tankers calling at Black Sea MoU member ports under flag-of-convenience registries with opaque ownership chains have driven enhanced scrutiny of class certificates, P&I cover, MARPOL Annex I tank-condition records, and STCW certificate authenticity. Bulgaria and Romania have aligned this work closely with Paris MoU CIC themes.
- **Joint CIC participation** with the Paris MoU and Tokyo MoU since approximately 2010 gives Black Sea MoU CICs alignment with global rotating themes (e.g. fire-safety systems, STCW hours-of-rest, stability and cargo-securing) while preserving regionally-salient stand-alone tanker and chokepoint-navigation campaigns.
- **Winter-season operational coordination** — Black Sea winter weather (November–March) produces heavy-weather conditions that affect port operations, anchorage holding, and tanker loading/discharge sequences. The Secretariat coordinates with member-authority emergency-management offices to phase inspection priorities around storm windows and post-event port-reopening sequences.

## Notable enforcement / events

- **2007 *Volgoneft-139* breakup (Kerch Strait, November 2007)** — a Russian-flag tanker broke apart in heavy weather in the Kerch Strait, releasing ~1,300 tonnes of fuel oil and prompting coordinated Russian, Ukrainian, and Black Sea MoU PSC response on aged-tanker structural surveys and severe-weather operational limitations. The incident drove a regional emphasis on river–sea tanker class restrictions and seasonal trading limits.
- **2022+ Russia–Ukraine war disruption** — full-scale war from February 2022 has closed Ukrainian commercial ports for extended periods, displaced shipping toward Romanian and Bulgarian ports, complicated AIS reporting across the basin, and effectively suspended Russian–Ukrainian Black Sea MoU coordination. PSC inspection volume in the basin has been redistributed among the four non-Russian/Ukrainian members and the adjacent Paris MoU.
- **Black Sea Grain Initiative (2022–2023)** — the UN- and Turkey-brokered grain-corridor agreement created an extraordinary inspection regime for outbound bulk-carrier traffic from Ukrainian ports, with Turkey performing inspection clearances at the Bosphorus and Black Sea MoU coordination supplementing the Joint Coordination Centre's work. The initiative was suspended by Russia in July 2023, after which alternate corridor-along-Ukrainian-coastline operations took over.
- **2020+ COVID-19 pandemic** — port-state crew-change protocols were coordinated with adjacent MoUs, with Black Sea MoU members applying MLC 2006 Title 2 and Title 4 inspection emphasis on maximum service period overruns, repatriation arrangements, and onboard medical-care provisions.
