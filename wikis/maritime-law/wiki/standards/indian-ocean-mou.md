# Indian Ocean MoU — Indian Ocean Memorandum of Understanding on Port State Control

## Scope

The Indian Ocean MoU is an **administrative arrangement among 20 maritime authorities** — Australia (participating member), Bangladesh, Comoros, Djibouti, Eritrea, France (La Réunion), India, Iran, Kenya, Madagascar, Maldives, Mauritius, Mozambique, Myanmar, Oman, Seychelles, South Africa, Sri Lanka, Sudan, Tanzania, UAE, and Yemen — coordinating port-state-control (PSC) inspections of foreign-flag vessels visiting Indian Ocean ports. It is an administrative agreement among port-state authorities, not a treaty and not a private codification; the controlled-vocabulary `instrument_type` for this page is therefore `mou`.

The geographic scope spans the **East Africa coastline**, **Madagascar and the western Indian Ocean island states**, the **Persian Gulf entry** (Strait of Hormuz region), the **Indian subcontinent ports**, and the **Andaman/Nicobar interface** with Tokyo MoU coverage further east.

The operational goals are to:

- **Detect and correct sub-standard ships** through a risk-based regional inspection regime;
- **Address tanker-density and chokepoint-specific safety concerns** unique to the Indian Ocean basin (Strait of Hormuz oil transit, Bab-el-Mandeb security pressures, Cape of Good Hope rounding traffic), with attendant focus on MARPOL Annex I cargo-system integrity, ISPS security, and STCW manning on long-haul tanker voyages;
- **Harmonize regional inspection priorities** with the adjacent Paris MoU (to the west and north) and Tokyo MoU (to the east) so that owners cannot port-shop their way around member-state enforcement.

The Indian Ocean MoU is the structural sibling of the Paris MoU and Tokyo MoU and operationalises the same underlying source-authority stack — SOLAS Chapter XI, MARPOL Article 5(2), MLC 2006 Title 5, ILO C147, COLREGs, and the Load Lines Convention — across the Indian Ocean basin.

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **Founded**: Pretoria, South Africa, **5 June 1998**, at a ministerial conference of Indian Ocean rim maritime authorities.
- **Entry into force**: **9 January 1999**, when the inspection regime began operating among the original signatories.
- **Secretariat**: Goa, India.
- **Current parties**: 20 member authorities as of 2024 (with Australia participating under different terms than other members — see below).
- **Methodology track**: the Indian Ocean MoU has been a Paris/Tokyo MoU follower in inspection methodology — NIR-equivalent risk-based selection has been adopted gradually post-2014, with prior White/Grey/Black-list ranking informing flag-state targeting decisions during the transition.

For non-treaty MoU instruments such as this, the equivalent of "adoption resolution + entry-into-force threshold" is the ministerial-conference signature plus the date the inspection regime began operating among the original signatories.

## Member authorities + flag-state context

- **Members (20)**: Australia (participating member), Bangladesh, Comoros, Djibouti, Eritrea, France (La Réunion), India, Iran, Kenya, Madagascar, Maldives, Mauritius, Mozambique, Myanmar, Oman, Seychelles, South Africa, Sri Lanka, Sudan, Tanzania, UAE, Yemen. **Australia is a "participating member"** under different terms than other MoU memberships — Australia is also a Tokyo MoU full member, and its Indian Ocean MoU role reflects its operational footprint on the western Australian coast rather than full-member commitments.
- **Critical chokepoint coverage**: the Indian Ocean MoU member states ring three of the world's most important maritime chokepoints — the **Strait of Hormuz** (~25% of seaborne global oil trade), **Bab-el-Mandeb** (the Suez/Red Sea entry that funnels Asia–Europe trade), and the **Cape of Good Hope** (the Atlantic–Indian Ocean transition for vessels avoiding Suez or unable to transit it).
- **Flag-state convenience**: Mauritius, Maldives, Comoros, India, and the UAE all operate Open Registries of varying scale; a significant proportion of ships inspected at Indian Ocean MoU port calls fly Indian Ocean MoU member-authority flags themselves. As with the Caribbean MoU, this creates a structural overlap between port-state and flag-state roles that shapes the regime's flag-list calibration.
- **Heavy oil-tanker traffic** through the basin drives an Annex I-focused inspection emphasis — cargo-system integrity, oil-record-book completeness, MARPOL discharge controls, and tanker-specific structural surveys are weighted more heavily in Indian Ocean MoU CICs than the cruise-passenger themes that dominate the Caribbean MoU.

## Inspection priorities

- **Tanker-focused Concentrated Inspection Campaigns (CICs)** are run more frequently than in other regional MoUs, reflecting the Indian Ocean basin's tanker-heavy traffic mix.
- **Piracy and maritime-security considerations**: during the **2008–2012 Somali piracy peak**, ISPS Code compliance and STCW security-training-focused inspections were a major Indian Ocean MoU emphasis; post-2015 attention shifted to **Yemen and Strait of Hormuz security incidents**, with PSC verification of citadel arrangements, BMP-aligned anti-piracy measures, and crew-training records.
- **Joint CIC participation** with the Paris MoU and Tokyo MoU since approximately 2010 gives Indian Ocean MoU CICs alignment with global rotating themes (e.g. fire-safety systems, STCW hours-of-rest, stability and cargo-securing) while preserving regionally-salient stand-alone tanker and security campaigns.
- **Cyclone-season operational coordination** — the Northern Hemisphere cyclone season (October–April) affects the Arabian Sea and Bay of Bengal, while the Southern Hemisphere season (April–November) affects Madagascar, Mauritius, Mozambique, and the western Australian approaches. The Secretariat coordinates with national meteorological services and member-authority emergency-management offices to phase inspection priorities around storm-track forecasts and post-event port-reopening sequences.

## Notable enforcement / events

- **2008–2012 Somali piracy peak** — Indian Ocean MoU members coordinated ISPS-compliance verification, BMP (Best Management Practices) implementation checks, and counter-piracy practitioner training across member port states; the regime served as a regional clearinghouse for piracy-area transit inspection findings.
- **2010 MV *Gulf Honor* incident (Persian Gulf)** — Indian Ocean MoU member authorities coordinated emergency PSC for a vessel involved in a Persian Gulf incident, illustrating the regime's role in chokepoint-incident response.
- **2019–2020 Strait of Hormuz tanker incidents** — limpet-mine attacks and vessel seizures in 2019 drove enhanced PSC focus on tankers transiting the region, with member authorities tightening security-plan and crew-briefing inspections for vessels arriving at Indian Ocean MoU ports after Hormuz transits.
- **2020+ COVID-19 pandemic** — port-state crew-change protocols were coordinated with adjacent MoUs, with Indian Ocean MoU members applying MLC 2006 Title 2 and Title 4 inspection emphasis on maximum service period overruns, repatriation arrangements, and onboard medical-care provisions.
