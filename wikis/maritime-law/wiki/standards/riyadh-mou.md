# Riyadh MoU — GCC Riyadh Memorandum of Understanding on Port State Control

## Scope

The Riyadh MoU is an **administrative arrangement among 6 maritime authorities** — Bahrain, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the United Arab Emirates — coordinating port-state-control (PSC) inspections of foreign-flag vessels visiting Persian Gulf and adjacent Arabian Sea ports. It is an administrative agreement among port-state authorities, not a treaty and not a private codification; the controlled-vocabulary `instrument_type` for this page is therefore `mou`.

The geographic scope spans the **Persian Gulf proper**, the **Strait of Hormuz** entry/exit corridor, and the **Arabian Sea coastline of Oman** (extending the regime's reach outside the Gulf into deep-water Arabian Sea anchorages and the approaches to the Strait). Approximately **17 million barrels of crude oil per day** transit the Strait of Hormuz — roughly 25% of seaborne global oil trade — making the Riyadh MoU's port-state mandate one of the most consequential per-capita of any regional PSC arrangement.

The operational goals are to:

- **Detect and correct sub-standard ships** through a risk-based regional inspection regime;
- **Address tanker-density and chokepoint-specific safety concerns** unique to the Persian Gulf and the Strait of Hormuz, with attendant focus on MARPOL Annex I cargo-system integrity, ISPS security (under sustained regional security pressure), and STCW manning on long-haul VLCC and ULCC voyages from Gulf load ports to Asian and European discharge ports;
- **Harmonize regional inspection priorities** with the adjacent Paris MoU (to the west via Suez), Tokyo MoU (to the east via Asian discharge ports), and the **Indian Ocean MoU** (significant geographic and membership overlap — Oman and the UAE are members of both regimes) so that owners cannot port-shop their way around member-state enforcement.

The Riyadh MoU is the structural sibling of the Paris MoU and Tokyo MoU and operationalises the same underlying source-authority stack — SOLAS Chapter XI, MARPOL Article 5(2), MLC 2006 Title 5, ILO C147, COLREGs, and the Load Lines Convention — across the Persian Gulf and adjacent Arabian Sea.

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **Founded**: Riyadh, Saudi Arabia, **30 June 2004**, at a ministerial conference of GCC and adjacent Gulf maritime authorities.
- **Entry into force**: **1 January 2005**, when the inspection regime began operating among the original signatories.
- **Secretariat**: Muscat, Oman.
- **Current parties**: 6 member authorities as of 2024 — the six Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) states (Bahrain, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, UAE).
- **Methodology track**: the Riyadh MoU has been a Paris/Tokyo MoU follower in inspection methodology — White/Grey/Black-list ranking has informed flag-state targeting decisions, and **NIR-equivalent risk-based selection adoption is pending** as of 2024 (lagging the adjacent Indian Ocean MoU on full NIR migration). Oman and the UAE, as Indian Ocean MoU members, apply Indian Ocean MoU criteria in parallel.

For non-treaty MoU instruments such as this, the equivalent of "adoption resolution + entry-into-force threshold" is the ministerial-conference signature plus the date the inspection regime began operating among the original signatories.

## Member authorities + flag-state context

- **Members (6)**: Bahrain, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, UAE.
- **Critical chokepoint coverage**: the Riyadh MoU member states ring the **Strait of Hormuz** — the world's most important oil-transit chokepoint, with ~17 million barrels per day (~25% of seaborne global oil trade) passing through a navigable channel that is in places less than 3 nautical miles wide. PSC focus on tankers transiting Hormuz is a defining feature of the regime.
- **Heavy oil-tanker traffic**: an estimated **~30% of global crude tanker traffic** transits through Riyadh MoU member ports — Saudi Arabia (Ras Tanura, Yanbu via overland pipeline reload), UAE (Fujairah on the Arabian Sea side, Jebel Ali, Ruwais), Kuwait (Mina al-Ahmadi), Qatar (Ras Laffan, with significant LNG carrier traffic alongside crude), and Oman (Sohar, Salalah). This drives an Annex I + LNG-carrier-focused inspection emphasis — cargo-system integrity, oil-record-book completeness, MARPOL discharge controls, IGC Code compliance for gas carriers, and tanker-specific structural surveys are weighted heavily in Riyadh MoU CICs.
- **Geographic and membership overlap with Indian Ocean MoU**: **Oman and the UAE are members of both the Riyadh MoU and the Indian Ocean MoU** — partial overlap with different inspection-regime focus. Oman applies Indian Ocean MoU criteria at Salalah (its principal Arabian Sea port outside the Gulf) while running Riyadh MoU criteria at Sohar and Muscat; the UAE runs Riyadh MoU criteria at Jebel Ali and Khor Fakkan and Indian Ocean MoU criteria at Fujairah's Arabian Sea anchorage operations. This dual-membership pattern produces inspection-regime layering that is unique among regional PSC arrangements.
- **Flag-state convenience**: the UAE and Bahrain operate Open Registries of varying scale; a moderate proportion of ships inspected at Riyadh MoU port calls fly Riyadh MoU member-authority flags themselves, creating a structural overlap between port-state and flag-state roles.

## Inspection priorities

- **Tanker-focused Concentrated Inspection Campaigns (CICs)** are run frequently, reflecting the Persian Gulf's tanker-heavy traffic mix and Hormuz transit safety profile.
- **ISPS / maritime-security considerations**: the Persian Gulf has a sustained maritime-security pressure profile distinct from any other regional MoU. ISPS Code compliance, ship-security-plan currency, citadel arrangements, and BMP-aligned crew-briefing records are inspection priorities driven by **2019–2020 Strait of Hormuz tanker incidents** (limpet-mine attacks, vessel seizures by IRGC forces, and Limburg-style attack-pattern indicators), the long shadow of the **2002 *MV Limburg* attack** off Yemen, and ongoing freedom-of-navigation tensions.
- **LNG-carrier specialisation**: Qatar's Ras Laffan is among the world's largest LNG export terminals; Riyadh MoU members have developed LNG-carrier-specific PSC competencies — IGC Code compliance, cargo-containment-system surveys, ESD valve testing, and LNG bunkering arrangements receive specialist inspection coverage rare in other regional MoUs.
- **Joint CIC participation** with the Paris MoU and Tokyo MoU since approximately 2010 gives Riyadh MoU CICs alignment with global rotating themes (e.g. fire-safety systems, STCW hours-of-rest, stability and cargo-securing) while preserving regionally-salient stand-alone tanker and security campaigns.
- **Summer-season operational coordination** — Persian Gulf summer ambient temperatures (June–September) produce extreme cargo-handling, deck-work, and crew-welfare conditions. The Secretariat coordinates with member-authority emergency-management offices on heat-stress MLC inspection priorities, IGC Code compliance for cargo-temperature management, and crew rotation arrangements during the hottest months.

## Notable enforcement / events

- **2017 Qatar diplomatic crisis (June 2017 – January 2021)** — Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Bahrain imposed a diplomatic and economic blockade on Qatar; vessels calling at Qatari ports faced denied port entry at the three blockading members' ports for the duration. This created an intra-GCC port-state divergence inside the Riyadh MoU regime that the Secretariat had to manage administratively without recasting inspection criteria. The crisis ended with the AlUla Declaration in January 2021.
- **2019–2020 Strait of Hormuz tanker incidents** — limpet-mine attacks on tankers in May and June 2019 (including the *Front Altair* and *Kokuka Courageous*) and the July 2019 Iranian seizure of the *Stena Impero* drove enhanced PSC focus on tankers transiting Hormuz, with Riyadh MoU member authorities tightening security-plan, ship-to-ship communication, and crew-briefing inspections for vessels arriving at GCC ports after Hormuz transits. The incident pattern echoed the 2002 *MV Limburg* attack off Yemen.
- **2010 onwards Saudi Aramco terminal upgrades** — large-scale Saudi terminal modernisation produced new VLCC berthing and SBM mooring infrastructure with attendant PSC focus on tanker-mooring arrangements, hose-management procedures, and shore-side ESD interface compliance during loading operations.
- **2020+ COVID-19 pandemic** — port-state crew-change protocols were coordinated with adjacent MoUs, with Riyadh MoU members applying MLC 2006 Title 2 and Title 4 inspection emphasis on maximum service period overruns, repatriation arrangements, and onboard medical-care provisions during a period when GCC member states had divergent border policies.
