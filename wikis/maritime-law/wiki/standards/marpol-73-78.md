# MARPOL 73/78 — International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships

## Scope

MARPOL 73/78 is the foundational IMO treaty regulating ship-source pollution. Six annexes partition the regime by pollutant class: oil (Annex I), noxious liquid substances carried in bulk (Annex II), harmful substances in packaged form (Annex III), sewage (Annex IV), garbage (Annex V), and air pollution (Annex VI). The "73/78" naming reflects that the 1978 Protocol absorbed the 1973 Convention before either entered force — they are treated as a single instrument and amended together. MARPOL is the most-amended IMO treaty: the IMO Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC) revises the annexes on roughly an annual cadence under the tacit-amendment procedure.

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **Adopted:** London, 1973-11-02 (Convention) and 1978-02-17 (Protocol).
- **Entry-into-force:** 1983-10-02 (Annexes I and II — the mandatory annexes).
- **Subsequent annexes:** added through 1988–2008 as parties-of-states accumulated to trigger entry-into-force for each optional annex (Annex III in 1992, Annex IV in 2003, Annex V in 1988, Annex VI in 2005 via the 1997 Protocol).
- **States parties:** 160+, covering effectively the entire world fleet by tonnage.

## Why "73/78"

The 1973 Convention adopted in London never entered force on its own — the parties-of-states required threshold (15 states representing 50% of world tonnage) was not reached before the 1978 Tanker Safety and Pollution Prevention Conference, called in response to the **Amoco Cadiz** stranding (March 1978) off Brittany. The 1978 Protocol absorbed and modified the 1973 Convention, principally by **deferring Annex II implementation by three years** to allow chemical-tanker industry adaptation, and was treated as the operative instrument from adoption forward. Both instruments came into force as a unitary regime on 1983-10-02 once the combined ratification threshold was met. Hence the convention's persistent dual-date naming: "MARPOL 73/78" rather than "MARPOL 1973" or "MARPOL 1978" alone.

### Annex VI sub-regime detail

Annex VI is the most operationally complex annex because it overlays four distinct sub-regimes on every ship:

- **Sulphur** — Reg. 14: global cap 0.50% m/m since 2020-01-01 (down from 3.50% pre-2020); ECA cap 0.10% m/m since 2015-01-01.
- **NOx** — Reg. 13: Tier I (engines built 2000-2010), Tier II (2011+), Tier III (2016+ in NOx ECAs only — North American and US Caribbean since 2016, North Sea and Baltic NOx ECA since 2021).
- **EEDI** — Reg. 21: technical efficiency index for new-build ships, Phase 0 (2013-2014) → Phase 1 (2015-2019) → Phase 2 (2020-2024) → Phase 3 (2025+) with progressive `gCO2/t·nm` reductions.
- **EEXI + CII** — Reg. 23 + 28 (effective 2023): Energy Efficiency Existing-ship Index (one-off technical) + Carbon Intensity Indicator (annual operational rating A-E, with E and 3×D triggering corrective action plans).
- **Methane + N2O** — added under MEPC.391(82) (2024) extending GHG accounting beyond CO2-only EEDI/CII to Well-to-Wake basis from 2027.

## Worked-example named-incidents — casualty-to-amendment causal chain

The MARPOL regime evolves predominantly through **post-casualty amendment cycles**: a high-profile pollution incident exposes a regulatory gap, MEPC tasks a correspondence group, and an amendment lands within 2-5 years. The table below is the canonical causal-chain reference for case digests citing a specific casualty as the driver of a specific MARPOL provision.

| Casualty | Year | Annex impact | Amendment driver |
|----------|------|--------------|------------------|
| **Torrey Canyon** | 1967 | Annex I origin | Foundational driver for the 1973 Convention itself; first global awareness of supertanker pollution risk |
| **Amoco Cadiz** | 1978 | 1978 Protocol | Conference convened in direct response; Protocol absorbed 1973 Convention + deferred Annex II to 1987 |
| **Exxon Valdez** | 1989 | Annex I Reg. 13F | Single-hull phase-out trajectory under MEPC.95(46) (1995); US OPA 90 followed in parallel |
| **Erika** | 1999 | Annex I Reg. 13G/13F acceleration | MEPC.117(52) (2003) accelerated single-hull phase-out; EU Erika I/II/III packages (Directive 2002/59/EC monitoring; Directive 2005/35/EC ship-source pollution) |
| **Prestige** | 2002 | Annex I Reg. 13G/13F further acceleration | Reinforced MEPC.117(52); EU Directive 2005/35/EC criminal-penalty regime |
| **Hebei Spirit** | 2007 | Annex I + CLC + IOPC interaction | Reinforced flag-state vs. coastal-state criminal-prosecution boundary (Korean criminal proceedings against master + chief officer); IOPC Fund Convention claim-handling case study |
| **Sanchi** | 2018 | Annex I edge-case (condensate/light crude) | Highlighted fire/explosion risk for ultra-light cargoes; condensate is regulated as oil under Annex I but behaves differently from heavy crude in casualty response |
| **Wakashio** | 2020 | Annex VI VLSFO compliance + Annex I | First post-2020 sulfur-cap-era major bunker-spill casualty; demonstrated the 0.50% VLSFO category as a new pollutant class with distinctive shoreline-impact signature; reinforced Mauritius EEZ enforcement under UNCLOS Part XII |

## Regime-distinction multi-criteria comparison — MARPOL vs. CLC vs. Bunkers vs. HNS

A persistent confusion in casualty digests is the **conflation of pollution-prevention (MARPOL)** with **pollution-liability (CLC + Bunkers + HNS)**. They operate on different axes and a single casualty typically engages all four regimes simultaneously through different procedural channels.

| Dimension | MARPOL 73/78 | CLC 1992 | Bunkers Convention 2001 | HNS Convention 2010 |
|-----------|--------------|----------|-------------------------|---------------------|
| **Function** | Pollution prevention (technical standards) | Civil liability for oil-pollution damage from tankers | Civil liability for bunker-fuel-pollution damage from any ship | Civil liability for hazardous + noxious substances pollution |
| **Scope** | All ships (vessel-source pollution) | Persistent oil from tankers carrying oil as cargo | Bunker fuel oil from any ship (incl. non-tanker) | HNS as defined (~6,000 substances) |
| **Cap structure** | None — pollution prevention has no liability cap; criminal penalties + detentions only | SDR-denominated tonnage-based cap (revised under 2000 Protocol; max ~89.77M SDR for largest tankers) | Same cap regime as LLMC 1996 (general limitation) | Two-tier: shipowner liability + HNS Fund supplementary liability up to 250M SDR |
| **Enforcement** | Port-state control (PSC) + flag-state survey + coastal-state under UNCLOS Art. 211 | Civil claims in courts of pollution-damage state | Civil claims in courts of pollution-damage state | Civil claims + HNS Fund (when in force — pending more ratifications as of 2026) |
| **Compensation regime** | None (criminal penalties + detentions + remedial-action orders) | Strict liability on shipowner + IOPC Funds (1992 Fund + Supplementary Fund) supplementary tier | Strict liability on shipowner + LLMC limitation | Strict liability + HNS Fund supplementary tier |
| **Certificate** | IOPP, NLS Certificate, ISPP, IAPP/EIAPP per Annex | CLC Certificate (Blue Card) — mandatory before entering CLC-state port | Bunkers Certificate — mandatory before entering Bunkers-state port | HNS Certificate (when in force) |
| **Mandatory insurance** | No (financial responsibility is national-law overlay, e.g., OPA 90 COFR) | Yes — direct-action right against P&I insurer | Yes — direct-action right against P&I insurer | Yes — direct-action right against insurer |
| **Cross-reference** | This page | [clc-1992](../concepts/clc-1992.md) | [bunkers-convention-2001](../concepts/bunkers-convention-2001.md) | [hns-convention-2010](../concepts/hns-convention-2010.md) |

**Practitioner rule of thumb:** A pollution casualty triggers MARPOL (criminal/PSC) **and** CLC-or-Bunkers (civil) **simultaneously**. The two regimes do not foreclose each other — Hebei Spirit (2007) is the canonical worked example where Korean criminal proceedings under MARPOL/national law ran concurrently with civil claims under CLC + IOPC Fund.

## Regional implementation overlays

MARPOL is implemented globally but enforced through a layered architecture of **regional Memoranda of Understanding (MoUs) on Port State Control** and **regional treaty frameworks** that translate IMO standards into binding regional inspection regimes.

- **Paris MoU** (Europe + North Atlantic Canada) — coordinated PSC inspections; annual Concentrated Inspection Campaigns (CICs) often target MARPOL Annex VI (sulphur cap, EIAPP), Annex I (oil-record-book entries), or Annex V (garbage management).
- **Tokyo MoU** (Asia-Pacific) — sister regime; coordinates with Paris MoU on CIC themes.
- **Mediterranean MoU** + **Indian Ocean MoU** + **Caribbean MoU** + **Riyadh MoU** + **Black Sea MoU** + **Abuja MoU** + **Viña del Mar Agreement** — regional PSC regimes covering remaining major-ocean coastal states.
- **EU Erika III package** (2009) — Directive 2009/16/EC (port-state control), Directive 2009/17/EC (vessel-traffic monitoring), Directive 2009/18/EC (casualty investigation), Regulation (EC) 391/2009 (recognized organizations / classification societies), Regulation (EC) 392/2009 (passenger-liability) — collectively the most rigorous regional implementation of MARPOL + SOLAS + STCW combined.
- **EU Directive 2005/35/EC** on ship-source pollution criminal penalties (post-Erika/Prestige) — criminalizes negligent or intentional pollution discharges; survived ECJ challenge in *Intertanko et al. v Secretary of State for Transport* (Case C-308/06, 2008).
- **EU Directive 2016/802/EU** — sulphur in marine fuels (transposes Annex VI Reg. 14 into binding EU law with criminal-penalty backstop).
- **EU FuelEU Maritime Regulation** (2023/1805, effective 2025) — Well-to-Wake GHG intensity limits for ships calling EU ports; layers on top of Annex VI EEXI/CII regime.
- **EU ETS extension to maritime** (2024) — emissions-trading-system inclusion of maritime sector; financial-instrument complement to MARPOL Annex VI technical instrument.

## Notable enforcement / case law

- **Erika (2000)** — TotalFinaElf single-hull tanker breakup off Brittany, France; drove MEPC acceleration of the double-hull phase-in schedule and informed the EU Erika I/II/III packages.
- **Prestige (2002)** — single-hull tanker casualty off Galicia, Spain; accelerated the global single-hull tanker phase-out under MEPC.95(46) and triggered EU Directive 2005/35/EC on ship-source pollution.
- **Hebei Spirit (2007)** — South Korean oil-spill litigation; large-scale Annex I and CLC/IOPC interaction with national criminal-prosecution overlay.
- **The "Volga" (2002)** — ITLOS prompt-release case touching flag-state versus port-state jurisdiction in the broader Part XII enforcement architecture under which MARPOL operates.
- **Intertanko v. Secretary of State for Transport (Case C-308/06, ECJ 2008)** — challenged Directive 2005/35/EC on grounds of inconsistency with MARPOL's discharge-criteria thresholds; ECJ upheld the Directive on basis that MARPOL itself permits stricter regional measures, cementing the EU's authority to layer criminal penalties on MARPOL technical standards.
- **Wakashio (2020)** — Mauritius EEZ enforcement under UNCLOS Art. 220; first post-2020-cap major VLSFO spill; ongoing civil + criminal litigation.

**Cross-wiki (engineering-standards)** *(for Annex VI numeric constants consumed by emissions calcs)*

- MARPOL Annex VI Reg. 14 sulfur-cap figures and Reg. 13 NOx Tier I/II/III curves should be cited via the engineering-standards page mirroring the technical Annex, not via this maritime-law page (per the calc-citation contract guard against doctrinal-resolver mis-routing).
- [api-rp-578](../../../engineering-standards/wiki/standards/api-rp-578.md) — **bidirectional bridge**: API RP 578 (Material Verification Program for New and Existing Alloy Piping Systems) defines the Positive Material Identification (PMI) practice — XRF and OES alloy-composition verification — that gates **material-of-construction traceability** for chemical-tanker piping and cargo-containment systems regulated under **MARPOL Annex II** (Noxious Liquid Substances in Bulk). Annex II's discharge-criteria, prewash-cycle, and IOPP/NLS-certificate survey regime — together with the **IBC Code** (mandatory under SOLAS Ch. VII Part B for chemical tankers) — presumes the cargo-handling system contains the specified alloy (316L for category-X/Y service, duplex stainless for chloride-containing cargo, nickel alloys for sour-service residues). **PMI is the verification step that converts the IBC Code material specification into traceable as-built compliance** — closing the alloy-mix-up failure mode that would silently invalidate Annex II compliance. Annex II-relevant material-misidentification failure modes include **sour-service hydrogen-induced cracking (HIC/SOHIC)** in cargo-tank piping, **chloride stress-corrosion cracking (SCC)** in austenitic stainless lines exposed to seawater + chloride-containing cargo residues, and **naphthenic-acid corrosion** in slop-tank and cargo-heating coil circuits — each capable of failing-open cargo containment and triggering an Annex II reportable discharge. The RP 578 ↔ MARPOL Annex II bridge is the **construction-stage / pre-incident analogue** to the RP 571 ↔ MARPOL bridge (post-incident damage-mechanism diagnosis): RP 578 supplies pre-incident material-correctness assurance whose absence the RP 571 catalogue exposes post-incident. Use the pairing for chemical-tanker construction-quality and IBC-Code-survey-finding digests that link PMI non-conformance to MARPOL Annex II reportable-discharge exposure.
- [api-rp-571](../../../engineering-standards/wiki/standards/api-rp-571.md) — **bidirectional bridge**: API RP 571 (Damage Mechanisms Affecting Fixed Equipment in the Refining Industry) is the technical-content reference invoked when MARPOL incident investigations and survey non-conformance findings need a mechanism-level metallurgical catalogue for tanker hull, cargo-tank, and slop-tank failures. **Annex I (oil)** and **Annex II (noxious liquid substances in bulk)** inspection regimes — including the IOPP/NLS certificate survey programme and the double-hull tanker fitness-for-service evaluation under MEPC.95(46) — overlap directly with RP 571 entries for sour-water corrosion, CO2 corrosion, sulfide stress cracking, naphthenic-acid corrosion, microbiologically-influenced corrosion (MIC), and erosion-corrosion in cargo-handling and ballast-water circuits. Cargo-tank atmospheric corrosion under Annex I crude-oil-washing residues, Annex II category-X/Y prewash-cycle attack on tank linings, and slop-tank bottom-side MIC are the highest-frequency RP 571 mechanism hits in the casualty record (Erika 2000, Prestige 2002, Hebei Spirit 2007 each involved hull-integrity damage mechanisms catalogued in RP 571). Use the pairing for incident-investigation digests that bridge regulatory non-conformance to mechanism-level evidence.
- [api-17j](../../../engineering-standards/wiki/standards/api-17j.md) — **bidirectional bridge**: API Spec 17J's design, materials, fabrication, and integrity-management requirements for **unbonded flexible pipe** in offshore subsea production directly affect oil-pollution-prevention performance of subsea-tieback, FPSO, and FSO systems regulated under **MARPOL Annex I**. The dominant flexible-pipe failure modes — **carcass collapse** (annulus flooding, hydrostatic crush), **tensile-armor wire fracture** (corrosion-fatigue, HISC of duplex wires under cathodic protection per DNV-RP-F112), **end-fitting leakage** (epoxy-resin debonding, vent-port blockage), **outer-sheath rupture** (seawater ingress to annulus, accelerated tensile-armor corrosion), and **pressure-sheath aging** (CO2/H2S permeation, polymer plasticization) — feed pollution-incident root-cause analysis when subsea releases reach the marine environment. The API 17J integrity-management envelope and the MEPC casualty-reporting regime share root-cause-analysis vocabulary through this bridge; industry-loss databases (OGP, IADC, DNV) map flexible-riser failures onto Annex I reportable thresholds. The pairing also feeds **Annex VI** air-pollution accounting where flexible-riser failure triggers blowdown/flaring on host floating production systems. Use the pairing for casualty digests that bridge subsea-system specification non-conformance to MARPOL Annex I and Annex VI consequences.
- [api-rp-17b](../../../engineering-standards/wiki/standards/api-rp-17b.md) — **bidirectional bridge**: API RP 17B (Recommended Practice for Flexible Pipe Systems) is the **operations + integrity** sister to API Spec 17J's **design + qualification** envelope; together they bracket the flexible-pipe lifecycle relevant to **MARPOL Annex I** subsea-release reporting. RP 17B governs in-service fitness-for-service, inspection campaigns (annulus testing, vent-port monitoring, MEC/MFL armor-wire inspection), and end-of-design-life retirement decisions for flexible risers, flowlines, jumpers, and dynamic FPSO/FSO connections. The same failure-mode taxonomy that 17J addresses at the design stage (carcass collapse, tensile-armor wire fracture, end-fitting leakage, outer-sheath rupture, pressure-sheath aging) recurs in 17B at the operations stage with field-data-driven retirement criteria. **Casualty causation typically traces to the integrity-management gap, not the design specification** — making RP 17B the operationally salient reference for MARPOL Annex I incident investigations. The pairing also feeds **Annex VI** air-pollution accounting where in-service flexible-riser failure forces host-platform blowdown/flaring. Use the 17B pairing for in-service casualty digests; use the 17J pairing for as-built / qualification-failure digests; both bridges resolve to MARPOL Annex I + VI consequences.
- [asme-b31-4](../../../engineering-standards/wiki/standards/asme-b31-4.md) — **bidirectional bridge**: ASME B31.4 (Pipeline Transportation Systems for Liquids and Slurries) governs design, construction, inspection, and operation of crude-oil, refined-product, and slurry pipelines including offshore liquid pipelines under Chapter IX (subsea-tieback flowlines, export trunklines, FPSO-to-shore pipelines). **Pipeline-integrity failures** — internal/external corrosion, third-party mechanical damage, fatigue cracking from pressure cycling, manufacturing-defect propagation, and stress-corrosion cracking — cause **oil-pollution incidents** regulated under **MARPOL Annex I** when releases reach the marine environment from offshore-pipeline scope or shore-based pipelines discharging to navigable waters. B31.4's design-pressure factors (`F = 0.72` for liquid pipelines), class-location-equivalent wall-thickness requirements, post-construction hydrotest acceptance (1.25 × MAOP), and Chapter VII operations-and-maintenance integrity-management programme feed directly into **pollution-prevention compliance analysis** under the MARPOL Annex I survey, IOPP-certificate, and casualty-reporting regimes. **Sister bridge** to the API RP 571 ↔ MARPOL pairing above: RP 571 is post-incident damage-mechanism diagnosis; **B31.4 supplies the pre-incident design-prevention envelope** whose non-conformance the RP 571 mechanisms expose. Use the pairing for offshore-pipeline casualty digests linking design-code non-conformance to MARPOL Annex I reportable-release thresholds.
