# Athens Convention 2002 — Carriage of Passengers and Their Luggage by Sea

## Scope

The IMO **Athens Convention relating to the Carriage of Passengers and their Luggage by Sea (PAL)** is the global treaty providing the **passenger-claim liability framework** for international carriage of passengers and their luggage by sea: cruise ships, ferries, and other passenger ships carrying more than 12 passengers. It establishes a **two-tier carrier-liability regime** — strict liability up to a per-passenger SDR ceiling for **shipping-incident**-related death, personal injury, and luggage loss (shipwreck, capsizing, collision, stranding, explosion, fire, defect in ship), and **fault-based liability** for non-shipping-incident causes, with a fault-inversion band sitting between the two.

The convention is the **passenger-claims complement to LLMC 1996** (which sets the overall **per-incident aggregate cap** across all claimants and constrains the Athens per-passenger sum at the ship-fund level) and the **safety-of-life complement to SOLAS 1974** (which sets the construction, equipment, and operational standards for the same passenger-ship class). ## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **Athens 1974** (the original convention) adopted at the **IMO Diplomatic Conference, Athens, 13 December 1974**; entered into force **28 April 1987**. Replaced a fragmented patchwork of regional passenger-carriage liability instruments with a uniform IMO-administered framework.
- **1976 Protocol** adopted **London, 19 November 1976**; switched the unit-of-account to SDR. Subsequently subsumed by the 2002 Protocol between 2002-Protocol parties.
- **1990 Protocol** adopted **London, 29 March 1990**; raised limits but never reached force-threshold ratification. Subsumed by the 2002 Protocol.
- **2002 Protocol** adopted at the **IMO Diplomatic Conference, London, 1 November 2002**; entered into force **23 April 2014** — a 12-year delay primarily attributable to industry concerns over **compulsory insurance** and **war-risk + terrorism cover** in the wake of the post-2001 hardening of the marine reinsurance market. Once in force, the 2002 Protocol superseded the 1974 + 1976 Protocol + 1990 Protocol regimes between states-parties.
- **State-party count**: 35+ states-parties to the 2002 Protocol as of the most recent IMO catalog snapshot. **EU Regulation 392/2009** directly incorporates the 2002 Protocol into EU law, effectively making it binding on all EU member-state-flagged carriers and on all carriage involving an EU port — substantially extending the Protocol's practical reach beyond its formal ratification list.

## Two-tier liability framework

The 2002 Protocol restructured carrier liability into a **two-tier framework with a fault-inversion band**, organised around the distinction between **shipping incidents** and **other causes**. The figures below reflect the 2002 Protocol's per-passenger SDR ceilings.

- **Tier 1 — Strict liability for shipping incidents** (Article 3.1): up to **250,000 SDR per passenger** for death or personal injury caused by a **shipping incident**. "Shipping incident" is defined as **shipwreck, capsizing, collision, stranding of the ship, explosion, fire, or defect in the ship**. The carrier is liable on a **strict** basis up to this ceiling — fault need not be shown, and the carrier's only escape is to prove that the incident resulted from an act of war, hostilities, civil war, insurrection, or a natural phenomenon of an exceptional, inevitable and irresistible character; or was wholly caused by an intentional act or omission of a third party.
- **Tier 2 — Strict liability with fault inversion** (Article 3.5): from **250,001 SDR up to 400,000 SDR per passenger**, the carrier is liable **unless it proves that the incident occurred without its fault or neglect**. The presumption of liability is rebuttable, but the burden is squarely on the carrier.
- **Above 400,000 SDR — fault-based**: the carrier may plead the Article 3.5 defence; the **claimant** bears the burden of proving the carrier's fault or neglect for any portion above the 400,000 SDR ceiling.
- **Non-shipping-incident causes**: for death, personal injury, or luggage loss arising from causes **other** than a shipping incident (e.g. slip-and-fall, food poisoning, medical-care incidents on board), the carrier is liable on a **fault-based** standard with the claimant bearing the burden — the strict-liability tiers do not apply.

## Compulsory insurance

- **Article 4bis** (added by the 2002 Protocol): compulsory insurance or other financial security for ships licensed to carry **more than 12 passengers** covering the carrier's liability **up to 250,000 SDR per passenger** (i.e. the Tier 1 strict-liability ceiling).
- **Insurance certificate** issued by the **flag-state** evidencing the cover. The certificate is required to be carried on board and produced on demand.
- **Direct action** against the insurer: the insurer can be **sued directly** by claimants for liability covered by the certificate, mirroring the direct-action rule in CLC 1992 Article VII(8) and the Bunkers Convention 2001. The insurer retains the same defences the carrier would have, plus a **wilful-misconduct defence** unique to direct-action claims.
- **War-risk + terrorism reservation**: introduced into the IMO regime to address industry concerns about the cost and availability of war-risk reinsurance after the post-2001 hardening of the market. **States-parties may opt out** of war-risk + terrorism cover; in practice **nearly all 2002-Protocol parties have made this reservation**. The resulting cover gap is partially filled by the **IMO Reservation Guidelines** (which set a lower mandatory war-risk insurance floor) and by **commercial war-risk insurance** placed by carriers; the gap remains a known structural feature of the regime.

## Athens vs. LLMC 1996 interaction

Athens caps liability **per-passenger**; LLMC 1996 caps overall ship-incident liability **per-incident across all claimants** (including passengers, cargo claimants, third-party property claimants, and others). The two regimes interlock through **Article 7(2) of the 2002 Protocol**: where the **LLMC limit** applicable to the ship is **lower than the aggregate of all Athens claims** for a given incident, the LLMC limit governs and Athens claimants share the LLMC passenger-claim fund pro-rata.

In practice this means **Athens controls the per-individual ceiling** for any one passenger's claim, while the **LLMC ceiling caps the aggregate** payable across all passengers for the incident as a whole. For high-passenger-count casualties (cruise-ship sinkings, ferry capsizes), the LLMC aggregate cap can become the operative constraint and the per-passenger Athens ceiling can be unrealised. LLMC 1996 itself contains a dedicated **passenger-claim fund** under Article 7 separate from the personal-injury and property-damage funds under Article 6, reflecting the special status of passenger claims in the LLMC architecture.

## Notable enforcement / case law

- **Costa Concordia (2012, off Isola del Giglio, Italy)** — Carnival-flagged cruise ship grounded and capsized off the Tuscan coast with 32 fatalities and ~3,200 passengers exposed. **First major application of Athens 2002 in the EU context** following the entry into force of EU Regulation 392/2009. Resulted in a wave of class-action and individual settlements that exercised both the Tier 1 strict-liability regime (the grounding plainly being a "shipping incident") and the LLMC 1996 aggregate cap. The case established the practical operating model for Athens 2002 + EU 392/2009 + LLMC 1996 interlock.
- **Sewol (2014, Republic of Korea)** — passenger ferry capsize with ~304 fatalities. **Korea is not a party to the 2002 Protocol**; claims were litigated under **Athens 1974 + Korean national tort law**. The case illustrates the practical consequences of non-uniform protocol-ratification status and the residual relevance of the 1974 regime in non-2002 jurisdictions.
- **Express Samina (2000, off Paros, Greece)** — Greek ferry capsize with 81 fatalities, **pre-2002 Protocol**. Litigated under **Athens 1974 + 1976 Protocol** with Greek national law overlay. A reference precedent for the lower per-passenger ceilings under the 1974 + 1976 regime that the 2002 Protocol was designed to raise.
- **Princess Mary capsize cases (pre-2002)** — historic precedents under Athens 1974 establishing baseline doctrinal interpretations of "shipping incident," carrier-fault burden allocation, and luggage-loss ceilings that survived into the 2002-Protocol re-codification.
