# CLC 1992 — Civil Liability Convention for Oil Pollution Damage

## Scope

The IMO **International Convention on Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Damage (1992 Protocol)** is the foundational global treaty providing a **strict-liability** framework for **oil-pollution damage from tankers carrying persistent oil cargo** — crude oil, heavy fuel oil, heavy diesel oil, and lubricating oil. Liability is **channelled** to the **registered owner of the tanker** (Article III), insulating the cargo-owner, charterer, manager, operator, master, and crew from direct claims under the convention. The owner's liability is **capped on a tonnage-banded formula** (Article V), backed by **compulsory insurance** evidenced by a CLC certificate (Article VII). The geographic trigger is pollution damage in the **territorial sea + Exclusive Economic Zone** of states-parties (Article II), including pollution-prevention measures taken anywhere.

CLC 1992 is **tier-1** in a three-tier global compensation architecture: where owner liability is exhausted or unavailable, **IOPC Funds 1992** (tier-2) compensate up to a combined ceiling, and the **2003 Supplementary Fund** (tier-3) extends total compensation further for states that have opted into the supplementary regime. ## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **CLC 1969** (the original convention) adopted at the **IMO Diplomatic Conference, Brussels, 29 November 1969**; in force **19 June 1975**. Drafted in the wake of the Torrey Canyon casualty (1967) which had exposed the absence of a uniform civil-liability regime for tanker-source oil pollution. Replaced between 1992-Protocol parties by the 1992 instrument.
- **1992 Protocol** adopted **London, 27 November 1992**; entered into force **30 May 1996**. The 1992 Protocol broadened geographic reach to the EEZ, raised the limits, expanded "pollution damage" to include reasonable reinstatement measures, and re-denominated limits in **Special Drawing Rights (SDR)**.
- **2000 Amendments** to the 1992 Protocol adopted by IMO Legal Committee under the **tacit-acceptance procedure**; entered into force **1 November 2003**. Raised limits by **50.37%** without a fresh diplomatic conference.
- **State-party count**: 130+ states-parties to the 1992 Protocol as of the most recent IMO catalog snapshot, including most major flag-states. The original 1969 + 1971 instruments have been substantially superseded but remain in force between residual parties that have not ratified the 1992 Protocol.

## Three-tier compensation system

CLC sits as the inner ring of a three-tier global compensation architecture for tanker-source oil pollution. The cap figures below are **per-incident, after the 2000 Amendments**, denominated in SDR.

| Tier | Source | Per-incident cap (after 2000 Amendments) |
|------|--------|------------------------------------------|
| 1 | CLC 1992 owner liability (tonnage-banded) | 4.51 M – 89.77 M SDR |
| 2 | IOPC Funds 1992 (top-up of tier-1) | Up to 203 M SDR total (CLC + Fund) |
| 3 | Supplementary Fund 2003 (opt-in) | Up to 750 M SDR total (CLC + Funds + Supplementary) |

The funds are financed by **levies on contributing oil receivers** (importers of more than 150,000 tons of contributing oil per year) in states-parties to the respective Fund instruments — not by states-parties directly. Refer to the IMO + IOPC Funds catalog for authoritative current figures; the values above are illustrative of the calculation method.

## Key Articles

- **Article I — Definitions.** Defines "ship" (sea-going tanker carrying persistent oil cargo in bulk), "persistent oil," "pollution damage" (including reasonable reinstatement of impaired environment + costs of preventive measures), "owner" (person registered as owner; flag-state in absence of registration), and "incident."
- **Article II — Liability scope.** Convention applies to pollution damage caused **in the territory + territorial sea + EEZ** of a state-party, and to **preventive measures** taken anywhere to prevent or minimise such damage.
- **Article III — Channelling of liability.** Claims for pollution damage may be brought **only against the registered owner**. Claims against the owner's servants/agents, the pilot, charterer, manager, operator, salvor, or any person performing preventive measures are **barred** unless the loss resulted from their **personal act or omission committed with intent or recklessly with knowledge** that pollution damage would probably result.
- **Article V — Limitation of liability.** Owner may **limit liability** under a tonnage-banded SDR formula. The right to limit is **lost** where the pollution damage "**resulted from the personal act or omission of the owner, committed with the intent to cause such damage, or recklessly and with knowledge that such damage would probably result**" — the same gross-negligence gateway used in LLMC Article 4.
- **Article VII — Compulsory insurance.** Owners of ships carrying more than 2,000 tons of persistent oil cargo in bulk must maintain insurance or financial security covering their CLC liability up to the limit, evidenced by a **CLC certificate** issued by the flag-state. Direct action against the insurer is permitted (Article VII(8)).
- **Article XI — Jurisdictional rules.** Claims may be brought **only in the courts of the state(s) where the pollution damage occurred or preventive measures were taken**. Judgments rendered by competent CLC-state courts are recognised + enforced in other states-parties (Article X).

## US carve-out

The **United States is not a party** to CLC. The US-domestic equivalent is the **Oil Pollution Act of 1990 (OPA 90)** — see [`standards/opa-90.md`](./opa-90.md). OPA-90 is generally more aggressive than CLC on multiple axes: higher liability caps that **fall away entirely** for gross negligence, willful misconduct, regulatory violation, or failure to report (versus CLC's limited intent-or-recklessness gateway under Article V); a domestic **Oil Spill Liability Trust Fund (OSLTF)** that runs independently of the international IOPC Funds; and **non-preemption of state law**, allowing state regimes (notably California, Washington, Alaska) to impose unlimited liability on top of the federal floor. The CLC channelling-of-liability principle is also weaker in OPA-90, where multiple "responsible parties" can be jointly liable.

## Notable enforcement / case law

- **Erika (1999, off Brittany, France)** — Maltese-flag tanker chartered by TotalFinaElf broke up and released ~20,000 tons of heavy fuel oil onto the French Atlantic coast. CLC 1992 + IOPC Funds 1992 compensation regime was applied; subsequent French criminal proceedings (Cour de cassation, 2012) tested the interplay between CLC's civil-liability channelling and domestic criminal pollution offences, upholding criminal liability of the charterer notwithstanding CLC's civil channelling.
- **Prestige (2002, off Galicia, Spain)** — Bahamian-flag single-hull tanker broke up off the Galician coast releasing ~63,000 tons of heavy fuel oil. CLC 1992 owner-liability limit (~22 M SDR for that tonnage) was rapidly exhausted; IOPC Funds 1992 activated to its combined CLC+Fund ceiling; the political controversy over the gap between actual damage and available compensation was a primary driver of the **2003 Supplementary Fund Protocol** that established the tier-3 regime.
- **Hebei Spirit (2007, off Taean, Republic of Korea)** — Hong Kong-flag tanker holed by a drifting crane barge, releasing ~10,500 tons of crude oil. First **major activation of the 2003 Supplementary Fund**, demonstrating the tier-3 regime in operation. Korean courts also tested the boundary of CLC's channelling provision against the third-party crane-barge owner.
- **The Nagasaki Spirit (1997, UK House of Lords)** — leading authority on the interface between CLC 1992 + the **Salvage Convention 1989 Article 14 special compensation** regime; held that "**fair rate**" for Article 14 salvor remuneration does not include a profit element, with downstream effect on how environmental-salvage incentives interlock with CLC owner-liability funds.
