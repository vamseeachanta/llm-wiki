# LLMC 1996 — Convention on Limitation of Liability for Maritime Claims

## Scope

The IMO **Convention on Limitation of Liability for Maritime Claims** (LLMC) is the global treaty providing **shipowner-aggregated limitation of liability** for maritime claims (other than oil-pollution claims, which fall under the CLC/Bunkers regime). It permits a shipowner — and, by extension, the salvor, charterer, manager, and operator — to **cap total liability across all claimants arising out of one incident** at a figure calculated by formula on the ship's gross tonnage under Articles 6 and 7.

LLMC sits as the **umbrella aggregation regime** alongside the claim-side conventions: **Hague-Visby** (cargo carriage package limit), **CLC + Fund + Bunkers** (oil-pollution liability), **HNS Convention 2010** (hazardous-and-noxious-substances liability), and **Athens Convention 2002** (passenger claims). Where those instruments cap liability per-claim, per-package, or per-passenger, LLMC caps liability **per-incident across the entire claim-pool** for all claims that fall outside those carve-outs. The shipowner constitutes a **limitation fund** (Article 11) and claims are then proven against the fund and shared pro-rata.

The page is pinned to the **LLMC Consolidated 2022** edition reflecting the **2012 Amendments** (LEG.5(99)), which raised the 1996-Protocol limits by ~51 % and entered into force on 8 June 2015.

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **LLMC 1976** adopted at the IMO Diplomatic Conference, **London, 19 November 1976**; **entered into force 1 December 1986**. Replaced the **Brussels Convention 1957** (limitation of liability of owners of seagoing ships) between states-parties.
- **1996 Protocol** adopted **London, 2 May 1996**; **entered into force 13 May 2004**. Increased the LLMC 1976 limits by approximately 250 % and switched the unit-of-account to **Special Drawing Rights (SDR)** with a tacit-acceptance amendment regime.
- **2012 Amendments** to the 1996 Protocol adopted by IMO Legal Committee resolution **LEG.5(99)** (April 2012); **entered into force 8 June 2015** under the tacit-acceptance procedure of Article 8 of the 1996 Protocol. Raised limits by a further ~51 %.
- **State-party count**: the 1996 Protocol counts 60+ states-parties as of the most recent IMO catalog snapshot, including most major flag-states. Some states remain party only to LLMC 1976.

## Two operational regimes

LLMC operates as **two coexisting regimes** depending on which instruments the state-of-jurisdiction has ratified:

- **LLMC 1976** (without 1996 Protocol): the older, lower-limit regime. A handful of states remain party to LLMC 1976 only and have not ratified the 1996 Protocol — claims litigated in those forums attract the original 1976 limits.
- **LLMC 1996 Protocol** (with 2012 Amendments where also ratified): the modern higher-limit regime with SDR-denominated units and a tacit-acceptance amendment mechanism that allows IMO to raise limits without a fresh diplomatic conference. Most major maritime nations are 1996-Protocol parties.
- **Forum-shopping consequence**: because limits can differ materially between jurisdictions, claimants and shipowners alike scrutinise the **state-of-jurisdiction party-status** when constituting a limitation fund. The Ever Given 2021 multi-state proceedings are a contemporary illustration (see Notable enforcement below).

## Tonnage-based limits (Articles 6 + 7, after 2012 Amendments)

LLMC limits are **tonnage-banded** and computed by accreting per-ton SDR figures across the bands. The table below is **illustrative of the calculation method** under the 2012 Amendments — refer to the IMO consolidated text for authoritative figures.

| Ship gross tonnage | Personal-injury limit | Property-damage limit |
|--------------------|----------------------|-----------------------|
| Up to 2,000 GT | 3.02 million SDR | 1.51 million SDR |
| 2,001 – 30,000 GT | + 1,208 SDR per ton | + 604 SDR per ton |
| 30,001 – 70,000 GT | + 906 SDR per ton | + 453 SDR per ton |
| 70,001 + GT | + 604 SDR per ton | + 302 SDR per ton |

Two distinct funds are constituted under Article 6 — one for **personal-injury / death** claims and one for **property** claims. Where the personal-injury fund is exhausted, the unsatisfied balance ranks rateably against the property fund (Article 6(2)).

## Excluded claims + the gross-negligence gateway

The convention's limitation right does **not** apply to:

- **Oil-pollution claims** falling under the **CLC 1992** and **Bunkers Convention 2001** regimes — those instruments carry their own tonnage-based limits.
- **Nuclear damage claims** — covered by the dedicated nuclear-liability conventions.
- **Claims by salvors** for services rendered (Article 3(b)) — including **special compensation** under Salvage Convention 1989 Article 14.
- **Wreck-removal and cargo-removal claims** — Article 18(1) permits states-parties to reserve the right to exclude these; many have done so.
- **Servants' claims** where the contract of service is governed by limitation-incompatible law.

The **gross-negligence gateway** (Article 4): the shipowner **loses the right to limit** if the loss "**resulted from his personal act or omission, committed with the intent to cause such loss, or recklessly and with knowledge that such loss would probably result**". The bar is deliberately high — mere negligence does not break the limit, and case-law (notably **The Western Neptune** below) has held the burden of proof rests squarely on the claimant.

## Notable enforcement

- **Bay of Algeciras 1997 (TT Club v. Hellenic)** — early multi-jurisdiction litigation on the coexistence of LLMC 1976 and the 1996 Protocol limits before the latter entered into force; foreshadowed the forum-shopping pattern.
- **The Volga 2002** (Australia / Russia) — IUU-fishing forfeiture proceedings before ITLOS; the LLMC limit's interaction with state-imposed bond/forfeiture regimes was peripherally engaged.
- **The Western Neptune 2008** (UK Commercial Court) — leading modern authority on the **burden of proof** for breaking the LLMC limit under Article 4; reaffirmed that the claimant bears the heavy burden of proving recklessness with knowledge.
- **The Ever Given 2021** (Suez Canal blockage) — multi-state proceedings (UK, Egypt, Netherlands) involved competitive constitution of LLMC limitation funds in different jurisdictions; the case is a textbook contemporary illustration of LLMC forum-shopping under coexisting 1976 / 1996 / 2012-amended regimes.
