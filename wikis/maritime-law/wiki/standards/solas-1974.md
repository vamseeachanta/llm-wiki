# SOLAS 1974 — International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea

## Scope

SOLAS is the foundational IMO treaty for the safety of merchant ships at sea. The 1974 instrument is the successor-by-replacement to the original SOLAS adopted in 1914 in response to the *Titanic* loss, and to the intermediate 1929, 1948, and 1960 conventions; it is widely cited as the most important and most-amended of all maritime safety treaties.

It is mandatory for merchant vessels of 500 GT and above engaged on international voyages (with chapter-level exceptions for passenger ships, tankers, bulk carriers, gas carriers, and high-speed craft regardless of tonnage). Coverage spans ship construction (Ch. II-1 subdivision and stability; Ch. II-2 fire protection), life-saving appliances (Ch. III), radiocommunications (Ch. IV — GMDSS), navigation (Ch. V), carriage of cargoes (Ch. VI), dangerous goods (Ch. VII), nuclear ships (Ch. VIII), management for safe operation (Ch. IX — ISM Code mandate), high-speed craft (Ch. X — HSC Code mandate), maritime safety and security (Ch. XI-1 and XI-2 — ISPS Code mandate), bulk carrier safety (Ch. XII), verification of compliance (Ch. XIII — IMSAS), and polar operations (Ch. XIV — Polar Code mandate).

This page is pinned to the **SOLAS Consolidated 2022 (IMO publication)** edition and to the **MSC.521(106) (2022, e-navigation) / MSC.532(108) (2024, cybersecurity)** amendment cohort recorded in `amendment_status`.

## Adoption and entry-into-force

- **Adopted:** London, 1974-11-01, at the IMO International Conference on Safety of Life at Sea.
- **Entry-into-force:** 1980-05-25, after ratification by 25 States representing not less than 50% of world merchant shipping tonnage.
- **Successor relationship:** replaces SOLAS 1948 and SOLAS 1960; the 1914 and 1929 conventions are of historical interest only.
- **Protocols:** the 1978 Protocol (post-*Amoco Cadiz* tanker safety) and the 1988 Protocol (Harmonized System of Survey and Certification — HSSC) are integral and in force.
- **States parties:** 167+ contracting governments covering ~99% of world merchant shipping tonnage as of 2024.

## Amendment regime

SOLAS evolves through the IMO Maritime Safety Committee (MSC) under a **tacit-acceptance procedure**: amendments adopted by MSC enter into force on a defined date unless objected to by a specified fraction of contracting governments. This explains the velocity of SOLAS revision relative to other treaty regimes and is the reason `amendment_status` here is recorded at MSC-resolution granularity rather than as a linear edition number.

| Amendment / protocol | Year adopted | Year in force | Driver | Summary |

## Chapters — 14-Chapter architecture

Chapter *titles only* — Regulation text lives at the publisher catalog (link below). The 14-chapter architecture is the operating skeleton of the convention; each chapter has its own amendment cadence under MSC and its own primary practitioner audience (designer, builder, master, flag-state surveyor, port-state inspector, security officer, or company DPA).

| Ch. | Title (short) | Primary scope | Subordinate code(s) | Primary practitioner audience |
|----|---------------|---------------|---------------------|-------------------------------|
| I | General Provisions | Application, definitions, surveys, certificates, port-state control verification | — | Flag-state surveyors, port-state-control officers, classification societies |
| II-1 | Construction — Subdivision, Stability, Machinery, Electrical | Damage stability (deterministic + probabilistic indices A/R), watertight subdivision, machinery redundancy, electrical-system safety; IGF Code carriage in Part G | IGF Code (Part G) | Naval architects, hull-and-machinery designers, classification societies, IACS |
| II-2 | Construction — Fire Protection, Detection, Extinction | Structural fire protection (FTP Code), fire detection, fixed extinguishing systems, escape arrangements, ventilation | FSS Code, FTP Code | Fire-safety engineers, ship designers, class-society plan reviewers |
| III | Life-Saving Appliances and Arrangements | Survival craft, lifeboats, free-fall lifeboats, MES, immersion suits, drills, evacuation analysis | LSA Code | Designers, masters, port-state inspectors |
| IV | Radiocommunications | GMDSS mandate; sea-area-A1/A2/A3/A4 equipment matrix; modernization toward MF/HF + Inmarsat + Iridium | — | Radio operators, flag-state radio inspectors |
| V | Safety of Navigation | Nautical charts (paper or ECDIS), VDR/S-VDR, AIS, LRIT, BNWAS, e-navigation, voyage planning, distress signals, master's discretion | — | Masters, navigators, hydrographic offices |
| VI | Carriage of Cargoes and Oil Fuels | Grain Code, Cargo Securing Manual, low-flashpoint oil-fuel use; IMSBC Code for solid bulk cargoes | IMSBC Code, Grain Code | Cargo officers, masters, marine-warranty surveyors |
| VII | Carriage of Dangerous Goods | Mandatory IMDG Code (packaged DG), IBC Code (chemical tankers), IGC Code (gas carriers), INF Code (irradiated nuclear fuel) | IMDG, IBC, IGC, INF Codes | Chemical/gas/dangerous-goods specialists, port-facility operators |
| VIII | Nuclear Ships | Operational requirements for nuclear-propelled merchant ships (very small population — historic *NS Savannah*, *Otto Hahn*, *Mutsu*, *Sevmorput*) | — | Nuclear regulators, flag-state authorities |
| IX | Management for the Safe Operation of Ships | Mandatory **ISM Code**: Safety Management System (SMS), Designated Person Ashore (DPA), Document of Compliance (DOC), Safety Management Certificate (SMC) | ISM Code | Company management, DPAs, masters, ISM auditors |
| X | Safety Measures for High-Speed Craft | Mandatory **HSC Code** (1994 + 2000 editions) for craft >25 m capable of >7.16 × Δ^0.1667 m/s | HSC Code | HSC operators, classification societies |
| XI-1 | Special Measures to Enhance Maritime Safety | Authorization of recognized organizations (RO Code), enhanced surveys, IMO ship-identification numbers, Continuous Synopsis Record (CSR) | RO Code, ESP | Flag states, classification societies (ROs), surveyors |
| XI-2 | Special Measures to Enhance Maritime Security | Mandatory **ISPS Code** (post-9/11); Ship Security Plan, Ship Security Officer, port-facility security plans, security levels 1/2/3 | ISPS Code | Ship Security Officers (SSOs), port-facility security officers (PFSOs), flag/port states |
| XII | Additional Safety Measures for Bulk Carriers | Damage stability and structural strength for bulk carriers ≥150 m; double-side-skin requirements; loading manuals | — | Bulk-carrier designers, classification societies, IACS (CSR) |
| XIII | Verification of Compliance | Mandatory IMO Member State Audit Scheme (IMSAS) | III Code | IMO secretariat, flag-state administrations |
| XIV | Safety Measures for Ships Operating in Polar Waters | Mandatory **Polar Code** (safety part); Polar Ship Certificate, Polar Water Operational Manual (PWOM) | Polar Code | Polar-operating shipowners, classification societies, masters with ice training |

For per-chapter doctrinal expansion (subdivision indices, GMDSS sea-area matrix, ISPS security-level cascades) the Regulation-level synthesis lives at [`concepts/solas-1974`](../concepts/solas-1974.md); this page records only the architecture and the practitioner-audience map.

### Selected high-citation Chapter cohorts

The following sub-sections capture the highest-citation Regulation cohorts inside the architecture above. Treat them as **anchor points** for downstream calc citations — when a calc cites SOLAS at sub-Chapter granularity, it is almost always citing one of these cohorts.

**Chapter II-1 Part B (Subdivision and Stability)** — Regulations 4 through 8 establish the **probabilistic-damage-stability framework** with the attained-index `A` and required-index `R` paradigm; Regulations 9 through 17 cover watertight integrity, watertight bulkhead penetrations, and double-bottom requirements. Chapter II-1 Part B-1 (Regulations 6–7) is the most-cited cohort for calc-side damage-stability work. The framework is ship-type-stratified: passenger-ship `R` is more demanding than cargo-ship `R`, and the SOLAS 2020 amendments tightened passenger-ship `R` materially.

**Chapter II-2 (Fire Safety)** — Regulations 4–6 cover probability of ignition and fire-growth potential; Regulations 7–10 cover detection, alarms, and suppression; Regulations 13–16 cover means of escape; Regulations 17–20 cover special-category arrangements (vehicle decks, ro-pax, special-category spaces). The chapter mandates the **FSS Code** (Fire Safety Systems Code) and the **FTP Code** (Fire Test Procedures Code) as subordinate normative references.

**Chapter III (Life-Saving Appliances)** — mandates the **LSA Code** (International Life-Saving Appliance Code, MSC.48(66)). Sub-Chapter cohorts cover survival-craft capacity (Reg. 21–22 passenger ships, Reg. 31 cargo ships), launching arrangements, drills, and crew-familiarization.

**Chapter IV (Radiocommunications) — GMDSS sea-area matrix**:

| Sea area | Definition | Required equipment cohort |
|----------|------------|---------------------------|
| A1 | Within VHF coverage of at least one coast station providing continuous DSC alerting | VHF DSC (radio Reg. 7.1) |
| A2 | Beyond A1 but within MF DSC coverage of at least one coast station | A1 + MF DSC |
| A3 | Beyond A1 + A2 but within Inmarsat (or recognized GMDSS provider) geostationary coverage | A1 + A2 + Inmarsat (or Iridium under MSC.521(106)) |
| A4 | The remaining sea areas (predominantly polar regions) | A1 + A2 + HF DSC + (Iridium under MSC.521(106)) |

**Chapter V (Safety of Navigation)** — Reg. 19 sets the carriage-requirement matrix for shipborne navigational equipment (VDR, AIS, ECDIS, BNWAS, gyrocompass, magnetic compass, radar, depth-sounder); Reg. 22 covers visibility from the navigating bridge; Reg. 28 covers records of navigational activities. Reg. 34 (voyage planning) and Reg. 34-1 (master's discretion) are the doctrinal anchors.

**Chapter VII Part C (IGC Code mandate)** — the IGC Code is the most-elaborated subordinate code under SOLAS, governing the design, construction, and equipment of gas carriers; resolved separately at [`igc-code`](../../../lng-projects/wiki/standards/igc-code.md).

**Chapter IX (ISM Code mandate)** — Regulations 1–6 establish the SMS, DOC, SMC, and audit framework; sub-Chapter resolution at [`ism-code`](../concepts/ism-code.md).

**Chapter XI-2 (ISPS Code mandate)** — Regulations 1–13 establish the ISPS framework; security-level cascades (1=normal, 2=heightened, 3=imminent threat) drive port-facility and shipboard response. Sub-Chapter resolution at [`isps-code`](../concepts/isps-code.md).

**Chapter XII (Bulk Carrier Safety)** — Regulations 4–6 set damage-stability and structural strength for bulk carriers ≥150 m; Reg. 6.3 mandates double-side-skin construction for new bulk carriers ≥150 m carrying solid bulk cargoes of density ≥1,000 kg/m³ (post-*Derbyshire* loop).

## Major SOLAS-driven Codes

SOLAS is the parent instrument for an extensive system of subordinate IMO codes. Each is mandatory under the chapter cited and is itself a candidate for a dedicated standards-page in the relevant wiki domain:

- **IGC Code** — International Code for the Construction and Equipment of Ships Carrying Liquefied Gases in Bulk; mandatory under Ch. VII for gas carriers (LNG, LPG, ethylene, ammonia). Already first-class at [`lng-projects/wiki/standards/igc-code.md`](../../../lng-projects/wiki/standards/igc-code.md).
- **IBC Code** — International Code for the Construction and Equipment of Ships Carrying Dangerous Chemicals in Bulk; mandatory under Ch. VII for chemical tankers.
- **ISM Code** — International Safety Management Code; mandatory under Ch. IX for company-side and ship-side safety management.
- **ISPS Code** — International Ship and Port Facility Security Code; mandatory under Ch. XI-2.
- **IGF Code** — International Code of Safety for Ships Using Gases or Other Low-Flashpoint Fuels; mandatory under Ch. II-1 Part G for LNG- and methanol-fuelled vessels.
- **HSC Code** — High-Speed Craft Code (1994 and 2000 editions); mandatory under Ch. X.
- **Polar Code** — International Code for Ships Operating in Polar Waters; mandatory under Ch. XIV (and under MARPOL for environmental provisions).
- **IMDG Code** — International Maritime Dangerous Goods Code; mandatory under Ch. VII for packaged dangerous goods.
- **IMSBC Code** — International Maritime Solid Bulk Cargoes Code; mandatory under Ch. VI for solid bulk cargoes.

## Notable enforcement / case law and incident-driven amendments

SOLAS evolves substantially through a casualty-then-amendment loop. The convention is **preventive-by-design**, but its amendment cadence is reactive — almost every chapter has a named-incident driver in its history, and that history is the most reliable navigator of *why* a given Regulation looks the way it does. The most-cited incidents in this loop include:

### Foundational driver

- **RMS Titanic 1912** — North Atlantic iceberg-collision sinking with 1,503 fatalities. The casualty exposed the absence of any binding international regime on lifeboat capacity, watertight-bulkhead subdivision, radio watchkeeping, or distress procedure. The 1914 SOLAS Convention was negotiated within months and is the genetic ancestor of the 1974 instrument; the **lifeboats-for-all** principle, **24-hour radio watch** doctrine, and **subdivision-and-stability** Chapter all trace to *Titanic*. Although the 1914 instrument never entered into force as written (it was overtaken by World War I and replaced by the 1929, 1948, 1960, and 1974 successors), every subsequent SOLAS edition has carried the *Titanic* legacy in its structural DNA. See the doctrinal companion at [`concepts/solas-1974`](../concepts/solas-1974.md) for the foundational-driver narrative.

### Subdivision and stability drivers (Ch. II-1)

- **SS Andrea Doria 1956** — Italian passenger liner collided with the *Stockholm* off Nantucket; sank the following morning with 46 fatalities. The *Andrea Doria* loss was a lighthouse case for **damage stability under collision flooding** — the ship had nominally compliant SOLAS 1948 subdivision but the post-collision heel exceeded the lifeboat-launch limit on one side, halving effective survival-craft capacity. The IMO probabilistic-damage-stability framework (now Ch. II-1 Part B-1, indices `A` ≥ `R`) is the doctrinal descendant; the case also drove early radar-collision-avoidance work that fed into `COLREGs 1972` (future-promotion candidate).
- **MS Estonia 1994** — Roll-on/roll-off passenger (ro-pax) ferry sinking in the Baltic Sea with 852 fatalities after bow-visor failure and water on the open vehicle deck. Drove SOLAS Ch. II-1 damage-stability amendments and Ch. III life-saving-appliance amendments for ro-pax vessels (the "Stockholm Agreement" regional baseline preceded the global IMO uptake).
- **MV Sewol 2014** — Republic of Korea ferry capsize with 304 fatalities (mostly schoolchildren) after illegal modification, cargo overload, and inadequate ballast. Drove regional and cross-flag domestic amendments and reinforced ISM-Code (Ch. IX) audit scrutiny on stability-data accuracy, cargo-securing manuals (Ch. VI), and crew training. *Sewol* is the canonical study case for **stability data integrity under sequential modification**.

### Construction and structural-strength drivers (Ch. II-1, Ch. XII)

- **MV Derbyshire 1980** — UK bulk carrier lost in Typhoon Orchid in the Pacific with 44 fatalities; the casualty was the catalyst for SOLAS Chapter XII (Additional Safety Measures for Bulk Carriers) adopted 1997 and entered into force 1999, including double-side-skin requirements, hatch-cover strength, and loading-manual enhancements. The **IACS Common Structural Rules (CSR)** for bulk carriers descend from the same loop.
- **MOL Comfort 2013** — Container ship broke in two in the Indian Ocean during heavy weather; total constructive loss with no fatalities but the entire cargo and the vessel sank. The casualty drove **IACS UR S11A** longitudinal-strength updates and reinforced the IACS Common Structural Rules for Bulk Carriers and Oil Tankers (CSR-BC&OT) hull-girder bending-moment envelopes; the ship's class society was implicated in plan-review and built-condition divergence findings. SOLAS Ch. II-1 enhanced-survey provisions and the IACS UR M53 mobile-offshore-unit harmonization both cite the *MOL Comfort* lessons-learned package.

### Fire-safety and dangerous-goods drivers (Ch. II-2, Ch. VII)

- **Maersk Honam 2018** — 15,000-TEU container ship cargo fire in the Arabian Sea with five fatalities; uncontrolled fire in cargo hold 3 forward of the accommodation block. The casualty triggered the **CINS / IUMI joint position on misdeclared and undeclared dangerous goods** and fed directly into MSC.1/Circ.1666 (2022) and successor amendments to SOLAS Ch. II-2 on cargo-area fire detection, water-mist systems, and IMDG-Code declaration scrutiny. The case is the canonical container-ship analogue to the *Sewol* stability-data-integrity case: misdeclaration of cargo is structurally indistinguishable from misdeclaration of stability data, and both cascade into ISM-Code (Ch. IX) audit findings against the operator.

### Management-system drivers (Ch. IX — ISM Code)

- **MS Herald of Free Enterprise 1987** — Townsend Thoresen ro-pax ferry capsize off Zeebrugge with 193 fatalities after sailing with the bow door open. The Sheen Inquiry's "the body corporate was infected with the disease of sloppiness" finding drove the **adoption of the ISM Code** as a mandatory SOLAS Ch. IX instrument (adopted 1994, in force 1998 for passenger/tanker; 2002 for cargo). The case is the *raison d'être* of company-side safety-management-system regulation in international shipping.
- **MV Costa Concordia 2012** — Italian-flag passenger ship grounding off Isola del Giglio with 32 fatalities after master-deviation from passage plan. Drove the IMO "Costa Concordia Lessons Learned" review and resulting Ch. II-1 amendments on emergency lighting, damage-control drills, and passenger musters; reinforced ISM-Code findings on **master-pilot interface, bridge-team management, and SMS audit depth**. The case sits at the intersection of Ch. V (navigation), Ch. III (life-saving), Ch. II-1 (damage stability), and Ch. IX (ISM SMS).

### Pollution-coupled drivers (Ch. II-1, Ch. XI-1)

- **Amoco Cadiz 1978** — VLCC tanker grounded off Brittany; the casualty drove the **1978 SOLAS Protocol** on tanker safety (inert-gas systems, steering-gear redundancy, enhanced surveys) in parallel with the MARPOL 1978 Protocol on pollution-prevention.
- **Erika 1999 / Prestige 2002** — Although primarily [`marpol-73-78`](../concepts/marpol-73-78.md) and CLC casualties, both reinforced SOLAS Ch. II-1 enhanced-survey requirements (Resolution A.744(18) → ESP Code) and Ch. XI-1 recognized-organization audit chains; both drove EU Directive 2002/59/EC on vessel-traffic monitoring.

This casualty-then-amendment loop is the dominant evolutionary mechanism; the page-level approach is to keep this section incident-anchored while letting the [`concepts/solas-1974`](../concepts/solas-1974.md) doctrinal companion carry the per-Regulation gloss.

## Harmonization with parallel offshore-structure regimes

SOLAS Chapter II-1 (subdivision, stability, watertight integrity, machinery and electrical installations) is the **ship-shape jurisdictional pillar**. Mobile and fixed offshore structures interface with it through three harmonization instruments:

| Instrument | Scope | SOLAS interface |
|------------|-------|-----------------|
| **IMO MODU Code** (Resolution A.1023(26), 2009 consolidation) | Mobile offshore drilling units — column-stabilized, self-elevating (jack-up), surface-type, submersible | Provides the harmonization precedent that lets ship-shape MODUs operate under flag-state SOLAS jurisdiction; sets stability and structural-integrity baselines that align with Ch. II-1 + Ch. II-2 + Ch. III for crew-safety provisions. |
| **ABS GUI-002 — Guide for Building and Classing FPSOs** | Floating Production, Storage, and Offloading installations (ship-shape, column-stabilized, spar, FLNG, FSRU) | ABS class-rules implement hull/structure scantlings and station-keeping interfaces; ship-shape and self-propelled-or-detachable FPSOs operate under SOLAS Ch. II-1 stability margins simultaneously with GUI-002 hull classification. The disconnectable-turret FPSO is the most operationally-loaded intersection — disconnect-mechanism design and post-disconnect transit are sized to GUI-002 + ISO 19901-7 hull/mooring envelopes while preserving SOLAS Ch. II-1 intact and damage-stability margins. |
| **ISO 19901-7** | Station-keeping for floating offshore structures (catenary, taut-leg, thruster-assist, dynamic positioning) | Sized concurrent with SOLAS Ch. II-1 stability for FLNG and FSRU; cyclone-corridor disconnect-and-depart cases (NW Australia, Gulf of Mexico hurricane) resolve at this interface. |

The harmonization is operationally load-bearing: a disconnectable-turret FPSO in NW Australia or the Gulf of Mexico is *simultaneously* (a) an ABS GUI-002 / ISO 19901-7 floating offshore structure and (b) a SOLAS Ch. II-1 ship-shape vessel under flag-state jurisdiction. Class-society rules harmonize the two regimes via **IACS UR M53** (mobile offshore units). See the cross-wiki engineering-standards bridges below for the integrity-management methodology shared with API RP 2SIM (fixed-platform structural-integrity-management).

## IMO MSC + IACS UR + Class-society interaction

SOLAS does not contain primary numeric design rules for hull strength, fatigue life, or structural failure modes. Numeric scantlings are delegated to **classification-society rules**, which in turn are harmonized through **IACS Unified Requirements (UR)** and **Common Structural Rules (CSR)**. The interaction stack:

1. **IMO MSC** sets the high-level treaty obligation in SOLAS (e.g., "ships shall be subdivided into watertight compartments").
2. **IACS** issues a **Unified Requirement (UR)** or **Unified Interpretation (UI)** that harmonizes how the obligation is implemented across member class societies — examples: UR S11 (longitudinal strength), UR S31 (renewal criteria for hull structure), UR M53 (mobile offshore units), UR Z10 (hull surveys).
3. **Class society rules** (ABS, DNV, Lloyd's Register, Bureau Veritas, ClassNK, KR, RINA, CCS, IRS, RS — the ten IACS members) implement UR/UI in society-specific rule books with proprietary numerical envelopes.
4. **IACS CSR** (Common Structural Rules for Bulk Carriers and Oil Tankers, 2015 harmonization) is the single most prominent example of cross-society numeric harmonization; CSR-BC&OT is mandatory for all IACS-classed bulk carriers ≥150 m and oil tankers ≥150 m.

The implication for citation discipline: a calc that uses a numeric scantling under SOLAS Ch. II-1 should resolve `code_id` against the **class-society rule** (or IACS UR if non-society-specific), not against `solas-1974`. SOLAS itself is the *jurisdictional* citation — the numeric envelope lives downstream. See [`concepts/ism-code`](../concepts/ism-code.md) for the parallel argument on the management-system side: SOLAS Ch. IX mandates an SMS, but the numeric audit-frequency envelopes live in the ISM Code and in flag-state implementing rules.

## Practitioner stakeholder map

SOLAS compliance is a multi-stakeholder system. The page-level architecture maps each stakeholder to the chapters they own:

| Stakeholder | Primary chapters | Role |
|-------------|------------------|------|
| **Shipowner / operator (the Company under ISM)** | Ch. IX (ISM), Ch. XI-2 (ISPS), Ch. V (e-navigation policy) | Holds the Document of Compliance (DOC); appoints the DPA; bears ultimate operational liability under SOLAS, MARPOL, MLC. |
| **Master** | All chapters; specifically Ch. III (drills), Ch. V (navigation, master's discretion), Ch. IX (SMS at the ship-side), Ch. XI-2 (security level response) | The on-board enforcer of SOLAS; holds master's overriding authority in safety matters under Ch. V Reg. 34-1. |
| **Flag state** | All chapters via certificate issue | Issues SOLAS certificates (Cargo Ship Safety Construction, Cargo Ship Safety Equipment, Cargo Ship Safety Radio, Passenger Ship Safety, Safety Management Certificate, International Ship Security Certificate); delegates survey to ROs under the RO Code. |
| **Port state (PSC)** | All chapters via inspection regimes (Paris MoU, Tokyo MoU, USCG, Caribbean MoU, etc.) | Verifies SOLAS certificates and operational compliance on visiting foreign-flag vessels; can detain non-compliant ships. See [`concepts/port-state-control`](../concepts/port-state-control.md). |
| **Classification society (Recognized Organization)** | Primarily Ch. II-1, Ch. II-2, Ch. XII; also Ch. III, Ch. V, Ch. XI-1 | Conducts plan approval, construction survey, and periodic survey on flag-state delegation; ten IACS members (ABS, DNV, LR, BV, ClassNK, KR, RINA, CCS, IRS, RS) cover ~94% of world tonnage. |
| **IMO MSC** | All chapters | Adopts amendments under tacit-acceptance; promulgates MSC resolutions and MSC.1/Circ. interpretive guidance. |
| **IACS** | Ch. II-1, Ch. II-2, Ch. XII (numeric scantlings); Ch. XI-1 (RO Code) | Harmonizes class-society rules; publishes UR, UI, REC, and CSR. |
| **Ship Security Officer (SSO) / Port Facility Security Officer (PFSO)** | Ch. XI-2 | Implements the ISPS Code at ship-side and port-side respectively; reports to the Company Security Officer (CSO). |
| **Designated Person Ashore (DPA)** | Ch. IX (ISM) | The shore-side single-point-of-contact between master and senior management on safety/pollution-prevention matters; mandatory ISM Code role. |

II-1 intact and damage-stability margins govern post-disconnect transit; the **MODU Code (IMO Resolution A.1023(26))** provides the harmonization precedent that lets fixed-platform SIM doctrine map onto ship-shape mobile-unit life-extension cases. The two standards converge on **mobile offshore units operating as ships under flag-state jurisdiction** — particularly FLNG and FSRU classification, where the floating asset is simultaneously a moored offshore structure (ISO 19901-7 station-keeping basis) and a SOLAS-registered ship (Ch. II-1 stability margins.
