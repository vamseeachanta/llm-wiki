# Nairobi WRC 2007 — International Convention on the Removal of Wrecks

## Scope

The IMO **Nairobi International Convention on the Removal of Wrecks (2007)** ("WRC" or "Nairobi WRC") is the global treaty providing a **strict-liability and compulsory-insurance framework** for the **location, marking, and removal of wrecks** that pose a hazard to navigation or to the marine environment in a coastal state's **Exclusive Economic Zone**. The convention closes the post-incident cost-allocation gap that the prior IMO compensation stack (**CLC 1992**, **Bunkers 2001**, **HNS 2010**, **LLMC 1996**) did not specifically resolve: those instruments cap or channel **pollution-damage** and **general-claim** liability, but **wreck-removal cost** — frequently the dominant single line item in a major casualty — was either excluded from LLMC under Article 2(1)(d) or fell outside the cargo-specific scope of the pollution conventions, leaving coastal states to bear the cost.

WRC mirrors the CLC architectural pattern: **strict liability** channelled to the **registered shipowner** (Article 10), backed by **compulsory insurance evidenced by a Wreck Removal Certificate** for ships ≥300 GT (Article 12), with **direct action against the insurer** permitted. The **Costa Concordia 2012** casualty — Italy ultimately bore approximately €1.5 billion of direct wreck-removal cost — was the primary political driver that converted the convention's pre-2012 stalled ratification trajectory into the **10-state threshold reached on 14 April 2014**, with entry-into-force following one year later on **14 April 2015**.

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **Adopted:** Nairobi, 2007-05-23 (IMO Diplomatic Conference, document LEG/CONF.16/19).
- **Entry-into-force:** 2015-04-14, after the **10-state ratification threshold** (Article 18) was reached on 14 April 2014, with the standard one-year delay before in-force.
- **States parties:** 60+ as of 2024, including most major flag-states (UK, Germany, Netherlands, Denmark, Bulgaria, Liberia, Marshall Islands, India, Malaysia, Singapore, Japan, Tuvalu) and a growing share of coastal-state ratifiers.
- **Notable non-parties:** the **United States** (covers the same ground domestically via the Wreck Act of 1899, 33 USC §§ 401–415, and 33 CFR Part 64).

## Why the gap existed

- **LLMC 1996** Article 2(1)(d) lists "claims in respect of the raising, removal, destruction or the rendering harmless of a ship which is sunk, wrecked, stranded or abandoned, including anything that is or has been on board such ship" as a claim **subject to limitation** — but Article 18(1) permits states-parties to **reserve the right to exclude** these claims from the LLMC fund, and many major maritime states have done so. The result: in reservation states, wreck-removal claims fall **outside** the LLMC limitation regime; in non-reservation states they are inside but must compete pro-rata against personal-injury and property claims within a single tonnage-banded cap.
- **CLC 1992** is scoped to **persistent oil cargo from tankers**; wreck-removal cost is recoverable only insofar as it falls within "preventive measures" or "reinstatement of impaired environment" — narrow on its face and actively litigated.
- **Bunkers Convention 2001** is scoped to **bunker-fuel pollution from non-tankers**; same narrow recovery posture as CLC for wreck-removal cost.
- **HNS 2010** is scoped to **hazardous-and-noxious-substance cargoes**; HNS not yet in force, and even on entry will not cover the bulk of dry-cargo wreck cases.
- **Net effect prior to WRC**: wreck-removal claims often fell **outside all four IMO compensation pillars**, and coastal states bore the cost. **Costa Concordia 2012** — €1.5 billion+ direct cost absorbed by Italy — converted long-standing IMO Legal Committee discussion into ratification momentum.

## Three-tier scope

WRC applies on a layered geographical and ship-classification basis:

- **Article 1 — geographical scope.** Original WRC scope is the **Exclusive Economic Zone** of states-parties (the area where coastal states have UNCLOS Article 56 jurisdiction over economic resources). **Article 3(2) opt-in** permits a state-party to extend WRC's wreck-removal authority to its **territorial sea** by notifying the IMO Secretary-General — most major ratifying states (UK, Germany, Netherlands, Denmark, India, Malaysia) have made this election, eliminating the EEZ-only gap inside their own waters.
- **Article 5 — wreck-classification triggers.** "Wreck" includes a ship that has **sunk, stranded, broken up, or otherwise become incapable of providing transport service**, as well as **cargo lost from a ship** and **objects that are/were on board** a ship in distress. The classification is hazard-driven: a wreck is in scope only when it poses a hazard (Article 6 criteria) — to navigation, to the marine environment, or to coastal/related interests.
- **Article 6 — locating and marking.** The coastal state may **require the ship-owner to locate and mark the wreck** by appropriate means in accordance with applicable international standards. Costs of locating and marking are recoverable from the owner under Article 10.

## Key Articles

- **Article 9 — Removal of wreck.** Coastal-state may set a **reasonable deadline** for owner-led removal; if the owner fails, the state may **take action itself** and recover cost. The article preserves the owner's right to engage a salvor of choice (subject to safety oversight).
- **Article 10 — Liability of owner.** **Strict liability** for the costs of locating, marking, and removing the wreck, with the standard CLC-style narrow exceptions (act of war, exceptional natural phenomenon of an inevitable and irresistible character, third-party act done with intent to cause damage, and wholly negligent governmental act in maintaining navigational aids).
- **Article 11 — Limitation of liability.** WRC liability is **subject to limitation under any applicable national or international regime — most commonly LLMC 1976 or 1996 Protocol** — where the forum state is party to such an instrument. WRC does not create a standalone wreck-removal liability cap (architectural parallel to Bunkers Convention Article 4).
- **Article 12 — Compulsory insurance and Wreck Removal Certificate.** Ships **≥300 GT** registered in a state-party (and, by Article 12(11) port-state enforcement, ships of any flag calling at a state-party port) must maintain insurance or other financial security covering the cost of locating, marking, and removing the wreck up to the LLMC limit, evidenced by a **Wreck Removal Certificate (WRC certificate)** issued by the flag-state administration. **Direct action against the insurer is permitted** (Article 12(10)).
- **Article 13 — Time limits.** Claims under WRC must be brought within **3 years of the wreck-determination date** under Article 6 and in any event within **6 years of the maritime casualty**.
- **Article 15 — Settlement of disputes.** Disputes between states-parties are referred to the dispute-resolution procedures of **UNCLOS Part XV** (negotiation, mediation, conciliation, ITLOS or Annex VII arbitration as elected).

## Wreck Removal Certificate (WRC certificate)

- **Mandatory** for all ships **≥300 GT** registered in a WRC-state-party (the GT threshold is materially lower than CLC's 2,000-ton cargo trigger and lower than Bunkers' 1,000 GT, reflecting the broader applicability of the wreck-removal hazard).
- **Coverage:** financial guarantee or insurance for the costs of **locating + marking + removing** the wreck up to the **LLMC limit** applicable in the forum state (most commonly the LLMC 1996 Protocol limits as raised by the 2012 Amendments — see [[llmc-1996]](./llmc-1996.md)).
- **Issuance:** flag-state administration. For ships flagged in **non-parties** that nonetheless call at state-party ports, the port-state may require equivalent evidence of cover (Article 12(11)). This port-state enforcement is the practical mechanism by which the convention's reach extends well beyond its formal ratification footprint.
- **Direct action against the insurer** is available (Article 12(10)), preserving the recovery route where the owner is insolvent or non-cooperative.

## Notable enforcement / case law

- **Costa Concordia 2012** (Italy, Giglio) — Carnival Corporation cruise ship grounded and capsized off the island of Giglio. **Pre-WRC-in-force casualty** (WRC entered force 2015), but the **wreck-removal cost — approximately €1.5 billion** absorbed by the operator, owner, and the Italian state across the parbuckling and floating-dry-tow operations — became the textbook case-study for WRC's necessity. The casualty's compensation gap converted IMO Legal Committee ratification momentum into the 10-state threshold reached April 2014.
- **MV Wakashio 2020** (Mauritius, Pointe d'Esny) — Japanese-operated bulk carrier grounded on the reef off Mauritius. **First major application of the WRC framework** post-entry-into-force; operator Mitsui OSK Lines and P&I insurer Britannia covered approximately **USD 36 million in wreck-removal cost**, executed within the WRC liability + WRC certificate architecture. Mauritius is a WRC state-party and had elected the Article 3(2) territorial-sea opt-in.
- **MV Solomon Trader 2019** (Solomon Islands, Rennell Island) — bauxite carrier grounded on the reef during cyclonic conditions. **WRC framework applied** for wreck-removal cost allocation; Solomon Islands engaged the Australian Maritime Safety Authority for technical support. Demonstrated the convention's applicability in capacity-constrained coastal states.
- **MV Ever Given 2021** (Egypt, Suez) — large container ship grounded across the Suez Canal. **Not formally a wreck under WRC** (the vessel was refloated and returned to service); the casualty was salvage rather than wreck-removal. The case-law interaction is the contrast between the **Salvage Convention 1989** (ongoing-rescue, "no cure, no pay" + Article 14 special compensation) and **WRC** (post-incident, cost-allocation for vessels that have become incapable of transport service) — the two instruments occupy adjacent but distinct rungs of the maritime-incident response stack.

- [[salvage-convention-1989]](./salvage-convention-1989.md) — **sibling but distinct scope**: salvage regulates **ongoing-rescue** of vessels still capable of transport service (no cure, no pay; Article 14 special compensation for environmental services). WRC regulates **post-incident cost-allocation for wrecks** — vessels that have already become incapable of transport service. The Ever Given 2021 sequence illustrated the contrast; the Costa Concordia 2012 sequence engaged the salvage layer first (Smit + Titan parbuckling) and then the wreck-removal layer (controlled refloat + tow).
- [[unclos-1982]](./unclos-1982.md) — **Article 56** (EEZ jurisdiction over economic resources) is the source-of-authority foundation for WRC's geographical scope; **Article 221** (state's right to take measures beyond the territorial sea to protect coastline/related interests after a casualty) is the customary-law backdrop that WRC codifies into a treaty regime.

**US-domestic parallel**

- [[opa-90]](./opa-90.md) — US is **not** a party to WRC; the US-domestic equivalent for wreck-removal authority is the **Wreck Act of 1899** (33 USC §§ 401–415) and the implementing regulations at **33 CFR Part 64**, which empower the US Coast Guard to require removal of wrecks obstructing navigable waters and to recover cost from the owner. OPA-90 covers the oil-pollution portion of any tanker wreck under US jurisdiction; the Wreck Act covers the wreck-removal portion.
