# Hague-Visby Rules — Bills of Lading Liability Convention

## Scope

The Hague-Visby Rules are the dominant carriage-of-goods-by-sea liability regime governing the carrier's responsibilities under bills of lading for goods carried by sea. They allocate risk between cargo-owner and carrier through a statutory minimum-and-maximum framework: the carrier owes a non-delegable "due diligence" duty to make the ship seaworthy and to properly handle cargo (Article III), is excused from liability under 17 enumerated perils (the "Hague exceptions" — Article IV.2), and benefits from a package-limitation cap re-denominated in IMF Special Drawing Rights by the 1979 SDR Protocol (Article IV.5).

The regime governs roughly 80% of world trade by tonnage. It is mandatory under English law (UK Carriage of Goods by Sea Act 1971), throughout the EU, in Japan, Korea, and most Commonwealth jurisdictions. The United States is the most significant outlier — it is party to the Hague Rules 1924 only, implemented domestically by COGSA 1936, and has ratified neither the Visby Protocol nor the SDR Protocol. This creates the well-known "Hague vs. Hague-Visby" jurisdictional split that drives forum-selection and choice-of-law disputes in cargo claims.

This page is pinned to the consolidated composite — Hague 1924 + Visby Protocol 1968 + SDR Protocol 1979 — and is the citation target for `code_id: hague-visby-rules`.

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **Hague Rules 1924** — adopted at Brussels, 1924-08-25, under the auspices of the Comité Maritime International (CMI); entered into force **1931-06-02**. Original package limit set at £100 sterling (gold value).
- **Visby Protocol 1968** — adopted at Brussels, 1968-02-23; entered into force **1977-06-23**. Modernized the package-limitation regime (then 10,000 Poincaré francs per package or 30 francs per kilo, whichever is higher) and added the container provision at Article IV.5(c).
- **SDR Protocol 1979** — adopted at Brussels, 1979-12-21; entered into force **1984-02-14**. Re-denominated the Visby package-limit in IMF Special Drawing Rights (666.67 SDR per package or unit, or 2 SDR per kilogram, whichever is higher) replacing the gold-franc unit of account.
- **Drafting body**: CMI (Comité Maritime International), the private international maritime-law body founded 1897. Subsequent ratification was carried out by IMO member states bilaterally and through national implementing legislation.
- **Modernization**: a 1992 effort within UNCITRAL led to the Rotterdam Rules 2008 (UN Convention on Contracts for the International Carriage of Goods Wholly or Partly by Sea). Rotterdam has been ratified by approximately 5 states and is **not yet in force**; Hague-Visby therefore remains the live regime for the foreseeable horizon.

## Three-layer composite

Most parties treat the three Brussels instruments as a single composite regime. National implementing legislation typically references all three together (e.g., UK COGSA 1971 schedules them as the "Hague-Visby Rules").

| Component        | Year | Scope                                                                                              |
|------------------|------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Hague Rules      | 1924 | Original carrier-liability regime; package limit £100 / £200 sterling (gold value)                 |
| Visby Protocol   | 1968 | Modernized package limit (then 666.67 SDR + per-kg alternate); container provision (Art. IV.5(c))  |
| SDR Protocol     | 1979 | Re-denominated limit in IMF Special Drawing Rights instead of gold-franc value                     |

## Key Articles

Article *titles only* — full text lives at the publisher catalog (link below).

- **Article I — Definitions.** "Carrier", "contract of carriage", "goods", "ship", "carriage of goods" — the boundary terms that determine whether the regime applies.
- **Article II — Responsibilities and Liabilities.** Statement that, subject to Article VI, the carrier is bound by the responsibilities and entitled to the rights set out in Articles III and IV.
- **Article III — Risks attached to the carriage of goods.** Carrier's affirmative due-diligence obligations to make the ship seaworthy, properly man/equip/supply, and to properly load, handle, stow, carry, keep, care for, and discharge the goods. Article III(6) imposes the **one-year suit-or-bar** limitation period that runs from delivery (or date when the goods should have been delivered).
- **Article IV — Rights and Immunities.** Hosts the 17 enumerated Hague exceptions at Article IV(2)(a)-(q), and the package-limitation cap at Article IV(5) (as modified by Visby and SDR Protocols).
- **Article V — Surrender of rights / increase of obligations.** Carrier may surrender rights or increase responsibilities, but cannot lessen liabilities below the Article III/IV minimum (the regime is one-way mandatory in favour of the cargo interest).
- **Article VI — Ad-hoc agreement.** Carrier and shipper free to contract on any terms whatsoever for non-ordinary commercial shipments, where no bill of lading is issued and the terms are embodied in a non-negotiable receipt.
- **Article VII — Period of carriage.** Tackle-to-tackle scope: the regime governs from the time the goods are loaded on the ship to the time they are discharged. Pre-loading and post-discharge periods are outside the regime (one of the gaps Rotterdam Rules attempted to close).
- **Article IX — Convention's interplay with national laws.** Reservations regarding monetary units and conversion to national currency.

## Carrier exceptions (the 17 "Hague exceptions" — Article IV.2)

The 17 enumerated exceptions are the operational core of the carrier's defence. A representative selection of the most-cited heads:

- **Act of God** + perils, dangers, and accidents of the sea or other navigable waters
- **Act of war** + acts of public enemies
- **Quarantine restrictions**
- **Strike** + lockouts or stoppages of labour from whatever cause, partial or general
- **Saving / attempting to save** life or property at sea
- **Inherent vice** of the goods (latent defect or inherent quality / wastage in bulk or weight)
- **Insufficient packing** / insufficiency or inadequacy of marks
- **Latent defects** not discoverable by due diligence
- **Article IV(2)(q) — "any other cause"** without actual fault or privity of the carrier or its agents (the residual catch-all; carrier bears burden of proof)

The Article IV(2)(a) "act, neglect, or default of the master, mariner, pilot, or servants of the carrier in the navigation or in the management of the ship" — the **navigational fault exception** — is the most commercially significant and the most criticized; its abolition is one of the headline differences in the Rotterdam Rules.

## US Hague-only carve-out

The United States ratified the Hague Rules 1924 but never the Visby Protocol or the SDR Protocol. The **US Carriage of Goods by Sea Act 1936 (COGSA)** implements Hague-only domestically and prescribes a US$500 per package limitation (statutory, not SDR-indexed). Outbound and inbound US shipments under bills of lading routinely trigger the Hague-vs.-Hague-Visby choice-of-law contest, with US federal admiralty jurisdiction generally applying COGSA via paramount-clause incorporation.

The Rotterdam Rules 2008, signed by the US in 2009, was the international harmonization attempt — but ratification has stalled at approximately 5 states and the convention is not in force.

## Notable case law

- **The Muncaster Castle [1961] AC 807** — UK House of Lords. Confirmed that the Article III(1) due-diligence obligation to make the ship seaworthy is **non-delegable**: the carrier remains liable for unseaworthiness caused by independent contractors (here, ship-repair-yard fitters). The leading authority on the scope of the due-diligence duty.
- **The Eurasian Dream [2002] 1 Lloyd's Rep 719** — UK Commercial Court. Carrier identity and the application of Hague-Visby to charterer-issued bills; cross-references the charterparty doctrine page.
- **Volcafe Ltd v Compania Sud Americana de Vapores SA [2018] UKSC 61** — UK Supreme Court. Burden of proof under the Article IV(2)(m) inherent-vice exception: carrier bears the legal burden of proving that loss arose from inherent vice and that the carrier exercised due care. Realigned the English law position with the structure of Article III/IV.
- **The Erechthion [1987] 2 Lloyd's Rep 180** — UK Commercial Court. Package-limitation under the Visby Protocol; on the construction of "package or unit" for containerized cargo under Article IV.5(c).
