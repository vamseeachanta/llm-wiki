# OPA 90 — US Oil Pollution Act of 1990

## Scope

US federal statute providing the comprehensive regulatory framework for oil-spill prevention, response, liability, and compensation in US waters and on the US Outer Continental Shelf. Enacted in direct response to the Exxon Valdez 1989 spill in Prince William Sound, Alaska. OPA-90 establishes:

- The **Oil Spill Liability Trust Fund (OSLTF)** funded by an 8¢/barrel tax (suspended at intervals; re-imposed 2017 through 2025);
- **Strict liability** for designated "responsible parties" with caps that fall away for gross negligence, regulatory violation, or failure to report;
- **Certificate of Financial Responsibility (COFR)** requirements for vessels >300 GT;
- A **double-hull mandate** for tankers calling at US ports (phased through 2015);
- **Area Contingency Plans**, **Vessel Response Plans**, and certified **Oil Spill Response Organizations (OSROs)**.

## Adoption

- **Signed:** 1990-08-18 by President George H.W. Bush as Public Law 101-380.
- **Driver:** Exxon Valdez grounding (1989-03-24, Prince William Sound, AK) — the prior CLC framework was not in force in the US, leaving a regulatory gap that OPA-90 closed.
- **Codification:** 33 U.S.C. § 2701-2762.
- **2010 amendments:** Following Deepwater Horizon (Macondo, Gulf of Mexico, 2010-04-20), Congress and BSEE/BOEM strengthened liability caps, financial-responsibility thresholds, and offshore-platform safety regulations (the SEMS rulemaking and Well Control Rule operationalized many of these via 30 CFR 250 Subparts O & S; OPA-90's Title VI was the statutory hook).
- **2018 adjustments:** BSEE rulemaking refinements to financial-responsibility and well-control regulatory regime under OPA-90's Title VI authority.

## Key Titles + Subtitles

- **Title I — Oil Pollution Liability + Compensation** (33 U.S.C. § 2701-2720). Defines "responsible party," strict-liability standard, liability caps and their loss conditions, OSLTF claim procedures, and the natural-resource-damage assessment regime.
- **Title II — Conforming Amendments.** Technical updates to prior statutes (Federal Water Pollution Control Act, Intervention on the High Seas Act, Deepwater Port Act) to align them with Title I.
- **Title III — International Oil Pollution Prevention + Removal.** US Coast Guard responsibilities for international cooperation and the OPRC 1990 implementing authority.
- **Title IV — Prevention + Removal** (33 U.S.C. § 2731-2738). Federal removal authority, the National Response System, and spill-management organization requirements.
- **Title V — Prince William Sound Provisions.** Alaska-specific provisions (Regional Citizens' Advisory Councils, tanker-escort requirements, navigation-safety measures).
- **Title VI — Miscellaneous.** Outer Continental Shelf platform provisions; this is the statutory hook subsequently amended by the 2010 BSEE/BOEM rulemaking strengthening offshore safety post-Macondo.
- **Title VII — Oil Spill Trust Fund + Coast Guard Operations.** OSLTF financing mechanism (8¢/barrel tax) and Coast Guard appropriations.

## Liability framework

- **Strict liability** of the "responsible party" — vessel owner/operator, offshore-facility owner/operator, or onshore-facility owner/operator. No proof of fault required for the underlying liability.
- **Liability caps** depend on vessel type and gross tonnage (e.g., tank vessels, non-tank vessels, offshore facilities, deepwater ports each have distinct cap formulas, periodically inflation-adjusted by USCG rulemaking). **Caps fall away entirely** if the responsible party is grossly negligent, willfully misconducts, violates an applicable federal safety/construction/operating regulation, or fails to report the incident or cooperate with removal.
- **Oil Spill Liability Trust Fund (OSLTF)** pays damages exceeding caps or where the responsible party is unable, unknown, or insolvent. Funded by the 8¢/barrel tax (suspended at intervals; re-imposed 2017 through 2025). Administered by USCG's National Pollution Funds Center (NPFC).
- **COFR (Certificate of Financial Responsibility)** is required for vessels over 300 gross tons operating in US waters; demonstrates the responsible party's financial capacity to meet its OPA-90 liability up to the applicable cap.

## Key incidents

- **Exxon Valdez (1989)** — Prince William Sound, AK. The driver incident: ~11M gallons of Alaska North Slope crude released after grounding on Bligh Reef. Pre-OPA-90 liability framework proved inadequate; Congress enacted OPA-90 16 months later.
- **Deepwater Horizon / Macondo (2010)** — US Gulf of Mexico OCS. Drove the 2010-2015 BSEE/BOEM rulemaking strengthening (SEMS regulation under 30 CFR 250 Subpart S; Well Control Rule under Subpart O; API RP 75 incorporated by reference). OPA-90 Title VI was the statutory authority for the OCS-specific portions of that rulemaking.
- **American Trader (1990)** — Huntington Beach, CA. Early post-OPA-90 spill that informed early implementing-regulation development.
- **Tampa Bay (1993)** — Bouchard Barge B-155 incident; tested OPA-90's response-organization framework in a constrained-waterway context.
