# STCW Convention 1978/2010 — Standards of Training, Certification, and Watchkeeping for Seafarers

## Scope

STCW is the IMO convention establishing **minimum standards for the training, certification, watchkeeping, and qualification of seafarers** serving on commercial merchant ships engaged in international voyages. It mandates that flag states issue **Certificates of Competency (CoC)** and **Endorsements** following a standardized curriculum, assessment regime, and minimum sea-time, and that those certificates be recognized internationally subject to flag-state verification. STCW applies to officers and ratings across all major rank-tracks — masters, deck officers, engineering officers, electro-technical officers, GMDSS radio operators, and able seafarers — with additional special-training overlays for tanker crews, LNG-carrier crews, passenger-ship crews, and personnel operating in polar waters.

STCW is widely cited as **"the fourth pillar"** of international maritime regulation alongside SOLAS (safety of life at sea), MARPOL (pollution prevention), and MLC 2006 (labor rights). Under the more recent four-pillar-with-MLC framing, STCW is sometimes folded as MLC's training-side companion: STCW governs *what a seafarer is trained and qualified to do*; MLC governs *the conditions under which they work*. Together they bookend the seafarer lifecycle from pre-service training through ongoing employment. STCW is the operational complement to **SOLAS Ch. V** (navigation rules and bridge procedures), to **SOLAS Ch. IV / GMDSS** (radio competency), and to the **Polar Code** (additional polar-waters training).

This page is pinned to the **consolidated STCW 1978 + 1995 Amendments + 2010 Manila Amendments** edition and to the post-Manila amendment cohort recorded in `amendment_status`.

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **STCW 1978 — Adopted:** London, 1978-07-07, at the IMO International Conference on Training and Certification of Seafarers.
- **STCW 1978 — Entry-into-force:** 1984-04-28.
- **1995 Amendments:** comprehensive overhaul; entered into force 1997-02-01. Made the STCW Code mandatory and introduced the flag-state-validation regime now known as the **IMO Whitelist**.
- **2010 Manila Amendments:** most-recent comprehensive overhaul; adopted at a Diplomatic Conference in Manila and entered into force 2012-01-01, with a transitional period running through 2017.
- **States parties:** 165+ flag states, covering substantially the entire commercial fleet engaged in international trade.

## Three-pillar instrument structure

STCW is structured as three interlocking documents — the convention plus a two-part code — analogous to the MLC Articles + Regulations + Code architecture:

- **STCW Convention** — the treaty itself; high-level requirements and framework, ratified by states-parties.
- **STCW Code Part A (mandatory)** — technical-level training and assessment standards; states-parties must give effect to Part A in national law.
- **STCW Code Part B (recommended)** — implementation guidance; non-mandatory recommended practice for flag-state administrations and training providers.

## Key Chapters / Sections

- **Chapter I** — General provisions: definitions, certification framework, IMO Whitelist mechanism, recognition of foreign certificates.
- **Chapter II** — Master and deck department: certification and training standards for masters, chief mates, officers in charge of a navigational watch, and deck ratings.
- **Chapter III** — Engineering department: certification and training standards for chief engineers, second engineers, officers in charge of an engineering watch, and engine-room ratings; electro-technical officers and ratings.
- **Chapter IV** — Radio communications and radio personnel: GMDSS radio operator certification.
- **Chapter V** — Special training requirements: tanker crews (oil, chemical, liquefied gas), LNG carrier personnel, passenger-ship personnel, and personnel operating ships in polar waters under the Polar Code.
- **Chapter VI** — Emergency, occupational safety, security, medical care, and survival functions: basic safety training, advanced fire-fighting, medical care, security awareness, ship security officer.
- **Chapter VII** — Alternative certification: function-based pathways permitting non-traditional rank structures where flag-state administrations can demonstrate equivalent competency.
- **Chapter VIII** — Watchkeeping: bridge watchkeeping standards, engine-room watchkeeping standards, hours of rest, fitness for duty.

## IMO Whitelist

The **IMO Whitelist** is the mechanism by which the IMO Maritime Safety Committee confirms that a state-party's implementation of STCW gives "full and complete effect" to the convention. States not on the Whitelist see their Certificates of Competency not recognized by other flag states for endorsement purposes, which in practice excludes their seafarers from the international labor market. As of 2024, approximately 150 states are on the Whitelist. Periodic re-evaluation under the **STCW Independent Evaluation** procedure is required, with submission of training-system documentation to IMO for review.

## Notable enforcement / incidents

- **Costa Concordia (2012, Italy)** — the captain's deviation from passage plan and delayed evacuation order led to revocation of his STCW Certificate of Competency and was a driver for reinforcement of **Bridge Team Management (BTM)** and **Bridge Resource Management** training in subsequent MSC amendment cycles.
- **Sewol (2014, South Korea)** — crew abandonment during the capsize, attributed in part to inadequate emergency-response training, drove regional and amendment-cycle changes around passenger-ship crew preparedness and emergency-leadership training under STCW Chapter V and Chapter VI.
- **Marine Electric (1983, US Atlantic Coast)** — pre-STCW-effective-date sinking; cited historically for demonstrating the need for mandatory survival-craft training and cold-water survival competency, which were strengthened in the 1995 and 2010 cycles.

## Cross-references

**Sibling pillars** *(the "four pillars" of international maritime regulation)*

- [solas-1974](./solas-1974.md) — IMO sibling; STCW provides the human-element complement to SOLAS Ch. V (navigation rules), Ch. III (life-saving appliances), and Ch. IV (GMDSS radiocommunications). SOLAS sets what the ship and its equipment must be; STCW sets who is qualified to operate them.
- [marpol-73-78](./marpol-73-78.md) — IMO sibling; STCW Chapter V special-training requirements for tanker crews implement the human-element side of MARPOL Annexes I–II pollution-prevention compliance.
- [mlc-2006](./mlc-2006.md) — ILO sibling; STCW = training and certification, MLC = labor and working conditions. Together they cover the seafarer's full lifecycle from pre-service training through working conditions. The 2010 Manila hours-of-rest provisions were explicitly aligned with MLC 2006 Title 2 hours-of-rest standards.

**Umbrella treaty**

- [unclos-1982](./unclos-1982.md) — UNCLOS supplies the law-of-the-sea umbrella; flag-state jurisdiction (UNCLOS Articles 91, 92, 94) provides the constitutional basis for STCW flag-state implementation and certificate issuance.

**Related sub-regimes and companion instruments**

- **IMO Whitelist** — administrative validation regime under STCW Chapter I; states not on the Whitelist see their CoCs not recognized internationally.
- **STCW-F 1995** — sister IMO convention covering training and certification of fishing-vessel personnel, which are not covered by STCW proper.
- **GMDSS** — Global Maritime Distress and Safety System; STCW Chapter IV governs the training and certification of GMDSS radio operators, complementing SOLAS Ch. IV equipment-carriage requirements.
- **Polar Code** — IMO Polar Code (in force 2017); STCW Chapter V Polar-waters training requirements added by the 2010 Manila Amendments and subsequent cycles operationalize the Polar Code human-element provisions.
- **ECDIS** — Electronic Chart Display and Information System; STCW Chapter II competency requirements for ECDIS operation were added by the 2010 Manila Amendments.
