# STCW Convention 1978/2010 — Standards of Training, Certification, and Watchkeeping for Seafarers

## Scope

STCW is the IMO convention establishing **minimum standards for the training, certification, watchkeeping, and qualification of seafarers** serving on commercial merchant ships engaged in international voyages. It mandates that flag states issue **Certificates of Competency (CoC)** and **Endorsements** following a standardized curriculum, assessment regime, and minimum sea-time, and that those certificates be recognized internationally subject to flag-state verification. STCW applies to officers and ratings across all major rank-tracks — masters, deck officers, engineering officers, electro-technical officers, GMDSS radio operators, and able seafarers — with additional special-training overlays for tanker crews, LNG-carrier crews, passenger-ship crews, and personnel operating in polar waters.

STCW is widely cited as **"the fourth pillar"** of international maritime regulation alongside SOLAS (safety of life at sea), MARPOL (pollution prevention), and MLC 2006 (labor rights). Under the more recent four-pillar-with-MLC framing, STCW is sometimes folded as MLC's training-side companion: STCW governs *what a seafarer is trained and qualified to do*; MLC governs *the conditions under which they work*. Together they bookend the seafarer lifecycle from pre-service training through ongoing employment. STCW is the operational complement to **SOLAS Ch. V** (navigation rules and bridge procedures), to **SOLAS Ch. IV / GMDSS** (radio competency), and to the **Polar Code** (additional polar-waters training).

This page is pinned to the **consolidated STCW 1978 + 1995 Amendments + 2010 Manila Amendments** edition and to the post-Manila amendment cohort recorded in `amendment_status`.

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **STCW 1978 — Adopted:** London, 1978-07-07, at the IMO International Conference on Training and Certification of Seafarers.
- **STCW 1978 — Entry-into-force:** 1984-04-28.
- **1995 Amendments:** comprehensive overhaul; entered into force 1997-02-01. Made the STCW Code mandatory and introduced the flag-state-validation regime now known as the **IMO Whitelist**.
- **2010 Manila Amendments:** most-recent comprehensive overhaul; adopted at a Diplomatic Conference in Manila and entered into force 2012-01-01, with a transitional period running through 2017.
- **States parties:** 165+ flag states, covering substantially the entire commercial fleet engaged in international trade.

## Three-pillar instrument structure

STCW is structured as three interlocking documents — the convention plus a two-part code — analogous to the MLC Articles + Regulations + Code architecture:

- **STCW Convention** — the treaty itself; high-level requirements and framework, ratified by states-parties.
- **STCW Code Part A (mandatory)** — technical-level training and assessment standards; states-parties must give effect to Part A in national law.
- **STCW Code Part B (recommended)** — implementation guidance; non-mandatory recommended practice for flag-state administrations and training providers.

## Eight-Chapter architecture — multi-criteria comparison

STCW splits the seafarer-competency space into eight chapters. Each chapter targets a distinct rank/function cohort, applies a distinct certification regime, and feeds a distinct enforcement loop. The matrix below is the page-level navigator: rank cohort, certification artifact, sea-time + age threshold, refresher cadence, port-state-control inspection focus.

| Chapter | Cohort scope | Certification artifact | Indicative sea-time / age | Refresher / revalidation cadence | PSC inspection focus |
|---------|--------------|------------------------|---------------------------|----------------------------------|---------------------|
| **I — General** | All seafarers | Convention-wide framework; Whitelist; recognition endorsements | n/a (umbrella) | n/a | Document-set verification: CoC, medical fitness, endorsements, Whitelist status of issuing state |
| **II — Master + deck** | Master, chief mate, OOW (deck), deck rating | CoC II/1 (OOW), II/2 (chief mate / master ≥3,000 GT or near-coastal), II/3 (near-coastal master/OOW <500 GT), II/4 (deck rating), II/5 (AB deck) | OOW: 12 months bridge sea-time + age 18+; Master ≥3,000 GT: 36 months as OOW or 24 as chief mate | 5-year revalidation; ECDIS + BRM refresher every 5 years | Bridge watchkeeping arrangements; voyage planning under Reg. 34; ECDIS familiarization; lookout posting |
| **III — Engineering** | Chief engineer, second engineer, EOOW, ETO, engine-room rating | CoC III/1 (EOOW), III/2 (chief eng / second eng ≥3,000 kW), III/3 (chief eng / second eng <3,000 kW), III/4 (engine-room rating), III/5 (AB engine), III/6 (ETO), III/7 (electro-technical rating) | EOOW: 12 months engine-room sea-time + age 18+; Chief Eng ≥3,000 kW: 36 months as EOOW | 5-year revalidation; ERM refresher every 5 years | UMS-watch arrangements; engine-room resource management; electrical-installation competency for ETO |
| **IV — Radio (GMDSS)** | GMDSS Radio Operator | GOC (General Operator's Certificate) or ROC (Restricted) | Function-specific examinations under ITU Radio Regulations | 5-year revalidation tied to flag-state radio licensing | GMDSS log; equipment-test cadence; DSC-watchkeeping; Inmarsat/Iridium operational status |
| **V — Special training** | Tanker crews (oil/chem/gas), LNG carrier, passenger ship, polar waters | Tanker Familiarization + Advanced (V/1-1, V/1-2); Pax-ship crowd/crisis (V/2); Polar Code Basic + Advanced (V/4) | Pre-assignment Basic; sea-time-tied Advanced (e.g., 3 months on tankers for Advanced V/1-1) | Refresher every 5 years; Polar Code Advanced ties to MARPOL Annex I and Polar Code Part I-A | Tanker endorsement on CoC; pax-ship crowd/crisis-management certificates; Polar Code training records |
| **VI — Emergency / safety / security / medical** | All seafarers (basic), security-designated personnel (SSO, advanced) | Basic Safety Training (BST); Advanced Fire-Fighting; Medical First Aid + Medical Care; Proficiency in Survival Craft + Rescue Boats; Security Awareness; Designated Security Duties; Ship Security Officer | BST: pre-assignment; SSO: function-tied with sea-time | Refresher every 5 years (BST cohort) | BST records; SSO endorsement under [isps-code](../concepts/isps-code.md); muster-list familiarization |
| **VII — Alternative certification** | Function-based crews (non-traditional rank structure) | Equivalence-of-competency certificate where flag-state demonstrates Whitelist-compatible function coverage | Function-specific | Function-specific | Equivalence audit; Whitelist alignment |
| **VIII — Watchkeeping** | All deck + engineering watchkeepers | Hours-of-rest records; fitness-for-duty; UMS-watch arrangements | Continuous (10 hours of rest per 24-hour period; 77 hours per 7-day period) | Continuous (records subject to PSC inspection) | Hours-of-rest records cross-checked against MLC Title 2; alcohol limit 0.05% BAC; fatigue-risk-management evidence |

The chapter set is **rank-track-additive**: a tanker chief mate carries a Ch. II CoC + Ch. VI BST/AFF/MED + Ch. V/1-1 + Ch. V/1-2 advanced-tanker + Ch. VIII hours-of-rest record concurrently. Each chapter generates an independent PSC inspection trail — see [paris-mou](./paris-mou.md), [tokyo-mou](./tokyo-mou.md), and [port-state-control](../concepts/port-state-control.md) for the inspection-side enforcement loop.

## Major amendment history — incident-then-amendment timeline

STCW evolves through a **casualty-then-amendment** loop closely paralleling SOLAS, but with the human-element rather than ship-equipment lens. Each major amendment cycle has named-incident drivers, and most cycles also reflect technological inflection (radar/ARPA, ECDIS, cybersecurity, autonomous-systems prep).

| Year | Amendment cohort | Incident drivers | Subject matter |
|------|------------------|------------------|----------------|
| **1978** | Original convention | *Marine Electric* 1983 (post-hoc reinforcement); pre-existing fragmented national CoC regimes | First IMO crew-training convention; in force 1984-04-28; baseline for masters, deck officers, engineering officers, GMDSS radio |
| **1995** | Comprehensive overhaul | *Exxon Valdez* 1989 (master-fitness-for-duty); *Scandinavian Star* 1990 (passenger-ship crew preparedness) | Made STCW Code mandatory; introduced flag-state-validation regime (**IMO Whitelist**); STCW Independent Evaluation procedure; Code Part A (mandatory) + Part B (recommended) split |
| **2010 (Manila)** | Most-recent comprehensive overhaul | [costa-concordia-2012](../entities/) (post-effective amplification); *Sewol* 2014 (post-effective ratification of crowd-crisis); ECDIS-related groundings (*Ovit* 2013, *CFL Performer* 2008) | Polar-waters training; ECDIS competency; **Bridge Resource Management (BRM)** + **Engine Room Resource Management (ERM)**; refresher-training requirements; alcohol limits (0.05% BAC); hours-of-rest aligned with MLC 2006 Title 2; ETO + electro-technical rating new functions; security-related training (SSO, security awareness, designated security duties) under post-ISPS expansion |
| **2014–2016** | Polar Code training | Polar-waters traffic increase + Northern Sea Route opening | STCW Ch. V/4 added; basic + advanced Polar Code training requirements; alignment with [Polar Code](../) (mandatory under SOLAS Ch. XIV + MARPOL from 2017-01-01) |
| **2016–2017** | Cyber-risk + e-navigation | Maersk NotPetya 2017 (post-hoc reinforcement); MSC.428(98) maritime cyber-risk-management resolution | Cybersecurity awareness training added to BRM/ERM; integration with ISM Code SMS cyber-risk obligations |
| **2017–2020** | IGF Code training | LNG-fuelled fleet expansion (CMA CGM, Maersk dual-fuel orders) | STCW Ch. V/3 — basic + advanced training for personnel on ships subject to the IGF Code (LNG- + methanol-fuelled vessels); aligns with [SOLAS](./solas-1974.md) Ch. II-1 Part G |
| **2020–2021** | COVID-19 crew-change crisis | Pandemic supply-chain crew-change collapse (~400,000 seafarers stranded at peak, Sept 2020) | IMO/ILO joint guidance on STCW certificate-extension exemptions; flag-state issuance of dispensations under STCW Reg. | **2022–2025** | MASS + e-certificate + ECDIS-2 + cybersecurity-2 | Autonomous-systems trial regulatory gap; e-Cert globalization; *X-Press Pearl* 2021 (cargo-handling + emergency response) | 2025 STCW amendments cohort under MSC + HTW work programme: comprehensive update for Maritime Autonomous Surface Ships (MASS) crew competencies; e-Certificate framework (electronic CoCs harmonized across flag states); ECDIS-2 (anomaly-detection + cybersecurity-hardened ECDIS); refreshed BRM/ERM under cyber-risk lens |

## IMO Whitelist + STCW-Audit + flag-state enforcement

The **IMO Whitelist** is the mechanism by which the IMO Maritime Safety Committee confirms that a state-party's implementation of STCW gives "full and complete effect" to the convention. States not on the Whitelist see their Certificates of Competency not recognized by other flag states for endorsement purposes, which in practice excludes their seafarers from the international labor market. As of 2024, approximately 150 states are on the Whitelist. Periodic re-evaluation under the **STCW Independent Evaluation** procedure is required, with submission of training-system documentation to IMO for review.

Three interlocking enforcement layers sit on top of the Whitelist:

- **Flag-state primary issuance.** Flag states issue CoCs and endorsements under their own national legislation giving effect to STCW + STCW Code Part A. Flag states maintain a **Quality Standards System (QSS)** under STCW Reg. I/8, audited by independent evaluators on a 5-year cycle.
- **STCW Independent Evaluation + IMO Audit Scheme (IMSAS).** Whitelist re-evaluation occurs on a 5-year cycle. Since 2016, the **IMO Member State Audit Scheme (IMSAS)** has been mandatory for all Member States and audits flag-state implementation of STCW alongside SOLAS, MARPOL, Load Lines, COLREGs, and Tonnage 1969.
- **Port-state-control verification.** PSC inspection regimes (Paris MoU, Tokyo MoU, USCG, Caribbean MoU, etc.) verify on visiting foreign-flag vessels that (a) seafarers hold valid CoCs from a Whitelisted flag state, (b) the issuing state has an endorsement-recognition agreement with the flag of registry, and (c) the watchkeeping arrangements aboard match Ch. VIII hours-of-rest evidence. PSC may detain vessels for STCW deficiencies — see [paris-mou](./paris-mou.md), [tokyo-mou](./tokyo-mou.md).

## Worked-incident anchors

STCW evolves substantially through a casualty-then-amendment loop on the human-element side. The most-cited incidents in this loop:

### Bridge Resource Management driver

- **Costa Concordia 2012, Italy.** The captain's deviation from the approved passage plan, delayed evacuation order, and breakdown of bridge-team challenge-and-response cascaded into 32 fatalities. Italian criminal proceedings convicted the master and revoked his STCW Certificate of Competency. The casualty was the principal driver for the **Bridge Team Management (BTM) and Bridge Resource Management (BRM)** training reinforcement amplifying the 2010 Manila Amendments package; subsequent MSC amendment cycles tightened pre-departure briefing, master-pilot interface, and challenge-and-response doctrine. The case sits at the intersection of STCW Ch. II (deck competency), Ch. V (passenger-ship crew preparedness), and Ch. VIII (watchkeeping). See doctrinal companion [solas-1974](./solas-1974.md) for the parallel SOLAS Ch. III/Ch. V loop.

### Passenger-ship crew preparedness driver

- **MV Sewol 2014, South Korea.** The Republic of Korea ferry capsize with 304 fatalities (mostly schoolchildren) involved master/officer competency failures and a crew-training deficit on emergency-response, abandon-ship, and crowd-management procedures. The crew abandoned the vessel before passenger evacuation — directly contradicting the STCW Ch. V/2 crowd-crisis-management standards. Drove regional and amendment-cycle changes around passenger-ship crew preparedness, emergency-leadership training, and **mandatory Crowd Management + Crisis Management certificates** for personnel with safety duties on passenger ships under STCW Reg. V/2. The case is also a load-bearing study for [solas-1974](./solas-1974.md) Ch. II-1 stability-data integrity (illegal modification + cargo overload + ballast inadequacy).

### Survival-training driver

- **Marine Electric 1983, US Atlantic Coast.** Pre-STCW-effective-date sinking of a US-flag bulk carrier off the coast of Virginia in winter conditions; 31 of 34 crew died largely from cold-water exposure, not from drowning. The casualty exposed the absence of mandatory survival-craft training, cold-water immersion-suit competency, and abandon-ship drilling. Cited historically as a driver for the **Proficiency in Survival Craft + Rescue Boats (PSCRB)** standard later codified at STCW Ch. VI/2 and strengthened in the 1995 and 2010 cycles. The casualty also drove US-flag survival-craft regulation (46 CFR Subchapter W) ahead of international consensus.

### COVID-19 crew-change crisis driver (2020–2021)

- **2020-2021 COVID-19 crew-change crisis.** At the September-2020 peak an estimated 400,000 seafarers were stranded aboard or unable to embark due to pandemic border restrictions. Flag-state issuance of **STCW certificate-extension exemptions** under Reg. I/8 — typically time-limited 3-month rolling extensions on expired CoCs and medical fitness — became near-universal. The crisis surfaced systemic tension between STCW certificate-cycle rigidity and MLC 2006 Title 2 maximum-service-on-board limits (11 months); the IMO/ILO Joint Tripartite Working Group recommendations and ITF-IMO 12-step crew-change protocol now form the durable post-pandemic procedure. Designation of seafarers as **"key workers"** by 60+ states was the principal regulatory unlock. The crisis is the most consequential test of the STCW + MLC integration since MLC entered into force in 2013.

### Incident-response training driver

- **Nord Stream 2022, Baltic Sea.** Subsea pipeline sabotage incident. Although not a vessel-casualty in the SOLAS sense, the incident reinforced the importance of **incident-response training** for masters and officers operating in the immediate vicinity (debris reporting, navigational-warning compliance, coordination with coastal-state authorities). Cybersecurity-awareness amplification under the post-2017 MSC.428(98) cyber-risk-management resolution finds part of its operational test in the Nord Stream + adjacent Baltic incidents (sensor-spoofing, AIS-anomaly detection). The training-side response feeds the 2022–2025 cyber-risk-2 amendment cohort.

## STCW + MLC 2006 integration — hours-of-rest doctrine

The 2010 Manila Amendments explicitly aligned STCW Reg. VIII/1 hours-of-rest provisions with [mlc-2006](./mlc-2006.md) Title 2 Regulation 2.3 — this is the single most operationally-loaded integration between the two pillars. The two instruments now run on a coordinated envelope:

| Provision | STCW Reg. VIII/1 (watchkeeping) | MLC A2.3 (working conditions) |
|-----------|--------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Minimum rest in any 24-hour period | 10 hours | 10 hours |
| Minimum rest in any 7-day period | 77 hours | 77 hours |
| Maximum hours of rest split | 2 periods (one ≥6 hours) | 2 periods (one ≥6 hours) |
| Maximum hours of work alternative | n/a (rest-based) | 14 hours per 24; 72 hours per 7-day (alternative) |
| Exception scope | Drills + emergency situations | Drills + emergency situations |
| Records | Records of hours of rest (Reg. VIII/1) | Records of hours of work or rest (A2.3.10) |
| Enforcement layer | STCW + flag-state QSS + PSC | MLC + flag-state DMLC + PSC |

The doctrinal split between the two pillars is **competency vs working conditions**: STCW asks "is the watchkeeper trained, certified, and rested enough to stand a competent watch?", MLC asks "are the seafarer's labor conditions decent, including rest hours that protect them from overwork?" Both instruments converge on the same rest-hours envelope but enforce it through different administrative chains — STCW via flag-state QSS audit + PSC verification of rest-hours records; MLC via DMLC certification + PSC inspection of MLC Part I/Part II declarations.

The integration also runs in the other direction. **STCW competency is a precondition for MLC seafarer-employment** under MLC Reg. 1.3 (training and qualifications) — a seafarer cannot lawfully be employed under an MLC SEA without holding the STCW certifications appropriate to their function. MLC Reg. 1.3 thus explicitly defers to STCW for the competency content while imposing its own employment-conditions overlay.

## Practitioner stakeholder map

STCW compliance is a multi-stakeholder system spanning training providers, employers, certification authorities, and inspection regimes:

| Stakeholder | Primary chapters | Role |
|-------------|------------------|------|
| **Shipowner / operator (the Company under ISM)** | All chapters via crewing | Verifies crew CoC validity at engagement; bears ISM Code Ch. IX responsibility for ensuring crewing satisfies STCW; signs MLC SEAs that reference STCW certifications |
| **Master + officers** | Ch. II, III, IV, VIII | Hold the operational CoCs; enforce hours-of-rest and watchkeeping doctrine; maintain training records aboard |
| **Seafarer (rating)** | Ch. II/4, II/5, III/4, III/5, III/7, VI | Hold rank-appropriate CoCs; complete BST + function-specific endorsements |
| **Training providers (MET institutions)** | All chapters | Deliver STCW Code Part A curricula; subject to flag-state QSS approval and STCW Independent Evaluation |
| **Flag state** | All chapters via certificate issue | Issues CoCs and endorsements under national STCW-implementing law; maintains QSS; submits to STCW Independent Evaluation |
| **IMO Maritime Safety Committee + HTW Sub-Committee** | All chapters | Adopts amendments under tacit-acceptance procedure; HTW (Sub-Committee on Human Element, Training and Watchkeeping) is the primary working body for STCW work programme |
| **IMO Member State Audit (IMSAS)** | All chapters | Audits flag-state implementation on a mandatory rotational cycle since 2016 |
| **Port state (PSC)** | All chapters via inspection | Verifies STCW certificate validity, hours-of-rest records, and watchkeeping arrangements; can detain non-compliant ships under [paris-mou](./paris-mou.md) / [tokyo-mou](./tokyo-mou.md) regimes |
| **Classification societies (Recognized Organizations)** | Ch. II, III via SMS audit | Audit ISM Code SMS for compliance with crewing-related provisions; verify crewing-list against CoC validity during port-state surveys on flag-state delegation |
| **ILO (in MLC pairing)** | Ch. VIII via MLC A2.3 alignment | ILO authority over MLC Title 2 hours-of-rest provisions; STCW-MLC Joint Tripartite Working Group is the bilateral coordination body |
| **Crewing agencies** | All chapters via recruitment | Verify CoC + endorsement validity at recruitment; maintain MLC Reg. 1.4-compliant placement service practices |

## Cross-references

**Sibling pillars** *(the "four pillars" of international maritime regulation)*

- [solas-1974](./solas-1974.md) — IMO sibling; STCW provides the human-element complement to SOLAS Ch. V (navigation rules), Ch. III (life-saving appliances), and Ch. IV (GMDSS radiocommunications). SOLAS sets what the ship and its equipment must be; STCW sets who is qualified to operate them. The Costa Concordia 2012 + Sewol 2014 incident-pair are co-anchors across the two pages.
- [marpol-73-78](./marpol-73-78.md) — IMO sibling; STCW Chapter V special-training requirements for tanker crews implement the human-element side of MARPOL Annexes I–II pollution-prevention compliance. The IGF Code training (Ch. V/3) maps to MARPOL Annex VI dual-fuel + low-flashpoint compliance.
- [mlc-2006](./mlc-2006.md) — ILO sibling; STCW = training and certification, MLC = labor and working conditions. Together they cover the seafarer's full lifecycle from pre-service training through working conditions. The 2010 Manila hours-of-rest provisions were explicitly aligned with MLC 2006 Title 2 hours-of-rest standards (see "STCW + MLC 2006 integration" section above).

**Umbrella treaty**

- [unclos-1982](./unclos-1982.md) — UNCLOS supplies the law-of-the-sea umbrella; flag-state jurisdiction (UNCLOS Articles 91, 92, 94) provides the constitutional basis for STCW flag-state implementation and certificate issuance.

**Related sub-regimes and companion instruments**

- **IMO Whitelist** — administrative validation regime under STCW Chapter I; states not on the Whitelist see their CoCs not recognized internationally.
- **STCW-F 1995** — sister IMO convention covering training and certification of fishing-vessel personnel, which are not covered by STCW proper.
- **GMDSS** — Global Maritime Distress and Safety System; STCW Chapter IV governs the training and certification of GMDSS radio operators, complementing SOLAS Ch. IV equipment-carriage requirements.
- **Polar Code** — IMO Polar Code (in force 2017); STCW Chapter V/4 Polar-waters training requirements added by the 2014–2016 amendment cycle operationalize the Polar Code human-element provisions.
- **IGF Code** — STCW Chapter V/3 LNG-fuel + low-flashpoint-fuel training implements the human-element side of [solas-1974](./solas-1974.md) Ch. II-1 Part G.
- **ECDIS** — Electronic Chart Display and Information System; STCW Chapter II competency requirements for ECDIS operation were added by the 2010 Manila Amendments; ECDIS-2 (cybersecurity-hardened, anomaly-detection) is in the 2025 amendment cohort.
- **ISPS Code** — STCW Chapter VI security-training (security awareness, designated security duties, SSO) operationalizes the human-element side of [isps-code](../concepts/isps-code.md).
- **ISM Code** — STCW + ISM are the two human-element pillars; STCW certifies individuals, ISM certifies the company SMS that employs them. See [ism-code](../concepts/ism-code.md).

**Port-state-control inspection regimes** *(STCW certificate verification surface)*

- [paris-mou](./paris-mou.md) — North Atlantic + European PSC; uses the New Inspection Regime ship-risk profile; STCW deficiencies (CoC validity, hours-of-rest records) are detention-eligible.
- [tokyo-mou](./tokyo-mou.md) — Asia-Pacific PSC; harmonized with Paris MoU; STCW deficiencies are detention-eligible.
- [port-state-control](../concepts/port-state-control.md) — port-state-control inspection of STCW certificates and watchkeeping arrangements is part of the Paris/Tokyo MoU inspection regimes.

**maritime-law concepts**

- [stcw-convention](../concepts/stcw-convention.md) — doctrinal companion (concept-side resolver pair).
- [flag-state-jurisdiction](../concepts/flag-state-jurisdiction.md) — flag-state issuance of STCW Certificates of Competency under UNCLOS Article 94.
- [ism-code](../concepts/ism-code.md) — Ch. IX SOLAS company-SMS instrument; the corporate-side complement to STCW's individual-CoC instrument.
