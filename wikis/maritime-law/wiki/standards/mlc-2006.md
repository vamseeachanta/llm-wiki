# MLC 2006 — Maritime Labour Convention

## Scope

The Maritime Labour Convention 2006 is the **International Labour Organization** consolidated convention establishing a comprehensive seafarer-labor-rights regime covering recruitment, employment conditions, accommodation, food and catering, health protection and medical care, social security, and welfare. Compliance is verified through mandatory port-state-control inspections backed by the **Maritime Labour Certificate** plus the two-part **Declaration of Maritime Labour Compliance (DMLC Parts I and II)**.

MLC 2006 is widely referred to as **"the fourth pillar"** of international maritime regulation, sitting alongside [solas-1974](../concepts/solas-1974.md) (safety of life at sea), [marpol-73-78](../concepts/marpol-73-78.md) (pollution prevention), and [stcw-convention](../concepts/stcw-convention.md) (training, certification, watchkeeping). Unlike the other three pillars — adopted under the IMO — MLC originates in the ILO, a distinct UN-system body whose tripartite structure (governments, shipowners' representatives, seafarers' representatives) governs the convention's amendment cycle.

This page is a metadata-only resolver for `code_id: mlc-2006` and is the companion standards-tier entry to the existing concepts/ doctrinal page at [mlc-2006](../concepts/mlc-2006.md).

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **Adopted:** Geneva, at the 94th (Maritime) Session of the International Labour Conference, 2006-02-23.
- **Entry-into-force:** 2013-08-20, twelve months after the threshold of **30 ILO member states representing at least 33 per cent of world gross tonnage** was met.
- **States parties:** 100+ as of 2024, covering more than **90 per cent of world tonnage**, making MLC one of the most widely-implemented maritime instruments by tonnage coverage.
- **Tripartite governance:** the Special Tripartite Committee (STC) under the ILO Governing Body negotiates amendments; this cadence is independent of IMO MSC/MEPC sessions.

## Five-Title architecture (multi-criteria comparison)

The convention is structured as Articles + Regulations + a two-part Code (Part A mandatory standards, Part B non-mandatory guidelines), grouped into five Titles. The table below summarizes coverage scope, principal Standards, the certificate inspection trigger, and the practitioner stakeholders most directly engaged with each Title.

| Title | Coverage scope | Principal Standards (Part A, illustrative) | DMLC inspection touchpoint | Primary practitioner stakeholders |
|-------|----------------|--------------------------------------------|----------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Title 1 — Minimum Requirements for Seafarers to Work on a Ship** | Minimum age, medical certification, training and qualification, recruitment and placement services. | A1.1 minimum age (16 / 18 for night work and hazardous tasks); A1.2 medical certificate; A1.4 recruitment and placement services. | Inspector verifies medical certificates onboard, confirms no underage seafarer, audits crewing-agent contracts. | Crewing agents, flag-state medical authorities, seafarer unions ([itf-international-transport-workers-federation](../entities/itf-international-transport-workers-federation.md)), shipowners. |
| **Title 2 — Conditions of Employment** | Seafarers' Employment Agreement (SEA), wages, hours of work and hours of rest, leave entitlement, repatriation, manning levels, career and skill development. | A2.1 SEA; A2.3 hours of work / hours of rest (10 hours rest in 24 / 77 hours rest in 7 days); A2.5 repatriation including the **A2.5.2 financial-security standard added by 2014 amendments**. | Inspector audits SEAs onboard, reviews work / rest records against A2.3, verifies financial-security certificate posted in seafarer-accessible location. | Shipowners, masters, P&I clubs (financial-security providers under A2.5.2), flag-state maritime authority, seafarer unions, [port-state-control](../concepts/port-state-control.md) officers. |
| **Title 3 — Accommodation, Recreational Facilities, Food and Catering** | Onboard living conditions, cabin and recreational space dimensions, ventilation and heating, sanitary facilities, food provisioning, qualified ship's cook, catering standards. | A3.1 accommodation (with grandfather provisions for ships built before 2013-08-20); A3.2 food and catering. | Inspector physically inspects cabin dimensions, mess rooms, galley, food-storage facilities, recreational spaces. | Shipbuilders, shipowners, ship's cook, classification societies (build-stage compliance), flag-state surveyors. |
| **Title 4 — Health Protection, Medical Care, Welfare and Social Security Protection** | Medical care on board and ashore, shipowner's liability, occupational health and safety, accident prevention, access to shore-based welfare facilities, social security. | A4.1 medical care; A4.2.1 shipowner's liability for sickness and injury including the **A4.2.2 financial-security standard added by 2014 amendments**; A4.3 health and safety; A4.4 access to shore-based welfare; A4.5 social security. | Inspector verifies medical chest, telemedical-advice arrangements, financial-security certificate for shipowner-liability claims, social-security branches declared by flag state. | Shipowners, P&I clubs (financial-security providers under A4.2.2), telemedical-advice services, port welfare boards, ILO, flag-state social-security authority. |
| **Title 5 — Compliance and Enforcement** | Flag-state responsibilities, port-state-control inspection, labour-supplying-state responsibilities, complaint-handling procedures both on board and ashore. | A5.1.3 Maritime Labour Certificate and DMLC; A5.1.5 onboard complaint procedure; A5.2.1 port-state-control inspections; A5.3 labour-supplying responsibilities. | The whole title *is* the inspection regime — DMLC Parts I and II, MLC certificate, more-detailed-inspection trigger, complaints handling. | Flag states, port-state-control regimes (Paris MoU / Tokyo MoU / Caribbean MoU / Indian Ocean MoU / etc.), recognized organizations (RO classification societies), ILO, seafarer unions. |

## Key concepts

- **Maritime Labour Certificate (MLC):** required onboard ships of **500 GT or more** engaged in international voyages or operating from a port (or between ports) in another country; valid for up to **5 years**; issued by the flag state (or a recognized organization on its behalf) and subject to port-state-control inspection.
- **DMLC Part I:** the flag state's statement of national requirements implementing the convention's 14 inspection areas.
- **DMLC Part II:** the shipowner's statement of measures adopted to ensure ongoing compliance with the Part I requirements between inspections.
- **Substantial equivalence:** flag states may adopt national provisions that are *substantially equivalent* to Code Part A standards, rather than literal compliance, where strict literal implementation is impracticable.
- **Tacit acceptance:** Code amendments enter into force on a defined date unless a sufficient number of ratifying states formally object — the same rapid-amendment regime used by [solas-1974](../concepts/solas-1974.md) and [marpol-73-78](../concepts/marpol-73-78.md) Annexes, allowing the convention to evolve at industry pace without re-ratification.
- **Recognized organizations (ROs):** classification societies and similar bodies authorized by the flag state to conduct MLC inspections and issue certificates on the flag state's behalf — most large registries delegate this to IACS-member class societies.
- **Onboard complaint procedure:** Standard A5.1.5 mandates a written procedure giving seafarers access to escalation without retaliation; this is the seafarer's primary in-service remedy and is audited at every inspection.
- **More-detailed inspection trigger:** under Standard A5.2.1, port-state-control officers escalate from a documentary check to a full onboard inspection when the certificate is missing, expired, or when there is "clear grounds" — including a seafarer complaint received ashore.

## Amendments timeline

The MLC's tacit-acceptance amendment cycle has produced a steady cadence of revisions tracking real-world enforcement experience. Each amendment package typically arrives roughly three years after STC adoption.

| Amendment package | STC adoption | Entry into force | Subject and trigger |
|-------------------|--------------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Original convention** | 2006-02-23 (ILC 94th Session) | 2013-08-20 | Consolidation of 37 prior ILO maritime-labor conventions and 31 recommendations dating from 1920-1996 — the foundational instrument. |
| **2014 amendments** | 2014-04-11 (STC 1st Meeting) | 2017-01-18 | Mandatory **financial security for abandonment** (Standard A2.5.2) and for shipowner's liability for death and long-term disability (Standard A4.2.2). Direct response to the abandonment-pattern of the late-2000s and early-2010s, including the **Stella Maris (2014)** abandonment that became a contemporaneous reference incident. |
| **2016 amendments** | 2016-04-08 (STC 2nd Meeting) | 2019-01-08 | **Anti-harassment and anti-bullying** provisions; clarifications to medical-certificate validity when a certificate expires during a voyage; war-risk and piracy zone clarifications. Triggered by ITF reporting on shipboard harassment and by piracy-zone abandonment cases. |
| **2018 amendments** | 2018-04-27 (STC 3rd Meeting) | 2020-12-26 | War-risk and piracy detention — extension of seafarers' employment agreements when the seafarer is held captive on board or ashore as a result of acts of piracy or armed robbery against ships; harassment and bullying onboard; validity-period clarifications; requirement to keep onboard certified copies of MLC and DMLC documents. |
| **2022 amendments** | 2022-05-13 (STC 4th Meeting) | 2024-12-23 | **Anti-shore-leave-restriction** provisions in the post-COVID environment; recognition of seafarers as **key workers**; expanded financial-security obligations; mental-health protections; ship-to-ship-transfer welfare protections; expanded shore-leave rights for seafarers. Direct policy response to the **2020-2021 COVID-19 crew-change crisis**. |
| **2025 / future amendments** | STC 5th Meeting scheduled mid-decade | TBD | Discussion topics include: cybersecurity and seafarer-data protections; further mental-health protections; clarifications to recruitment-and-placement-service supervision; revisions to social-security branches in light of remote-work patterns. Status: under STC working-group review. |

The 2022 amendments entered into force on 2024-12-23 under the tacit-acceptance procedure and are reflected in the **MLC Consolidated 2022** edition that this page is pinned to.

## Worked examples — named-incident references

These named incidents are the principal contemporaneous references practitioners cite when discussing MLC enforcement, gap-detection, and amendment-driver dynamics. Each is a doctrine-shaping reference; companion entity pages are linked where they exist.

### MV Wakashio (2020) — crew welfare during prolonged-incident

The grounding of the **MV Wakashio** off Pointe d'Esny, Mauritius, on 2020-07-25 created a months-long incident-response situation in which the crew remained engaged through fuel-oil release, salvage, sinking of the bow section, and a Mauritian criminal investigation. MLC enforcement issues raised:

- **Repatriation under Title 2 / A2.5:** the crew's repatriation became politically entangled with the Mauritian criminal investigation against the master and chief officer, illustrating the tension between MLC repatriation rights and host-state criminal jurisdiction.
- **Health and welfare under Title 4:** prolonged on-scene engagement during a sustained pollution event triggered medical-care, accommodation, and welfare obligations under A4.1 and A4.4, with shoreside welfare access constrained by Mauritian COVID-era restrictions.
- **Onboard-complaint and shore-side complaint pathways under Title 5:** the case is cited in subsequent ILO guidance for the difficulty of exercising A5.1.5 onboard-complaint rights when the ship itself has effectively ceased to operate.

Companion entity page: [mv-wakashio-2020](../entities/mv-wakashio-2020.md).

### COVID-19 crew-change crisis (2020-2021) — the largest stress-test of MLC enforcement

At the peak of the crisis, ILO/IMO joint statements estimated **~400,000 seafarers stranded** on ships beyond their contractual repatriation date, with a comparable number unable to join ships and earn wages. MLC enforcement gaps surfaced in real time:

- **Shore-leave denials** by port states, ostensibly on public-health grounds, conflicted with MLC welfare protections — a primary driver of the 2022 amendment package's anti-shore-leave-restriction provisions.
- **Repatriation breakdown** under A2.5 stretched contracts past the maximum 11-month service period in many cases, and revealed that the financial-security regime introduced by the 2014 amendments was sufficient for individual abandonment events but not for system-wide repatriation breakdown.
- **Key-worker recognition** by IMO, ILO, ITF, and the International Chamber of Shipping pressed flag and port states to designate seafarers as key workers — the doctrinal basis for the 2022 amendments.
- **Mental-health protections** were absent from the original convention; the crisis produced extensive documentation of seafarer suicide and self-harm and drove the 2022 amendments' mental-health provisions.

The crisis is the single most-cited reference event in modern MLC discourse and is the explicit policy-driver for the 2022 amendment package.

### Stella Maris (2014) — pre-MLC-2014 abandonment driving the financial-security amendment

The **Stella Maris** (Liberian-flagged) abandonment in 2014 is cited as one of the contemporaneous reference cases for the 2014 financial-security amendments. The crew was abandoned without wages or repatriation; recovery depended on ad-hoc support from the seafarer-welfare network rather than a binding flag-state or shipowner financial-security mechanism. The case illustrated:

- **Pre-2014 enforcement gap:** the original 2006 convention required repatriation under A2.5 but did not require a pre-funded financial-security mechanism, leaving abandoned crews dependent on welfare charity.
- **Flag-state implementation drift:** Liberia, like other open-registry flag states, ratified MLC but had limited enforcement capacity against shipowners domiciled outside the registry's jurisdiction.
- **Direct doctrinal driver:** the Stella Maris case sits in the cohort of abandonment incidents that pressed ILO toward the 2014 financial-security amendments to A2.5.2 and A4.2.2, which remediated the gap by mandating shipowner-level financial-security certificates.

### Ledra (2017) — Cyprus abandonment + flag-state failure

The **Ledra** abandonment off Cyprus in 2017 illustrated the post-2014 enforcement reality:

- **Financial-security amendment in force, but enforcement uneven:** even after the 2017 entry-into-force of the 2014 amendments, abandonment incidents continued where the shipowner's financial-security provider failed to perform or where the certificate was disputed.
- **ITF intervention:** the International Transport Workers' Federation maintained the day-to-day support pattern (provisions, fuel for the ship, eventual repatriation) that the convention's financial-security regime was supposed to mechanize, exposing residual gaps in claim-handling procedures.
- **Port-state-control + flag-state coordination:** Cyprus port-state-control authorities and the flag state coordinated with the ILO Abandonment Database and ITF, producing a workflow pattern subsequently codified in ILO guidance.

The case fed into the doctrinal critique that financial security on paper does not equal financial security in practice — a critique reflected in the 2022 amendments' expanded financial-security provisions.

## MLC vs STCW — multi-criteria comparison

MLC and STCW are the two seafarer-facing pillars and are deliberately complementary; together they cover the seafarer's full lifecycle from training through working conditions. The table below makes the distinction explicit.

| Dimension | **MLC 2006 (ILO)** | **STCW 1978 (as amended) (IMO)** |
|-----------|--------------------|----------------------------------|
| **Issuing body** | ILO (tripartite: governments, shipowners, seafarers) | IMO (governmental representation only) |
| **Subject** | Seafarers' rights and working conditions | Seafarers' training, certification, watchkeeping |
| **Pillar** | Fourth pillar | Third pillar |
| **Primary instrument** | Maritime Labour Certificate + DMLC Parts I and II | Certificate of Competency (CoC) + endorsements + Standards of Training |
| **Enforcement vehicle** | Flag-state-issued MLC certificate + port-state-control inspection under A5.2.1 | Flag-state-issued CoC + port-state-control verification under STCW Reg I/4 |
| **Inspection trigger** | Routine PSC inspection of MLC certificate; "clear grounds" trigger under A5.2.1 (e.g. seafarer complaint, expired certificate) | Routine PSC inspection of CoC; "clear grounds" trigger under STCW Reg I/4 (e.g. competence in evidence) |
| **Detention authority** | Detention for serious deficiencies under MLC (e.g. expired certificate, unsafe accommodation) | Detention for unsafe manning or invalid certification under STCW Reg I/4 |
| **Amendment regime** | Tacit acceptance via Special Tripartite Committee (STC) | Tacit acceptance via IMO MSC (Maritime Safety Committee) |
| **Coverage threshold** | Ships ≥500 GT international voyages (certificate mandatory); applies to all ships in scope, regardless of GT | All ships engaged in international voyages and crews in covered capacities |
| **Seafarer remedy** | Onboard complaint procedure (A5.1.5), shore-side complaint to PSC | Discipline-based; seafarer remedy is via flag-state CoC suspension procedures |
| **Financial-security regime** | A2.5.2 (repatriation/abandonment) + A4.2.2 (death and long-term disability) | None — STCW does not regulate financial protection |

The **shared "fourth pillar" doctrine** is that no single pillar is sufficient: [stcw-convention](../concepts/stcw-convention.md) says the seafarer is competent, MLC says the seafarer is treated lawfully, [solas-1974](../concepts/solas-1974.md) says the ship is safe, [marpol-73-78](../concepts/marpol-73-78.md) says the ship does not pollute. A vessel is not "compliant" without all four.

## Inspection regime — DMLC + Maritime Labour Certificate + ILO/IMO joint working group

The Title 5 inspection regime is the operational core of MLC enforcement and is the most-cited interaction surface with IMO instruments via [port-state-control](../concepts/port-state-control.md) coordination.

### Document hierarchy onboard

A compliant vessel carries three layered documents available to inspectors and seafarers:

1. **Maritime Labour Certificate** — issued by the flag state (or RO on its behalf), valid up to 5 years, subject to intermediate inspection in years 2-3.
2. **DMLC Part I** — the flag state's declaration of national requirements implementing the **14 areas** subject to inspection (minimum age; medical certification; qualifications; SEAs; recruitment; hours of work / rest; manning; accommodation; food/catering; health and safety; medical care; onboard complaint; payment of wages; financial security).
3. **DMLC Part II** — the shipowner's declaration of measures ensuring ongoing compliance with Part I, including audit and verification arrangements between inspections.

### Inspection cadence

- **Initial inspection** prior to first MLC certificate issuance (new build or new entrant to flag).
- **Intermediate inspection** between the second and third anniversary of certificate issuance.
- **Renewal inspection** in the final year of the 5-year cycle.
- **Port-state-control inspection** at any time the vessel calls at a port of a state party — documentary check by default; "more detailed inspection" triggered by clear grounds under A5.2.1.

### Port-state-control regime coordination

MLC inspection results feed into the regional [port-state-control](../concepts/port-state-control.md) performance lists, which themselves operate under the broader [flag-state-jurisdiction](../concepts/flag-state-jurisdiction.md) framework:

- **[paris-mou](../entities/paris-mou.md)** — North Atlantic and European PSC; MLC deficiencies count toward the Paris MoU "white/grey/black list" of flag-state performance.
- **Tokyo MoU** — Asia-Pacific PSC; equivalent performance regime, MLC inspections feed the Tokyo MoU performance list.
- **Caribbean MoU** — Caribbean basin PSC.
- **Indian Ocean MoU**, **Black Sea MoU**, **Mediterranean MoU**, **Riyadh MoU**, **Vina del Mar Agreement** (Latin America), **Abuja MoU** (West and Central Africa) — full regional coverage of the global PSC patchwork.

### ILO-IMO joint working group

The ILO-IMO Joint Tripartite Working Group is the doctrinal coordination surface between MLC enforcement and IMO instruments — particularly:

- **MLC + STCW interaction** — joint guidance on hours of rest reconciliation between MLC A2.3 and STCW Section A-VIII/1.
- **MLC + ISM Code interaction** — joint guidance on how the [ism-code](../concepts/ism-code.md) Safety Management System must integrate MLC obligations into the shipowner's documented procedures.
- **MLC + ISPS interaction** — joint guidance on shore-leave protections (MLC A3.1) reconciliation with port-facility security restrictions under [isps-code](../concepts/isps-code.md).
- **Abandonment Database** — jointly maintained ILO database tracking abandonment cases, fed by ITF, P&I clubs, and flag-state reports.

## Practitioner stakeholder map

| Stakeholder | Role under MLC | Day-to-day touchpoint |
|-------------|----------------|-----------------------|
| **Shipowner** | Primary duty-bearer under the convention; signs the SEA, holds the financial-security certificate, signs DMLC Part II. | Owns DMLC Part II, contracts with P&I, contracts with crewing agent, runs onboard complaint procedure. |
| **Master** | Implementation officer onboard; responsible for hours-of-rest records, complaint-procedure operation, food and accommodation supervision, repatriation triggering. | Daily SEA and rest-record management, monthly inspection-readiness checks. |
| **Seafarer / seafarer unions ([itf-international-transport-workers-federation](../entities/itf-international-transport-workers-federation.md))** | Beneficiary of convention rights; ITF acts as collective representative including in abandonment-rescue. | SEA negotiation, onboard complaint, ITF inspector ship visits, abandonment intervention. |
| **Flag state** | Primary enforcement state; issues MLC certificate (directly or via RO), signs DMLC Part I. | Initial / intermediate / renewal inspections, RO oversight, complaint receipt under A5.1.5. |
| **Port state / port-state-control officer** | Secondary enforcement state under A5.2.1; performs documentary and (on clear grounds) more-detailed inspections. | PSC routine boarding, MLC certificate verification, deficiency recording, PSC MoU performance reporting. |
| **Recognized organization (RO)** | Classification society or similar body authorized to issue certificates on the flag state's behalf. | New-build inspection, intermediate inspection, certificate issuance, audit of DMLC Part II. |
| **ILO** | Convention secretariat; maintains the convention text, the STC amendment process, and the abandonment database. | Tripartite consultation, amendment cycle, abandonment-database maintenance, country-level technical assistance. |
| **Crewing / recruitment-and-placement agent** | Subject to A1.4 supervision and licensing; primary interface for seafarers entering employment. | SEA preparation, qualification verification, recruitment-and-placement-service license maintenance under flag-state oversight. |
| **P&I club** | Financial-security provider under A2.5.2 and A4.2.2; receives abandonment / shipowner-liability claims. | Financial-security certificate issuance, abandonment claim handling, repatriation cost reimbursement. |
| **Port welfare board / ICMA / Apostleship of the Sea / Mission to Seafarers** | Shore-based welfare under A4.4 — the convention's required shore welfare facilities depend on this welfare-provider network. | Seafarer center operation, ship visits, port-state-control interface during abandonment. |
