# York-Antwerp Rules — General Average Private Codification

## Scope

Private codification of the **General Average** institution: when a vessel and cargo are engaged in a "common maritime adventure", and an extraordinary sacrifice or expenditure is **voluntarily and reasonably made** to preserve the property in peril, the cost is shared **proportionally among all parties** (shipowner + cargo-owner + freight-receiver) according to the value of their respective interests at the time of the General Average act.

The York-Antwerp Rules (YAR) are not legislation. They are a private rule-set promulgated by the Comité Maritime International (CMI) and given binding force only when parties incorporate them by reference into bills of lading, voyage charters, time charters, or marine-insurance policies. This page is pinned to the **YAR 2016** consolidated edition; market-incumbent contracts still frequently reference YAR 1994.

## Why "private codification" not treaty

- General Average is one of the oldest institutions in maritime law (traceable to Lex Rhodia ~700 BC and Justinian's Digest, 533 AD).
- The York-Antwerp Rules are CMI's modern attempt to harmonize what is otherwise national-law-fragmented private contract — civil-law and common-law jurisdictions historically diverged on what counted as a recoverable sacrifice or expenditure.
- Parties incorporate by reference: most modern bills of lading and charterparties contain a clause such as "General Average shall be settled in accordance with the York-Antwerp Rules 2016" or similar.
- No state-ratification regime; no IMO/UN parent body; no entry-into-force date. The Rules bind only by contractual incorporation, which is the inverse of the IMO treaty pattern (UNCLOS, SOLAS, MARPOL).
- Consequence for amendment cadence: CMI revises by international conference at irregular intervals (see history below); parties using older incorporations remain governed by the older edition until they re-paper.

## Revision history

CMI revises by conference at irregular intervals; there is no protocol or resolution mechanism analogous to IMO MEPC/MSC.

| Year | Notable change |
|------|----------------|
| 1864 (York) | First codification |
| 1877 | Antwerp conference revisions |
| 1890 (Antwerp) | Renamed York-Antwerp Rules |
| 1924 | Modernized; widely adopted |
| 1950 | Post-WWII revisions |
| 1974 | Common-law-jurisdiction-friendly rebalancing |
| 1994 | Tightened "non-separation" rule; addressed salvage interaction |
| 2004 | Removed time-charterer + commission-agent rules; controversial — many bills retained 1994 |
| 2016 | Most recent revision; rebalanced toward shipowner; broader market acceptance than 2004 |
| 1994 vs. 2016 | Some bills of lading still incorporate 1994 (industry adoption is slow) |

## Three classical conditions for a General Average act

- **Common danger**: peril must be common to ship + cargo + freight, not just one interest. A peril threatening only the cargo (e.g. spoilage of a single shipper's perishables) is not GA.
- **Voluntary + reasonable**: sacrifice or expenditure must be voluntarily incurred (not accidental) and reasonable (not panic-driven). Master's good-faith judgement at the time of the act controls.
- **Successful preservation**: the act must result in saving some property of the adventure. Failed sacrifice (e.g. jettisoned cargo where the ship is then lost anyway with no remaining saved values) is not GA — there is nothing left to contribute.

## Modern application

- Container vessel jettisons containers to lighten ship in heavy weather → GA sacrifice; surviving cargo and ship contribute to the loss.
- Tug and salvor expenses to free a stranded vessel → GA expenditure.
- Cost of forced port-of-refuge to repair → GA (refuge expenses + repairs + cargo handling charges, subject to the detailed lettered + numbered Rules).
- Most large-vessel casualties (Maersk Honam fire 2018, Ever Given 2021) trigger formal GA declarations, with cargo-release contingent on shippers posting GA bonds and underwriter guarantees.

## Notable cases / disputes

- **The Tojo Maru (1972)** — UK House of Lords; clarified the boundary between salvage services and General Average expenditure where a salvor's negligence injured the vessel during the operation.
- **Maersk Honam (2018)** — large container-fire GA declaration; multi-year settlement involving thousands of cargo interests; widely cited as a stress test of YAR-2016 cargo-handling rules.
- **Ever Given (2021)** — Suez blockage; owner Shoei Kisen declared GA; salvage by Suez Canal Authority and resulting compensation negotiation interacted with the GA adjustment process. See [[entities/mv-ever-given-2021]](../entities/mv-ever-given-2021.md).
