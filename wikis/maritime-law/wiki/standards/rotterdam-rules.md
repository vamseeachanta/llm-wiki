# Rotterdam Rules — UN Convention on Carriage of Goods Wholly or Partly by Sea

## Scope

The Rotterdam Rules are a UN UNCITRAL convention modernizing the carriage-of-goods-by-sea liability regime. They were drafted to replace the three overlapping prior regimes — the **Hague Rules 1924**, the **Hague-Visby Rules 1968/1979**, and the **Hamburg Rules 1978** — with a single instrument fit for container-era, multimodal, and electronic-document trade. Three substantive innovations distinguish the Rotterdam Rules from Hague-Visby:

1. **Maritime-plus coverage** (Article 1.1): the regime extends to multi-modal contracts where sea is one segment of a longer door-to-door movement (e.g. truck → port → ship → port → truck). Hague-Visby is "tackle-to-tackle" — it stops at the ship's rail. Rotterdam follows the cargo from inland origin to inland destination.
2. **Electronic transport documents** (Articles 35–58): an explicit framework for paperless bills of lading (electronic B/L), including issuance, transfer, surrender, and the legal equivalence of electronic and paper records. This is intended to unblock the industry-blocker that has delayed digitization for decades.
3. **Higher carrier liability + extended periods** (Articles 17 and 24): a higher per-package limit, a longer (2-year) suit-or-bar period vs. Hague-Visby's 1-year, and a narrower set of carrier-defence exceptions paired with stronger carrier due-diligence obligations.

The convention is pinned to its 2008 adopted text. There is no consolidated edition because no amendment has been adopted — the convention is not in force.

## Adoption status

- **Adopted:** UN General Assembly resolution **A/RES/63/122**, 2008-12-11. The instrument was opened for signature at a ceremony in **Rotterdam** on 2009-09-23 — hence the informal name "Rotterdam Rules."
- **Signed by:** 25 states (including the United States, France, the Netherlands, Norway, Denmark, Greece, Poland, and Switzerland).
- **Ratified by:** 5 states as of 2024 — **Spain, Togo, Republic of the Congo, Cameroon, Benin**.
- **Entry-into-force threshold (Article 94):** 20 ratifications, then the convention enters into force on the first day of the month following the expiration of one year after deposit of the 20th instrument.
- **Status:** **not in force.** Widely viewed as unlikely to enter into force in current form; the major maritime trading nations have not advanced ratification through their legislatures since the 2009 signature wave.

## Why it stalled

Documenting the political-economy reasons matters for citation: a doctrinal memo or compliance matrix that names "Rotterdam Rules" as a target regime should disclose the in-force gap.

- **Industry resistance.** Carriers have been reluctant to accept higher per-package limits, the longer 2-year suit-or-bar window, and the narrower defence catalogue (only 15 listed carrier exceptions vs. 17 in Hague-Visby Article IV.2). The cost-of-insurance signal moved against ratification.
- **Fragmentation of legal regimes.** Parties already bound by Hague-Visby — and used to ~100 years of decided case law interpreting its language — have been reluctant to switch to a new instrument with no body of jurisprudence and broader (door-to-door) scope.
- **Amend-rather-than-replace preference.** Several Hague-Visby states preferred amending Hague-Visby (or its 1979 SDR Protocol) rather than adopting a wholly new regime that also subsumes the multimodal layer.
- **Competing approaches.** Rotterdam competed with private-sector codification efforts (CMI Lex Mercatoria modernization, BIMCO standard-form clauses for multimodal carriage, and digital-B/L industry initiatives such as essDOCS, BoltTrade, and TradeLens-era platforms) which advanced operationally without requiring a binding new treaty.

## Three-tier coverage if in force

The table compares the three carriage-of-goods regimes that the Rotterdam Rules would, between contracting parties, supersede. Hamburg is included because it is itself in force in ~30 states (mostly Latin American + African) and represents a competing modernization path that Rotterdam was designed to displace.

| Aspect | Hague-Visby (1968/1979 SDR Protocol) | Rotterdam Rules (2008, not in force) |
|--------|---------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Geographic scope | Tackle-to-tackle (port-to-port) | Door-to-door (multi-modal possible) |
| Documents | Paper bills of lading | Electronic transport records explicit |
| Liability period | 1 year suit-or-bar | 2 years |
| Carrier exceptions | 17 listed defences (Hague Art. IV.2) | 15 listed defences (broader scope but with stronger carrier due-diligence) |
| Package limit | 666.67 SDR / 2 SDR per kg | 875 SDR / 3 SDR per kg |
| Volume contracts | Silent | Explicit derogation regime (Art. 80) |

Hamburg Rules 1978 sit between these two columns: 2-year suit-or-bar, no listed-defence catalogue (replaced by a presumed-fault regime), 835 SDR / 2.5 SDR per kg, port-to-port only, paper documents.
