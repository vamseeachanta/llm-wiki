# Tokyo MoU — Asia-Pacific Memorandum of Understanding on Port State Control

## Scope

The Tokyo MoU is an **administrative arrangement among 21 maritime authorities** — Australia, Canada, Chile, China, Fiji, Hong Kong (China), Indonesia, Japan, Republic of Korea, Malaysia, Marshall Islands, New Zealand, Papua New Guinea, Peru, Philippines, Russian Federation, Singapore, Solomon Islands, Thailand, Vanuatu, and Vietnam — coordinating port-state-control (PSC) inspections of foreign-flag vessels visiting Asia-Pacific ports. It is an administrative agreement among port-state authorities, not a treaty and not a private codification; the controlled-vocabulary `instrument_type` for this page is therefore `mou`.

The operational goals are to:

- **Detect and correct sub-standard ships** through a risk-based regional inspection regime;
- **Prevent flag-of-convenience exploitation** by raising the cost of non-compliance through the port-call channel rather than relying on flag-state enforcement (which is historically weak for open registries);
- **Harmonize regional inspection priorities** across the Asia-Pacific so that owners cannot port-shop their way around member-state enforcement.

The Tokyo MoU is the structural sibling of the Paris MoU and operationalises the same underlying source-authority stack — SOLAS Chapter XI, MARPOL Article 5(2), MLC 2006 Title 5, ILO C147, COLREGs, and the Load Lines Convention — across a different region.

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **Founded**: Tokyo, **1 December 1993**, at a ministerial conference of Asia-Pacific maritime authorities.
- **In force**: **1 April 1994**.
- **Current parties**: 21 member authorities as of 2024.
- **Most recent overhaul**: **2017** adoption of the **New Inspection Regime (NIR)**, replacing the prior Black / White / Grey List ranking system. The 2017 reorganization brings the Tokyo MoU into substantive alignment with the Paris MoU, which adopted NIR in 2011.

For non-treaty MoU instruments such as this, the equivalent of "adoption resolution + entry-into-force threshold" is the ministerial-conference signature plus the date the inspection regime began operating among the original signatories.

## NIR (New Inspection Regime)

The 2017 NIR replaced the prior White/Grey/Black List ship-targeting model with a **multi-factor ship risk profile** harmonised with the Paris MoU NIR.

- **Risk-based selection** — each ship is categorised as **High Risk Ship (HRS)**, **Standard Risk Ship (SRS)**, or **Low Risk Ship (LRS)** based on flag-state performance tier, Recognized Organization (RO) performance, company performance, ship type, and age.
- **Inspection frequency** —
  - **HRS**: 5–6 months between inspections;
  - **SRS**: 10–12 months between inspections;
  - **LRS**: 24–36 months between inspections.
- **Selection scheme** — ships are assigned **Priority I (must inspect)** or **Priority II (may inspect)** at a port call, derived from time-since-last-inspection plus any overriding or unexpected factors (e.g. recent detention, accident, or third-party report).

## Member authorities + flag-state ranking

- **Members (21)**: Australia, Canada, Chile, China, Fiji, Hong Kong (China), Indonesia, Japan, Republic of Korea, Malaysia, Marshall Islands, New Zealand, Papua New Guinea, Peru, Philippines, Russian Federation, Singapore, Solomon Islands, Thailand, Vanuatu, Vietnam.
- **Flag-state performance ranking** — similar to the Paris MoU, the Tokyo MoU publishes a **performance-tiered flag-state ranking** (high / medium-high / medium / medium-low / low / black-list-equivalent), updated annually based on a 3-year rolling window of inspection and detention statistics observed in member ports.
- **Recognized Organization (RO) performance index** — a separate ranking covering classification societies and other ROs that issue statutory certificates on behalf of flag administrations. IACS members and non-IACS ROs are tracked independently, and an RO's performance tier feeds into the ship-risk-profile calculation.

## Notable enforcement / concentrated-inspection-campaigns (CICs)

- **Annual joint Concentrated Inspection Campaigns (CICs) with the Paris MoU** on rotating themes, coordinated for global impact maximisation. Recent examples:
  - **2023** — fire-safety systems;
  - **2022** — STCW hours-of-rest compliance;
  - **2021** — stability and cargo-securing.
- **Stand-alone Tokyo MoU CICs** — periodically run on regionally salient themes (e.g. ballast-water management implementation, lifeboat-launching arrangements) when global coordination with Paris MoU is not yet timed.
- During a CIC, member authorities apply an expanded inspection checklist for the campaign topic to every applicable Priority I and Priority II inspection, generating a region-wide deficiency snapshot that informs subsequent Committee resolutions.
