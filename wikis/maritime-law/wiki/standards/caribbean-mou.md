# Caribbean MoU — Caribbean Memorandum of Understanding on Port State Control

## Scope

The Caribbean MoU is an **administrative arrangement among 22 maritime authorities** — Antigua & Barbuda, Aruba, Bahamas, Barbados, Belize, Cayman Islands, Cuba, Curaçao, Dominica, Dominican Republic, France (Guadeloupe + Martinique), Grenada, Guyana, Haiti, Jamaica, Netherlands (Bonaire + St. Eustatius + Saba + St. Maarten), Panama, St. Kitts & Nevis, St. Lucia, St. Vincent & Grenadines, Suriname, and Trinidad & Tobago — coordinating port-state-control (PSC) inspections of foreign-flag vessels visiting Caribbean ports. It is an administrative agreement among port-state authorities, not a treaty and not a private codification; the controlled-vocabulary `instrument_type` for this page is therefore `mou`.

The operational goals are to:

- **Detect and correct sub-standard ships** through a risk-based regional inspection regime;
- **Address cruise-ship-density-specific safety concerns** unique to the Caribbean basin (the highest cruise-ship operational density of any PSC region globally), with attendant focus on passenger-ship safety, life-saving appliance (LSA) integrity, STCW manning, ISPS security, and MARPOL Annex V garbage compliance;
- **Harmonize regional inspection priorities** across the Caribbean so that owners cannot port-shop their way around member-state enforcement.

The Caribbean MoU is the structural sibling of the Paris MoU and Tokyo MoU and operationalises the same underlying source-authority stack — SOLAS Chapter XI, MARPOL Article 5(2), MLC 2006 Title 5, ILO C147, COLREGs, and the Load Lines Convention — across the Caribbean basin.

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **Founded**: Christ Church, Barbados, **9 February 1996**, at a ministerial conference of Caribbean maritime authorities.
- **Secretariat**: Kingston, Jamaica.
- **Current parties**: 22 member authorities as of 2024 (including 3 metropolitan French overseas territories and 5 Dutch overseas territories listed as separate authorities).
- **Methodology track**: the Caribbean MoU has been a Paris/Tokyo MoU follower in inspection methodology — adoption of an NIR-equivalent risk-based selection regime is in progress, with the prior White/Grey/Black-list ranking model still informing flag-state targeting decisions during the transition.

For non-treaty MoU instruments such as this, the equivalent of "adoption resolution + entry-into-force threshold" is the ministerial-conference signature plus the date the inspection regime began operating among the original signatories.

## Member authorities + flag-state ranking

- **Members (22)**: Antigua & Barbuda, Aruba, Bahamas, Barbados, Belize, Cayman Islands, Cuba, Curaçao, Dominica, Dominican Republic, France (Guadeloupe + Martinique), Grenada, Guyana, Haiti, Jamaica, Netherlands (Bonaire + St. Eustatius + Saba + St. Maarten), Panama, St. Kitts & Nevis, St. Lucia, St. Vincent & Grenadines, Suriname, Trinidad & Tobago. Three metropolitan French and five Dutch overseas territories participate as separate authorities, reflecting the constitutional autonomy of the Kingdom of the Netherlands' Caribbean entities and the French *départements et collectivités d'outre-mer* in maritime-administration matters.
- **Cruise-ship operational density**: Caribbean MoU members host the highest cruise-ship density globally. Inspection focus accordingly skews toward passenger-ship safety — life-saving appliances and evacuation systems, STCW certification of bridge and engineering watchkeepers, ISPS security plan compliance, and MARPOL Annex V garbage and food-waste handling at the high port-call frequencies typical of Caribbean itineraries.
- **Flag-state convenience focus**: Bahamas, Panama, and Aruba are major Open Registries; a significant proportion of ships inspected at Caribbean MoU port calls fly Caribbean MoU member-authority flags themselves. This creates a structural overlap between port-state and flag-state roles unique to the Caribbean MoU among the regional MoUs and shapes the regime's flag-list calibration.

## Inspection priorities

- **Cruise- and passenger-ship-focused Concentrated Inspection Campaigns (CICs)** are run more frequently than in other regional MoUs, reflecting the Caribbean basin's traffic mix.
- **Joint CIC participation** — with the **Paris MoU since 2009** and the **Tokyo MoU since 2018** — gives Caribbean MoU CICs alignment with global rotating themes (e.g. fire-safety systems, STCW hours-of-rest, stability and cargo-securing) while preserving regionally-salient stand-alone campaigns.
- **Hurricane-season operational impacts** (June–November) are addressed via regional cooperation arrangements: the Caribbean MoU coordinates with the US National Hurricane Center, regional national meteorological services, and member-authority emergency-management offices to phase inspection priorities around storm-track forecasts and post-event port-reopening sequences.

## Notable enforcement / events

- **2010 Haiti earthquake** — Caribbean MoU member authorities coordinated emergency-PSC arrangements for relief vessels arriving at Haitian and neighbouring ports, balancing the urgency of humanitarian-cargo throughput against safety-critical inspection of vessels that would not have transited under routine commercial conditions.
- **2017 hurricane season (Irma + Maria)** — multiple member authorities suspended routine PSC inspections for emergency-period port operations, with phased resumption tied to port-reopening certifications and member-authority coordination through the Secretariat.
- **2020+ COVID-19 pandemic** — cruise-ship inspection intensified post-*Diamond Princess*, with Caribbean MoU members applying cruise-line-specific outbreak-response protocols (passenger health declarations, isolation-cabin capacity verification, crew-rotation MLC compliance) layered on top of standard PSC inspection scope.
