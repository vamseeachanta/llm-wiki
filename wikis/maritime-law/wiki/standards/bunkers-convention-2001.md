# Bunkers Convention 2001 — Civil Liability for Bunker Oil Pollution

## Scope

The **International Convention on Civil Liability for Bunker Oil Pollution Damage, 2001** (the "Bunkers Convention") is the IMO treaty providing a **strict-liability and compulsory-insurance framework for fuel-oil ("bunker oil") pollution from non-tanker vessels**. It closes the long-standing gap left by the CLC regime: the **CLC 1969 / 1992 Protocol** covers persistent oil cargo carried on tankers, but until the Bunkers Convention entered force in 2008, fuel-oil spills from container ships, general-cargo vessels, bulk carriers, ferries, and other non-tankers had **no international civil-liability regime**. The Bunkers Convention now applies to all commercial vessels of states-parties **above 1,000 GT**, requiring each to maintain a **Bunker Oil Pollution Insurance Certificate** (the analogue to the CLC certificate) and channeling strict liability to a **broader liability circle than CLC** — registered shipowner, bareboat charterer, manager, **and** operator.

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **Adopted:** London, 2001-03-23 (IMO Diplomatic Conference, document LEG/CONF.12/19).
- **Entry-into-force:** 2008-11-21, after 18 states (including 5 with combined fleets ≥1 million GT) had deposited instruments of ratification.
- **States parties:** 95+ as of 2024, covering the substantial majority of world non-tanker tonnage by flag.
- **Notable non-parties:** the **United States** (covers the same gap domestically via OPA 90).

## Why the gap existed

- **CLC 1969 + 1992 Protocol** were drafted in direct response to the *Torrey Canyon* (1967) tanker casualty and were scoped to **persistent oil cargo carried on tankers** — the regime's "ship" definition required carriage of bulk persistent oil to trigger.
- **Non-tankers** — container ships, bulk carriers, general-cargo vessels, ferries, RoRo, fishing vessels, cruise ships — carry only **fuel-oil for ship propulsion**, not persistent oil cargo. They were **outside CLC's scope** despite carrying thousands of tonnes of bunker oil that could (and did) spill in casualties.
- **Non-tanker fuel-oil casualties** through the late 1990s and 2000s built the political pressure for a gap-fill: **Cosco Busan (2007)** in San Francisco Bay, **Rena (2011)** off New Zealand, **Solar 1 (2006)** in the Philippines, and a long tail of grounding and collision casualties demonstrated that fuel-oil spills from non-tankers caused damage of the same character as CLC-covered tanker spills, but with no comparable strict-liability or compulsory-insurance backstop.
- The IMO Legal Committee responded with a deliberately **CLC-modelled instrument** — same strict-liability core, same compulsory-insurance certificate architecture, same direct-action against insurers — but with a **broader liability circle** and a **liability-cap interface to LLMC** rather than a standalone Bunkers cap.

## Key Articles

- **Article 1 — Definitions.** "Bunker oil" means *any hydrocarbon mineral oil, including lubricating oil, used or intended to be used for the operation or propulsion of the ship, and any residues of such oil*. "Pollution damage" mirrors the CLC formulation.
- **Article 3 — Liability of the shipowner.** Strict liability for pollution damage caused by bunker oil from the ship, with the standard CLC-style narrow exceptions (act of war, exceptional natural phenomenon of an inevitable and irresistible character, third-party act done with intent to cause damage, government negligence in maintaining lights or navigational aids).
- **Article 4 — Exclusions and limitation interface.** Bunkers liability is **subject to limitation under the LLMC 1976 or 1996 Protocol** (whichever the forum state is party to). The Bunkers Convention does **not** create a standalone liability cap — this is the principal architectural divergence from CLC.
- **Article 7 — Compulsory insurance.** Ships **above 1,000 GT** registered in a state party must maintain insurance or other financial security covering the LLMC limit, certified by a **Bunker Oil Pollution Insurance Certificate** (the analogue to the CLC certificate). Direct action against the insurer is permitted.
- **Article 9 — Jurisdiction.** Actions for compensation may be brought only in the courts of the state party in whose territory (including territorial sea or EEZ) the pollution damage was caused.

## Liability framework

- **Strict liability** is channeled to a **broader circle than CLC**: the **registered shipowner OR bareboat charterer OR manager OR operator** (jointly and severally). CLC channels to the registered owner only; Bunkers' wider channeling reflects the more diffuse commercial structure of non-tanker operation.
- **Liability cap** is **not standalone**. Bunkers liability is capped by reference to the **LLMC 1976 or 1996 Protocol** (whichever applies in the forum state — see [llmc-1996](./llmc-1996.md)). This means the cap rises with future LLMC amendments without requiring re-amendment of Bunkers itself.
- **Compulsory insurance** for ships **>1,000 GT**, certified by a **Bunker Oil Pollution Insurance Certificate** issued by the flag state (or, for ships flagged in non-parties calling at party ports, by a party state). Direct action against the insurer is available (Article 7).
- **No second-tier compensation fund.** Unlike the CLC + IOPC Funds two-tier architecture (where the IOPC 1992 Fund tops up beyond the shipowner's CLC cap), Bunkers has **no fund tier** — claimants beyond the LLMC cap have no Bunkers-Convention route. This is a recognized compensation gap; some delegations have proposed a Bunkers fund expansion at IMO Legal Committee, but no such instrument has been adopted.

## Notable enforcement / case law

- **Cosco Busan (2007)** — container ship struck the San Francisco–Oakland Bay Bridge and spilled bunker fuel in San Francisco Bay. The **United States is not a party to the Bunkers Convention** (nor to CLC); the casualty was litigated under **OPA 90** and illustrated precisely the gap that Bunkers fills internationally.
- **Rena (2011)** — Liberian-flagged container ship grounded on Astrolabe Reef off Tauranga, New Zealand, releasing roughly 350 tonnes of fuel oil. **First major application of the Bunkers Convention** (New Zealand acceded to Bunkers in 2013, after the casualty, but litigation and regulatory frameworks already reflected Bunkers principles); the casualty drove NZ accession.
- **Solar 1 (2006)** — small tanker that sank south of Guimaras Island, Philippines, releasing roughly 2,100 tonnes of bunker fuel. Pre-dating Bunkers entry-into-force; treated under transitional national-law regimes and CLC analogues.
- **MV Wakashio (2020)** — Japanese-operated bulk carrier grounded on the reef off Pointe d'Esny, Mauritius, releasing roughly 1,000 tonnes of fuel oil. **Bunkers Convention applied for the fuel-oil portion** (Mauritius is a party); operator Mitsui OSK Lines accepted civil-liability exposure under the Bunkers framework.

**Significant compensation-architecture gap**

- **IOPC Funds 1992** — the second-tier compensation fund for **CLC** does **not** cover Bunkers-Convention claims. Where a CLC casualty exhausts the shipowner's cap, the IOPC 1992 Fund tops up to roughly 203 million SDR; **no equivalent fund** exists for Bunkers casualties, and claims beyond the LLMC cap have no further route. Some IMO Legal Committee delegations have proposed expanding IOPC or creating a parallel Bunkers fund; no instrument has been adopted as of 2024.
