# HNS Convention 2010 — Hazardous and Noxious Substances Liability Convention

## Scope

The IMO **International Convention on Liability and Compensation for Damage in Connection with the Carriage of Hazardous and Noxious Substances by Sea (1996, as amended by the 2010 Protocol)** is the planned global treaty extending the **strict-liability + compulsory-insurance + IOPC-Funds-style three-tier compensation** regime — proven by [CLC 1992](./clc-1992.md) for persistent-oil cargo — to **HNS cargo**: the 6,500+ chemicals listed under the **IMDG Code**, **liquefied natural gas (LNG)**, **liquefied petroleum gas (LPG)**, bulk grain, bulk fertilizers, and other bulk hazardous solids carried at sea. The instrument closes the most material remaining gap in the global ship-source-pollution liability stack: crude/heavy fuel oil enjoys the CLC + IOPC Fund + Supplementary Fund three-tier compensation regime, but **chemical, LNG, and LPG cargoes** sit outside it — claims today fall through to **LLMC 1996** tonnage-based limitation plus the residue of national civil-liability law plus private commercial insurance, which is materially less coverage on a per-incident basis.

This page is the metadata-resolver companion to the existing concept page [`concepts/hns-convention-2010`](../concepts/hns-convention-2010.md). Pinned to the **HNS 1996 + 2010 Protocol consolidated** text. Amendment-status records that the **2010 Protocol** modified the unworkable 1996 LNG-side compulsory-insurance allocation and the 1996 bulk-cargo cargo-receivers levy mechanism. **The convention is not yet in force.**

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **HNS 1996** (the original convention) adopted at the **IMO Diplomatic Conference, London, 3 May 1996**. **Not in force** — never reached the entry-into-force threshold. The principal sticking points were the **LNG cargo-owner liability allocation** (1996 text made the cargo-owner, rather than the shipowner, liable for LNG, which the LNG sector treated as commercially unworkable) and the **bulk-cargo receivers' levy** (which large bulk-cargo importers resisted ratifying domestically).
- **2010 Protocol** adopted at the **IMO Diplomatic Conference, London, 30 April 2010**. The Protocol re-allocated LNG liability to the registered owner (aligning with the CLC 1992 channelling pattern) and refined the cargo-receivers' levy to address the implementation objections. **Not yet in force.**
- **Entry-into-force threshold**: ratification by **at least 12 states**, including 4 states each with not less than 2 million units of gross tonnage, with persons in those states having received during the preceding calendar year a total quantity of at least **40 million tonnes** of contributing cargo to the general account of the HNS Fund. The threshold is a **large-minority** rather than majority test, but combines state count, tonnage, and cargo-receipt volume — making it harder to clear than CLC 1969 was.
- **State-party count**: as of 2024, only a handful of states had ratified the 2010 Protocol; the convention remains substantially short of its entry-into-force threshold. Refer to the IMO catalog for the current ratification snapshot.

## Why it stalled

- **Bulk-cargo cargo-receivers' levy.** The HNS Fund (tier-2) is financed by a levy on receivers of more than 20,000 tons per year of HNS cargo in a state-party. Major bulk-chemical and bulk-fertilizer importing states have resisted creating the domestic legal infrastructure to identify, audit, and collect from those receivers — an order-of-magnitude harder administrative problem than the CLC/IOPC oil-receivers levy because HNS cargo spans 6,500+ distinct substances rather than the narrow set of "contributing oil" categories.
- **LNG-side liability allocation.** The original 1996 text channelled LNG liability to the **cargo-owner** (the title-holder at the time of the incident) rather than the **registered shipowner**. The LNG sector treated this as commercially unworkable: title can pass mid-voyage, cargo-owners may not carry the relevant P&I cover, and the allocation broke the CLC channelling-of-liability pattern that the convention otherwise mirrors. The 2010 Protocol reverted to **shipowner channelling**, but ratification momentum had already dissipated.
- **Implementation complexity.** The HNS Fund requires the IMO Secretariat (and each state-party's domestic implementing authority) to track 6,500+ distinct chemical substances under the IMDG Code, plus separate LNG and LPG sub-accounts, plus per-receiver volume reporting. The administrative burden is materially higher than the IOPC Funds' single contributing-oil category.
- **Industry resistance + insurer hesitation.** The LNG sector and the chemical-tanker sector have actively opposed ratification in major flag-states; P&I clubs and the International Group expressed early concerns about the insurability and certificate-issuance workflow under the original 1996 text, which slowed flag-state momentum even after the 2010 Protocol corrections.

## Three-tier compensation if in force

If the 2010 Protocol enters into force, HNS Convention 2010 would establish a compensation architecture **structurally parallel to** but **financially separate from** the CLC + IOPC Funds 1992 + Supplementary Fund 2003 regime. The cap figures below are **per-incident** and denominated in **Special Drawing Rights (SDR)** under the consolidated 1996 + 2010 Protocol text.

| Tier | Source | Per-incident cap |
|------|--------|------------------|
| 1 | HNS shipowner liability (tonnage-banded; compulsory insurance under Article 12) | 11.5 M – 100 M SDR |
| 2 | HNS Fund (financed by levies on cargo-receivers >20,000 tons HNS per year) | Up to 250 M SDR total (Tier 1 + Tier 2 combined) |
| 3 | _No third-tier supplementary fund yet established for HNS_ | n/a |

There is **no HNS-equivalent of the 2003 Supplementary Fund** at this stage. Should the 2010 Protocol enter into force and a major HNS incident exhaust the 250 M SDR combined ceiling — analogous to how the **Prestige (2002)** drove the creation of the Supplementary Fund — a follow-on protocol establishing tier-3 HNS supplementary compensation would likely be tabled at the IMO Legal Committee.

## Key Articles / Annexes

- **Article 1 — Definitions.** Defines "hazardous and noxious substances" by reference to the **IMDG Code**, **IBC Code** (chemical-tanker construction code), and **IGC Code** (gas-tanker construction code) carriage lists, plus separate carve-outs for LNG, LPG, bulk solids, and packaged dangerous goods.
- **Article 7 — Strict liability of the shipowner.** Mirrors CLC Article III channelling: claims for HNS damage may be brought only against the registered shipowner, with the same intent-or-recklessness gateway for cap-loss as CLC.
- **Article 11 — Limitation of liability.** Tonnage-banded SDR limit between 11.5 M and 100 M SDR per incident.
- **Article 12 — Compulsory insurance.** Owners of ships carrying HNS cargo must maintain insurance or financial security, evidenced by an HNS Insurance Certificate issued by the flag-state. Direct action against the insurer is permitted.
- **Articles 14–19 — HNS Fund.** Establishes the HNS Fund (tier-2), its general account + separate LNG / LPG / oil sub-accounts, the cargo-receivers' levy mechanism (>20,000 tons per year threshold), assembly governance, and claims-handling procedures. The sub-account structure is the principal structural deviation from the IOPC Funds 1992 single-account model.
- **Annexes I–IV — Cargo-classification references.** Cross-reference the IMDG Code (packaged dangerous goods), IBC Code (chemical tankers), IGC Code (gas tankers), and the bulk-grain / bulk-fertilizer + bulk hazardous-solids classifications. The Annexes are pointers to the operational codes — they do not reproduce them.

## Why the gap matters

- **Chemical-tanker incidents.** **Bow Mariner (2004)**, a Bahamas-flag chemical tanker, exploded and sank off the US Atlantic coast carrying ethanol cargo and ~3,200 tonnes of bunker fuel — under HNS the chemical-cargo loss would attach to a tier-1 + tier-2 regime; under the current legal stack it falls to LLMC 1996 plus US national law for the bunker layer. **Stolt Valor (2008)**, a chemical tanker, exploded in the Persian Gulf with significant chemical-cargo release; outside the HNS regime, claims fell through to flag-state national law plus private P&I cover.
- **LNG / LPG-tanker incidents.** Catastrophic LNG/LPG carrier incidents are rare — the sector's safety record is materially better than oil tankers — but the **per-incident consequence envelope** is large: cryogenic burns, asphyxiation hazards from boil-off vapour, BLEVE flammability under fire exposure, and shoreline-receiver-terminal exposure. A serious incident at an LNG receiving terminal or in a confined waterway would expose the absence of a dedicated HNS compensation tier.
- **Without HNS Convention in force**, HNS-cargo incidents fall under: **(i)** the ship-flag state's national civil-liability law, **(ii)** the **LLMC 1996** tonnage-based global limitation ceiling (which applies to all maritime claims not otherwise excluded), and **(iii)** private commercial insurance (P&I cover, cargo insurance). This stack lacks the **tier-2 industry-funded compensation pool** that has proven essential in the CLC/IOPC pattern when shipowner liability is exhausted, the owner is insolvent, or limitation rights are validly invoked.

**Parent / framework-precedent regimes**
- [`standards/clc-1992`](./clc-1992.md) — HNS Convention 2010 is **modelled on the CLC 1992 + IOPC Funds 1992 architecture**: strict liability channelled to the registered shipowner, tonnage-banded SDR caps, compulsory insurance evidenced by a state-issued certificate, and a tier-2 industry-funded compensation pool financed by cargo-receivers' levies. The HNS instrument is the planned chemical/LNG/LPG-cargo extension of the same template. **IOPC Funds 1992 do NOT cover HNS** — the HNS Fund is a sibling-but-separate pool with its own governance, its own assembly, and its own LNG/LPG/oil sub-account structure.

**Sibling pollution-liability instruments**
- [`standards/bunkers-convention-2001`](./bunkers-convention-2001.md) — sibling regime covering **bunker-fuel pollution from non-tankers**. The three-way split that HNS would complete: CLC = persistent oil **cargo** from tankers; Bunkers = bunker **fuel** from any other vessel >1,000 GT; HNS = chemical / LNG / LPG / bulk hazardous **cargo** from any vessel.
- [`standards/llmc-1996`](./llmc-1996.md) — the LLMC tonnage-based **global limitation ceiling** is the residual cap that currently applies to HNS-cargo claims in the absence of an in-force HNS Convention. If HNS enters into force, HNS owner liability would be subject to the HNS-specific Article 11 cap rather than LLMC, with the LLMC interlock following the CLC/LLMC Article 3(b) carve-out pattern.
- [`standards/opa-90`](./opa-90.md) — the **United States** is not a party to HNS (mirroring its non-party status under CLC). OPA-90 covers oil pollution domestically; for **non-oil hazardous substances**, the US-domestic equivalents are the **Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA)** and the **Clean Water Act § 311 hazardous-substances regime** rather than OPA-90 itself — a different scope split than the CLC/OPA-90 pairing.
- [`standards/marpol-73-78`](./marpol-73-78.md) — sibling **operational** regime: **Annex II** governs the operational discharge of noxious liquid substances in bulk (chemical tankers); **Annex III** governs harmful substances carried by sea in packaged form. MARPOL Annexes II/III are the operational counterpart to HNS Convention 2010's civil-liability layer.

**Companion compensation tier (sibling-but-separate fund)**
- **HNS Fund** (tier-2; established under Articles 14–19) — financed by levies on receivers of >20,000 tons HNS cargo per year, with separate LNG / LPG / oil sub-accounts plus a general account. Sibling to but **financially separate from** the IOPC Funds 1992 (which cover persistent oil only).

**Cargo-classification operational codes** _(referenced by Article 1 + Annexes I–IV)_
- **IMDG Code** — International Maritime Dangerous Goods Code. The 6,500+ HNS substance list is anchored to IMDG cargo classification.
- **IBC Code** — International Code for the Construction and Equipment of Ships Carrying Dangerous Chemicals in Bulk. Mandatory under SOLAS Ch. VII for chemical tankers.
- **IGC Code** — International Code for the Construction and Equipment of Ships Carrying Liquefied Gases in Bulk. Mandatory under SOLAS Ch. VII for LNG/LPG carriers.
