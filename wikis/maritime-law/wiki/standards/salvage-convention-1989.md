# International Convention on Salvage 1989

## Scope

The IMO **International Convention on Salvage 1989** (the "Salvage Convention") is the global treaty governing salvage operations at sea. It defines the rights and obligations of salvors, property owners (vessel + cargo), and their respective insurers, and replaces — between states-parties — the centuries-old regime under the **Brussels Salvage Convention 1910**.

The convention's defining innovation is the **Special Compensation** rule (Article 14), which entitles a salvor to compensation **regardless of success** when the operation prevents or minimises damage to the marine environment, coastal or inland waters, or natural resources adjacent thereto. This breaks with the centuries-old "no cure, no pay" maxim and creates a partial-compensation hybrid: full reward on success under Article 13, fallback environmental compensation under Article 14, capped at the salvor's expenses plus a 30 % uplift (or up to 100 % in exceptional cases of skilful salvage).

The page is pinned to the convention as adopted 1989 with awareness of the **SCOPIC 2000** contractual addendum (which substitutes Article 14 in practice for most LOF salvages — see below). There is no formal treaty amendment.

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **Adopted** at the IMO Diplomatic Conference, London, **28 April 1989**.
- **Entered into force** **14 July 1996** under Article 29, after 15 states deposited instruments of ratification.
- **State-party count**: 70+ states-parties as of the most recent IMO catalog snapshot (the convention is open to accession by any state).
- **Predecessor**: supersedes the **Brussels Salvage Convention 1910** between states-parties; the 1910 instrument continues to govern relations with non-parties.

## Doctrinal evolution timeline — from Brussels 1910 to LOF 2011

The 1989 Convention is the modernization waypoint in a longer doctrinal arc that began with the codification of pure no-cure-no-pay salvage in Brussels 1910 and continues through industry-contractual elaborations into the 2010s. The full timeline is the page-level navigator for understanding why Article 14 looks the way it does, what SCOPIC fixes, and which salvage incidents drove which step.

| Year | Instrument / event | Doctrinal contribution |
|------|---------------------|------------------------|
| **1910** | Brussels Convention on Assistance and Salvage at Sea | First codification of international salvage law; pure **"no cure, no pay"** rule; reward determined by danger, salvor effort, salved value; no environmental dimension |
| **1967** | [torrey-canyon-1967](../entities/torrey-canyon-1967.md) | Cornwall grounding + 119,000-tonne crude spill; first global wake-up that pure no-cure-no-pay disincentivized environmental-protection salvage; spawned MARPOL drafting and ultimately the Article 14 reform impulse |
| **1978** | [amoco-cadiz-1978](../entities/amoco-cadiz-1978.md) | Brittany VLCC grounding; 220,000-tonne spill; salvors abandoned the casualty when commercial reward looked unrecoverable; the canonical cited driver of Article 14 negotiation |
| **1980** | CMI Lisbon Draft | Comité Maritime International draft incorporating environmental-protection special compensation; the textual ancestor of Article 14; debated through the 1980s diplomatic conferences |
| **1989** | **IMO Salvage Convention 1989** | Treaty adoption (London, 28 April 1989); Article 14 environmental-special-compensation enters black-letter law; supersedes Brussels 1910 between parties |
| **1995** | **LOF 95** | Lloyd's Open Form revised to incorporate Article 14; first commercial-form integration of the new treaty regime |
| **1996** | Entry into force (Article 29) | Convention enters into force after 15-state ratification threshold |
| **1997** | [Nagasaki Spirit](../entities/) — *Semco Salvage v. Lancer Navigation* [1997] AC 455 (UK House of Lords) | Leading Article 14 interpretation; held "expenses fairly attributable" excludes profit element; salvors paid out-of-pocket only, no commercial-rate-of-return; widely seen as immediate driver of SCOPIC reform |
| **1999** | **SCOPIC clause v1** | Special Compensation P&I Club Clause introduced jointly by International Salvage Union (ISU) + International Group of P&I Clubs to substitute Article 14 in practice; **fixed daily-tariff** + uniform 25% bonus replaces Article 14(2) discretion |
| **2000** | **LOF 2000** | Lloyd's Open Form revised to incorporate SCOPIC as an opt-in clause; LOF 2000 + SCOPIC 2000 becomes the de-facto industry standard for LOF salvages |
| **2007** | **SCOPIC 2007 revision** | Tariff schedule refresh; Special Casualty Representative (SCR) procedural updates |
| **2011** | **LOF 2011 + SCOPIC 2011** | Current LOF generation (in continuing use); minor procedural refinements; preserves the 1989 Article 14 / SCOPIC dual-track architecture |
| **2014** | **SCOPIC 2014 (current revision)** | Tariff refresh; preserves daily-rate + 25% bonus + cap-at-property-salved-value architecture; remains the current text in 2025 LOF salvages |

The arc is **pure-success-only → success-OR-environmental → contractual-tariff substitution**. Each step has named-incident drivers (Torrey Canyon 1967 → Article 14 impulse; Nagasaki Spirit 1997 → SCOPIC creation; Erika 1999 / Prestige 2002 → SCOPIC 2007 refresh).

## Key Articles — doctrinal notes

The convention runs to 34 Articles. The most-cited operative provisions, with doctrinal gloss:

### Article 1 — Definitions

Defines `vessel`, `salvage operation`, `property`, `damage to the environment`, and `salvor` for the purposes of the convention. The **`damage to the environment`** definition is the doctrinal lynchpin of Article 14 — it is restricted to "substantial physical damage to human health or to marine life or resources in coastal or inland waters or areas adjacent thereto, caused by pollution, contamination, fire, explosion or similar major incidents." This restrictive definition (coastal or inland; not high-seas) bounds the geographical scope of Article 14 special compensation and was a contested negotiation point at the 1989 Conference.

### Article 6 — Salvage contracts

Validity of salvage contracts; jurisdiction of the flag-state and the master's authority to bind owners. **Master's actual or implied authority** to enter into a salvage contract on behalf of the owner is a load-bearing point — Lloyd's Open Form is signed by the master, not the owner, in nearly every case; Article 6(2) preserves the master's traditional authority. Article 6(3) preserves the right of any party to seek annulment or modification on grounds of unfair influence or unequal bargaining power (the "salvage-extortion" carve-out).

### Article 8 — Duties of the salvor and the owner

Mutual cooperation duties. The salvor owes a duty of due care to the salved property and a duty to **exercise due care to prevent or minimise damage to the environment** (Art. 8(1)(b)) — this is the **statutory hook** that justifies Article 14 compensation. The owner owes a duty to render reasonable assistance, comply with salvor's reasonable requests, and cooperate fully with the salvor.

### Article 12 — Reward

The classical "no cure, no pay" rule — a reward is payable **only on a useful result**. Article 12(1): "Salvage operations which have had a useful result give right to a reward." Article 12(2): "Except as otherwise provided, no payment is due under this Convention if the salvage operations have had no useful result." Article 14 is the exception; everything else under the convention is success-conditional.

### Article 13 — Criteria for fixing the reward

Ten enumerated factors govern reward calculation:

1. Salved value of the vessel and other property
2. Skill and efforts of the salvors in **preventing or minimising damage to the environment**
3. Measure of success obtained by the salvor
4. Nature and degree of the danger
5. Skill and efforts of the salvors in salving the vessel, other property, and life
6. Time used and expenses + losses incurred by the salvors
7. Risk of liability and other risks run by the salvors or their equipment
8. Promptness of the services rendered
9. Availability and use of vessels or other equipment intended for salvage operations
10. State of readiness and efficiency of the salvor's equipment + the value thereof

Note that **factor 2** is the embedded environmental-protection consideration *within* the success-based reward — this is conceptually distinct from Article 14, which provides a fallback when factors 1–10 do not yield enough. Practitioners sometimes refer to factor 2 as "Article 13 environmental uplift" to distinguish it from Article 14 special compensation.

### Article 14 — Special Compensation (the doctrinal centerpiece)

The convention's defining innovation. Where salvors carry out a salvage operation in respect of a vessel which by itself or its cargo threatened damage to the environment, and the Article 13 reward fails to cover their expenses, the salvor is entitled to **special compensation from the owner equivalent to the salvor's expenses** (Art. 14(1)).

Where the salvor actually **prevents or minimises** environmental damage, the special compensation may be increased by up to **30 %** of expenses, or by up to **100 %** in exceptional circumstances of skilful salvage (Art. 14(2)).

Article 14(3) defines "salvor's expenses" as out-of-pocket reasonably incurred + a fair rate for equipment and personnel **actually and reasonably used** in the operation — the **Nagasaki Spirit** ruling held that "fair rate" is a reimbursement-rate not a profit-rate, which became the immediate trigger for SCOPIC reform.

Article 14(4) makes the special compensation due **only to the extent that it exceeds any reward recoverable under Article 13** — so Article 14 operates as a top-up against the Article 13 reward, not as an independent claim.

Article 14(5) bars special compensation where the salvor has been **negligent** in failing to prevent or minimise environmental damage — the misconduct cap.

Article 14(6) provides that the total special compensation may not exceed the limits established under Article 13 reward — i.e. the salved value caps Article 14 just as it caps Article 13.

### Article 18 — Misconduct

Bars or reduces salvage reward and special compensation where the salvor's negligence has caused or contributed to the casualty, or where the salvor has been guilty of fraud or other dishonest conduct. The misconduct cap operates at the reward-fixing stage, not as a separate cause of action.

## SCOPIC 2000 contractual replacement — opt-in mechanics

The **Special Compensation P&I Club Clause (SCOPIC)** is a **contractual mechanism** introduced in **2000** by the International Salvage Union together with the International Group of P&I Clubs to replace Article 14 in practice for salvors operating under **Lloyd's Open Form (LOF)**. SCOPIC has substantially **replaced Article 14 in practice** for an estimated 70–90 % of LOF salvages since 2000.

SCOPIC operates under an **opt-in** structure that runs alongside, not instead of, the underlying treaty:

| Decision point | SCOPIC mechanic |
|----------------|-----------------|
| LOF execution | Property owners + salvor sign LOF; SCOPIC clause is a checkbox addendum, opt-in by salvor only (not by owner) |
| Salvor invokes SCOPIC | Written notice to property owner; **SCOPIC is invoked by salvor**, who reads early-stage operational signals on whether Article 13 is going to suffice |
| P&I club guarantee | Within 2 working days of SCOPIC invocation, the property underwriters' P&I club must post a USD 3 million security guarantee (initial level; can be adjusted upward by SCR) |
| Salvor compensation framework | **Daily tariff** for tugs + equipment + personnel as published in SCOPIC schedules; fixed-rate replacing Article 14(3) "fair rate" interpretive uncertainty |
| Bonus | Uniform **25 %** bonus on tariff (replaces Article 14(2) discretionary 30%/100% uplift band) |
| Article 13 vs SCOPIC choice | At final award, the salvor takes the higher of Article 13 reward or SCOPIC tariff (with deductions if SCOPIC was invoked but Article 13 won) |
| Discount for premature SCOPIC invocation | If salvor invokes SCOPIC and the Article 13 reward ultimately exceeds it, the Article 13 reward is reduced by 25% of the difference (the "S-clause discount") to discipline against premature invocation |
| Special Casualty Representative (SCR) | A salvage-arbitrator-appointed neutral observer who attends on-scene, reports daily to all stakeholders, and acts as procedural buffer — distinct from the LOF arbitrator |

The dual-track architecture is **opt-in vs opt-out**:

- **Article 14 (treaty)** is opt-out — it applies automatically wherever the salvage falls within scope and the convention is in force between the parties; only an express contractual exclusion can disapply it.
- **SCOPIC (contractual)** is opt-in — it applies only where (a) LOF incorporates the SCOPIC clause and (b) the salvor invokes it.

Practitioners cite three architectural distinctions:

1. **Daily-tariff vs expenses-only.** SCOPIC pays a published-schedule daily rate for tugs, divers, ROV, and personnel — fixed-tariff, no dispute over what was "fair" at incident-time. Article 14 pays only documented expenses + a "fair rate" exposed to the Nagasaki Spirit interpretive risk.
2. **Commercial-rate-of-return vs out-of-pocket framework.** The 25% SCOPIC bonus is a commercial profit margin built into the tariff. Article 14 (post-Nagasaki Spirit) is an out-of-pocket reimbursement framework with no profit element — a 30%/100% uplift on zero-profit reimbursement is still anchored to expenses, not to a commercial rate of return.
3. **Predictability vs upside.** Salvors choose SCOPIC for income predictability; they decline (or invoke late) when an exceptional-skill Article 14(2) 100% uplift looks plausible. The 25% S-clause discount disciplines this strategic choice.

The treaty obligation remains Article 14 — SCOPIC operates as **contractual elaboration, not treaty amendment**. A non-LOF salvage, or a LOF salvage where the SCOPIC clause is not triggered, falls back to the bare Article 14 framework.

## Salvage 1989 vs Nairobi WRC 2007 — boundary doctrine

The Salvage Convention covers **in-progress salvage**: the casualty is afloat or refloatable, the salvor's operation aims to prevent total loss or environmental damage, and the salved property has commercial value. The **[nairobi-wrc-2007](./nairobi-wrc-2007.md)** Wreck Removal Convention covers **post-incident wreck removal**: the casualty is sunk, beached, or constructively a total loss; the operation aims to remove a hazard to navigation or the environment; and there is typically no salvable commercial value.

| Dimension | Salvage Convention 1989 | Nairobi WRC 2007 |
|-----------|--------------------------|------------------|
| Triggering condition | Vessel in danger; salvable property | Wreck constituting a hazard |
| Operation type | Refloating, towing, environmental intervention | Marking, removal, disposal |
| Compensation framework | Article 13 reward + Article 14 special compensation (or SCOPIC) | Owner-pays cost-recovery + mandatory wreck-removal insurance |
| Liability principle | Reward-based (incentive) | Strict-liability cost-recovery |
| Insurance interface | P&I club + hull underwriters | **Compulsory insurance** under WRC Art. 12 (≥300 GT) |
| Geographic scope | Global (where convention applies) | Convention Area = EEZ + opt-in territorial sea |
| State-party coverage as of 2024 | 70+ | 60+ |

The boundary is operationally fluid in casualties that transition from rescue to abandonment to wreck removal — see *X-Press Pearl* 2021 (Sri Lanka), where initial salvage attempts under LOF transitioned to wreck-removal once total loss was declared.

## Worked-incident anchors

### Article 14 driver

- **[amoco-cadiz-1978](../entities/amoco-cadiz-1978.md), Brittany.** VLCC grounding off the French coast; ~220,000 tonnes of crude oil released; salvors initially declined to engage on the basis that the casualty looked likely unrecoverable and pure no-cure-no-pay would yield zero. The case crystallized the policy argument that pure no-cure-no-pay structurally **disincentivized salvors from intervening early in environmentally-threatening casualties** and was the cited driver of the CMI Lisbon Draft 1980 → Article 14 1989 sequence. Amoco Cadiz remains the canonical Article 14 origin-story incident in salvage-law literature.

### Article 14 interpretation crisis driver — SCOPIC origin

- **Nagasaki Spirit 1997, Strait of Malacca.** Collision between *Nagasaki Spirit* and *Ocean Blessing* in the Strait of Malacca; salvors engaged under LOF; environmental-damage threat was substantial. In *Semco Salvage v. Lancer Navigation* [1997] AC 455 the UK House of Lords held that "expenses fairly attributable" under Article 14(3) excluded any profit element — salvors are paid out-of-pocket plus the discretionary uplift, but no commercial rate of return was built into the baseline. The ruling triggered industry alarm: salvors argued (with International Group support) that the post-Nagasaki Spirit Article 14 framework removed the commercial-rate-of-return that justified investment in standby fleet and specialized equipment. The **SCOPIC 2000 clause** was the joint ISU/IG response, fixing a daily-tariff + 25% bonus framework that restored predictable commercial returns. Nagasaki Spirit is the proximate driver of SCOPIC and is cited in nearly every modern Article 14 commentary.

### Pre-1989 reward-architecture driver

- **Tojo Maru 1972, AC 242 (UK House of Lords).** Pre-1989 salvage incident where a salvor's diver-employee negligently fired an explosive bolt that ruptured a tank, causing further damage. The House of Lords held the salvor liable in tort despite the salvage relationship — establishing that salvage **does not insulate the salvor from negligence liability** for damage caused during the operation. Tojo Maru shaped the negotiation of Article 18 (misconduct) and continues to govern non-Article-14 salvor-liability questions where the convention is silent.

### Modern environmental-special-compensation case

- **[mv-wakashio-2020](../entities/mv-wakashio-2020.md), Mauritius.** Bulk carrier grounding on Pointe d'Esny coral reef; ~1,000 tonnes of fuel oil released; significant environmental damage to the Blue Bay Marine Park. Salvage operation under SMIT-led contract; the case sits at the intersection of Salvage Convention Article 14 / SCOPIC, [bunkers-convention-2001](./bunkers-convention-2001.md) bunker-spill liability, and the [nairobi-wrc-2007](./nairobi-wrc-2007.md) wreck-removal regime once the vessel was declared a constructive total loss and broken in two for removal. The case illustrates the multi-instrument layering of modern salvage-environmental-wreck casualties.

### Solomon Trader environmental-special-compensation case

- **Solomon Trader 2019, Solomon Islands.** Bulk carrier grounded on Rennell Island reef; bunker-fuel release; salvage operation invoked Article 14 / SCOPIC framework with substantial environmental-protection effort in coral-reef ecosystem. Cited in salvage-industry literature as a modern test of Article 14 environmental-special-compensation scope when the affected area is a remote-Pacific coral-atoll ecosystem outside conventional shipping lanes.

### Place-of-refuge interface case

- **Stena Polaris (Article 14 application).** Modern Mediterranean tanker incident where Article 14 special compensation was litigated alongside coastal-state place-of-refuge refusal; illustrates the doctrinal interaction between Salvage Convention Article 14 and **place-of-refuge** doctrine — the more difficult the coastal-state response, the longer the salvage duration and the larger the Article 14 / SCOPIC bill. The case feeds into broader [unclos-1982](./unclos-1982.md) Articles 221 + 225 coastal-state intervention doctrine.

## Practitioner stakeholder map

Salvage Convention compliance and practice involves a multi-stakeholder system structurally distinct from most maritime treaties because it operates principally through **contractual** rather than regulatory channels:

| Stakeholder | Primary instruments | Role |
|-------------|----------------------|------|
| **Salvors (commercial salvage companies — ISU members)** | LOF + SCOPIC + Articles 13, 14 | Sign LOF on master's invitation; invoke SCOPIC where appropriate; submit claim to Lloyd's Salvage Arbitration; international fleet under ISU coordination |
| **Shipowners** | LOF + Articles 6, 8, 13, 14, 18 | Bound by LOF signed by master under Article 6(2); pay reward under Article 13 / special compensation under Article 14; insurance-financed via H&M + P&I |
| **P&I clubs (International Group)** | SCOPIC + Article 14 | Post property-side guarantee on SCOPIC invocation; ultimate financial bearer of Article 14 special compensation through Pollution Cover; co-author of SCOPIC framework |
| **Hull + Machinery underwriters** | LOF + Article 13 | Bear Article 13 reward (success-based) via H&M policy under Institute Time Clauses subrogation; cargo proportion settled via cargo underwriters |
| **Cargo interests + cargo underwriters** | Article 13 + Article 17 (cooperation) | Cargo proportion of Article 13 reward; cooperation duty under Article 17; settlement via cargo underwriters and General Average adjusters where invoked |
| **Coastal states** | Article 9 (rights of coastal states) + UNCLOS Articles 221, 225 | Right of intervention to prevent pollution; place-of-refuge decisions affect salvage operation feasibility and duration |
| **Flag states** | Article 6 + UNCLOS Article 94 | Constitutional jurisdiction over master's authority to bind owners; flag-state law governs LOF interpretation absent contractual choice |
| **Lloyd's Salvage Arbitration Branch (SCAB)** | LOF + SCOPIC + Articles 13, 14 | London-based arbitration forum specified in standard LOF; sole-arbitrator + Appeal Arbitrator structure; produces the principal modern body of salvage-law authority |
| **Special Casualty Representative (SCR)** | SCOPIC | On-scene neutral observer appointed under SCOPIC; daily reports to all stakeholders; procedural buffer between salvor and property interests during operation |
| **International Salvage Union (ISU)** | LOF + SCOPIC | Industry association of commercial salvors; co-author of SCOPIC; published annual Pollution Prevention Survey quantifying environmental-protection impact |
| **CMI (Comité Maritime International)** | Convention drafting + ongoing reform | Authored Lisbon Draft 1980; continues to host Salvage Working Group for reform proposals (e.g., the 2017 ISU "fair-treatment-of-salvors" reform discussion) |
| **IMO Legal Committee** | Convention text + amendment proposals | Treaty custodian; convention has not been formally amended, but Legal Committee periodically considers reform questions raised by ISU + CMI |
