# International Convention on Salvage 1989

## Scope

The IMO **International Convention on Salvage 1989** (the "Salvage Convention") is the global treaty governing salvage operations at sea. It defines the rights and obligations of salvors, property owners (vessel + cargo), and their respective insurers, and replaces — between states-parties — the centuries-old regime under the **Brussels Salvage Convention 1910**.

The convention's defining innovation is the **Special Compensation** rule (Article 14), which entitles a salvor to compensation **regardless of success** when the operation prevents or minimises damage to the marine environment, coastal or inland waters, or natural resources adjacent thereto. This breaks with the centuries-old "no cure, no pay" maxim and creates a partial-compensation hybrid: full reward on success under Article 13, fallback environmental compensation under Article 14, capped at the salvor's expenses plus a 30 % uplift (or up to 100 % in exceptional cases of skilful salvage).

The page is pinned to the convention as adopted 1989 with awareness of the **SCOPIC 2000** contractual addendum (which substitutes Article 14 in practice for most LOF salvages — see below). There is no formal treaty amendment.

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **Adopted** at the IMO Diplomatic Conference, London, **28 April 1989**.
- **Entered into force** **14 July 1996** under Article 29, after 15 states deposited instruments of ratification.
- **State-party count**: 70+ states-parties as of the most recent IMO catalog snapshot (the convention is open to accession by any state).
- **Predecessor**: supersedes the **Brussels Salvage Convention 1910** between states-parties; the 1910 instrument continues to govern relations with non-parties.

## Key Articles

The convention runs to 34 Articles. The most-cited operative provisions:

- **Article 1 — Definitions.** Defines `vessel`, `salvage operation`, `property`, `damage to the environment`, and `salvor` for the purposes of the convention.
- **Article 6 — Salvage contracts.** Validity of salvage contracts; jurisdiction of the flag-state and the master's authority to bind owners.
- **Article 8 — Duties of the salvor and the owner.** Mutual cooperation duties, including the salvor's duty of due care and the owner's duty to render reasonable assistance.
- **Article 12 — Reward.** The classical "no cure, no pay" rule — a reward is payable **only on a useful result**.
- **Article 13 — Criteria for fixing the reward.** Ten enumerated factors: salved value of vessel and other property; skill and efforts of the salvors in preventing or minimising environmental damage; measure of success obtained by the salvor; nature and degree of the danger; salvors' skill and efforts in salving the vessel, other property, and life; time used and expenses + losses incurred; risk of liability; promptness of services; availability and use of vessels intended for salvage; and state of readiness and efficiency of the salvor's equipment + their value.
- **Article 14 — Special Compensation.** The convention's defining innovation: where salvors carry out a salvage operation in respect of a vessel which by itself or its cargo threatened damage to the environment, and the Article 13 reward fails to cover their expenses, the salvor is entitled to special compensation from the owner equivalent to the salvor's expenses (Art. 14(1)). Where the salvor actually prevents or minimises environmental damage, the special compensation may be increased by up to **30 %** of expenses, or by up to **100 %** in exceptional circumstances of skilful salvage (Art. 14(2)).
- **Article 17 — Cooperation.** Duty of all parties (salvors, owners, port authorities, coastal state) to cooperate during salvage.

## SCOPIC 2000 contractual replacement

- The **Special Compensation P&I Club Clause (SCOPIC)** is a **contractual mechanism** introduced in **2000** by the International Salvage Union together with the International Group of P&I Clubs to replace Article 14 in practice for salvors operating under **Lloyd's Open Form (LOF)**.
- SCOPIC introduces a **fixed-tariff method**: salvors invoke the SCOPIC clause when LOF rewards under Article 13 appear insufficient; the property underwriters' P&I club posts a guarantee at LOF execution; the salvor's compensation is then determined by a **published tariff plus a uniform 25 % bonus**, rather than the discretionary "fair rate plus uplift" calculus of Article 14(2).
- SCOPIC has substantially **replaced Article 14 in practice** for an estimated 70–90 % of LOF salvages since 2000. It is now in its current revision (SCOPIC 2014, refreshed periodically) and is the de-facto industry standard for environmental-salvage compensation, even though the underlying **treaty obligation remains Article 14** — SCOPIC operates as contractual elaboration, not treaty amendment.

## Case law citing this instrument

- **The Nagasaki Spirit (1997) AC 455** (UK House of Lords) — leading interpretation of Article 14, holding that "expenses fairly attributable" did not include a profit element; widely seen as the immediate driver of the **SCOPIC 2000** reform. See `../entities/` for the case if a digest page lands.
- **The Cape Bari (2016) UKPC 20** — Privy Council on application of Article 13 reward criteria in modern salvages and the interaction with limitation-of-liability clauses.
- **The Castor (2002)** — Mediterranean / Spanish litigation on refusal of place-of-refuge and the consequent salvage-and-environmental interplay.
- **The Erika (2000–2008)** — French post-spill litigation arising from the 1999 grounding; environmental-compensation interface between the Salvage Convention, CLC, and the IOPC Funds. See [[mv-erika-1999]](../entities/mv-erika-1999.md).
- **The Sea Empress (1996)** — Milford Haven grounding; salvors' claims against port authority became the subject of subsequent UK litigation. See [[sea-empress-1996]](../entities/sea-empress-1996.md).
