# Paris MoU — Memorandum of Understanding on Port State Control

## Scope

The Paris MoU is a regional Memorandum of Understanding establishing a harmonized port-state-control (PSC) inspection regime among **27 maritime authorities** — EU member states plus Canada, Croatia, Iceland, Norway, the Russian Federation, and the United Kingdom. It is an **administrative agreement among port-state authorities**, not a treaty and not a private codification; the controlled-vocabulary `instrument_type` for this page is therefore `mou`.

The MoU implements port-state-control authority sourced from a stack of underlying conventions:
- **SOLAS** Chapter XI (special measures to enhance maritime safety)
- **MARPOL** Article 5(2) (port-state inspection of certificates and the ship)
- **MLC 2006** Title 5 (compliance and enforcement, including port-state inspection)
- **ILO C147** (Merchant Shipping (Minimum Standards) Convention)
- **COLREG** and **Load Lines** Convention

The operational goal is to **detect substandard ships and apply detention or banning sanctions** so that flag-state non-compliance is made costly through the port-call channel rather than through flag-state enforcement (which is historically weak for open registries).

This page is pinned to the **2024 consolidated edition**, with the most recent material amendments covering cybersecurity inspection items and integration of EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) environmental tier verification.

## Adoption + signatory states

- **Adopted**: Paris, **26 January 1982**, at a ministerial conference of European maritime authorities.
- **In force**: **1 July 1982**.
- **Current parties**: 27 maritime authorities (Belgium, Bulgaria, Canada, Croatia, Cyprus, Denmark, Estonia, Finland, France, Germany, Greece, Iceland, Ireland, Italy, Latvia, Lithuania, Malta, Netherlands, Norway, Poland, Portugal, Romania, Russian Federation, Slovenia, Spain, Sweden, United Kingdom).
- **Sister organization**: the **Tokyo MoU** (1993) implements a parallel PSC regime across the Asia-Pacific region. It is a future-promotion candidate for its own standards page on this wiki.

For non-treaty MoU instruments such as this, the equivalent of "adoption resolution + entry-into-force threshold" is the ministerial-conference signature plus the date the inspection regime began operating among the original signatories.

## Inspection regime

- **Risk-Based Targeting Matrix (RBTM)** — each ship is rated on performance history, flag-state list position, classification-society (recognised-organisation) performance, ship type, and age. **High-Risk Ships (HRS)** are inspected more frequently; **Low-Risk Ships (LRS)** are inspected less. This is the operational evolution of the New Inspection Regime introduced in 2011.
- **Initial inspection** — documents and visual external check: ship classification certificate, MARPOL/SOLAS/MLC/Load Lines/STCW certificates, crew documents.
- **More-detailed inspection** — triggered by **clear grounds**: deficient documents, unsafe practices observed during the initial inspection, ship-detention history, and physical verification of deadweight, draft, and freeboard.
- **Detention** — when deficiencies present a clear hazard to safety, health, or the environment, the ship cannot leave port until the deficiencies are rectified. Each detention is recorded in the public PSC database.
- **Banning** — repeat offenders may be banned from MoU ports under a three-strike progression: typically **3 months → 1 year → permanent** banning, calibrated by the ship's flag-list tier and prior-detention pattern.

## White / Grey / Black flag-state lists

- **Annual ranking** of flag states by deficiency-detection rate and detention rate observed during MoU port calls over a rolling three-year window.
- **White list** — best-performing flags (illustratively: Marshall Islands, Singapore, Liberia, United Kingdom, Norway). Subject to less-frequent inspection.
- **Grey list** — medium-performance flags; transitional tier.
- **Black list** — worst-performing flags (often small open-registry "flags of convenience" — illustratively: Belize, Sierra Leone, Tanzania, Tuvalu). Inspected at higher rates; functions as a quasi-blacklist and feeds into the banning progression.
- **Re-rating annually** — flag states can move between lists year to year, which gives the regime its incentive structure: poor performance causes commercial pain (more inspection, more detention risk, harder port access for owners flying that flag), and improvement is rewarded with reduced inspection burden.

## Ship-type targeting

The MoU applies an **enhanced inspection regime** to ship types whose failure modes have outsized safety or pollution consequences:

- Bulk carriers
- Oil tankers
- Gas tankers
- Chemical tankers
- Passenger vessels
- Ro-ro passenger ferries

These types attract more frequent inspection independent of flag-list tier and trigger expanded inspection scope (structural surveys for bulk carriers, cargo-system surveys for tankers, life-saving and evacuation surveys for passenger vessels).

## Major incidents driving MoU evolution

- **Erika (1999/2000)** — Maltese-flag oil tanker that broke up off Brittany, France, causing a major oil spill. Drove the EU **Erika I, Erika II, and Erika III** legislative packages, which tightened the Paris MoU framework, accelerated the phase-out of single-hull tankers, and raised inspection-target rates and classification-society oversight.
- **Prestige (2002)** — Bahamas-flag oil tanker that sank off Galicia, Spain, after Spanish authorities denied it a place of refuge. Drove the **Erika III** package and the EU place-of-refuge directive, which sits at the port-state-control / coastal-state interface.
- **Bulk-carrier sinkings** — *Derbyshire* (1980) and *Marika* (1981), among others, drove the **SOLAS Chapter XII** bulk-carrier amendments. Paris MoU then operationalised those amendments via its bulk-carrier enhanced-inspection regime.
