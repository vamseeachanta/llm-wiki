# Mediterranean MoU — Mediterranean Memorandum of Understanding on Port State Control

## Scope

The Mediterranean MoU is an **administrative arrangement among 12 maritime authorities** — Algeria, Cyprus, Egypt, Israel, Jordan, Lebanon, Malta, Morocco, Tunisia, and Turkey, with the European Commission participating as observer, and several other North African states holding associated-member status — coordinating port-state-control (PSC) inspections of foreign-flag vessels visiting Mediterranean ports. It is an administrative agreement among port-state authorities, not a treaty and not a private codification; the controlled-vocabulary `instrument_type` for this page is therefore `mou`.

The geographic scope spans the **Mediterranean Sea**, **adjacent Atlantic coastal portions** (Moroccan Atlantic seaboard, the western approaches to the Strait of Gibraltar), and **Red Sea coastal portions** at the Suez/Egyptian interface. Member-state coverage straddles the **southern European, Levantine, and North African shores**.

The geographic-overlap structure is distinctive: most Mediterranean MoU members are simultaneously **Paris MoU full members for their EU port-state operations** (Cyprus, Malta, plus the EU Commission), so the Mediterranean MoU primarily extends harmonised PSC discipline into the **North African and Levantine port-states** — Algeria, Egypt, Israel, Jordan, Lebanon, Morocco, Tunisia, and Turkey — that handle significant cargo, cruise, and intra-Mediterranean ferry traffic outside the Paris MoU footprint.

The operational goals are to:

- **Detect and correct sub-standard ships** through a risk-based regional inspection regime aligned with Paris MoU methodology;
- **Address chokepoint and high-density-passenger-traffic concerns** unique to the Mediterranean basin (Strait of Gibraltar transit, Suez Canal approaches, Strait of Bonifacio, Strait of Messina, intra-regional ferry network), with attendant focus on SOLAS passenger-ship safety, MARPOL Annex I/V compliance, and ISPS post-Arab-Spring security verification;
- **Harmonize regional inspection priorities** with the adjacent Paris MoU (with which it shares most EU member-state authorities) and through joint CICs with the Tokyo MoU since 2010.

The Mediterranean MoU is the structural sibling of the Paris MoU and Tokyo MoU and operationalises the same underlying source-authority stack — SOLAS Chapter XI, MARPOL Article 5(2), MLC 2006 Title 5, ILO C147, COLREGs, and the Load Lines Convention — across the Mediterranean basin.

## Adoption + entry-into-force

- **Founded**: Malta, **11 July 1997**, at a ministerial conference of Mediterranean rim maritime authorities.
- **Entry into force**: **1 December 1998**, when the inspection regime began operating among the original signatories.
- **Current parties**: 12 member authorities as of 2024, with the European Commission participating as observer.
- **Methodology track**: the Mediterranean MoU has been a Paris MoU follower in inspection methodology — NIR-equivalent risk-based selection has been adopted post-2014, with prior White/Grey/Black-list ranking informing flag-state targeting decisions during the transition.

For non-treaty MoU instruments such as this, the equivalent of "adoption resolution + entry-into-force threshold" is the ministerial-conference signature plus the date the inspection regime began operating among the original signatories.

## Member authorities + flag-state context

- **Members (12)**: Algeria, Cyprus, Egypt, Israel, Jordan, Lebanon, Malta, Morocco, Tunisia, Turkey, plus the European Commission as observer; several North African states hold associated-member status. Several Mediterranean port-states have **dual MoU memberships** — EU member states (Cyprus, Malta) are full Paris MoU members and operate Mediterranean MoU activity in coordination with their Paris MoU obligations rather than as their primary PSC framework.
- **Critical-chokepoint coverage**: Mediterranean MoU member states ring four of the most consequential maritime chokepoints in the Mediterranean basin — the **Strait of Gibraltar** (~50,000 ships per year transit), the **Suez Canal approaches** (~12% of global trade by volume passes through Suez), the **Strait of Bonifacio** (between Corsica and Sardinia), and the **Strait of Messina** (between Sicily and the Italian peninsula).
- **Heavy cruise + ferry traffic**: the Mediterranean cruise season runs **April–October** and drives a passenger-ship-focused inspection emphasis broadly comparable to the Caribbean MoU's cruise-density orientation; an extensive **intra-Mediterranean ferry network** (Italy–Greece, Italy–Tunisia, Spain–Morocco, Greece–Turkey, Adriatic ro-pax routes) further reinforces passenger-ship priority.
- **Refugee-crisis maritime operations**: from **2014 onwards**, member-authority coordination on humanitarian and safety-of-life-at-sea operations in the central and eastern Mediterranean has shaped both inspection priorities (ISPS interface, lifesaving-equipment compliance) and the regime's interaction with Frontex, EUNAVFOR Med, and national coast guards.

## Inspection priorities

- **Cruise and passenger-ship-focused Concentrated Inspection Campaigns (CICs)**, structurally similar to the Caribbean MoU's emphasis — driven by the volume of cruise calls during the April–October season and by post-Costa-Concordia regulatory tightening.
- **Ferry safety**: the Mediterranean intra-regional ferry network is high-density and high-frequency; the **Costa Concordia (2012)** grounding and the **Norman Atlantic (2014)** Adriatic ferry fire drove ferry-PSC tightening across both Mediterranean MoU and Paris MoU activity, with concentrated attention to fire-safety systems, evacuation arrangements, and stability documentation.
- **Joint CIC participation** with the Paris MoU since founding (most members operate dual programmes) and with the Tokyo MoU since approximately 2010, giving Mediterranean MoU CICs alignment with global rotating themes (e.g. fire-safety systems, STCW hours-of-rest, stability and cargo-securing) while preserving regionally-salient stand-alone passenger-ship campaigns.
- **Maritime-security emphasis post-Arab Spring (2011)**: enhanced ISPS-Code compliance verification became a Mediterranean MoU priority following the 2011 political transitions across North Africa and the Levant, with attention to ship security plans, port-facility-security-officer interfaces, and crew security-training records on vessels calling at member ports after transits through unstable coastal regions.

## Notable enforcement / events

- **Costa Concordia (2012, Italy)** — the grounding off Isola del Giglio drove Mediterranean MoU and Paris MoU tightening of cruise-ship PSC, with renewed inspection emphasis on Bridge Team Management (BTM) under STCW, voyage-planning documentation, and passenger-mustering arrangements.
- **Norman Atlantic (2014, Adriatic ferry fire)** — the ro-pax fire incident drove a coordinated Mediterranean MoU and Paris MoU CIC on fire-safety systems, including fire-detection coverage, fixed fire-fighting installations, and crew fire-drill records.
- **2014+ Mediterranean refugee crisis** — humanitarian operations coordination across the central and eastern Mediterranean shaped member-authority interaction with PSC at the ISPS interface, with attention to vessel-security-plan provisions for unscheduled rescue operations and to crew-welfare implications under MLC 2006 Titles 2 and 4 for ships engaged in extended search-and-rescue activity.
- **2020+ COVID-19 pandemic** — port-state crew-change protocols were coordinated with adjacent MoUs, with Mediterranean MoU members applying MLC 2006 Title 2 and Title 4 inspection emphasis on maximum service period overruns, repatriation arrangements, and onboard medical-care provisions.
