# M/V *Saiga* Cases (No 1 and No 2): Saint Vincent and the Grenadines v Guinea (ITLOS 1997-1999)

## Case overview

**Facts.** The *Saiga* was a small oil tanker registered in Saint Vincent and the Grenadines and operated as an offshore bunkering vessel supplying fuel to fishing vessels off the West African coast. On 28 October 1997, while bunkering in the exclusive economic zone of Guinea (and, on Saint Vincent's account, beyond Guinea's contiguous zone), the vessel was arrested by Guinean customs patrol boats. Guinean authorities asserted that the *Saiga* had violated Guinean customs laws by supplying duty-unpaid fuel within the customs radius of Guinea, claiming a right to enforce customs jurisdiction extra-territorially in the EEZ. Crew members were detained, the ship and its cargo seized, and a criminal prosecution initiated. Saint Vincent and the Grenadines instituted proceedings against Guinea before the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea (ITLOS), Hamburg.

**Issue.** Two related but procedurally distinct disputes arose: first, whether ITLOS had jurisdiction under UNCLOS Article 292 to order prompt release of the *Saiga* and its crew on the posting of a reasonable bond; second, on the merits, whether Guinea's arrest, prosecution, and use of force against the *Saiga* violated UNCLOS, including its provisions on flag-state jurisdiction (Article 91), exclusive economic zone enforcement, and the limited customary right of hot pursuit (Article 111).

## Procedural history

- **ITLOS *Saiga* (No 1) — 4 December 1997** — First substantive judgment of the newly-constituted ITLOS. Applying UNCLOS Article 292, the Tribunal ordered Guinea to release the *Saiga* and its crew upon posting of a reasonable bond, fixed at approximately USD 400,000 (cargo value plus a security component). Established the operational template for prompt-release proceedings.
- **ITLOS *Saiga* (No 2) — 1 July 1999** — Merits judgment on Saint Vincent's substantive claims. The Tribunal held that Guinea had violated UNCLOS in arresting the *Saiga*, rejected Guinea's defence based on the customary doctrine of hot pursuit (Article 111) for failure to satisfy continuity and signal requirements, declined to allow Guinea to challenge the *Saiga*'s nationality under Article 91 ("genuine link"), and awarded approximately USD 2.1 million in damages to Saint Vincent for injury to the vessel, cargo loss, crew injury, and humiliation of the master.

## Key legal holdings

- **Prompt release jurisdiction (UNCLOS Article 292).** Confirmed that ITLOS's prompt-release jurisdiction is a fast-track procedural mechanism distinct from the merits. The bond-fixing exercise turns on factors including the gravity of alleged offences, penalties imposable, value of vessel and cargo, and reasonableness in totality — not on the merits of the underlying dispute.
- **Flag-state jurisdiction primacy and "genuine link" (UNCLOS Article 91).** The Tribunal declined to permit Guinea to contest the *Saiga*'s Saint Vincent registration by reference to a perceived absence of "genuine link" between vessel and flag state. The genuine-link requirement is a duty owed by the flag state to the international community at large, not a basis for individual coastal states to disregard a flag of their choosing in enforcement actions.
- **Hot pursuit limits (UNCLOS Article 111).** Hot pursuit is a strictly construed customary right preserved by UNCLOS. To be lawful it requires (a) a reasonable belief at the point of commencement that the pursued vessel has violated the laws of the pursuing state in waters where that state had jurisdiction, (b) a clear visual or auditory signal to stop given at a distance enabling the vessel to comply, and (c) continuity of pursuit. Guinea's pursuit failed each of these requirements.
- **Customs jurisdiction in the EEZ.** Coastal-state customs and fiscal jurisdiction does not extend into the exclusive economic zone. Guinea's attempt to project customs enforcement into the EEZ on the basis of a "customs radius" had no foundation in UNCLOS.
- **Use of force in maritime law enforcement.** Force used in the arrest must be reasonable, necessary, and graduated. The use of live ammunition that wounded crew members was excessive and breached the standard of reasonableness.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Founding ITLOS jurisprudence.** *Saiga* (No 1) is the inaugural substantive decision of ITLOS and laid down the operational pattern for the prompt-release procedure that has since been the most-used pathway to the Tribunal. *Saiga* (No 2) is the first full merits judgment of the Tribunal and the principal source of ITLOS reasoning on flag-state jurisdiction and hot pursuit.
- **Constraint on coastal-state extraterritorial enforcement.** Reinforced the EEZ as a sui generis zone with sharply limited coastal-state competences; customs and fiscal enforcement do not migrate seaward from the territorial sea.
- **Mitigation of the open-registry / "genuine link" debate.** Removed individual coastal states as legitimate venues to challenge another state's registration practices. The genuine-link debate is to be conducted through inter-state and IMO channels, not by ad hoc enforcement.
- **Codification of strict hot-pursuit pleadings.** Practitioner pleadings invoking Article 111 must now demonstrate each of the continuity, signal, and origination elements with documentary precision; the *Saiga* (No 2) pleading template is the universal reference.
- **Bridge to subsequent ITLOS practice.** The procedural and substantive analyses in *Saiga* fed directly into later ITLOS prompt-release cases (*Camouco* 2000, *Monte Confurco* 2000, *Volga* 2002, *Hoshinmaru* / *Tomimaru* 2007, *Virginia G* 2014) and into UNCLOS Article 91 litigation generally.

## Practitioner impact

- **For flag states and shipowners.** Established a meaningful international remedy against coastal-state over-reach, particularly for vessels under open-registry flags whose national consular protection may be limited. The prompt-release procedure provides a fast (typically 30-day) pathway to vessel release.
- **For coastal-state authorities.** Hot-pursuit and customs-enforcement procedures must conform tightly to UNCLOS articles; documentary precision is now load-bearing. Pursuit logs, signal records, distance estimates, and force-graduation records are routine evidentiary requirements.
- **For P&I clubs.** Prompt-release bond procurement is a standard P&I-cover scenario; the *Saiga* template informs bond pricing and structure, with cargo-value plus security-component remaining the canonical formula.
- **For arbitration counsel.** *Saiga* (No 2) is a frequently-cited authority on UNCLOS dispute-resolution architecture and on the relationship between Annex VII arbitration and ITLOS.
- **For port-state-control authorities.** Reinforced that PSC jurisdiction is grounded in voluntary port entry, not in extraterritorial customs reach.

## Subsequent application

- ***Camouco* (Panama v France) ITLOS 2000** — Applied the *Saiga* (No 1) reasonableness framework to a French arrest of a Panamanian fishing vessel in the Crozet Islands EEZ; reduced the bond.
- ***Volga* (Russia v Australia) ITLOS 2002** — Followed the *Saiga* prompt-release pattern; clarified that "non-financial" bond conditions (such as VMS installation) may exceed the proper scope of a reasonable bond.
- ***Virginia G* (Panama v Guinea-Bissau) ITLOS 2014** — Drew explicitly on *Saiga* (No 2) regarding bunkering operations in the EEZ and the limits of coastal-state regulatory jurisdiction; modified some *Saiga* reasoning on coastal-state regulation of bunkering as ancillary to fishing.
- ***ARA Libertad* (Argentina v Ghana) ITLOS 2012** — Cited *Saiga* on warship and state-owned-vessel sovereign immunity questions.
- **Annex VII arbitral tribunals (UNCLOS dispute-resolution).** *South China Sea* (2016), *Arctic Sunrise* (2015), and *Enrica Lexie* (2020) tribunals have engaged with *Saiga* reasoning on EEZ enforcement, flag-state jurisdiction, and proportionate use of force.
