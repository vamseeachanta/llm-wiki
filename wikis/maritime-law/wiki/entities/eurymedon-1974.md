# New Zealand Shipping Co Ltd v A M Satterthwaite & Co Ltd ('The Eurymedon') [1975] AC 154

## Case overview

**Facts.** A drilling machine was shipped from Liverpool to Wellington aboard the *Eurymedon* under a bill of lading issued by the carrier (the Federal Steam Navigation Co). The bill of lading contained a "Himalaya clause" purporting to extend the carrier's exemptions and liability limits to its servants, agents, and independent contractors — including stevedores. Stevedoring at Wellington was performed by the New Zealand Shipping Company Ltd (NZSC), a wholly-owned subsidiary of the carrier acting as an independent contractor. While unloading the machine, NZSC's stevedores damaged it through negligence. The cargo consignee sued NZSC for the full damage; NZSC pleaded the bill-of-lading time-bar and the carrier's exemption clauses.

**Issue.** Could a stevedore — an independent contractor that was not a party to the bill of lading — rely on the carrier's exemption-and-limitation clauses through a Himalaya clause? If so, what doctrinal mechanism brings the stevedore within the contract's protection in a regime that bars third-party contractual benefit (privity)?

## Procedural history

- **New Zealand Supreme Court (first instance) 1971** — Held for NZSC; the Himalaya clause protected the stevedore.
- **New Zealand Court of Appeal 1972** — Reversed; held that privity of contract barred NZSC from invoking the carrier's exemptions, and the Himalaya clause was ineffective to overcome that bar.
- **Privy Council, 25 February 1974** — On appeal from New Zealand. Lord Wilberforce delivering the majority opinion (with Lord Hodson and Lord Salmon concurring; Lord Viscount Dilhorne and Lord Simon of Glaisdale dissenting), the Board reversed the Court of Appeal and restored the trial judge's decision. The Himalaya clause operated to extend the carrier's exemption-and-limitation regime to NZSC as stevedore, by way of a unilateral collateral contract formed when NZSC began discharging the goods.

## Key legal holdings

- **Himalaya clause enforceability.** A bill-of-lading clause that purports to extend the carrier's exemption-and-limitation defences to stevedores and other contractors is contractually effective, where four conditions are met: (i) the bill of lading makes clear that the protections extend to the stevedore; (ii) the bill makes clear the carrier contracts both for itself and as agent for the stevedore; (iii) the carrier has authority from the stevedore to act as agent; (iv) consideration moves from the stevedore to the cargo owner.
- **Collateral contract via performance as consideration.** The four-part requirement is satisfied because the stevedore's act of unloading the goods constitutes consideration moving from the stevedore to the consignee, completing a unilateral collateral contract on the bill-of-lading's exemption terms.
- **Bridge to *Adler v Dickson* (1955).** The decision built directly on *Adler v Dickson* [1955] 1 QB 158 (CA), which had recognised the conceptual possibility of a contracting party authorising third-party protections, and supplied the missing privity-and-consideration mechanics that *Adler* itself had not resolved.
- **Privity preserved, not abolished.** The Board did not displace the privity rule; instead it located the stevedore's protection in a separate (collateral) contract formed at performance, leaving the doctrine of privity intact while neutralising its commercial inconvenience for cargo handling.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Foundational for modern bill-of-lading practice.** Established the canonical mechanism by which Hague-Visby Article IV.5 package limitations and Article III/IV exceptions extend beyond the contracting carrier to stevedores, terminal operators, port authorities, and other cargo-handling intermediaries.
- **Commercial reality over formalism.** Reflected the Privy Council's willingness to honour the parties' allocation of risk against the formal privity rule, where commercial practice — fixed insurance allocations, P&I cover, terminal-operator liability — had assumed Himalaya protection would hold.
- **Persistent under statutory reform.** Even after the Contracts (Rights of Third Parties) Act 1999 (UK) provided a statutory route to confer benefits on third parties, *The Eurymedon* remained the dominant analytical framework, and the bill-of-lading wording in international trade has continued to be drafted to satisfy the Wilberforce four-part test.
- **Cross-Commonwealth uptake.** Followed in Australia (*The New York Star* [1981] 1 WLR 138 (PC)), Canada (*ITO v Miida Electronics* [1986] 1 SCR 752), and Singapore.

## Practitioner impact

- **For carriers and bill-of-lading drafters.** Drafting practice converged on Wilberforce's four-element template; modern bills of lading typically include explicit Himalaya wording naming stevedores, terminal operators, and connecting carriers as protected parties with authority and agency expressly recited.
- **For stevedores and terminal operators.** The decision provides the contractual basis for relying on Hague-Visby package limits and time-bars when sued in tort by cargo claimants, which materially reduces P&I and commercial-general-liability exposure.
- **For cargo claimants.** Direct claims against negligent stevedores must contend with the Himalaya clause; tactical use of in-rem actions and forum selection can sometimes route around it but the framework constrains claimant recovery.
- **For modern multimodal carriage.** The doctrine adapts to extend Hague-Visby protections through chains of subcontractors, supporting the door-to-door framework that the (not-yet-in-force) Rotterdam Rules attempt to codify directly.

## Subsequent application

- ***The New York Star* [1981] 1 WLR 138 (PC)** — Confirmed and refined *The Eurymedon* on Australian appeal; reinforced consideration-via-performance analysis.
- ***Homburg Houtimport BV v Agrosin Pte ('The Starsin')* [2003] UKHL 12** — UK House of Lords applied the framework in determining the carrier's identity and the reach of the Himalaya clause across multi-tier carriage.
- ***ITO v Miida Electronics*** [1986] 1 SCR 752 — Supreme Court of Canada adopted the *Eurymedon* framework into Canadian maritime law.
- ***Pyrene v Scindia*** [1954] 2 QB 402 — Antecedent decision on the temporal scope of carrier liability; engaged in tandem with *Eurymedon* in disputes over when stevedore-handling falls within Hague-Visby coverage.
