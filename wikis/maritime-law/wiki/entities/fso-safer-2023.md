# FSO Safer (2023)

## Case overview

**Facts.** The FSO *Safer* was a 1976-built, 362-metre, 3-million-barrel-capacity Floating Storage and Offloading unit moored approximately 4.8 nautical miles off Ras Issa, Yemen, in the southern Red Sea. Originally converted from a VLCC tanker in 1987, the unit had served the Marib pipeline export terminal until production and maintenance ceased in 2015 following the outbreak of the Yemen conflict. Between 2015 and 2023 the unit held approximately 1.14 million barrels of Marib light crude oil with no inert-gas system, no ongoing maintenance, deteriorating hull integrity, and a documented risk of structural failure, fire, or explosion. Multiple UNEP, World Bank, and academic technical assessments characterised an uncontrolled release as posing the threat of one of the largest marine oil-spill events in history — orders of magnitude larger than *Exxon Valdez* (1989) at potential exposure scale — with cascading regional impact on Yemeni Red Sea fisheries, Saudi Arabian and Eritrean coastlines, Bab-el-Mandeb shipping, and Red Sea desalination intakes serving roughly 8 million people.

**Issue.** Pre-emptive (pre-incident) salvage rather than post-casualty salvage. Doctrinally novel: (i) what is the legal framework for state-actor / international-organisation-led salvage of a deteriorating but not-yet-failed asset where the registered owner (a Yemeni state-owned entity, SEPOC, in disputed-control territory) cannot or will not act? (ii) Does the Salvage Convention 1989 contemplate operations that are entirely preventive of an environmental casualty, distinct from Article 14 special-compensation post-casualty environmental-protection efforts? (iii) Funding and cost-allocation when the salvor is a coalition coordinated by the UN and the salved fund is partially returned to the cargo-owning entity.

## Procedural history

- **2015–2020 — Diplomatic stalemate.** Multiple UN, IMO, and bilateral initiatives (notably under UN Special Envoys Ould Cheikh Ahmed and then Hans Grundberg) attempted to broker access for inspection, maintenance, or transfer; access was repeatedly denied or delayed amid the Yemen conflict.
- **2020–2022 — UNEP / OCHA technical-readiness work.** UNEP, the UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA), the IMO, and the UN Resident Coordinator for Yemen developed an operational plan covering vessel acquisition, salvage-contractor selection, insurance and indemnity architecture, and phased ship-to-ship transfer.
- **2022 — UN-led funding pledge cycles.** Multiple pledging conferences raised approximately US$148 million in confirmed funding from member states (Saudi Arabia, the Netherlands, Germany, the United Kingdom, the United States, the European Commission, and others), private-sector contributors (HSA Group), and a public crowdfunding campaign — combined with an OPEC Fund for International Development loan.
- **March 2023 — Replacement vessel acquired.** The UN, through the UN Development Programme (UNDP) acting as administrative agent, acquired a 2008-built VLCC, the *Nautica*, subsequently renamed *Yemen* and transferred to SEPOC ownership for use as the long-term Marib export FSO replacement.
- **May–August 2023 — Salvage operation.** SMIT Salvage (Boskalis subsidiary) executed the operation on a contracted basis with UNDP. The operation included pre-transfer hull and tank stabilisation, deployment of an articulated emergency response vessel, ship-to-ship transfer of the cargo from *Safer* to *Yemen* between 25 July and 11 August 2023, and post-transfer hot-work cleaning of the *Safer*.
- **Late 2023 — Ongoing wind-down.** Final disposal of the *Safer* hull, residual tank-bottom waste, and decommissioning costs continued under separate funding arrangements; the *Yemen* (ex-*Nautica*) remained at the Ras Issa mooring as the operational FSO under SEPOC ownership.

## Key legal holdings

- **Pre-emptive (preventive) salvage as customary-international-law / Salvage Convention 1989 framework adaptation.** The operation was structured contractually between UNDP and SMIT Salvage on a fixed-cost basis (not LOF-style success-based remuneration), but the broader operational doctrine drew on Salvage Convention 1989 principles including the duty of care to the marine environment (Article 8) and the framework spirit of Article 14 (special compensation for environmental-protection efforts) extended to a preventive context.
- **State-actor / international-organisation-led salvage.** The UN system (UNDP, UNEP, OCHA, IMO) acted as salvage-coordinator in a role traditionally occupied by commercial salvors or flag-state authorities. This is the leading 21st-century example of multilateral state-actor salvage outside an active casualty.
- **Bunkers Convention 2001 contemplated but not directly invoked.** The *Safer*'s deteriorated condition contemplated bunker-spill scenarios, but the operation's preventive nature meant Bunkers Convention 2001 strict-liability machinery did not formally engage; the framework was operationally relevant as a backstop.
- **Funding and cost-allocation through multilateral pooling.** The funding architecture — combining state contributions, an OPEC Fund loan, private-sector contributions, and crowdfunded pledges — is novel as a salvage-funding mechanism distinct from owner / cargo / P&I / hull-insurer routes that govern conventional salvage.
- **Insurance and indemnity architecture.** UNDP procured bespoke pollution-incident and operational insurance for the operation, distinct from owner P&I cover (which was effectively unavailable given the disputed-control status of SEPOC and the deteriorated unit's uninsurability).

## Doctrinal significance

- **First major UN-led pre-emptive marine-pollution-prevention salvage.** *FSO Safer* is the doctrinal anchor for state-actor / international-organisation-led pre-emptive salvage outside an active casualty. Prior IMO-coordinated initiatives (notably post-tsunami debris-removal and post-conflict wreck-clearance operations) were reactive; *Safer* is the leading example of preventive intervention.
- **Extension of Article 14 environmental-protection logic to preventive context.** The operation extends the Article 14 "special compensation" rationale — that environmental-protection efforts deserve framework recognition independent of property-salvage success — into a context where there is no casualty yet to protect against.
- **Multilateral funding model as template.** The pooled-funding architecture is now studied as a template for analogous deteriorating-asset interventions (e.g., post-Hurricane wreck-removal in the Caribbean, abandoned-vessel cleanups, and potential future pre-emptive operations on deteriorating offshore installations).
- **UN system as salvage-coordinator.** The role of UNDP as administrative agent, UNEP as technical lead, OCHA as humanitarian coordinator, and IMO as flag-state-coordination conduit is the model for future operations of this kind.
- **Doctrinal connection to *Amoco Cadiz* and Article 14 origins.** *Safer* operationally demonstrates the policy logic that drove Article 14 of the Salvage Convention 1989 in the wake of *Amoco Cadiz* (1978) — that salvors and salvage-coordinators must have a framework to invest in environmental protection independent of property-salvage commercial returns.

## Practitioner impact

- **For salvors.** SMIT Salvage's contracted execution under fixed-cost terms (not LOF success-based) is a reference operating model for state-actor salvage engagements, complementing traditional LOF / SCOPIC commercial-casualty practice.
- **For flag states and international organisations.** The UNDP-UNEP-OCHA-IMO coordination architecture is a referenceable template for analogous interventions; subsequent 2024–2025 IMO discussions on abandoned-vessel and deteriorating-offshore-installation regimes draw on the *Safer* operational model.
- **For environmental-NGO and humanitarian advocacy.** The crowdfunded contribution component (managed through GoFundMe and partnerships with the HSA Group) extended public engagement with maritime environmental-protection funding into a new category of operational support.
- **For Red Sea littoral states.** The operation removed an existential threat to Saudi Arabian, Eritrean, Djiboutian, and Sudanese coastlines and to Bab-el-Mandeb shipping; littoral-state contributions to the funding pool reflected this direct interest. Saudi Arabia's contribution in particular signalled a regional state-actor model that may apply elsewhere.
- **For P&I clubs and pollution-cover practitioners.** *Safer* is a doctrinal reference point on the boundary of conventional pollution-cover applicability — where deteriorated and disputed-ownership assets fall outside standard insurability and require bespoke multilateral indemnity architecture.
- **For UN procurement and contracting practice.** UNDP's procurement model for the *Nautica* acquisition and SMIT engagement is a reference precedent for future high-stakes UN-led commercial-marine procurement.

## Subsequent application

- **2024 IMO discussions on deteriorating-asset regimes.** IMO Marine Environment Protection Committee and Legal Committee post-2023 work on regulatory framework gaps for deteriorating offshore installations, abandoned vessels, and stranded-asset risks cite *Safer* as the precedent operation.
- **Caribbean and West Africa post-incident application discussions.** Post-2023 discussions on analogous deteriorating-asset risks (notably FPSOs and storage units in conflict-affected or sanctions-affected regions) draw on the *Safer* multilateral-coordination model as a template.
- **Subsequent UNDP-led environmental and maritime operations.** UNDP's role as administrative agent for *Safer* shaped its subsequent engagements as coordinator for large-scale environmental-cleanup and deteriorating-asset operations.
- **Academic and IUCN scholarship.** The operation is now treated as the canonical case study in pre-emptive-salvage and multilateral-coordination doctrinal scholarship; UNEP's post-operation lessons-learned report (2024) is the leading public reference.
- **Post-2024 Yemen / Red Sea continuing context.** Subsequent Red Sea security developments (Houthi attacks on Bab-el-Mandeb shipping from late 2023) underscored the time-sensitivity of the operation's completion in 2023.
