# Volcafe Ltd v Compañía Sud Americana de Vapores SA [2018] UKSC 61

## Case overview

**Facts.** Volcafe Ltd shipped nine containers of bagged green Colombian coffee from Buenaventura (Colombia) to Bremen (Germany) aboard vessels operated by Compañía Sud Americana de Vapores SA (CSAV) in 2012. On outturn, the bags showed wet damage from condensation that had formed inside the unventilated containers as the cargo moved from a warm origin climate to a cooler discharge port. Volcafe sued for cargo damage; CSAV pleaded inherent vice under Article IV rule 2(m) of the Hague-Visby Rules, arguing that the natural moisture content of green coffee combined with an unavoidable temperature gradient in transit was an inherent characteristic of the goods.

**Issue.** Where carriage is governed by the Hague-Visby Rules, who bears the burden of proof when a carrier raises an Article IV.2 exception? Must the carrier prove that the exemption applies (defendant's burden), or must the cargo claimant prove that the exemption does not apply (claimant's burden)?

## Procedural history

- **High Court (Mercantile Court, Manchester) 2015** — Ruled for Volcafe; CSAV had not discharged the burden of proving non-negligence in cargo care, in particular the choice and adequacy of container kraft-paper lining.
- **Court of Appeal 2016** — Reversed; held that once a carrier showed that damage fell within an Article IV.2 category such as inherent vice, the claimant bore the burden of proving that the carrier's negligence caused or contributed to the loss.
- **UK Supreme Court, 5 December 2018** — Lord Sumption JSC delivering the leading judgment, the Court unanimously restored the trial judge's decision. The carrier bears the full burden of proving that an Article IV.2 exception applies, including the burden of proving absence of negligence in cargo handling and care.

## Key legal holdings

- **Hague-Visby burden of proof.** Under Article III rule 2, the carrier owes a positive, non-delegable obligation to "properly and carefully load, handle, stow, carry, keep, care for, and discharge" the goods. The Article IV.2 exceptions operate as defences. The carrier accordingly bears the legal burden of proof on each constitutive element of any exception it pleads.
- **Inherent vice (Article IV.2(m)).** A cargo's natural propensity to suffer a particular kind of damage does not by itself establish the inherent-vice defence. The carrier must show both that the damage was due to inherent vice **and** that the carrier exercised reasonable care in the cargo's handling. Inherent vice is not an automatic exemption triggered by the nature of the goods.
- **Bailment principles.** The Hague-Visby framework operates against the background of common-law bailment. As bailee for reward, the carrier owes a duty of reasonable care, and the burden of explaining loss or damage falls on the bailee. Hague-Visby modifies but does not displace the bailment burden.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Restored a 50-year common-law presumption.** The judgment realigned Hague-Visby with the longstanding common-law rule traceable to *The Glendarroch* [1894] P 226 — a bailee carrier bears the burden of proof when seeking to rely on a contractual or statutory exception.
- **Reversed the Court of Appeal's direction of travel.** Rejected the "claimant must prove negligence" framing that had developed in some first-instance and appellate decisions, eliminating an asymmetry that had crept into modern English cargo-claim practice.
- **Convergence with US COGSA and most other Hague-Visby jurisdictions.** Brought English law into closer alignment with the majority international interpretation of the Rules, including the Second Circuit line under the US Carriage of Goods by Sea Act.
- **Practical impact.** Cargo claimants under Hague-Visby now operate within a clearer burden-shifting framework: the carrier must affirmatively prove the cause of damage was within an exception and that it took reasonable care. In practice this requires contemporaneous cargo-care records — stowage plans, ventilation logs, container-selection rationale, and ambient-temperature monitoring — to be produced at trial.

## Practitioner impact

- **For carriers and P&I clubs.** Reinforced documentation discipline. Pre-*Volcafe* practice in some trades tolerated less-rigorous record-keeping; post-judgment, contemporaneous logs are effectively load-bearing evidence on which a defence stands or falls.
- **For shippers.** Streamlined the cargo-claim process. Where damage is established on outturn, the claimant's path to judgment narrows the carrier's evidential safe harbour.
- **For ships' crews.** Cargo-care log entries acquire direct evidentiary weight. Log quality — completeness, contemporaneity, and consistency — materially affects litigation outcomes.
- **For marine surveyors.** Pre-loading and outturn surveys gain enhanced evidentiary weight. Survey reports become primary documents in reconstructing the cargo's condition at each handover point.

## Subsequent application

- ***The Eternal Bliss* [2020] UKSC 11** — The UK Supreme Court drew on the *Volcafe* burden-of-proof framework when addressing demurrage-related claims and the boundary between liquidated damages and damages at large.
- **Singapore, Hong Kong, and Australia.** Common-law jurisdictions have cited *Volcafe* approvingly in post-2018 cargo-claim decisions, supporting a unified interpretation of Hague-Visby Article III and IV across the Commonwealth.
- **Continental civil-law jurisdictions.** Retain different burden-of-proof frameworks, in part influenced by the Hamburg Rules' express allocation of proof. *Volcafe* has not closed this divide but has narrowed the gap on the common-law side.
