# Costa Concordia (2012)

## Case overview

**Facts.** On the evening of 13 January 2012, the Italian-flagged cruise ship *Costa Concordia* (114,137 GT, ~4,229 persons aboard) struck a granite outcrop off the island of Isola del Giglio, Tuscany, while executing an unauthorised close-pass "salute" manoeuvre to the island. The contact tore a 50 m gash along the port-side hull below the waterline, flooded the engine rooms and main switchboard, and caused a progressive list to starboard. Master Francesco Schettino delayed declaring "abandon ship" and left the vessel before the evacuation was complete. Thirty-two people died and more than 150 were injured. The wreck rested partially submerged on the seabed for ~20 months until parbuckled upright in September 2013 and refloated for tow to Genoa for scrapping in July 2014.

**Issue.** Whether the master's command decisions, the operator's bridge-team and Safety Management System (SMS) controls, and Italian flag-state oversight collectively breached SOLAS Chapter II-1 stability and Chapter III life-saving-appliances obligations, the ISM Code, MLC 2006 crew-welfare provisions, and Italian maritime criminal law; and how the salvage of the largest passenger-ship wreck removal in history would be structured and compensated under the Salvage Convention 1989.

## Procedural history

- **Italian criminal proceedings, 2012-2017** — Master Schettino indicted for multiple manslaughter, causing a shipwreck, and abandoning ship. Tribunale di Grosseto convicted in February 2015 (16 years' imprisonment); Florence Court of Appeal upheld conviction in May 2016; Italian Supreme Court (Corte di Cassazione) affirmed final conviction in May 2017.
- **Plea bargains** — Five Costa Crociere personnel and bridge officers entered plea agreements in 2013-2014 covering manslaughter and negligence-related counts; sentences ranged from ~18 months to ~34 months suspended.
- **Civil and operator settlements** — Costa Crociere (Carnival Corporation subsidiary) settled the corporate-administrative liability under Italian Decree 231/2001 with a €1M fine and class-action and individual-passenger settlements proceeded in parallel.
- **Salvage and wreck removal, 2012-2014** — Titan Salvage / Micoperi consortium executed the parbuckling on 16-17 September 2013 — the largest maritime salvage operation by displacement in history. Total wreck-removal and environmental-protection cost exceeded €1.2 billion.

## Key legal holdings

- **Master's command responsibility under Italian penal code and STCW.** The Cassation confirmed that the master's deviation from the planned route, late general-emergency declaration, and departure from the vessel before evacuation completion constituted multiple manslaughter and abandonment offences; STCW 2010 Manila amendments to Chapter VIII (watchkeeping) framed the standard-of-care analysis.
- **SMS and ISM Code failure cascade.** Tribunal findings traced the casualty to systemic SMS deficiencies — unauthorised "salute" manoeuvres tolerated as informal practice, bridge-team authority gradient suppressing junior-officer challenge, deficient passage-planning verification — establishing the ISM Code (SOLAS Chapter IX) as a benchmark Italian flag-state oversight had not enforced.
- **SOLAS Chapter II-1 and Chapter III performance under casualty.** The post-casualty MAIB-equivalent Italian investigation (Marine Casualties Investigative Body, 2013) documented progressive flooding paths, watertight-door management failures, and lifeboat-launch limitations under heavy list — feeding subsequent SOLAS amendments on damage stability and large-passenger-ship lifeboat practice.
- **Salvage Convention Article 14 and SCOPIC engagement.** The wreck-removal contract drew on Lloyd's Open Form architecture and Article 14 special-compensation principles for the environmental-protection element (fuel-oil removal pre-parbuckling); Costa Concordia became a benchmark for state-coordinated complex-casualty salvage governance.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Catalyst for Bridge Team Management (BTM) reinforcement in STCW 2010 Manila amendments.** Although the Manila amendments pre-dated the casualty, post-Concordia maritime-administration practice elevated BTM, command authority gradient, and challenge-and-response training as core large-passenger-ship competency expectations; class-society notations and operator-internal training programmes converged on Concordia-driven competencies.
- **EU Erika III package continuation.** The casualty reinforced the EU's post-Erika regulatory trajectory on flag-state and port-state oversight of passenger vessels; subsequent EMSA inspection guidance referenced Concordia in passenger-ship-safety priority-setting.
- **Nairobi WRC ratification driver.** The cost and complexity of the Concordia wreck-removal influenced state ratification trajectories for the Nairobi Wreck Removal Convention 2007, particularly for coastal states with heavy cruise traffic.
- **Modern benchmark for mass-casualty passenger-ship salvage.** The parbuckling operation set a new technical and contractual reference; subsequent salvage tenders for large-passenger-ship casualties cite Concordia methodology.

## Practitioner impact

- **For passenger-ship operators and cruise lines.** Reinforced SMS-discipline expectations: deviation tolerance is treated as latent failure, not folkloric practice; bridge-resource-management training, deviation-authorisation logging, and route-conformance monitoring carry direct evidentiary weight.
- **For masters and bridge teams.** Command-authority gradient is now an explicit topic in STCW-aligned training; junior-officer challenge protocols are auditable.
- **For salvors and salvage underwriters.** Concordia validated complex-casualty salvage feasibility at unprecedented scale; insurance towers for cruise tonnage now plan against Concordia-comparable scenarios.
- **For flag-state administrations.** The Italian flag-state SMS-oversight findings reshaped EU-state Authorised-Recognised-Organisation (RO) audit practice for large-passenger-ship operators.
- **For passengers and class-action counsel.** The Italian Decree 231/2001 corporate-liability path established a route alongside individual-master prosecution that subsequent passenger-ship casualty practice references.

## Subsequent application

- **MLC 2006 crew-welfare scrutiny.** Crew accounts of the chaotic evacuation drove subsequent MLC 2006 enforcement attention to large-passenger-ship crew accommodation, working-hours, and emergency-training compliance.
- **Subsequent passenger-ship casualties** — Sewol (2014, South Korea, not yet authored) and Viking Sky (2019, Norway) — referenced Concordia in master-responsibility and bridge-team-training analysis.
- **Post-2014 SOLAS amendments.** MSC has progressed amendments on damage-stability assumptions, watertight-door management, and large-passenger-ship recovery-of-persons-from-water in part on Concordia-driven evidence.
