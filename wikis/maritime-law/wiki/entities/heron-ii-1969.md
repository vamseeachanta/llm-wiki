# Koufos v C Czarnikow Ltd ('The Heron II') [1969] 1 AC 350

## Case overview

**Facts.** C Czarnikow Ltd, a sugar trader, chartered the *Heron II* from the shipowner Koufos to carry 3,000 tons of bagged sugar from the port of Constanța (Romania) on the Black Sea to Basrah (Iraq). The expected voyage was approximately 20 days. The shipowner deviated from the contractual route to make extra ports of call for his own commercial benefit, with the result that the vessel arrived nine days late. During the period of delay, the Basrah sugar market price fell. Czarnikow had intended to sell the sugar promptly on arrival; the late delivery meant the cargo realised a lower price. The charterer claimed the difference as damages — the reduction in market value attributable to the delay. The shipowner conceded breach but disputed remoteness, arguing that fluctuation of a commodity market and a charterer's intent to sell promptly were not within the parties' contemplation at fixture.

**Issue.** What standard of foreseeability is required for losses to be recoverable as not-too-remote consequences of a breach of contract? Is the *Hadley v Baxendale* (1854) "natural and probable" formulation the same as, or different from, the tort-law "reasonable foreseeability" test articulated in *The Wagon Mound* [1961] AC 388 and *Overseas Tankship No 2* [1967] 1 AC 617? How probable must the loss be at the time of contract formation?

## Procedural history

- **Commercial Court (McNair J) 1965** — Found for the charterer Czarnikow on the basis that the loss was within the parties' reasonable contemplation; the shipowner knew there was a sugar market at Basrah and could be taken to have contemplated price-fluctuation risk.
- **Court of Appeal 1966** — Affirmed by majority, with reasoning that aligned the contract foreseeability test with the tort *Wagon Mound* "reasonably foreseeable" standard.
- **House of Lords, 17 October 1967 (reported 1969)** — Affirmed the result for the charterer but expressly **rejected** the Court of Appeal's alignment of contract and tort foreseeability standards. Lords Reid, Morris, Hodson, Pearce, and Upjohn each delivered concurring speeches articulating a contract-specific standard distinct from, and narrower than, tort foreseeability.

## Key legal holdings

- **"Not unlikely" standard for contract foreseeability.** Lord Reid's leading formulation: damages are recoverable as not-too-remote where the loss was "of a kind which the defendant, when he made the contract, ought to have realised was not unlikely to result from the breach." "Not unlikely" was glossed as a "real danger" or "serious possibility" — a higher probability threshold than tort's "reasonably foreseeable."
- **Contract / tort divergence on remoteness.** The House of Lords rejected the Court of Appeal's attempt to merge contract and tort tests. In contract, the parties had the opportunity to allocate risk by contract terms; the policy basis for charging the breaching party with low-probability losses is therefore weaker than in tort, where no prior risk-allocation opportunity existed.
- **Lord Pearce's "serious possibility" gloss.** Lord Pearce reformulated the standard as whether the loss was a "serious possibility" or "real danger" — language adopted in subsequent cases as the operative gloss on Lord Reid's "not unlikely."
- **Application to the facts.** The shipowner knew the cargo was sugar destined for Basrah, knew Basrah was a known sugar market, and could be taken to have contemplated that delay would expose the charterer to price-fluctuation risk. The kind of loss — market-price reduction during delay — was a "serious possibility" at fixture. Quantum then followed orthodox computation.
- **Reformulation of Hadley's first limb.** The "natural" / "in the usual course of things" language of *Hadley v Baxendale* was restated in modern probabilistic terms: losses that are "not unlikely" at contract formation are recoverable under the first limb.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Decisive refinement of foreseeability between *Hadley* (1854) and *The Achilleas* (2008).** *The Heron II* is the most important House of Lords decision on contract-damages remoteness in the 115 years between Alderson B's *Hadley* formulation and Lord Hoffmann's *Achilleas* assumption-of-responsibility filter. It modernised the first-limb language without disturbing the two-limb structure.
- **Contract-tort distinction reinforced.** By rejecting the *Wagon Mound* alignment, the House of Lords preserved a meaningful difference between contract and tort remoteness — a divergence that remains structurally important in commercial litigation, where the choice between contractual and tortious causes of action affects recoverable quantum.
- **Adopted across common-law jurisdictions.** Cited as authoritative in Australian, Canadian, New Zealand, Hong Kong, and Singaporean contract-damages jurisprudence; routinely invoked in cargo-claim, charterparty, and commercial-supply disputes.
- **Foundation for *The Achilleas*.** Lord Hoffmann in *The Achilleas* expressly built on *The Heron II* as the most recent prior House of Lords statement of the contract-damages remoteness test, treating his assumption-of-responsibility filter as a refinement rather than a rejection of *The Heron II*.

## Practitioner impact

- **For shipowners and charterers.** Market-fluctuation losses during periods of delay are recoverable where the carrier had general knowledge of the cargo, destination, and trade — even without specific notice of the consignee's resale plans. This default rule is contractually displaceable through cap clauses, but absent express provision *The Heron II* governs.
- **For cargo claimants.** Establishes that knowledge of "market" and "kind of loss" at contract formation is sufficient for first-limb recovery; the claimant need not show that the specific quantum was contemplated.
- **For arbitrators in commodity-shipping disputes.** *The Heron II* provides the standard analytical framework for delay-related market-fluctuation claims: classify the kind of loss, ask whether it was a "serious possibility" at fixture, and (post-*Achilleas*) whether the breaching party assumed responsibility for that kind of loss.
- **For drafters of charterparties and bills of lading.** Express market-loss exclusions (or caps) routinely appear in modern NYPE addenda, COA terms, and bespoke charters precisely because *The Heron II* would otherwise leave the breaching party exposed to commodity-market volatility.

## Subsequent application

- ***Transfield Shipping Inc v Mercator Shipping Inc (The Achilleas)* [2008] UKHL 48** — Lord Hoffmann treated *The Heron II* as the prior leading restatement of contract-damages remoteness; the assumption-of-responsibility filter is layered onto, not in place of, the *Heron II* "not unlikely" test.
- ***Brown v KMR Services Ltd* [1995] 4 All ER 598 (CA)** — Applied *The Heron II* in the context of professional-negligence consequential losses, reaffirming the contract-tort divergence.
- ***Sylvia Shipping Co Ltd v Progress Bulk Carriers Ltd (The Sylvia)* [2010] EWHC 542 (Comm)** — Hamblen J cited *The Heron II* as the orthodox starting point for contract-damages remoteness, with *The Achilleas* operating only in the unusual cases where market understanding displaces it.
