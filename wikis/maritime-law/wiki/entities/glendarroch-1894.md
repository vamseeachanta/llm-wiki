# The Glendarroch [1894] P 226

## Case overview

**Facts.** The *Glendarroch* was a British steamship carrying a cargo of cement under a bill of lading from a UK port. During the voyage the vessel grounded and the cargo was damaged by sea water. The bill of lading contained a "perils of the sea" exception clause typical of late-Victorian common-law carriage contracts. The cargo claimant sued the carrier for damage to the cement; the carrier pleaded the perils-of-the-sea exception as a complete defence.

**Issue.** When a carrier as bailee invokes a contractual exception clause (here, perils of the sea) to resist a cargo-damage claim, who bears the burden of proof? Must the carrier prove that the exception applies and that the loss was not caused by its own negligence, or must the cargo claimant prove that the carrier's negligence took the loss outside the exception?

## Procedural history

- **Admiralty Division, first instance** — Found for the cargo owner; the carrier had not satisfactorily explained the grounding or rebutted an inference of unseaworthiness/negligence.
- **Court of Appeal, 1894** — Lord Esher MR (with Lopes and Davey LJJ concurring) affirmed for the cargo owner and articulated the burden-of-proof framework that became the leading common-law authority for the next century.

## Key legal holdings

- **Bailee's burden on excepted perils.** Lord Esher MR held that where a carrier as bailee for reward seeks to rely on an excepted-perils clause, the carrier bears the burden of proving (i) that the loss fell within the exception **and** (ii) that the loss was not caused or contributed to by the carrier's own negligence. The exception is a defence; the bailee must make it good on both limbs.
- **Two-stage shifting framework.** Once the cargo owner establishes that goods were delivered in good order and outturned damaged, the evidential burden shifts to the carrier to bring the loss within the exception clause. If the carrier discharges that burden, the claimant may then put negligence in issue, at which point the carrier must again show it took reasonable care.
- **Bailment as foundation.** The decision rests on common-law bailment: the bailee's duty of reasonable care, and the bailee's superior access to evidence about how the goods were handled, justify allocating the proof burden to the carrier rather than the consignee.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Foundational common-law authority.** *The Glendarroch* became the canonical English statement of the carrier's burden of proof under bill-of-lading exception clauses for the half-century leading up to the Hague Rules 1924 and remained influential under the Rules.
- **Pre-Hague Rules but doctrinally enduring.** Although decided 30 years before the Brussels Convention codified Article III/IV of the Hague Rules, the case set the conceptual template — exceptions are defences, defences carry burdens — that shaped the international regime that followed.
- **Echoed across the Commonwealth.** Cited approvingly in Australian, Canadian, New Zealand, and Singaporean cargo-claim decisions through the 20th century as the authoritative bailee-burden rule.
- **Eclipse and restoration.** Mid- and late-20th-century English decisions began to relax the framework, allowing carriers more easily to shift the negligence burden onto claimants once an exception was triggered. *Volcafe Ltd v CSAV* [2018] UKSC 61 expressly restored the *Glendarroch* allocation, with Lord Sumption JSC describing it as the 50-year-misplaced common-law presumption.

## Practitioner impact

- **For 19th- and early-20th-century carriers.** Established that exception clauses were not automatic safe harbours; carriers had to maintain stowage and care records sufficient to negate negligence at trial.
- **For Hague-Rules era practice (1924-2018).** The framework continued to inform cargo-claim litigation under Article IV.2, even as some appellate decisions trended toward a claimant-burden interpretation.
- **For post-*Volcafe* practice.** Citations to *The Glendarroch* surged after 2018 as practitioners, P&I clubs, and academic commentators traced the restored Hague-Visby burden-of-proof rule back to its Victorian origin.

## Subsequent application

- ***Volcafe Ltd v CSAV* [2018] UKSC 61** — Lord Sumption JSC expressly invoked *The Glendarroch* as the authority restored in modern Hague-Visby practice; the Supreme Court treated the 1894 framework as the correct allocation under Article III/IV.
- **Commonwealth carriage authorities.** Cited in *Gosse Millerd v Canadian Government Merchant Marine* [1929] AC 223 (HL) and a long line of subsequent cargo-claim decisions.
- ***The Eternal Bliss* [2020] UKSC 11** — Indirectly engaged through the *Volcafe* burden-of-proof framework when the Supreme Court considered demurrage and consequential-loss boundaries.
