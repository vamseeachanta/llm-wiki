# THE TORREY CANYON (1967)

## Case overview

**Facts.** On 18 March 1967, the *Torrey Canyon*, a Liberian-flagged supertanker of approximately 120,000 dwt under bareboat charter to Union Oil of California, ran aground on Pollard's Rock between the Scilly Isles and Land's End off the Cornish coast while attempting a navigational shortcut on a passage from Kuwait to Milford Haven. The vessel was carrying approximately **119,000 tonnes of Kuwaiti crude oil**. The grounding tore open the hull and progressive structural failure over the following days released the entire cargo into the English Channel, fouling some 190 km of the Cornish, Devon, and Brittany coastlines.

**Parties.** Owner: Barracuda Tanker Corporation (a Bermuda-based single-ship company, beneficially controlled by Union Oil of California). Flag: Liberia. Coastal states materially affected: United Kingdom, France. No charterer-side cargo claim; the cargo was Union Oil's own.

**Response.** With no contemporary international instrument authorising coastal-state intervention against a foreign-flagged casualty on the high seas, the United Kingdom resorted to **Royal Navy and Royal Air Force aerial bombing of the wreck** from 28 to 30 March 1967 in an effort to ignite the residual cargo and incinerate it in situ. The bombing was only partially effective and the incident exposed the absence of a coordinated coastal-state legal framework for casualty intervention.

## Procedural history

- **No substantive contested liability litigation.** Because the *Torrey Canyon* sank in international waters and her owner was a single-ship company with limited assets, traditional in-rem and in-personam liability mechanisms produced little recoverable compensation.
- **Out-of-court settlements (1969-1970).** The United Kingdom and French governments obtained an aggregate settlement of approximately £3 million from Barracuda Tanker Corporation and Union Oil — a fraction of the estimated £6-15 million in cleanup and damage costs incurred by the two coastal states.
- **No appellate record.** The case is foundational not for any judicial holding, but for the **legislative and treaty response** it provoked at IMO (then IMCO).

## Key legal holdings

- **No coastal-state authority absent treaty basis (pre-1969).** Under customary international law as it stood in 1967, a coastal state had no clear right to intervene against a foreign-flagged vessel in distress on the high seas, even where the casualty threatened the coastal state's own territorial waters. The bombing decision was taken in the legal twilight between sovereign self-defence of the coastline and the principle of flag-state exclusivity.
- **Single-ship-company asset shield.** Barracuda Tanker Corporation's structure exposed the inadequacy of unlimited tort recovery against a thinly-capitalised single-ship owner — there was no compulsory insurance regime and no compensation fund of last resort.
- **Salvage law incentive misalignment.** Pre-1989, salvage awards under the Brussels 1910 Salvage Convention and Lloyd's Open Form depended on "no cure, no pay" success in saving property. Salvors had no financial incentive to prioritise pollution prevention where the property itself was unrecoverable — a structural gap not closed until **Article 14 of the 1989 Salvage Convention** introduced "special compensation" for environmental-protection efforts.

## Doctrinal significance

The *Torrey Canyon* casualty is the **founding event of the modern international maritime pollution-liability regime**. Within four years, IMCO had produced a coordinated legal architecture that did not exist before 1967:

- **Intervention Convention 1969 (INTERVENTION 1969).** Codified the coastal state's right to intervene on the high seas against pollution threats from foreign-flagged vessels.
- **Civil Liability Convention 1969 (CLC 1969).** Imposed strict liability on the registered owner of a tanker for oil-pollution damage and required compulsory insurance — directly addressing the *Torrey Canyon* asset-shield problem.
- **IOPC Fund Convention 1971.** Established the supplementary International Oil Pollution Compensation Fund, financed by oil-receiver levies, to compensate damage exceeding the CLC owner's limit.
- **MARPOL 73/78.** The International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships, adopted 1973 and modified by the 1978 Protocol, established the operational pollution-prevention regime — Annex I oil discharge standards, double-hull requirements (subsequent amendments), and segregated ballast tank requirements.

The case is also the proximate cause of the **flag-of-convenience policy debate** that culminated in UNCLOS Article 91's "genuine link" requirement, and an early data point in the development of port-state-control regimes that would emerge with the **Paris MoU 1982** following the later *Amoco Cadiz* casualty.

## Practitioner impact

- **Shipowners and P&I clubs.** Compulsory insurance under CLC 1969 (now CLC 1992) became a standing condition of trade for any tanker carrying persistent oil. P&I club coverage for pollution liability was reshaped around the CLC tonnage-banded limits and the IOPC Fund's supplementary tier.
- **Oil receivers.** Through the IOPC Fund Convention, oil-importing receivers became the second-tier payor for tanker spills exceeding the owner's limit — a financial-responsibility regime layered on top of the shipowner's primary liability.
- **Coastal-state regulators.** INTERVENTION 1969 + the developing port-state-control regime gave coastal states new tools to act prophylactically against substandard tonnage. The *Torrey Canyon* lesson — that coastal states had been legally exposed in 1967 — drove a generation of regulatory development.
- **Salvors.** The Article 14 environmental-special-compensation regime (1989 Salvage Convention) and the later **SCOPIC 2000** clause realigned salvage economics to reward pollution-prevention effort even where property salvage failed.

## Subsequent application

- **Cited as the originating casualty** in virtually every subsequent IMO instrument concerning oil-pollution liability (CLC 1992, IOPC Fund 1992, Supplementary Fund 2003).
- **Amoco Cadiz (1978)** — eleven years after *Torrey Canyon*, a structurally similar Liberian-flag tanker breakup off Brittany demonstrated that the 1969-1971 framework still left coastal states materially under-compensated, driving CLC 1992 / Fund 1992 increases and the founding of the Paris MoU.
- **Sea Empress (1996)**, **Erika (1999)**, **Prestige (2002)** — each reinforced the *Torrey Canyon*-initiated trajectory toward stricter limits, faster phase-out of single-hull tankers (MARPOL Annex I Regulation 13G/F/H), and stricter port-state control.
- **Salvage Convention 1989** — Article 14 special compensation is doctrinally traceable to the *Torrey Canyon* salvage-incentive gap.
