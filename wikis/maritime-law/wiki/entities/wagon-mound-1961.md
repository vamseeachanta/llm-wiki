# Overseas Tankship (UK) Ltd v Morts Dock & Engineering Co Ltd ('The Wagon Mound No 1') [1961] AC 388

## Case overview

**Facts.** The *Wagon Mound* was a tanker chartered to Overseas Tankship (UK) Ltd. While loading bunker fuel at Caltex Wharf in Sydney Harbour in October 1951, a substantial quantity of furnace oil was negligently discharged into the harbour. The oil drifted approximately 200 metres across the harbour and accumulated around the wharf of Morts Dock & Engineering Co Ltd, where the plaintiff was carrying out welding and oxy-acetylene cutting on a vessel under repair, the *Corrimal*. After receiving advice that furnace oil floating on water would not ignite at ambient temperatures, the plaintiff resumed welding. Some hours later, molten metal from the welding fell onto a piece of cotton waste or rag floating in the oil; this acted as a wick, ignited, and set the oil alight. The resulting fire severely damaged Morts Dock's wharf and two vessels under repair. Morts Dock sued Overseas Tankship in tort.

**Issue.** What is the proper test for remoteness of damage in tort? Is the defendant liable for **all** direct consequences of its negligent act, regardless of foreseeability — the rule articulated in *In re Polemis and Furness, Withy & Co Ltd* [1921] 3 KB 560 — or only for consequences that were **reasonably foreseeable** at the time of the negligent act? Specifically, did Overseas Tankship's negligent oil discharge make it liable for fire damage when ignition by floating debris was found by the trial judge to have been beyond reasonable foresight at the time?

## Procedural history

- **Supreme Court of New South Wales (Kinsella J) 1958** — Found Overseas Tankship negligent in the oil discharge but, applying *Re Polemis*, held the defendant liable for fire damage as a direct consequence of the discharge despite finding that ignition of furnace oil floating on water was not reasonably foreseeable.
- **Full Court of the Supreme Court of New South Wales 1959** — Affirmed the trial judge on the *Re Polemis* direct-consequence reasoning.
- **Privy Council (on appeal from the Full Court of NSW), 18 January 1961** — Allowed Overseas Tankship's appeal. Viscount Simonds, delivering the Board's advice (Lord Reid, Lord Radcliffe, Lord Tucker, and Lord Morris of Borth-y-Gest concurring), held that *Re Polemis* should no longer be regarded as good law and replaced its direct-consequence test with a reasonable-foreseeability test for tort remoteness. On the trial judge's finding that ignition of floating furnace oil was not reasonably foreseeable, the fire damage was too remote and the appeal succeeded.

## Key legal holdings

- **Reasonable foreseeability replaces direct consequence.** Viscount Simonds held that the proper test for remoteness in tort is whether damage of the kind that occurred was reasonably foreseeable by a reasonable person in the defendant's position at the time of the negligent act. Liability for "all direct consequences" under *Re Polemis* — "an isolated case which has not been followed" — was discarded as unprincipled and inconsistent with the moral basis of negligence liability.
- **Kind of damage, not extent.** The foreseeability inquiry operates on the **kind** of damage, not its precise mechanism or quantum. Once the kind of damage is reasonably foreseeable, the defendant takes the victim and the precise sequence as it finds them.
- **Application to oil-on-water ignition.** On the trial judge's specific finding that a reasonable person in Overseas Tankship's position would not have foreseen that bunker oil floating on water could ignite at ambient temperatures by contact with welding sparks, the fire damage was too remote, even though pollution-type damage (oil fouling of wharves and slipways) might have been foreseeable.
- **Doctrinal demotion of *Re Polemis*.** While the Privy Council could not formally overrule a Court of Appeal decision, it directed that *Re Polemis* should "no longer be regarded as good law" and was clear that future English and Commonwealth courts should follow *The Wagon Mound*'s reasonable-foreseeability framework.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Foundational tort-remoteness authority for the common-law world.** *The Wagon Mound No 1* is the canonical statement of the reasonable-foreseeability test for negligence remoteness across England, Wales, Australia, New Zealand, Canada, and most Commonwealth jurisdictions. US negligence doctrine had reached a similar position through *Palsgraf v Long Island Railroad Co* (1928) on different conceptual routes.
- **Maritime-tort liability bedrock.** Pre-*Wagon Mound*, marine collision and pollution liability ran through *Re Polemis*'s direct-consequence test, exposing shipowners to potentially unlimited downstream losses. The reasonable-foreseeability standard rebalanced the framework toward modern tort principles and underwrites all subsequent maritime-tort jurisprudence.
- **Coastal pollution claims pre-CLC.** Before the CLC 1969 / IOPC Fund 1971 strict-liability regime took effect, oil-spill liability ran through ordinary tort. *The Wagon Mound* is the foundational authority on the reach of negligence-based pollution liability and remained the operative test for non-CLC pollution events (bunker spills before the Bunkers Convention 2001 came into force in 2008; pollution from non-tanker vessels in jurisdictions outside the IMO compensation stack).
- **Companion case** — ***Overseas Tankship (UK) Ltd v The Miller Steamship Co Pty Ltd ('The Wagon Mound No 2')* [1967] 1 AC 617**. On essentially the same facts but with different findings on foreseeability — the trial judge in the second action found that a reasonable person would have foreseen the *possibility*, however remote, of ignition — the Privy Council held the defendant liable. Together the two cases stand for the propositions that (a) foreseeability is the test, and (b) any non-far-fetched possibility of the kind of damage suffices.

## Practitioner impact

- **For shipowners and P&I clubs.** *The Wagon Mound* contracted the scope of tortious liability for downstream consequences of negligent acts, providing a meaningful remoteness defence in non-CLC pollution cases, collision damage, and shore-installation damage claims.
- **For coastal-state claimants pre-Bunkers Convention.** Pollution claims against bunker discharges ran through *Wagon Mound* foreseeability until 2008. Even post-Bunkers, residual claims (for example, pure economic loss to coastal businesses not covered by the strict-liability regime) revert to *Wagon Mound* tort analysis.
- **For port authorities and harbour engineers.** The case stands as a perennial cautionary reference in waterfront-operations risk assessments — flammability of waterborne hydrocarbons and the foreseeability of welding-spark ignition near oil sheen.
- **For maritime litigators more broadly.** *The Wagon Mound* is the standard citation in any tortious cause of action involving a chain of causation, and it routinely appears alongside *Hadley v Baxendale* in mixed contract-and-tort actions to delineate the contract-tort divergence on remoteness.

## Subsequent application

- ***Overseas Tankship (UK) Ltd v The Miller Steamship Co Pty Ltd (The Wagon Mound No 2)* [1967] 1 AC 617** — Companion Privy Council decision applying the same foreseeability framework to the same casualty with different findings on the foresight of ignition risk; resulted in liability.
- ***Hughes v Lord Advocate* [1963] AC 837 (HL)** — The House of Lords applied *Wagon Mound No 1*'s "kind of damage" focus to hold a defendant liable where the precise mechanism of injury (a child falling into a manhole and an explosion of paraffin lamps) was unforeseeable but the kind of injury (burns) was.
- ***Doughty v Turner Manufacturing Co Ltd* [1964] 1 QB 518 (CA)** — Applied *Wagon Mound No 1* to deny liability where the kind of damage (eruption from chemical reaction) was distinct from any foreseeable kind (splash burns).
- ***Koufos v C Czarnikow Ltd (The Heron II)* [1969] 1 AC 350** — The House of Lords expressly rejected the alignment of contract-damages remoteness with the tort *Wagon Mound* foreseeability standard, preserving a meaningful contract-tort divergence.
