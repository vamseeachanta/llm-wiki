# MV Erika (1999)

## Case overview

**Facts.** The *Erika* was a Maltese-flag, single-hull product tanker of approximately 37,000 dwt built in 1975. On 12 December 1999, in heavy weather in the Bay of Biscay, the vessel broke in two off the coast of Brittany while carrying a cargo of approximately 30,000 tonnes of heavy fuel oil chartered by TotalFinaElf. Approximately 19,800–20,000 tonnes of cargo were released, contaminating around 400 km of French coastline from southern Brittany to the Vendée and triggering one of the largest ecological-damage and clean-up operations in European history. The vessel had been classed by RINA and had been the subject of ageing-tonnage condition concerns prior to the casualty.

**Issue.** Could the time charterer (a major oil company) be held criminally liable in French law for chartering a sub-standard vessel that subsequently caused pollution? Could a public-law concept of "ecological damage" — distinct from individual property or economic loss — be recognised in French civil-liability practice and compensated alongside CLC 1992 / IOPC Fund payments?

## Procedural history

- **French criminal prosecution (Tribunal correctionnel de Paris, 16 January 2008)** — Convictions of multiple defendants including the registered owner, the management company, the classification society RINA, and the time charterer Total SA, on charges of maritime pollution. Total received a fine and was ordered to contribute to civil-party damages.
- **Cour d'appel de Paris (30 March 2010)** — Affirmed convictions and addressed civil claims for ecological damage brought by local authorities, environmental NGOs, and the French state.
- **Cour de cassation, chambre criminelle, 25 September 2012** — The French Supreme Court (criminal chamber) rendered the final ruling: it confirmed the criminal liability of the upstream charterer and, critically, recognised *préjudice écologique* (pure ecological damage to the environment as such) as a head of compensable harm under French civil-liability law, distinct from individual proprietary or economic loss.

## Key legal holdings

- **Charterer criminal exposure.** A time charterer who knowingly or imprudently nominates a sub-standard vessel for the carriage of polluting cargo can be held criminally liable for the resulting pollution under the French Code de l'environnement, even where international civil-liability conventions (CLC 1992) channel civil liability to the registered owner.
- **Recognition of *préjudice écologique*.** Damage to ecosystems as such — beyond loss of use, market value, or amenity to identifiable claimants — is a recognised legal interest. Local authorities and environmental NGOs have standing to claim, and the courts can quantify the harm even where market-price proxies are unavailable.
- **Classification-society duty of care.** A classification society that issues or maintains class certificates on which charterers and insurers rely owes duties whose breach can attract criminal responsibility in addition to civil exposure.
- **Compatibility with CLC 1992.** The CLC channelling regime channels *civil* liability to the registered owner and provides a strict-liability route with compulsory insurance; it does not displace the criminal jurisdiction of coastal states over pollution offences and does not preclude national-law recognition of additional heads of damage in domestic civil proceedings.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Reshaped European environmental-liability doctrine.** *Erika* is the cornerstone authority for *préjudice écologique* in French law and has influenced doctrinal development across other civil-law jurisdictions. The 2016 codification of *préjudice écologique* in the French Code civil (Articles 1246–1252, Loi du 8 août 2016) traces directly to the case.
- **Driver of the EU "Erika III" maritime safety package.** The casualty triggered a series of EU legislative responses: Erika I (2000), Erika II (2002), and Erika III (2009) — comprising port-state-control reform (Directive 2009/16/EC), classification-society regulation (Regulation 391/2009 and Directive 2009/15/EC), flag-state oversight (Directive 2009/21/EC), accident investigation (Directive 2009/18/EC), and traffic monitoring upgrades.
- **Accelerated the IMO single-hull phase-out.** *Erika* (and subsequently *Prestige*) drove the IMO MARPOL Annex I amendments accelerating the phase-out of single-hull tankers, with category-1 vessels phased out from 2005 and category-2 / 3 by 2010 (with limited derogations).
- **Paris MoU New Inspection Regime (NIR) origin.** The casualty was a proximate driver of the Paris MoU's risk-based NIR adopted in 2011, replacing the earlier 25%-target inspection regime with a risk-tiered HRS / SRS / LRS allocation.

## Practitioner impact

- **For oil-major charterers.** Vetting (SIRE, CDI, RightShip) becomes operationally and legally load-bearing — gaps in vetting documentation are exposure surfaces in any post-casualty criminal investigation.
- **For classification societies.** Heightened scrutiny of survey rigour, port of survey, and the contemporaneity of class-maintenance records on ageing tonnage.
- **For coastal states and public claimants.** Established a template for asserting ecological-damage claims that operate in parallel with CLC / IOPC compensation.
- **For ship-finance and chartering counsel.** Ageing single-hull tonnage carried elevated reputational and criminal exposure long before the IMO regulatory phase-out completed; charter-party warranties on vessel condition gained operational weight.

## Subsequent application

- **MV Prestige (2002)**, the next major Atlantic casualty, accelerated the same regulatory trajectory and produced parallel — though more contested — Spanish criminal proceedings.
- **Codification in French Code civil (2016).** Articles 1246–1252 codified *préjudice écologique* as a general head of civil damage, citing the *Erika* line.
- **Cross-jurisdictional influence.** Italian, Spanish, and Belgian doctrine has drawn on the French model; the EU's Environmental Liability Directive 2004/35/EC operates in parallel with national-law ecological-damage actions.
