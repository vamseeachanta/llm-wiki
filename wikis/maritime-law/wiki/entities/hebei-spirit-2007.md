# Hebei Spirit (2007)

## Case overview

**Facts.** On 7 December 2007 in the Yellow Sea off Daesan, west coast of South Korea, the Hong Kong–flagged VLCC *Hebei Spirit* (146,848 GT, owned by Hebei Spirit Shipping / Hebei Ocean Shipping) lay at anchor awaiting pilotage when a Samsung Heavy Industries crane barge (*Samsung No. 1*), under tow by two tugs, broke free of its towline in deteriorating weather and struck the tanker's port side. The collision opened cargo tanks and released approximately 10,800 tonnes (~78,000 bbl) of crude oil, contaminating roughly 375 km of coastline along the Taean Peninsula in what became South Korea's largest marine oil spill. Coastal communities, oyster and seaweed aquaculture, and salt-pan operators across Chungcheongnam-do sustained extensive economic loss; the South Korean government mobilised a national response and tens of thousands of volunteers participated in shoreline cleanup.

**Issue.** Allocation of liability between the stationary tanker and the moving crane-barge / tug flotilla under the Korean implementation of CLC 1992; engagement of the IOPC 1992 Fund and the newly-in-force Supplementary Fund 2003 to bridge the compensation shortfall; and whether Korean criminal proceedings against ship and crane personnel could proceed in parallel with civil compensation under the international funds.

## Procedural history

- **Korean criminal proceedings, 2007-2009** — Daejeon and Seoul prosecutors charged the *Hebei Spirit* master and chief officer alongside Samsung tug-masters and crane-barge personnel. Initial Korean district-court convictions of the *Hebei Spirit* officers were reversed by the Korean Supreme Court in 2009, which ultimately found the tanker officers not criminally responsible; Samsung tug and crane personnel and operational supervisors received custodial sentences for navigational negligence under Korean penal and maritime law.
- **CLC 1992 + IOPC 1992 Fund engagement, 2007-2018** — Owner P&I cover (Assuranceforeningen Skuld) constituted the CLC 1992 limitation fund based on the *Hebei Spirit*'s tonnage; IOPC 1992 Fund administered the topping-up compensation tier through the IOPC Funds Director and Korean claims office.
- **Supplementary Fund 2003 — first major application** — Hebei Spirit became the first major casualty engaging the Supplementary Fund 2003 (which entered into force on 3 March 2005), demonstrating the third-tier mechanism's operational viability and triggering Supplementary-Fund Assembly procedural development.
- **Korean civil claims and Samsung restructuring** — Tens of thousands of individual claims and aggregated regional-cooperative claims processed through Korean courts and the IOPC claims office; total aggregate compensation exceeded ~$300M (USD-equivalent) across CLC + IOPC 1992 + Supplementary Fund tiers, with Samsung Heavy Industries also bearing substantial liability through Korean civil judgments and contributing to spill-response costs.
- **Korean Supreme Court rulings and final settlements, 2010-2018** — Successive judgments allocated fault primarily to the Samsung tug operation; the IOPC Funds maintained a Korean claims office for over a decade processing late-emerging claims.

## Key legal holdings

- **CLC 1992 strict liability of the registered owner.** Despite the Samsung tug's operational fault in the collision causation, CLC 1992 channeled spill liability strictly to the registered owner of the *Hebei Spirit*, with right of recourse against the negligent third party. The Korean courts confirmed the strict-liability channelling and the supplementary-fund three-tier compensation architecture.
- **Master / officer non-criminal responsibility for collision-driven spill.** The Korean Supreme Court's 2009 reversal clarified that absent operational fault by anchored-tanker watch officers, criminal liability does not attach for spill consequences arising from a third-party vessel's navigational negligence — a doctrinal anchor for subsequent Korean and East-Asian collision-spill prosecutions.
- **IOPC 1992 Fund + Supplementary Fund 2003 first-major-case operational confirmation.** The case validated the procedural and quantification machinery of the Supplementary Fund 2003 in a real high-claim-volume incident, fixing precedents for claims-acceptance criteria, evidentiary thresholds for aquaculture and tourism-sector claims, and assembly-level decision-making practice.
- **Tug / towage operator liability.** Samsung Heavy Industries bore exposure through Korean civil judgment and contractual arrangements; the case sharpened doctrine on tug-and-tow liability allocation when an under-tow object becomes a navigational hazard to anchored or stationary tonnage.

## Doctrinal significance

- **First major Supplementary Fund 2003 application.** Hebei Spirit established the operational benchmark for the third compensation tier under the IOPC architecture, demonstrating that the post-*Erika* / post-*Prestige* layered framework could absorb a large-scale incident without exhausting the topping-up tiers — a structural validation of the IMO compensation stack.
- **Korean maritime-incident response model.** Drove the development of the Korean Coast Guard's spill-response architecture, the Korean Marine Environment Management Corporation (KOEM) operational role, and the Korean delegate-engagement at IOPC Funds Assembly meetings — making South Korea a leading state-actor reference in IOPC governance.
- **Tug-and-tow doctrinal hardening.** Korean and broader East-Asian salvage and towage practice cite Hebei Spirit on the responsibilities of tow-master and towage-supervisor when towed objects are at risk of becoming navigational hazards, particularly in deteriorating weather.
- **Aquaculture and coastal-livelihood compensation methodology.** The IOPC Funds claims-office practice on Taean shoreline aquaculture and oyster-bed compensation became a methodological template subsequently applied at *Sanchi* (2018) and partial-spill incidents in East Asian coastal communities.

## Practitioner impact

- **For tanker owners and P&I clubs.** Confirmed that strict CLC 1992 liability flows through to the registered owner regardless of third-party fault in collision causation; right-of-recourse strategy and contemporaneous evidence preservation became baseline practice.
- **For tug and salvage operators.** Reinforced Korean and Asian-jurisdiction expectations on tow-management discipline, weather-window decision-making, and contemporaneous bridge-and-towage logging; Samsung Heavy Industries internal restructuring of marine-operations governance is cited in operator-practice literature.
- **For IOPC Funds claims officers and coastal-state administrators.** Hebei Spirit established the operational template for managing tens of thousands of claims through a single national claims office with sustained multi-year operation.
- **For coastal-community claimants and aquaculture-sector counsel.** Set evidentiary expectations for proving aquaculture-sector and tourism-sector economic loss under IOPC Funds methodology.
- **For Korean maritime-law practitioners.** The Korean Supreme Court's master / officer reversal is canonical authority on collision-driven-spill criminal-liability boundaries.

## Subsequent application

- **Sanchi (2018, East China Sea).** IOPC and broader liability architecture cited in Sanchi compensation analysis, although Sanchi-specific sanctions-and-war-risk-exclusion features distinguish the cases.
- **Sustained Supplementary Fund 2003 utilisation.** Subsequent IOPC incidents have engaged the third tier with greater procedural confidence, building on Hebei-Spirit-era assembly practice.
- **Korean Marine Environment Management Corporation (KOEM) maturation.** The post-Hebei-Spirit Korean coastal-spill response apparatus is cited in regional cooperation arrangements (NOWPAP, Korean-Chinese-Japanese trilateral coordination).
