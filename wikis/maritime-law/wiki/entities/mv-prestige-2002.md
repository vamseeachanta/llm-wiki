# MV Prestige (2002)

## Case overview

**Facts.** The *Prestige* was a 26-year-old single-hull aframax tanker of approximately 81,500 dwt, registered in the Bahamas and operated under Greek management, carrying approximately 77,000 tonnes of heavy fuel oil. On 13 November 2002 the vessel suffered hull failure in heavy weather off Cape Finisterre (Galicia, Spain). After Spanish authorities refused refuge in a port and ordered the vessel towed offshore, she broke in two on 19 November 2002 approximately 250 km west of the Galician coast, ultimately releasing approximately 63,000 tonnes of cargo that contaminated the Galician, Asturian, and Cantabrian coasts of Spain, and the Atlantic coasts of France and Portugal — Europe's largest peacetime offshore oil spill.

**Issue.** (i) Could the master be held criminally liable in Spain for an environmental offence after the vessel was ordered offshore by the same coastal-state authority that subsequently prosecuted? (ii) How do CLC 1992, the IOPC Fund 1992, and the Supplementary Fund 2003 interact in compensating losses that exceed the per-incident caps? (iii) Did Spain's pollution-response decisions — including the refusal of port of refuge — give rise to liability under the European Convention on Human Rights?

## Procedural history

- **Audiencia Provincial de A Coruña (13 November 2013)** — Acquitted the master on the principal environmental-criminal charges; convicted on a lesser charge of disobedience of authority. Acquittals attracted criticism domestically.
- **Tribunal Supremo (Sala de lo Penal), STS 865/2015 of 14 January 2016** — Spain's Supreme Court (Criminal Chamber) reversed in part and convicted the master, Apostolos Mangouras, of an offence against natural resources and the environment causing a catastrophe (recklessness), imposing custodial and civil-liability consequences. The judgment also addressed the civil-liability exposure of the registered owner, the P&I club (the London Steam-Ship Owners' Mutual Insurance Association), and the classification society ABS, and the interaction of these claims with CLC channelling and the IOPC Fund.
- **English enforcement litigation (2015–2024)** — Spain sought to enforce its Spanish judgments against the London P&I club in England under the Brussels I framework and via direct-action provisions; multiple decisions of the Commercial Court, Court of Appeal, and Supreme Court (UK) addressed *res judicata*, arbitration-award priority, and direct-action mechanics.
- **ECtHR, Mangouras v Spain (Application no. 12050/04), Grand Chamber 28 September 2010** — Found that the €3 million bail set for the master at the pre-trial stage did not violate Article 5 §3 ECHR in the circumstances, given the exceptional gravity of the pollution.

## Key legal holdings

- **Master's criminal liability.** Spanish Supreme Court (STS 865/2016) held that the master can be criminally liable under the Spanish Penal Code for environmental harm caused by a vessel he commands where his conduct meets the recklessness threshold, even where coastal-state directions contributed to the casualty's geographic outcome.
- **CLC 1992 channelling does not bar criminal proceedings.** Civil channelling to the registered owner under the CLC does not preclude national-law criminal prosecution of the master, charterer, or other parties.
- **Layered compensation engagement.** The casualty exceeded the CLC 1992 owner cap and the IOPC Fund 1992 limit, becoming the first major incident in which the Supplementary Fund 2003 (raising the per-incident ceiling to 750 million SDR inclusive of CLC and IOPC tiers) was engaged for European claimants whose states had ratified.
- **P&I club direct-action interface.** The Spanish judgments and the subsequent English-court enforcement decisions illuminated the limits of P&I direct-action recovery where club rules incorporate "pay to be paid" and arbitration clauses; the UK Supreme Court's ruling in *London Steam-Ship Owners' Mutual Insurance Association v Spain* (2024) addressed res-judicata effects of an arbitration award against subsequent court enforcement.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Catalysed accelerated single-hull phase-out.** Together with *Erika*, the *Prestige* drove the IMO MARPOL Annex I 2003 amendments that brought forward the phase-out timetable; category-1 single-hull tankers were effectively eliminated by 2005, and the carriage of heavy grades in single-hull tankers above 5,000 dwt was banned outright.
- **Stress-tested the IOPC compensation architecture.** Demonstrated the real-world gap between CLC + IOPC 1992 ceilings and aggregate proven losses on a Tier-1 spill; provided the empirical basis for the Supplementary Fund's enactment and for ratifications across affected European coastal states.
- **Hardened place-of-refuge doctrine.** The political controversy around Spain's refusal of refuge contributed to the IMO Resolution A.949(23) "Guidelines on places of refuge for ships in need of assistance" (2003) and to subsequent EU Directive 2009/17/EC obligations on member states to designate competent authorities and produce plans.
- **Flag-of-convenience scrutiny.** Highlighted the genuine-link concerns under UNCLOS Article 91 and contributed to Paris MoU NIR risk-tiering of flag registries with elevated detention rates.

## Practitioner impact

- **For masters.** Confirmed that command of a casualty-prone vessel in heavy-weather operations carries personal criminal exposure in coastal-state jurisdictions even where state actors materially shape the casualty path.
- **For coastal states and emergency-management authorities.** Place-of-refuge decisions are now structured around the IMO 2003 guidelines and EU Directive 2009/17/EC; refusal of refuge requires a documented risk-assessment process.
- **For P&I clubs.** Direct-action enforcement risk is jurisdiction-specific; club rules' arbitration and "pay-to-be-paid" provisions interact in complex ways with foreign-judgment enforcement.
- **For charterers and oil majors.** Vetting standards for ageing single-hull tonnage tightened materially post-2003; the phase-out closed most of the operational option-set within a few years.
- **For classification societies.** Civil-liability exposure (here, ABS) became a continuing litigation theme, alongside the *Erika*-line criminal exposure of RINA.

## Subsequent application

- **MSC Napoli (2007), Costa Concordia (2012)** — Place-of-refuge doctrine cited in subsequent casualty management.
- **UK Supreme Court, London Steam-Ship Owners' MIA v Spain [2024] UKSC 12** — Addressed enforcement architecture between Spanish judgments and a London arbitration award.
- **Codification of *préjudice écologique* in France (2016, Code civil arts. 1246–1252)** — Builds on the *Erika*–*Prestige* line of European pollution casualties.
- **IMO Place-of-Refuge Guidelines (Resolution A.949(23), 2003)** and **EU Directive 2009/17/EC** — Direct regulatory legacy.
