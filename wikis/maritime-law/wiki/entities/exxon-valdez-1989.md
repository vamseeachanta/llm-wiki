# Exxon Valdez (1989)

## Case overview

**Facts.** On 24 March 1989 at approximately 00:04 local time, the US-flagged single-hull crude tanker *Exxon Valdez* (211,469 DWT, owned by Exxon Shipping Company) grounded on Bligh Reef in Prince William Sound, Alaska, while transiting outbound from the Trans-Alaska Pipeline terminal at Valdez. The vessel had deviated from the assigned outbound traffic lane to avoid drift ice and failed to return in time. Master Joseph Hazelwood was not on the bridge at the moment of grounding; the third mate held the conn, lacking the higher unrestricted licence required for the manoeuvre in those waters. The grounding ruptured eight of eleven cargo tanks and released approximately 11 million US gallons (~257,000 bbl, ~37,000 t) of Alaska North Slope crude into a pristine ecologically sensitive sub-Arctic estuarine system. The spill remains, by volume in US waters, second only to *Deepwater Horizon* (2010) and remains the modern reference incident for North American maritime-pollution policy.

**Issue.** Whether the master's pre-departure alcohol consumption, the bridge-watch deficiency, and Exxon Shipping's manning, training, and oversight practices supported criminal liability and punitive damages at the scale claimed by the Alaska coastal communities and commercial-fishery plaintiffs; and what statutory framework should govern future US-waters tanker spills in light of pre-OPA-90 fragmentation across federal and state regimes.

## Procedural history

- **Federal criminal proceedings, 1990-1991** — Exxon Shipping pleaded guilty to violations of the Clean Water Act, the Refuse Act, and the Migratory Bird Treaty Act; agreed to ~$125M in criminal fines and restitution (partially remitted), and ~$900M civil settlement with the United States and the State of Alaska under a 1991 consent decree (paid over ten years).
- **Master's prosecution** — Captain Hazelwood was acquitted on the most serious counts (operating a vessel under the influence) but convicted of the misdemeanour of negligent discharge of oil; conviction upheld on appeal.
- ***In re the Exxon Valdez* (D. Alaska)** — Class actions and consolidated commercial-fishery, native-corporation, and municipality claims proceeded through the 1990s. Jury awarded $5 billion in punitive damages in 1994 against Exxon (in addition to compensatory damages).
- **Ninth Circuit reductions** — On successive appeals, the Ninth Circuit reduced the punitive award (first to $4B, then to $2.5B) on excessive-punishment due-process grounds.
- ***Exxon Shipping Co. v. Baker*, 554 US 471 (2008)** — The US Supreme Court, applying federal maritime common law, held that punitive damages in maritime cases should generally not exceed the compensatory-damages amount (1:1 ratio); reduced the punitive award to ~$507.5M against the ~$507.5M compensatory-damages base.

## Key legal holdings

- **Maritime punitive-damages 1:1 ratio under federal common law.** *Baker* established a presumptive 1:1 cap on punitive-to-compensatory damages in federal maritime cases, anchoring subsequent maritime-tort practice and converging with constitutional due-process doctrine for state-law cases (*State Farm v. Campbell*, 538 US 408 (2003)).
- **Master / shipowner agency for navigation faults.** The Ninth Circuit confirmed that the master's pre-watch alcohol consumption coupled with manning and oversight deficiencies attributable to Exxon Shipping supported corporate liability beyond the act of any single individual.
- **Federal-state regulatory dual-track preserved.** Pre-OPA-90, US tanker-pollution liability was fragmented across the Clean Water Act, the Trans-Alaska Pipeline Authorization Act, and state-law regimes. The litigation surfaced the need for a unified federal framework while preserving state-law remedies — codified afterward in OPA 90 §2718.
- **Single-hull design implicated as causal contributor.** Post-incident analysis treated the absence of a double-hull design as a material contributor to release volume; this directly shaped OPA 90 §4115 and MARPOL Annex I single-hull phase-out provisions.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Legislative trigger for OPA 90.** The *Exxon Valdez* directly drove enactment of the Oil Pollution Act of 1990 (33 U.S.C. §§ 2701-2762) — the most comprehensive US maritime-pollution liability statute in history. OPA 90's strict-liability framework, financial-responsibility regime, single-hull phase-out, vessel-response-plan requirements, and §2704(c) cap-loss gateway are all *Valdez*-driven.
- **Single-hull tanker phase-out 1990-2015.** OPA 90 §4115's domestic phase-out converged with the IMO MARPOL Annex I Regulation 13G/13H amendments (post-*Erika* 1999 and post-*Prestige* 2002 acceleration), retiring single-hull tonnage from world tanker trades by ~2015.
- **Pre-CLC-1992 US framework benchmark.** The United States is not a party to CLC 1969 or CLC 1992; *Valdez* fixed the US carve-out posture and shaped the OPA-90-vs-CLC divergence that persists today as a structural feature of the global oil-spill compensation stack.
- **Punitive-damages doctrine in maritime law.** *Baker* (2008) became the operative federal maritime common-law authority on punitive damages, cited routinely in subsequent maritime-tort and pollution litigation including *Deepwater Horizon* punitive-damages allocation analysis.
- **Natural Resource Damage Assessment (NRDA) precursor.** *Valdez*-driven NRDA practice (Trustee Council, restoration-projects portfolio, long-term ecological monitoring) provided the methodological foundation later scaled at *Deepwater Horizon*.

## Practitioner impact

- **For US tanker operators.** OPA 90 financial-responsibility, vessel-response-plan, and OPA-90-cap-loss exposure scenarios are baseline planning assumptions; *Valdez* is the foundational pre-condition.
- **For P&I clubs and US tanker-trade underwriters.** The OPA 90 financial-responsibility regime created the COFR (Certificate of Financial Responsibility) infrastructure; club-versus-direct-COFR coverage choice traces to the post-*Valdez* implementation.
- **For corporate boards and risk officers.** *Baker* delineated the upper bound on US maritime punitive exposure but reinforced that compensatory-damages quantification carries equal weight with punitive analysis.
- **For coastal-state regulators (Alaska, Washington, California, etc.).** OPA 90 §2718 preserves state-law remedies; states have layered additional spill-response and oil-handling rules atop OPA 90.
- **For trustee councils and NRDA practitioners.** *Valdez* methodologies (Habitat Equivalency Analysis, long-term population-monitoring, restoration-portfolio management) became the template for subsequent NRDA actions at scale.

## Subsequent application

- ***In re Deepwater Horizon* (MDL 2179, E.D. La. 2014-2015)** — The first major post-OPA-90 cap-loss application; *Valdez* is treated as the legislative-trigger antecedent and OPA 90's stress-test predicate.
- **Post-*Erika* and post-*Prestige* MARPOL amendments.** EU and IMO accelerated single-hull phase-out trajectories partly on *Valdez*-driven infrastructure.
- **Continued NRDA reference.** *Valdez* Trustee Council long-term monitoring (subsurface-oil persistence, sea-otter and orca population trajectories, intertidal-community recovery) remains an active scientific reference informing NRDA quantification methodology debates.
