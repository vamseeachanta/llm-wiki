# MV EVER GIVEN (2021)

## Case overview

**Facts.** The MV *Ever Given*, a 20,124 TEU Ultra-Large Container Vessel (ULCV) owned by Shoei Kisen Kaisha (Japan), bareboat-chartered to and operated by Evergreen Marine (Taiwan), Panama-flagged, with technical management by Bernhard Schulte Shipmanagement, ran aground diagonally across the Suez Canal at approximately km 151 (south of the Great Bitter Lake, single-lane southern section) at 07:40 local time on 23 March 2021. High winds during a sandstorm, combined with the ULCV's high freeboard and sail-area characteristics, produced bank-effect-amplified yaw that the bridge team and Suez Canal Authority (SCA) pilots could not correct. The vessel blocked the canal in both directions for six days, refloating after a coordinated SMIT / Nippon Salvage / SCA dredging-and-tug operation on 29 March 2021. Approximately 422 vessels were delayed and supply-chain disruption was estimated by various trade economists in the range of US$6-10 billion of delayed daily trade flow.

**Issue.** Multiple overlapping issues: (i) Suez Canal Authority's claim against the registered owner for canal-clearance costs, salvage costs, lost transit revenue, and reputational damage; (ii) general-average declaration by the owner against cargo interests under York-Antwerp Rules; (iii) salvor remuneration under Lloyd's Open Form / Salvage Convention 1989 framework; (iv) limitation of liability under LLMC 1996 as transposed into UK and other jurisdictions; (v) supply-chain consequential damages claims by ship-cargo-owners and downstream consumers.

## Procedural history

- **SCA arrest, 13 April 2021** — Egyptian Ismailia Economic Court ordered the arrest of the *Ever Given* in the Great Bitter Lake on the SCA's application. Initial SCA claim was approximately US$916 million, comprising salvage cost, canal-revenue loss, reputational damage, and reflotation expenses.
- **Owner / P&I (UK Club) defense** — Owner contested both quantum and venue, arguing for jurisdiction in the United Kingdom under bills-of-lading law-and-jurisdiction clauses and that LLMC 1996 limitation should apply to canal-blockage liability claims.
- **Settlement reached, July 2021 (released for sailing)** — Confidential settlement of disputed amount (widely reported in the press in the range of US$540 million); vessel released and sailed under canal pilotage to complete its voyage to Rotterdam.
- **General-average declaration by Shoei Kisen Kaisha** — Average adjuster Richards Hogg Lindley appointed; cargo interests required to post general-average bonds and security before cargo release at discharge ports. Adjustment proceeded under York-Antwerp Rules 1994 (incorporated by bill-of-lading paramount clause).
- **Salvor / SMIT and Nippon Salvage remuneration** — Salvage Convention 1989 framework engaged; Article 13 base award and Article 14 special-compensation analysis (where engaged) processed alongside general-average.
- **Supply-chain consequential-damages claims** — Multiple ship-cargo-owner and downstream-consumer claims filed in various venues; most resolved through commercial settlement or denied on remoteness/economic-loss-rule grounds rather than reaching merits.

## Key legal holdings

- **Salvage Convention 1989 framework engagement.** The salvage operation was undertaken on a contractual basis with SMIT / Nippon Salvage; Article 13 criteria (success, salved-fund value, skill, danger, etc.) provided the framework for remuneration. Article 14 special-compensation environmental-protection analysis was secondary, given limited environmental threat from the laden but undamaged hull, but the framework was reasoned within the settlement.
- **LLMC 1996 limitation analysis.** UK proceedings (had they gone to merits) would have engaged Article 4 "intent or recklessness with knowledge" cap-break analysis; canal-blockage claims fall within LLMC's general-claims aggregate, subject to the convention's tonnage-banded SDR limit. The 2012 LLMC tacit-acceptance amendments (in force 2015) governed limit calculation. Settlement avoided a definitive judicial ruling on whether canal-revenue-loss claims fall within or outside the LLMC aggregate.
- **General Average — York-Antwerp Rules application.** Owner's GA declaration triggered cargo-interest bond-posting at discharge; the average adjustment is the primary vehicle for sharing salvage and reflotation costs as common-adventure expenses. *Ever Given* is now the modern reference application of York-Antwerp Rules 1994 in a non-casualty (no cargo damage) context.
- **Nairobi WRC framework discussed but not directly applied.** The hull was refloated rather than wrecked; Nairobi WRC's wreck-removal-cost-allocation provisions did not engage, but practitioner commentary used the case to illustrate the WRC's framework boundaries (no actual wreck = no Article 12 certificate engagement).
- **Ship-cargo-owner consequential-damages claims — economic-loss-rule barriers.** Claims by cargo-on-other-ships (vessels delayed in queue) for delay damages encountered classical maritime economic-loss-rule barriers (*Robins Dry Dock* line in US; analogous approaches in UK and EU jurisdictions). Most such claims were not commercially pursued through to merits.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Modern reference for canal-blockage liability.** *Ever Given* is now the leading 21st-century reference for major canal-blockage liability — the prior reference cases (*Tropic Brilliance* 2004 minor blockage, mid-century Suez incidents) involved substantially smaller vessels and shorter durations.
- **ULCV-era pilot / bridge-team / windage doctrine.** The case crystallized concerns about ULCV bridge-resource-management in confined-waterway transits, accelerating SCA, Panama Canal Authority, and major-port pilot-training updates and tug-escort requirements.
- **General Average viability test.** The successful GA declaration and bond-collection process — at unprecedented cargo-interest scale (~18,300 containers) — validated the GA mechanism for ULCV-era incidents, partially answering pre-incident commentary that had questioned whether GA could realistically be administered at ULCV scale.
- **Forum and jurisdiction strategy.** The interplay between Egyptian Ismailia Economic Court arrest jurisdiction and UK contractual jurisdiction shaped subsequent practitioner advice on transit-state versus contract-state dispute strategy.
- **Supply-chain damages doctrinal stability.** The relatively limited successful-merits recovery for downstream consequential damages reaffirmed the maritime economic-loss rule's continuing vitality at ULCV-incident scale.

## Practitioner impact

- **For container-line operators and ULCV owners.** Underscored the operational risk of confined-waterway transits in degraded weather; insurance towers, P&I cover review, and chartered-tug-escort decisions are now scrutinized against the *Ever Given* benchmark.
- **For canal authorities.** SCA's claim methodology — combining direct cost, salvage cost, revenue-loss, and reputational damage — provided a template that other transit authorities (Panama Canal Authority, Kiel Canal) studied; settlement terms shaped subsequent transit-tariff and indemnity-clause practice.
- **For average adjusters.** Richards Hogg Lindley's *Ever Given* adjustment is a contemporary practitioner reference for ULCV-scale GA administration, contribution-bond mechanics, and cargo-interest-coordination at unprecedented scale.
- **For salvors.** The combined dredging + tug + tidal-coordination operation at the canal southern section is a reference operation for confined-waterway ULCV refloating, feeding into IMO Marine Salvage Services committee discussions and ISU member-firm operational-doctrine updates.
- **For cargo interests and freight-forwarders.** Bond-posting timelines, cargo-release sequencing, and GA-bond-versus-GA-guarantee decisions shaped post-incident risk-management practice.
- **For supply-chain risk and trade economists.** The incident accelerated routing-resilience analysis (Cape of Good Hope alternative, supply-chain-redundancy doctrine) and shaped post-2021 commercial decisions on canal-versus-cape routing in volatile geopolitical conditions.

## Subsequent application

- **Bridge-team-management and pilot-coordination training.** SCA, IMO STCW course providers, and major maritime academies updated curricula to incorporate *Ever Given* fact-patterns on bank-effect, ULCV-windage, and pilot-master coordination in confined waterways.
- **Subsequent canal-blockage and grounding incidents.** Practitioners cite *Ever Given* in advising on liability exposure, GA mechanics, and forum strategy for similar incidents (e.g., *Rubymar* 2024 Red Sea incident, various 2022-2024 Suez delays).
- **IMO and SCA tug-escort and weather-window guidance.** Post-incident coordination drove updates to SCA Rules of Navigation on weather-related transit suspension thresholds and tug-escort requirements for ULCVs.
- **2023 Black Sea grain-corridor and 2023-2024 Red Sea / Suez routing analysis.** Trade economists routinely use *Ever Given* as the calibration data point for canal-disruption supply-chain modeling.
