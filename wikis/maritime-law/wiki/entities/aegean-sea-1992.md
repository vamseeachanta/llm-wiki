# The *Aegean Sea* (1992)

## Case overview

**Facts.** The *Aegean Sea* was a Greek-flag single-hull crude-oil tanker (1973-built, approximately 57,000 GT, 114,000 DWT) operated by Athens-based interests and chartered to Repsol Petróleo for delivery of approximately 80,000 tonnes of Brent-blend North Sea crude to the Repsol refinery near La Coruña on the Galician coast of north-west Spain. On 3 December 1992, while approaching the port of La Coruña in heavy weather, the vessel grounded on rocks at the entrance to the harbour, broke in two, exploded, and burned. The forward section sank; the after section was carried by wind and tide along the coastline. Approximately 74,000 tonnes of crude oil were released into the sea, fouling roughly 300 km of Galician coastline and damaging coastal fisheries, mariculture, and tourism interests. There was no loss of life; the crew were rescued.

**Issue.** Two parallel doctrinal questions emerged: first, the application of the international civil-liability and compensation regime — at the time of the incident the Civil Liability Convention 1969 / IOPC Fund Convention 1971 framework was in force in Spain (the 1992 Protocols had been adopted in November 1992 but had not yet entered into force) — to a major coastal-pollution event; and second, the criminal prosecution under Spanish national law of the master, the pilot, and (later) administrative authorities for negligence in port approach, weather assessment, and pilotage decisions.

## Procedural history

- **Spanish criminal prosecution (Audiencia Provincial de A Coruña / Tribunal Supremo) 1992-1997** — Criminal proceedings opened against the master and the harbour pilot for negligent navigation and pilotage. Trial-court convictions and acquittals were partially modified on appeal to the Tribunal Supremo, which entered convictions for negligent navigation against the master and (by separate route) reflected criminal accountability for pilotage-decision failings. Sentences were modest; the symbolic and precedential weight of the conviction exceeded the penal weight.
- **CLC 1969 / IOPC Fund 1971 civil-liability proceedings** — Compensation claims were channelled through the CLC 1969 strict-liability regime (shipowner liability up to tonnage-banded cap) and the IOPC Fund 1971 supplementary tier. The aggregate available limit under the 1969/1971 framework was insufficient to meet the totality of established claims; the residual gap drove Spanish-state and EU-level dissatisfaction with the pre-1992-Protocol limits.
- **Spanish administrative and political follow-up 1993-1996** — Post-incident review of port-approach procedures, pilotage authority, and tanker-acceptance criteria at La Coruña; contributed to the Spanish state's subsequent advocacy of accelerated 1992-Protocol entry into force and (later) of double-hull retrofit requirements at EU level.

## Key legal holdings

- **Coastal-state criminal jurisdiction over flag-state masters.** The Spanish criminal prosecution of a Greek-flag master for negligent navigation in approach to a Spanish port confirmed the operational reach of coastal-state criminal jurisdiction over masters whose conduct in territorial waters and port-approach lanes results in pollution damage on the coastal state's coastline. The principle was a precursor to the more visible *Erika* (1999) and *Prestige* (2002) coastal-state criminal frameworks.
- **CLC 1969 / IOPC Fund 1971 limits as inadequate.** The aggregate compensation cap available under the 1969/1971 framework was demonstrably below the total established claim value. The shortfall reinforced the case for the higher 1992-Protocol limits and informed the political support for the CLC 1992 / Fund 1992 regime entering into force in 1996.
- **Pilotage and master shared accountability.** The Spanish proceedings allocated accountability between the master (overall command responsibility) and the pilot (port-approach navigation advice) without treating pilotage as a complete defence to master liability — consistent with the international rule that the master remains in command notwithstanding the presence of a pilot.
- **No corporate veil-piercing.** Unlike the US-venue *Amoco Cadiz* litigation (1978), the Spanish proceedings did not attempt to pierce the corporate veil of the operator's group. Liability was channelled through the conventional CLC shipowner regime and the master's personal criminal liability.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Mid-cluster member of the 1989-1996 single-hull tanker incident series.** The *Aegean Sea* (1992) sits in the doctrinal arc *Exxon Valdez* (1989) → *Aegean Sea* (1992) → *Braer* (1993) → *Sea Empress* (1996) → *Erika* (1999) → *Prestige* (2002), each contributing to the political momentum behind double-hull mandates (MARPOL Annex I Reg 13F/13G, US OPA 90 § 4115) and the strengthening of the international compensation framework.
- **Pre-Erika coastal-state-prosecution precedent.** The Spanish prosecution of the *Aegean Sea* master is a direct doctrinal antecedent to the master-criminal-liability outcomes in *Erika* (Cass. crim. 2012) and *Prestige* (TS STS 865/2016). The *Aegean Sea* prosecution attracted less international attention at the time but established the pattern that later cases consolidated.
- **Driver of CLC 1992 / Fund 1992 ratification momentum.** The aggregate-cap shortfall in *Aegean Sea* compensation (alongside *Braer* 1993 and *Sea Empress* 1996) was a recurring evidentiary argument for the higher 1992-Protocol limits, which entered into force in May 1996.
- **MARPOL Annex I double-hull policy momentum.** Although MARPOL Reg 13F (new-build double-hull mandate) had been adopted in 1992 (post-*Exxon Valdez*), the *Aegean Sea* loss intensified pressure for accelerated phase-out of existing single-hull tonnage; the eventual Reg 13G phase-out schedule, accelerated again post-*Erika* (2001) and again post-*Prestige* (2003), traces a portion of its political pedigree to the early-1990s incident cluster of which *Aegean Sea* was a member.
- **EU regulatory momentum.** Combined with subsequent EU-coast incidents, the *Aegean Sea* contributed to the build-up of Member-State support for what would become the Erika I, II, and III packages of EU maritime-safety legislation in 2000-2009.

## Practitioner impact

- **For tanker owners and managers.** Reinforced master-criminal-exposure risk in coastal-state ports and the reputational risk of single-hull operations on European coastal trades. Post-incident charterer due-diligence questionnaires (vetting regimes such as OCIMF SIRE) gained traction in part on the back of the early-1990s incident cluster.
- **For P&I clubs.** Spanish criminal-defence cover, master-personal-liability cover, and pollution-shortfall cover (the gap between CLC limits and total claim value) emerged or expanded as routine considerations.
- **For port and pilotage authorities.** Galician port-approach procedures, pilot-boarding criteria, and weather-window discipline were re-examined; the case is a frequent reference in pilotage-liability training.
- **For Spanish national authorities.** The *Aegean Sea* and the later *Prestige* (2002) anchored Spain's policy advocacy at IMO and EU level on coastal-state protections, place-of-refuge doctrine, and accelerated single-hull phase-out.
- **For environmental-liability counsel.** A reference data point on the inadequacy of pre-1996 cap regimes in mid-tier coastal incidents — useful for arguing convention-level reform pacing in subsequent doctrinal advocacy.

## Subsequent application

- **The *Braer* (Shetland 1993) and *Sea Empress* (Wales 1996)** — Sister single-hull tanker incidents within the same compensation-regime transition window; together with *Aegean Sea* they form the empirical case for CLC 1992 / Fund 1992.
- **The *Erika* (1999, France) — Cass. crim. 2012** — Coastal-state criminal prosecution of charterer and master extending the *Aegean Sea* coastal-prosecution template, with the additional doctrinal layer of *préjudice écologique*.
- **The *Prestige* (2002, Spain) — TS STS 865/2016** — Direct doctrinal successor in Spanish jurisdiction; master criminal conviction echoes the *Aegean Sea* precedent and applies it to a substantially larger casualty.
- **MARPOL Annex I Reg 13G phase-out amendments (2001, 2003)** — The single-hull phase-out schedule was twice tightened post-*Erika* and post-*Prestige*; *Aegean Sea* sits in the policy-history record as part of the cumulative case.
- **EU Directive 2005/35/EC on ship-source pollution** — The criminal-sanctions regime for ship-source pollution at EU level draws on the cluster of coastal-state-prosecution precedents to which *Aegean Sea* belongs.
