# MV WAKASHIO (2020)

## Case overview

**Facts.** The MV *Wakashio*, a 203,130 DWT Capesize bulk carrier owned by Nagashiki Shipping (Japan), bareboat-chartered to and operationally managed by Mitsui OSK Lines (MOL), Panama-flagged, was sailing in ballast from Lianyungang (China) to Tubarão (Brazil) when it grounded on the fringing reef at Pointe d'Esny on the south-east coast of Mauritius on 25 July 2020. Investigation findings — including the Panama Maritime Authority casualty report — pointed to navigational decisions that brought the vessel inshore to obtain a mobile-phone signal, with bridge-resource-management failures contributing. The hull breached on 6 August 2020, releasing approximately 1,000 tonnes of Very-Low-Sulfur Fuel Oil (VLSFO) and additional lubricants into a UNESCO-recognized biosphere reserve and Ramsar wetland. The vessel was deliberately broken in two on 15 August 2020; the bow section was scuttled in deep water, and the stern section was removed as wreck.

**Issue.** The incident raised — for the first time at full scale — the operational interaction of the Bunkers Convention 2001, the Nairobi Wreck Removal Convention 2007 (in force since 2015), MARPOL Annex VI's 0.50% sulfur cap (in force from 1 January 2020), and the Maritime Labour Convention 2006 (crew welfare during a multi-month coastal-state response), against a small island developing state's coastal-zone-management and tourism-economy backdrop.

## Procedural history

- **Mauritius Supreme Court — captain and chief officer prosecuted, 2020-2021** — Master Sunil Kumar Nandeshwar and chief officer charged under Mauritian merchant-shipping law (endangering safe navigation). Master pleaded guilty in December 2021 to a charge carrying a 20-month suspended sentence; charges proceeded against the chief officer.
- **Britannia P&I Club / Nagashiki Shipping wreck-removal cost coverage** — Britannia confirmed the vessel was entered with the club; wreck-removal and pollution-response costs were borne under the club's Nairobi WRC certificate of insurance and Bunkers Convention financial-responsibility certificate. Aggregate disclosed wreck-removal expenditure approximated $36 million; total response costs (including coastal cleanup, fauna recovery, fishery-closure compensation) were reported in the higher tens of millions of US dollars.
- **Mauritius compensation claim against Mitsui OSK Lines** — Government of Mauritius pursued claims under the Bunkers Convention 2001 framework as transposed into Mauritian law, alongside ex gratia commitments from MOL totaling approximately ¥1 billion (~US$9 million) for a Mauritius Natural Environment Recovery Fund and seafarer-training contributions.
- **Japan-Mauritius diplomatic engagement** — Government-to-government engagement between Tokyo and Port Louis ran in parallel to the litigation track, including Japan International Cooperation Agency response missions and a bilateral framework for biodiversity restoration.
- **Panama Maritime Authority casualty investigation report** — Issued findings on bridge-resource-management failure, deviation from passage plan, and inadequate watchkeeping practice.

## Key legal holdings

- **Bunkers Convention 2001 — first major application at scale.** The case operationalized the Bunkers Convention's strict-liability + compulsory-insurance + direct-action framework for a non-tanker bunker spill. Britannia's Bunkers Convention "Blue Card" certificate enabled direct claims against the P&I club, validating the convention's enforcement architecture.
- **Nairobi WRC 2007 — first major post-2015-in-force test.** The wreck-removal cost-allocation framework operated as designed: the registered owner's compulsory insurance under Article 12 (≥300 GT threshold satisfied; 203,130 DWT well above) covered the locating, marking, and removal costs. Mauritius was a party to the convention and exercised authority within its territorial sea (Article 3(2) opt-in). Britannia issued the Wreck Removal Certificate.
- **LLMC 1996 interaction.** Limitation-of-liability analysis under LLMC 1996 (which Mauritius applies via its Merchant Shipping Act) confined non-pollution claims; Bunkers Convention claims operate within LLMC's general aggregate cap, while Nairobi WRC wreck-removal costs operate outside the LLMC limitation aggregate per the convention's Article 10 framework.
- **MARPOL Annex VI sulfur-cap interface.** The released VLSFO carried distinct chemistry from heavy fuel oil (HFO) — lower aromatics, different weathering profile, higher acute-toxicity in some studies — which complicated traditional spill-response modeling that had been calibrated on HFO. The case became a first-order data point for post-2020 sulfur-cap spill behavior.
- **MLC 2006 crew-welfare obligations.** The crew was held under coastal-state authority for an extended period during the investigation; MLC 2006 Title 4 (medical care, repatriation) and the Master's continuing obligations engaged through the multi-month detention.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Validated the post-Erika compensation stack outside CLC.** Where *Erika* (1999) and *Prestige* (2002) had stress-tested the CLC 1992 / IOPC Funds regime for tanker-cargo oil, *Wakashio* validated the parallel non-tanker stack: Bunkers Convention 2001 (bunker-fuel pollution) + Nairobi WRC 2007 (wreck-removal) + LLMC 1996 (general-claims cap) operating in concert.
- **First high-visibility Nairobi WRC implementation.** Pre-Wakashio, the Nairobi WRC's compulsory-insurance and direct-action provisions had been theoretical for most practitioners. Wakashio made them operationally concrete.
- **Small island developing states (SIDS) capacity gap exposed.** The case highlighted the asymmetry between SIDS coastal-state response capacity and the scale of modern bulker incidents, driving subsequent IMO technical-cooperation programs and bilateral assistance frameworks.
- **Tourism-economy and ecosystem-services damages.** Compensation negotiations introduced ecosystem-services and tourism-revenue methodologies into Bunkers-Convention practice that had previously been under-developed relative to oil-spill-CLC NRDA practice.
- **VLSFO casuistic precedent.** The Wakashio spill became a global reference data set for VLSFO weathering, dispersant interaction, and bioremediation under tropical reef conditions — driving updates to IMO's OPRC manuals and ITOPF response guidance.

## Practitioner impact

- **For non-tanker shipowners and P&I clubs.** Confirms that bunker-spill scenarios on routine bulker / container / car-carrier voyages now carry CLC-tier financial-responsibility documentation discipline and direct-action exposure. Bunkers Convention "Blue Card" certificates and Nairobi WRC certificates are operational requirements, not paperwork.
- **For coastal states (especially SIDS).** Provides a template for invoking the Bunkers Convention and Nairobi WRC frameworks against P&I clubs directly, bypassing forum-shopping concerns that had complicated earlier response-cost recovery.
- **For master mariners and bridge teams.** The Panama investigation findings entered the maritime-academy curriculum as a case study in passage-plan deviation, bridge-resource-management failure, and the operational consequences of crew connectivity decisions.
- **For salvage and wreck-removal contractors.** Demonstrates the SMIT / Nippon Salvage class of operator working within compressed timelines under coastal-state direction; the deliberate-break-in-two technique and bow-scuttling decision became reference cases in subsequent IMO Marine Environment Protection Committee guidance.
- **For environmental damage assessors.** Established a workable methodology for tropical-reef and mangrove ecosystem damage quantification that had been under-developed in temperate-water OPA / CLC practice.

## Subsequent application

- **Subsequent bulker bunker-spill cases.** Practitioners routinely cite Wakashio as the operational reference for invoking the Bunkers Convention compulsory-insurance certificate and the Nairobi WRC direct-action provisions.
- **IMO Casualty Investigation Code (CIC) review.** The Panama-Mauritius dual-investigation track illustrated coordination challenges that fed into IMO Sub-Committee on Implementation of IMO Instruments (III) discussions.
- **Mauritius national legislation.** Post-incident, Mauritius accelerated implementation of Nairobi WRC and reviewed its Merchant Shipping Act for coastal-zone-management strengthening.
- **Costa Concordia (2012) ratification driver / Wakashio first major application.** The Wakashio case is now the canonical "first major application" reference paired with Costa Concordia as the "ratification driver" in narrative descriptions of the Nairobi WRC's adoption arc.
