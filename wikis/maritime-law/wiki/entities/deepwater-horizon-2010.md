# DEEPWATER HORIZON (2010)

## Case overview

**Facts.** On 20 April 2010, the *Deepwater Horizon* — a Transocean-owned, Marshall-Islands-flagged semi-submersible Mobile Offshore Drilling Unit (MODU) under bareboat charter to BP — suffered a blowout while temporarily abandoning the Macondo prospect exploration well in Mississippi Canyon Block 252, approximately 41 miles off the Louisiana coast in roughly 5,000 ft of water. Hydrocarbons displaced the cement barrier, surged up the riser, ignited on the drill floor, and killed 11 crew. The blowout preventer failed to shear and seal. The MODU burned for 36 hours and sank on 22 April. The Macondo well discharged uncontrolled into the Gulf of Mexico for 87 days until it was capped on 15 July 2010, releasing approximately 3.19 million barrels of crude oil per the District Court's penalty-phase finding.

**Issue.** Whether BP, as the lease-holder Responsible Party under the Oil Pollution Act of 1990 (OPA 90), engaged in gross negligence or willful misconduct sufficient to break the §2704(c) liability cap and to support enhanced Clean Water Act §311(b)(7) per-barrel civil penalties; and how OPA 90 liability, Limitation Act protection, and joint-and-several allocation among BP, Transocean, and Halliburton would interact in a Multidistrict Litigation consolidation.

## Procedural history

- **In re Oil Spill by the Oil Rig "Deepwater Horizon" — MDL 2179 (E.D. La.) 2010** — Judicial Panel on Multidistrict Litigation consolidated thousands of personal-injury, wrongful-death, economic-loss, and environmental claims before Judge Carl J. Barbier in the Eastern District of Louisiana. The court structured the litigation in three phases: Phase 1 (incident liability and fault apportionment), Phase 2 (source-control and quantification of discharge), Phase 3 (Clean Water Act penalty determination).
- **Phase 1 Findings of Fact and Conclusions of Law, 4 September 2014** — Judge Barbier found BP's conduct constituted gross negligence and willful misconduct. Apportionment of fault: BP 67%, Transocean 30%, Halliburton 3%. The Plaintiffs' Steering Committee 8 November 2014 ruling crystallized the gross-negligence finding for OPA cap-loss purposes.
- **Phase 2 ruling 15 January 2015** — Court found 3.19 million barrels of oil discharged into the Gulf (BP had argued for ~2.45 million; the United States argued ~4.19 million).
- **Phase 3 penalty ruling 2 July 2015** — Imposed Clean Water Act civil penalties at the enhanced gross-negligence rate.
- **Court of Federal Claims and parallel proceedings** — Concurrent proceedings addressed lease-royalty and federal-claim issues; criminal pleas by BP (manslaughter, environmental crimes) and Transocean closed the federal criminal track.
- **Aggregate settlement, October 2015** — BP agreed to a global settlement framework totaling approximately $20.8 billion (federal and state claims, Natural Resource Damage Assessment, Clean Water Act penalties), bringing BP's total disclosed cost to approximately $65 billion across all categories.

## Key legal holdings

- **OPA 90 §2704(c) cap-loss for gross negligence.** The District Court's Phase 1 findings held that BP's process-safety failures — the negative-pressure-test misinterpretation, displacement of mud with seawater before setting an adequate cement plug, and disregard of multiple well-control warning signs — rose to gross negligence and willful misconduct. This vacated the otherwise-applicable §2704(a) tank-vessel/offshore-facility liability cap and exposed BP to unlimited OPA 90 removal-cost and natural-resource-damage liability.
- **Clean Water Act enhanced penalty rate.** Same gross-negligence finding triggered the §311(b)(7)(D) enhanced per-barrel penalty rate (up to $4,300/bbl) instead of the ordinary $1,100/bbl rate, applied to 3.19 million barrels less the recovered/captured volume.
- **MODU as "vessel" and "facility" interplay.** *Deepwater Horizon* was simultaneously a vessel (Transocean MODU) and an OPA 90 "offshore facility" component while attached to the well. Allocation between BP (lease-holder Responsible Party) and Transocean (vessel-owner) was structured through OPA 90's Responsible Party framework, with Transocean's separate Limitation Act petition addressing vessel-side claims.
- **Limitation of Liability Act (46 U.S.C. § 30501 et seq.) interaction.** Transocean filed a 1851 Act limitation petition; the District Court's privity-and-knowledge findings constrained Transocean's ability to limit to vessel-value-plus-pending-freight, although the multi-defendant settlement structure ultimately resolved these claims without a definitive limitation-busting ruling on the Transocean record alone.

## Doctrinal significance

- **First major OPA 90 application post-Exxon Valdez.** Where the *Exxon Valdez* (1989) had been the legislative trigger for OPA 90's enactment, *Deepwater Horizon* was OPA 90's first stress-test in a catastrophic offshore-facility blowout. The case validated the cap-loss gateway as operative in practice, not merely on paper.
- **Gross-negligence threshold concretized.** The District Court's Phase 1 reasoning provided the most-developed contemporary US judicial articulation of "gross negligence" in an offshore process-safety context — a sustained pattern of risk-aware decisions departing from industry standard, not a single error of judgment.
- **MDL as the operative vehicle for catastrophic-spill litigation.** MDL 2179 became the model for managing tens of thousands of plaintiff claims with overlapping liability, allocation, and quantum questions; subsequent oil-and-gas mass-incident litigation routinely cites MDL 2179's phasing structure.
- **NRDA scale.** The $8.8 billion Natural Resource Damages component of the 2015 settlement set a new high-water mark for NRDA quantification under OPA 90 §2706 and shaped Trustee Council practice in later incidents.
- **Process-safety doctrine convergence.** Reinforced regulatory convergence between offshore-safety regimes (BSEE post-2011 Workplace Safety Rule, Safety and Environmental Management Systems) and OPA 90 financial-responsibility provisions.

## Practitioner impact

- **For E&P operators and lease-holders.** Treating OPA 90's $75M-$137M cap as a meaningful financial ceiling is unsafe in practice — a single sustained-process-safety-failure pattern collapses it. Insurance towers and well-control-event-cost (OEE) reserves must assume cap-loss as a realistic scenario for any deepwater operation.
- **For drilling contractors and MODU owners.** The Transocean experience clarifies that the 1851 Limitation Act provides limited protection where shore-side privity-and-knowledge can be shown through fleet-management decisions and corporate safety-culture findings.
- **For P&I clubs and offshore underwriters.** Underwriting deepwater operators requires SEMS-quality verification, well-control-program audit, and contemporaneous incident-management-system review — not just financial-strength analysis.
- **For BSEE and joint federal-state regulators.** The case shaped the post-2010 regulatory environment: Well Control Rule, Production Safety Systems Rule, and BSEE inspection regime all trace operational requirements to specific MDL 2179 fact-findings.
- **For NRDA practitioners and Trustee Councils.** Established quantification methodologies (Habitat Equivalency Analysis, Resource Equivalency Analysis) at unprecedented scale and shaped restoration-project portfolio governance.

## Subsequent application

- **Macondo as the modern OPA reference case.** Subsequent District Court and Fifth Circuit decisions cite MDL 2179 as the operative authority on §2704(c) cap-loss, §311(b)(7)(D) enhanced-penalty rate analysis, and OPA-CWA dual-track allocation.
- **Gulf RESTORE Act (2012).** Congress enacted the RESTORE Act partly in response to MDL 2179's NRDA scale, directing 80% of CWA penalties to Gulf-state restoration trust funds.
- **Wakashio (2020) and Sanchi (2018) comparators.** Maritime-pollution practitioners outside US waters cite *Deepwater Horizon* as the high-end comparator for bunker / cargo-spill incidents adjudicated under non-OPA frameworks (Bunkers Convention, CLC), illustrating cap-loss gateway analogues like LLMC 1996 Article 4.
- **BSEE Investigation Reports and CSB Macondo Final Report (2016).** Continued to drive technical-regulatory practice in cement-evaluation, negative-pressure-test interpretation, and BOP shear-ram performance specification.
