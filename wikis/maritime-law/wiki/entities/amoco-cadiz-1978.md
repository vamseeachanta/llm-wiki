# THE AMOCO CADIZ (1978)

## Case overview

**Facts.** On 16 March 1978, the *Amoco Cadiz*, a Liberian-flagged VLCC of approximately 234,000 dwt owned by Amoco Transport Co. (a Liberian subsidiary of Standard Oil Company of Indiana, "Amoco"), suffered a steering-gear failure in a Force 10 storm off the Brittany coast en route from the Persian Gulf to Rotterdam. The vessel drifted onto Portsall Rocks and broke up over the following two weeks, releasing **approximately 220,000 tonnes of Iranian and Arabian light crude oil** plus 4,000 tonnes of bunker fuel — at the time the largest tanker spill in history, fouling some 360 km of Brittany coastline and causing extensive damage to fisheries, oyster cultivation, tourism, and municipal infrastructure across more than 90 Breton communes.

**Parties.** Owner: Amoco Transport Co. (Liberia). Beneficial parent: Standard Oil Company (Indiana) and intermediate subsidiaries including Amoco International Oil Company. Charterer: Royal Dutch Shell. Salvor: Bugsier Reederei und Bergungs AG (Hamburg) — a salvage tug whose attempted tow line repeatedly parted, raising contested issues of salvor effort and tow-equipment adequacy. Claimants: French Republic, French départements (notably Côtes-du-Nord and Finistère), 90+ communes, and thousands of individual fishermen, oystermen, hoteliers, and businesses.

## Procedural history

- **US District Court, Northern District of Illinois (1978-1988).** *In re Oil Spill by the Amoco Cadiz off the Coast of France*, MDL 376. The French government and coastal claimants filed in Chicago, the headquarters venue of Standard Oil (Indiana), to reach the parent corporation's assets and to escape the CLC 1969 owner's limitation. **Judge Frank McGarr presided over a decade of consolidated multidistrict litigation.**
- **Liability ruling (1984).** Judge McGarr held Amoco Transport, Amoco International Oil Company, and Standard Oil (Indiana) **jointly liable** for the casualty, finding negligence in steering-gear maintenance and design and **piercing the corporate veil** to reach the parent. The court applied US maritime tort law (not CLC 1969) because Amoco's US-court strategy and the parent's US domicile supported jurisdiction.
- **Damages ruling (1988).** The court awarded approximately **US $85.2 million** in damages — at the time the largest pollution-damage judgment ever entered — and rejected the CLC 1969 limitation defence on the grounds that the parent corporation, not the registered owner, was the operative defendant.
- **Seventh Circuit appeal (1992).** *In re Oil Spill by the Amoco Cadiz off the Coast of France*, 954 F.2d 1279 (7th Cir. 1992) — the Court of Appeals largely affirmed liability and quantum, with modifications on prejudgment interest and certain damage categories.

## Key legal holdings

- **Corporate veil-piercing for tanker pollution.** Where a parent oil major operates a fleet through Liberian single-ship subsidiaries with shared technical management, common spares pooling, and centralised maintenance decision-making, US courts will pierce the veil to reach the beneficial parent. The single-ship-company asset shield exposed by *Torrey Canyon* did not survive a determined US-venue claimant.
- **Negligence in steering-gear maintenance.** The court found Amoco had received vendor (Bohler-Achs Werk) advisories regarding the steering-gear ram design and had failed to implement recommended modifications. Negligence in maintenance, not the storm, was the proximate cause.
- **CLC 1969 limitation defeated by venue strategy.** By suing the US parent in a US court under US maritime tort law, French claimants escaped the CLC 1969 owner's per-tonnage limitation. The case is the textbook example of **forum-driven defeat of a CLC limit**.
- **Quantum innovation: trans-boundary public-and-private claims aggregation.** The judgment recognised both sovereign coastal-state claims (for cleanup, infrastructure, ecological damage) and individual private claims (fishermen, oyster cultivators, tourism businesses) on a unified docket — a precedent later visible in *Erika* and *Prestige* litigation.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Salvage Convention 1989, Article 14.** The *Amoco Cadiz* salvor (Bugsier) and the protracted tow-line failure dispute exposed that 1910-Brussels-era salvage law gave salvors no financial incentive to prioritise pollution prevention where the casualty property was unrecoverable. **Article 14 "special compensation"** in the 1989 Salvage Convention is the direct doctrinal response, providing a salvage-award fallback for environmental-protection effort independent of property salvage success.
- **Paris MoU 1982 founding.** The *Amoco Cadiz* casualty was the proximate political driver for the **Paris Memorandum of Understanding on Port State Control (1982)**, the first regional port-state-control regime, founded by 14 European maritime authorities to share inspection data and target substandard tonnage. The Paris MoU concept was subsequently exported globally as the **nine regional MoUs** (Tokyo, Caribbean, Indian Ocean, Mediterranean, Black Sea, Riyadh, Viña del Mar, Abuja, Paris).
- **CLC 1992 / IOPC Fund 1992 limit increases.** The inadequacy of CLC 1969 / Fund 1971 limits relative to *Amoco Cadiz*-scale damage drove the 1992 Protocols' increased SDR-denominated limits and the later 2003 Supplementary Fund.
- **MARPOL Annex I single-hull phase-out.** *Amoco Cadiz* (single-hull) is one of the data points cited in the IMO Marine Environment Protection Committee debates leading to MARPOL Annex I Regulation 13G/F/H accelerated phase-out of single-hull tankers (final phase-out 2010-2015 depending on category).

## Practitioner impact

- **Shipowners and oil majors.** Single-ship-subsidiary asset shields no longer guarantee insulation of the parent. Modern fleet-corporate structures must consider centralised-maintenance veil-piercing risk in non-CLC venues (US courts, civil-law constitutional torts).
- **P&I clubs.** Pollution-liability cover and claims-handling protocols incorporate *Amoco Cadiz*-scale event scenarios. The post-*Amoco Cadiz* increases in CLC and Fund limits flow through directly to call rates and reinsurance arrangements.
- **Coastal-state regulators.** The Paris MoU and its export to other regions transformed flag-state-exclusive port jurisdiction into a regional-cooperation model, with shared inspection databases (THETIS for Paris MoU) supporting risk-based targeting of substandard tonnage.
- **Salvors.** Article 14 "special compensation" + the **SCOPIC 2000** Lloyd's-Open-Form supplementary clause provide the modern compensation pathway for environmental-protection effort in casualties analogous to *Amoco Cadiz*.

## Subsequent application

- ***In re Oil Spill by the Amoco Cadiz off the Coast of France*, 954 F.2d 1279 (7th Cir. 1992)** — the Seventh Circuit affirmance is the seminal US appellate authority on corporate veil-piercing for trans-boundary tanker pollution.
- **Erika (1999), Prestige (2002).** Subsequent European casualties revisited the CLC limitation question, this time with French and Spanish courts experimenting with national-law theories (French *préjudice écologique*, Spanish criminal-environmental liability) to escape CLC limits — a doctrinal trajectory whose origin is *Amoco Cadiz*'s US-venue strategy.
- **Deepwater Horizon (2010).** OPA 90 §2704(c) "gross negligence" cap-loss is the US-law analogue of the *Amoco Cadiz* veil-piercing logic — a domestic-statutory mechanism for defeating the standard liability cap when defendant conduct rises above ordinary negligence.
