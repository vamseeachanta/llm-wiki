# THE SEA EMPRESS (1996)

## Case overview

**Facts.** On 15 February 1996, the *Sea Empress*, a Liberian-flagged single-hull Suezmax tanker of approximately 147,000 dwt, grounded on rocks at the entrance to Milford Haven, Pembrokeshire (Wales) while inbound under compulsory pilotage to discharge a cargo of Forties Blend North Sea crude. The vessel grounded, refloated, and re-grounded multiple times over the following six days during a complex salvage operation in heavy weather, ultimately releasing **approximately 73,000 tonnes of crude oil and 480 tonnes of heavy fuel oil** into the Cleddau Estuary and Pembrokeshire coastline — at the time the third-largest spill in UK waters and a casualty in a Site of Special Scientific Interest with extensive marine and coastal ecology.

**Parties.** Owner: Acomarit (UK) Ltd. as managers for the registered owner. Flag: Liberia. Pilot: a Milford Haven Port Authority (MHPA) pilot, providing services under the compulsory-pilotage regime governing the port. Salvors: Smit Tak. Claimants: ~800 individual claimants (fishermen, mariculturists, tourism operators) plus public-authority claimants for cleanup costs.

**Compensation.** Aggregate compensation paid through the **CLC 1992 owner's limit + IOPC Fund 1992 supplementary tier** reached approximately **£37 million**. The casualty is the major UK application of the CLC 1992 / Fund 1992 framework and a key data point in the 2003 Supplementary Fund debate.

## Procedural history

- **Marine Accident Investigation Branch (MAIB) 1997 report.** Identified contributing factors including pilot navigation, port-authority procedures, and tanker handling characteristics — public-record technical findings that informed the subsequent litigation.
- **Crown Court (Cardiff), 1999.** Milford Haven Port Authority was prosecuted under section 85(1) of the **Water Resources Act 1991** for "causing" oil pollution into controlled waters. **MHPA pleaded guilty** and was fined **£4 million** (later reduced).
- **Court of Appeal (Criminal Division), R v Milford Haven Port Authority [2000] 2 Cr App R (S) 423.** The fine was reduced from £4 million to **£750,000**, the Court of Appeal holding that the original sentence had not adequately weighted the public-authority status of the defendant, the absence of recklessness, and the financial-resources test under the Sentencing Council framework.
- **Civil and CLC Fund proceedings (1996-2003).** Approximately 800 civil claims were resolved through the CLC 1992 / IOPC Fund 1992 mechanism, with the IOPC Fund Director-General's office administering claims and the UK Admiralty Court adjudicating disputed quantum issues.
- **House of Lords (related Article 1.6 question).** *R (Alabaster Shipping) v Secretary of State*-line proceedings explored the boundaries of "owner" and "registered owner" under the CLC 1992 regime in the *Sea Empress* compensation context, contributing to UK jurisprudence on CLC scope.

## Key legal holdings

- **Strict-liability "causing" pollution under WRA 1991 s.85.** The Court of Appeal in *R v MHPA* affirmed that "causing" is a strict-liability concept under the Water Resources Act 1991 — the prosecution need not prove intent or recklessness. A statutory port authority that controls pilotage, navigation aids, and harbour operations "causes" a pollution event arising from those operations, even where the proximate fault lies with an individual pilot.
- **Public-authority sentencing principles.** Where a statutory body's negligence causes pollution, the sentencing court must weigh the public-authority status of the defendant and the public-funding source of any fine. The Court of Appeal's reduction from £4M to £750K became a touchstone for sentencing of public bodies in environmental offences.
- **Compulsory-pilotage liability allocation.** The *Sea Empress* casualty contributed to the UK practitioner-level understanding that compulsory pilotage does not eliminate shipowner liability under CLC 1992 — the CLC strict-liability scheme attaches to the registered owner regardless of pilot status — but does open a parallel statutory-strict-liability path against the port authority under domestic environmental law.
- **CLC 1992 / IOPC Fund 1992 scope.** The casualty's claim-administration record clarified the operational application of CLC 1992 Article III strict liability, the Article V owner's limit, and the IOPC Fund 1992 supplementary tier — a UK reference point for civil-claim resolution under the post-1992 regime.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Port-authority duty of care precedent.** The *MHPA* prosecution is the leading UK authority for the proposition that statutory port authorities can be criminally liable for pollution arising from harbour operations, even where the immediate operational fault lies with a pilot or third-party operator.
- **Single-hull tanker phase-out.** *Sea Empress* (single-hull, built 1993) was a data point in the IMO MEPC debates that produced the **MARPOL Annex I Regulation 13G** accelerated single-hull phase-out (subsequent revisions through Regulation 13H), driving global retirement of single-hull tankers by 2010-2015.
- **CLC 1992 / Fund 1992 stress test.** With ~£37M in compensation paid, the casualty validated the post-*Amoco Cadiz* uplift in CLC and Fund limits but also surfaced the residual gap addressed by the 2003 **Supplementary Fund** Protocol — the third tier above CLC owner's limit and the standard IOPC Fund.
- **Salvage operational lessons.** The protracted salvage in heavy weather contributed to operational guidance development under the **Salvage Convention 1989** Article 14 / SCOPIC 2000 framework, particularly around early-engagement and salvage-tug-positioning requirements at major UK ports.

## Practitioner impact

- **Port authorities and pilotage providers.** *MHPA* established that compulsory-pilotage status does not insulate a port authority from strict statutory pollution liability. Modern UK port-authority risk programmes treat pilot-related pollution events as a primary insurable risk, with mirrored arrangements at major EU and Commonwealth ports.
- **Shipowners and P&I clubs.** Confirmed that CLC 1992 strict liability attaches to the registered owner regardless of pilot fault, reinforcing the standard P&I-club allocation of pollution liability and the role of CLC blue-card insurance certificates.
- **Coastal-state regulators (UK MCA / DEFRA).** The MAIB report and the MHPA prosecution drove operational guidance updates on harbour-approach risk assessment, navigation-aid maintenance, and pilot-training validation at UK major ports.
- **Salvors.** The Smit Tak operation in *Sea Empress* is a teaching case in the salvage industry for heavy-weather grounded-tanker salvage, contributing to the development of the SCOPIC 2000 contractual alternative to Article 14 special compensation.

## Subsequent application

- ***R v Milford Haven Port Authority* [2000] 2 Cr App R (S) 423** — leading UK sentencing authority for public-authority environmental offences; cited in subsequent prosecutions of statutory undertakers (water companies, harbour authorities, transport operators) for strict-liability environmental offences.
- **MV Erika (1999), MV Prestige (2002).** European single-hull tanker casualties in the years immediately following *Sea Empress* reinforced the IMO MEPC trajectory toward accelerated phase-out under MARPOL Annex I.
- **2003 Supplementary Fund Protocol.** The aggregate of *Sea Empress* + *Erika* + *Nakhodka* (1997) compensation experience drove the third-tier IOPC supplementary-compensation regime.
- **Nairobi WRC 2007 application.** The wreck-removal coordination lessons from *Sea Empress* and similar UK casualties contributed to the eventual Nairobi WRC 2007 framework for wreck-removal cost allocation.
