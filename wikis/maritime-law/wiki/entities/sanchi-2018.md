# Sanchi (2018)

## Case overview

**Facts.** On 6 January 2018 the Iranian-flagged Suezmax condensate tanker *Sanchi* (IMO 9356608, 164,154 dwt, built 2008) collided with the Hong Kong–flagged bulk carrier *CF Crystal* approximately 160 nautical miles east of the Yangtze estuary, in the East China Sea on the boundary of China's Exclusive Economic Zone. *Sanchi* was carrying approximately 113,000 tonnes (~960,000 barrels) of South Pars condensate from Assaluyeh, Iran, to Daesan, South Korea, on a voyage chartered to Hanwha Total. The vessel was operated by the National Iranian Tanker Company (NITC) through its Liberian-registered manager Bright Shipping Limited. After the collision, *Sanchi* burned uncontrolled for approximately one week before sinking on 14 January 2018 in roughly 115 metres of water. All 32 crew (30 Iranian, 2 Bangladeshi) were lost; only three bodies were recovered. *CF Crystal*'s 21 crew were rescued.

**Issue.** Doctrinally novel on multiple axes: (i) treatment of condensate (an ultra-light hydrocarbon) under MARPOL Annex I and the CLC 1992 — both instruments were drafted around persistent-crude release scenarios, and the *Sanchi* event tested the boundary of "persistent oil" definitions; (ii) Iranian sovereign-immunity and US-secondary-sanctions interaction with international P&I and hull-cover, in particular the war-risk exclusion landscape that dominated post-2018 NITC fleet insurability; (iii) NIOC / NITC v Bright Shipping insurance dispute over hull-and-cargo recovery, which proceeded through 2018-2024 in multiple fora.

## Procedural history

- **January 2018 — Casualty and immediate response.** Chinese, South Korean, Japanese, and US authorities coordinated firefighting and search-and-rescue. The Chinese Maritime Safety Administration (MSA) led on-scene response with assistance from the Japan Coast Guard. The vessel sank approximately 270 km off the Chinese coast on 14 January.
- **2018 — Joint China–Iran investigation.** A joint investigation team (PRC MSA, Iran Ports and Maritime Organization, Panama as flag state of *CF Crystal*'s registered owner, and Hong Kong as port state of *CF Crystal*) was established. The flag-state report (Iran) and coastal-state report (China) were issued separately, with diverging causation analyses on bridge-watch and AIS-handling factors.
- **2018–2019 — Environmental impact assessment.** UNEP, the State Oceanic Administration of China, and Japanese, South Korean, and Taiwanese fisheries authorities published assessments. Surface and water-column condensate dispersion was significantly faster than persistent-crude precedents (*Prestige*, *Erika*), but bunker fuel-oil release from the hull (estimated ~1,000 tonnes IFO and MGO) created a distinct persistent-pollutant footprint.
- **2018–2024 — NIOC / NITC v Bright Shipping insurance dispute.** The hull-and-machinery and protection-and-indemnity recovery process was fragmented by US secondary sanctions on Iranian-related insurance. Bright Shipping's underwriters invoked war-risk and sanctions-exclusion clauses; portions of the dispute proceeded in English commercial courts and via arbitration through the early 2020s.
- **2019–2023 — Wreck-monitoring and bunker-removal discussions.** Periodic Chinese MSA monitoring confirmed the wreck remained a residual bunker-pollution risk. Wreck-removal under Nairobi WRC 2007 contemplated but not formally engaged given the wreck's location and depth.

## Key legal holdings

- **Condensate under MARPOL Annex I.** Condensate falls within the Annex I "oil" definition (Regulation 1.1) but its physical behavior — high volatility, low persistence in surface films, near-complete evaporation within 5–10 days under typical sea-surface conditions — distinguishes its environmental footprint from persistent-crude scenarios. The *Sanchi* event is the leading post-1991 reference for condensate-specific Annex I treatment.
- **CLC 1992 application — partial.** Condensate is "persistent oil" within CLC 1992 Article I.5 only at the margin; the bunker-fuel release component fell unambiguously within Bunkers Convention 2001 strict-liability machinery. The interplay between CLC 1992 (cargo-derived persistent oil) and Bunkers 2001 (bunker fuel-oil) on a single-incident scenario is illustrated by *Sanchi* without a dispositive court ruling.
- **Iranian sovereign immunity and IRI war-risk landscape.** The intersection of NITC operational status, US secondary-sanctions reach into international insurance markets, and the war-risk-exclusion clauses of standard hull and P&I cover meant that conventional CLC / Bunkers / LLMC compensation pathways were operationally constrained. *Sanchi* is the leading post-2018 reference for sanctions-impacted incident-response and insurance-recovery.
- **Crew loss and seafarer-welfare framework.** The total-loss event with all 32 crew presumed dead engaged Maritime Labour Convention 2006 financial-security mechanisms (compulsory P&I cover for seafarer death and personal injury under MLC 2006 Standard A4.2.1, in force July 2014) — but the sanctions-impacted insurance landscape complicated practical disbursement to Iranian and Bangladeshi crew families.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Largest condensate-tanker spill in history (post-1991 Gulf War cluster).** The 113,000-tonne cargo release — even with rapid evaporation — exceeds prior single-incident condensate releases since the 1991 Gulf War tanker-attack cluster. *Sanchi* is the doctrinal anchor for condensate-tanker incident-response and Annex I marginal-persistence analysis.
- **Sanctions-exclusion as parallel-track to war-risk exclusion.** *Sanchi* extended the established doctrine on war-risk exclusions in marine insurance into the modern US-secondary-sanctions context. The *NIOC v Bright Shipping* dispute is now studied as the leading reference on sanctions-and-war-risk interaction.
- **Cross-jurisdictional collision-investigation coordination.** The fragmented investigation report architecture (Iran flag, China coastal, Panama and Hong Kong third-party) is a doctrinal reference on coastal-state vs flag-state authority in EEZ-boundary collisions, particularly where flag-state political access is constrained.
- **Marine-environment vs marine-casualty footprint divergence.** The rapid evaporation of the cargo against the persistent bunker-fuel footprint illustrates the doctrinal asymmetry between cargo-pollutant scenarios (CLC framework) and bunker-pollutant scenarios (Bunkers Convention framework) on a single casualty.
- **Geopolitical-incident class precursor.** *Sanchi* sits at the front of a 2018–2024 cluster of geopolitically-tinged tanker incidents in Asian and Middle Eastern waters that test conventional liability-and-insurance regimes — including the post-2019 Strait of Hormuz incidents (*Front Altair*, *Kokuka Courageous*) and Red Sea developments through 2024.

## Practitioner impact

- **For P&I clubs and war-risk underwriters.** *Sanchi* is the canonical post-2018 reference on sanctions-and-war-risk-exclusion interaction. Underwriters now treat sanctions-listed-flag operational risk as a discrete coverage category rather than a sub-case of conventional war-risk.
- **For coastal-state response authorities.** China MSA's *Sanchi* response is studied as the modern East China Sea coordinated-response template, with parallel learning by South Korea, Japan, and Taiwan on cross-EEZ casualty cooperation.
- **For tanker operators and charterers.** The condensate-specific physical-behavior profile (high volatility, low persistence, but high secondary fire/explosion risk) drives distinct response planning vs persistent-crude scenarios. Hanwha Total and Korean condensate-importers updated charter-party safety provisions post-*Sanchi*.
- **For seafarer welfare and MLC 2006 practitioners.** The fragmented MLC 2006 financial-security disbursement post-*Sanchi* informs ongoing IMO/ILO discussions on sanctions-impacted crew-welfare payment routing.
- **For marine surveyors and casualty investigators.** The collision-investigation report divergence (Iran vs China) is a teaching reference on bridge-watch and AIS-data interpretation in fragmented-jurisdiction casualties.

## Subsequent application

- **2019 Strait of Hormuz tanker-attack cluster.** The *Front Altair* and *Kokuka Courageous* limpet-mine attacks in June 2019 drew on *Sanchi*-precedent insurance-and-response frameworks, particularly on JWC-listed-area pricing and war-risk-exclusion engagement.
- **2024 Red Sea Houthi-attack cluster.** Subsequent Bab-el-Mandeb and southern Red Sea tanker-attack incidents (notably *Tutor*, *Sounion*) extended the sanctions-and-war-risk doctrine developed in the *Sanchi* aftermath.
- **IMO MEPC condensate-handling discussions.** Post-2018 IMO Marine Environment Protection Committee work on Annex I marginal-persistence treatment cites *Sanchi* as the leading modern reference.
- **Academic and IUCN scholarship.** *Sanchi* is treated as the canonical case study in condensate-spill response and sanctions-impacted incident-management; 2019–2023 scholarship in the *Marine Pollution Bulletin*, *Ocean Development & International Law*, and Lloyd's List Intelligence reporting forms the leading public-record corpus.
- **Insurance-market response.** Post-*Sanchi* hull and P&I cover for sanctions-listed-flag operations carries materially different premium and exclusion architecture; the market-shift is reflected in 2018–2020 underwriter-circular guidance.
