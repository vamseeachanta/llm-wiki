# The Eurasian Dream [2002] EWHC 118 (Comm)

## Case overview

**Facts.** The *Eurasian Dream* was a pure car carrier that suffered a catastrophic fire in July 1998 while loading vehicles at the port of Sharjah (UAE). The fire destroyed cargo carried under multiple bills of lading and gave rise to a string of cargo-claim disputes that proceeded as London Maritime Arbitrators Association (LMAA) arbitration with subsequent appeal to the Commercial Court. The disputes turned on (i) whether the vessel was seaworthy when she sailed for the loading port — in particular whether her crew had been properly trained for the specific fire-fighting demands of a vehicle carrier — and (ii) the identification of the contracting carrier under the bills of lading where the vessel was operated through a chain of charters (head-owner, demise-charterer, time-charterer, sub-charterer).

**Issue.** Under bills of lading governed by the Hague-Visby Rules, (a) what is the standard of seaworthiness in Article III rule 1 with respect to crew training and competence on a specialised vessel; and (b) which entity in a multi-tier chartering chain is the "carrier" answerable to bill-of-lading holders, and how does the answer interact with charterparty allocations of risk?

## Procedural history

- **LMAA arbitration (1999–2001)** — Cargo claims consolidated; tribunal made findings on unseaworthiness and on the identity of the contractual carrier under the bills issued for the voyage.
- **Commercial Court (Cresswell J), 7 February 2002** — On appeal under the Arbitration Act 1996, judgment in *Papera Traders Co Ltd v Hyundai Merchant Marine Co Ltd (The Eurasian Dream)* [2002] EWHC 118 (Comm); [2002] 1 Lloyd's Rep 719. The court upheld the tribunal's analytic framework on seaworthiness and clarified the test for carrier identification under the Hague-Visby Rules where bills are signed by or for a charterer rather than the registered owner.

## Key legal holdings

- **Seaworthiness extends to crew competence.** Article III rule 1's obligation to exercise due diligence to make the ship seaworthy includes the carrier's duty to ensure the crew is adequately trained and drilled for the specific operational profile of the vessel — here, vehicle-carrier fire-fighting, which differs materially from general dry-cargo or tanker fire response. A technically sound hull with an inadequately trained crew is unseaworthy.
- **"Specialised vessel" raises the bar.** The standard of due diligence is calibrated to the vessel type. Pure car carriers, chemical tankers, LNG carriers, and similarly specialised tonnage attract crew-training expectations beyond a general-cargo benchmark.
- **Carrier identification under bills of lading.** Where the bill of lading is signed by or on behalf of a charterer, the question of who is the "carrier" depends on the construction of the bill in light of the surrounding documentation — including the head charter, sub-charters, and the form of signature. The judgment reinforced the orthodox approach: the contracting carrier is identified objectively from the bill and the matrix of fact, not by unilateral declarations or post-hoc charterparty allocation.
- **Charterer-vs-disponent-owner liability allocation.** Allocation between head-owner, demise-charterer, and time-charterer is a matter of contract among them; it does not bind the bill-of-lading holder, who may sue whichever party is identified as the contracting carrier on the bill's face.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Modernised the seaworthiness obligation for the container and specialised-tonnage era.** Pre-*Eurasian Dream* authority focused largely on hull and machinery; the judgment expressly recognised that competent, trained crew is part of the seaworthiness package, particularly on vessel types whose hazards diverge from the historic dry-cargo baseline.
- **Reinforced carrier-identity orthodoxy.** Pushed back against attempts to defeat cargo claims by pointing to charterparty internal allocations — the bill of lading holder's contractual counterparty is determined by the bill, not by inter-charterer arrangements they cannot see.
- **Practical convergence with ISM Code expectations.** The judgment's emphasis on training and drills aligned the Hague-Visby seaworthiness duty with the operational obligations carriers were already taking on under the ISM Code (mandatory under SOLAS Chapter IX from 1998 for cargo ships of 500 GT and above).

## Practitioner impact

- **For cargo claimants and their solicitors.** Provides a route to challenge defences premised on technical hull-soundness alone — discovery on training records, drill schedules, and crew certification gaps becomes a productive line of inquiry.
- **For carriers and P&I clubs.** Reinforces investment in vessel-type-specific training programmes and contemporaneous drill records. ISM Code compliance documentation becomes evidentiarily load-bearing in any post-casualty dispute.
- **For charterers.** Charterparty indemnity and "employment-and-agency" clauses remain enforceable inter partes but do not insulate against direct bill-of-lading exposure where the charterer is the contracting carrier on the bill.
- **For ship-management companies.** Heightened exposure where the manager rather than the owner functionally controls crew selection and training; risk of being treated as the "carrier" in fact when the bill's presentation supports it.

## Subsequent application

- **Container-fire and ro-ro cases.** *The Eurasian Dream* has been routinely cited in post-2002 disputes involving fires on specialised tonnage, including container-vessel cargo-fire claims and ro-ro casualties, for the proposition that crew training is a seaworthiness component.
- **Charterparty allocation cases.** Cited in disputes over carrier identity in chains of NYPE / BIMCO time charters and in voyage-charter / liner-trade hybrids.
- **Cross-jurisdictional reception.** Followed in Singapore and Hong Kong common-law cargo claims; cited approvingly in Australian Federal Court decisions on Hague-Visby seaworthiness.
