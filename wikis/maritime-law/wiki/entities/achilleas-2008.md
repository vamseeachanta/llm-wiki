# Transfield Shipping Inc v Mercator Shipping Inc ('The Achilleas') [2008] UKHL 48

## Case overview

**Facts.** Mercator Shipping (owners) time-chartered the bulk carrier *Achilleas* to Transfield Shipping (charterers) under an NYPE-form time charter. As redelivery approached, Mercator entered into a follow-on fixture with a third-party charterer at a then-prevailing market rate of approximately USD 39,500 per day for a four-to-six-month period. Transfield redelivered the *Achilleas* nine days late. In the intervening days the time-charter market collapsed, and Mercator was forced to renegotiate the follow-on fixture at approximately USD 31,500 per day across the entire follow-on period. Mercator claimed the difference in earnings across the full follow-on charter (~USD 1.36 million) as consequential damages. Transfield argued damages should be limited to the difference between charter rate and market rate during the nine-day overrun (~USD 158,000).

**Issue.** Are damages for late redelivery of a time-chartered vessel limited to the difference between charter rate and market rate over the period of the overrun, or do they extend to the loss on a follow-on fixture that the late redelivery disrupted? More fundamentally: what is the proper test for recoverability of consequential damages under English contract law — the "reasonable foreseeability" test of *Hadley v Baxendale* (1854), or a narrower test grounded in the parties' assumption of responsibility?

## Procedural history

- **LMAA Arbitration (majority award) 2007** — Awarded Mercator the full follow-on-fixture loss (~USD 1.36 million) on standard *Hadley v Baxendale* second-limb reasoning: the loss was within the parties' reasonable contemplation given known market conditions.
- **Commercial Court (Christopher Clarke J) 2007** — Upheld the arbitration award; the loss was reasonably foreseeable.
- **Court of Appeal (Ward, Tuckey, Rix LJJ) 2007** — Affirmed the Commercial Court; reasonable foreseeability of follow-on-fixture losses in a volatile market sufficed.
- **House of Lords, 9 July 2008** — Reversed unanimously in result, with Lord Hoffmann delivering the leading speech (Lord Hope, Lord Rodger, Lord Walker concurring on different reasoning; Lord Walker emphasising market understanding). The Lords restricted Mercator's damages to the overrun-period rate differential and excluded the follow-on-fixture loss.

## Key legal holdings

- **Assumption of responsibility narrows *Hadley v Baxendale*.** Lord Hoffmann held that the second limb of *Hadley v Baxendale* — recoverability of losses that, while not arising naturally, were within the parties' reasonable contemplation — must additionally be filtered through whether the parties may be taken to have **assumed responsibility** for the type of loss claimed. Mere reasonable foreseeability is necessary but not sufficient.
- **Market understanding informs assumption.** Where the time-charter market understood damages for late redelivery to be measured by overrun-period rate differential, charterers could not be taken to have assumed responsibility for far larger downstream losses on follow-on fixtures, even if such losses were foreseeable.
- **Type of loss, not extent.** The novel filter operates on the **kind** of loss for which the contract-breaker assumed responsibility, not merely the quantum. Once a kind of loss is within the assumption, normal foreseeability and quantum rules apply.
- **Lord Walker's market-expectation route.** Concurred in the result on a similar but distinct foundation: the trade understanding of the measure of damages for late redelivery shaped the implied allocation of risk and constrained recoverable loss.
- **Lord Rodger's narrower route.** Reached the same outcome on the orthodox ground that follow-on-fixture losses were not within the parties' reasonable contemplation at contract formation, without needing the broader assumption-of-responsibility filter.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Significant contraction of consequential-damages liability.** Marked the most important narrowing of *Hadley v Baxendale* second-limb recovery in English law since *The Heron II* [1969] 1 AC 350. Defendants gained a new gateway argument — that the **type** of consequential loss falls outside the parties' assumed allocation of risk.
- **Tension within the law of contract damages.** The decision generated extensive academic debate about whether *The Achilleas* introduced a free-standing assumption-of-responsibility test or merely refined the application of *Hadley*'s second limb. Subsequent decisions have leaned toward the narrower, refinement reading.
- **Specific to commodity-charter context.** The reliance on market understanding ties the holding closely to the time-charter trade. Courts in non-shipping consequential-damages disputes have invoked the framework with caution, often distinguishing the *Achilleas* facts.
- **Reaffirmed by qualified subsequent authority.** *Sylvia Shipping Co Ltd v Progress Bulk Carriers Ltd ('The Sylvia')* [2010] EWHC 542 (Comm) treated *The Achilleas* as a fact-sensitive refinement; *John Grimes Partnership v Gubbins* [2013] EWCA Civ 37 followed the assumption-of-responsibility route in a non-shipping context but acknowledged the orthodox *Hadley* analysis remains the starting point.

## Practitioner impact

- **For charterparty drafters.** Express loss-allocation clauses dealing with late redelivery, follow-on fixtures, and consequential-loss recoverability migrated into NYPE addenda and bespoke charter forms after 2008. The *Achilleas* default rule is now contractually displaceable both ways — owners can negotiate explicit recoverability of follow-on losses, charterers can negotiate explicit caps.
- **For owners with redelivery risk.** Pre-*Achilleas* practice tolerated open-ended consequential-loss exposure; post-judgment, owners cannot assume that follow-on-fixture losses will be recoverable without express provision, and many charters now carry liquidated-damages mechanisms for late redelivery.
- **For charterers and their P&I clubs.** Provided a meaningful limitation on exposure where late redelivery cascades into market-driven follow-on losses; valuable in volatile markets like the 2008 collapse the case itself emerged from.
- **For commercial-court practitioners more broadly.** *The Achilleas* is routinely cited in non-shipping consequential-damages disputes (construction delay, supply-chain disruption, commercial-leasing) as authority for the assumption-of-responsibility framing.

## Subsequent application

- ***Sylvia Shipping Co v Progress Bulk Carriers ('The Sylvia')* [2010] EWHC 542 (Comm)** — Hamblen J characterised *The Achilleas* as an unusual case turning on market understanding; in the absence of such understanding the orthodox *Hadley v Baxendale* test continues to govern.
- ***John Grimes Partnership Ltd v Gubbins* [2013] EWCA Civ 37** — Court of Appeal applied the assumption-of-responsibility framing to a non-shipping engineering-contract dispute.
- ***Supershield Ltd v Siemens Building Technologies* [2010] EWCA Civ 7** — Cited as a counter-current decision retaining a broader foreseeability approach where assumption of responsibility was not displaced.
- ***Wellesley Partners LLP v Withers LLP* [2015] EWCA Civ 1146** — Confirmed that *The Achilleas* did not introduce a universal new test but a fact-sensitive refinement turning on market and contractual context.
