# X-Press Pearl (2021)

## Case overview

**Facts.** The *X-Press Pearl* was a Singapore-flag, X-Press Feeders-operated container vessel of approximately 2,700 TEU, three months out of the builder's yard at the time of casualty. While at anchor approximately 9.5 nautical miles northwest of Colombo, Sri Lanka, on 20 May 2021, smoke and fire were detected in cargo hold 2. The fire propagated rapidly across deck-stowed containers and burned for 13 days despite Sri Lanka Navy, Indian Coast Guard, and contracted-salvor (SMIT Salvage / T&T Salvage) intervention. On 2 June 2021 the vessel sank in approximately 21 metres of water while under tow toward deeper water. The casualty released approximately 1,486 containers (cargo and empty), an estimated 1,680 metric tonnes of low-density-polyethylene plastic nurdles (pre-production pellets), approximately 325 metric tonnes of bunker fuel, and a quantity of hazardous chemicals including caustic soda, methanol, and lead ingots. The plastic-pellet release is widely regarded as the largest single plastic-pellet pollution event in maritime history, with nurdles deposited along more than 80 km of Sri Lankan coastline.

**Issue.** (i) Under Article IV rule 6 of the Hague-Visby Rules, what is the scope of strict shipper liability where a container of nitric acid (8% concentration, declared but allegedly leaking before loading) is identified as the proximate ignition source? (ii) What is the allocation among the manufacturer / consignor, the booking shipper, the NVOCC, and the container line for misdeclared or off-specification dangerous goods? (iii) Sri Lanka civil and criminal liability under the Marine Pollution Prevention Act 2008 and engagement of the Bunkers Convention 2001 + Nairobi WRC 2007 for bunker spill, wreck removal, and remediation costs.

## Procedural history

- **Sri Lanka Marine Environment Protection Authority (MEPA) claim** — MEPA pursued a US$6.4 billion preliminary claim filed against the ship's owner (EOS RO Pte Ltd, Singapore) and operator (X-Press Feeders), supported by UNEP scientific damage-assessment reports. The Sri Lanka Supreme Court in *Centre for Environmental Justice v Attorney General* (SC FR 230/2021) directed an enhanced quantification process and ordered interim remediation deposits.
- **Singapore Limitation Fund (LLMC 1996)** — The owner constituted a limitation fund in Singapore under LLMC 1996 (as transposed into Singapore Maritime Conventions Act). Sri Lanka contested limitation, arguing that the Article 4 "intent or recklessness with knowledge" cap-break gateway was engaged by the alleged pre-loading awareness of the leaking nitric-acid container.
- **UK and London arbitration** — Bills-of-lading disputes between cargo interests and the carrier were channelled to London under standard X-Press Feeders bill-of-lading law-and-jurisdiction clauses; Article IV.6 strict-shipper-liability claims against the shipper of the nitric-acid container (booked through an NVOCC chain originating in Jebel Ali) advanced in parallel.
- **IMO + UNEP joint mission, July 2021** — Coordinated IMO/UNEP/OCHA technical mission produced the public damage-assessment report that anchored subsequent quantum claims and remediation planning.
- **Wreck removal and bunker recovery, 2021–2024** — Phased operations under contracted salvors (SMIT, then Resolve Marine on subsequent contracts) addressed bunker recovery from the wreck and structural-stability monitoring; Nairobi WRC 2007 framework was engaged operationally though Sri Lanka's instrument-specific transposition status shaped the cost-allocation route.

## Key legal holdings

- **Hague-Visby Article IV.6 strict shipper liability for misdeclared / off-specification dangerous cargo.** The framework first crystallised in *The Giannis NK* (1998 UK HL) and reinforced in *MSC Flaminia* (2012, US 2nd Cir. 2018) extends to containerised dangerous goods that were declared in name but whose actual condition (leaking, off-specification concentration, or compromised packaging) was not disclosed to the carrier. The "knowledge" requirement in Article IV.6 is assessed against what the shipper actually disclosed about the cargo's real condition, not what the carrier could infer from the UN-number alone.
- **Container-shipper-side liability extends through the booking chain.** Where the bill-of-lading shipper, the NVOCC, the consolidator, and the manufacturer are different entities, all may be exposed to Article IV.6 shipper-side liability proportionate to their functional control over the dangerous cargo's pre-shipment handling, packaging, and declaration accuracy.
- **Bunkers Convention 2001 strict-liability application.** The approximately 325 metric tonnes of bunker fuel released engaged the Bunkers Convention 2001 strict-liability regime against the registered owner, with compulsory insurance (Bunkers Convention Article 7 blue card) the primary recovery route for Sri Lanka's bunker-spill remediation costs.
- **Nairobi WRC 2007 framework engagement.** The submerged wreck triggered the Nairobi WRC 2007 cost-allocation framework for wreck-marking, wreck-removal, and ongoing-monitoring expenses; Sri Lanka's transposition status and Singapore's flag-state Wreck Removal Certificate insurance were operationally load-bearing.
- **MEPA / Marine Pollution Prevention Act 2008 civil and administrative liability.** Sri Lanka's domestic MPPA 2008 framework provided a parallel, non-convention-dependent route for damage quantification and remediation-cost recovery, complementary to the international-convention stack.

## Doctrinal significance

- **First major plastic-pellet (nurdle) pollution incident at convention scale.** *X-Press Pearl* is the doctrinal anchor for plastic-pellet pollution as a recognised marine-pollution category, accelerating IMO MEPC discussions on amendments to MARPOL Annex III (harmful substances in packaged form) and packaging-standard guidance for plastic-pellet shipments. Pre-incident, the IMO regime treated nurdles as ordinary packaged cargo; the casualty exposed the framework gap.
- **Container-shipping risk-allocation continuity with *MSC Flaminia*.** *X-Press Pearl* is now routinely paired with *Flaminia* (2012), *Maersk Honam* (2018), *Yantian Express* (2019), and *KMTC Hong Kong* (2019) in the post-2010 wave of container-fire casualties driving Article IV.6 strict-shipper-liability doctrine and IMDG-declaration discipline.
- **Multi-track convention engagement.** The simultaneous engagement of Hague-Visby (cargo claims and shipper liability), Bunkers Convention 2001 (bunker spill), Nairobi WRC 2007 (wreck removal), Salvage Convention 1989 (salvage remuneration and Article 14 special-compensation analysis), and LLMC 1996 (limitation) on a single casualty illustrates the layered structure of the modern IMO compensation stack and the need for coordinated practitioner advice.
- **Sri Lanka as port-state-claimant venue.** The MEPA claim's quantum and the Sri Lanka Supreme Court's intervention reinforced developing-coastal-state authority to drive damage-quantification and remediation-deposit timelines independently of flag-state and forum-state limitation proceedings.
- **Plastic-pellet IMDG / packaging tightening trajectory.** Post-incident IMO MEPC consideration accelerated 2023 guidelines on plastic-pellet packaging and stowage; *X-Press Pearl* is the calibration data point.

## Practitioner impact

- **For container-line operators and feeder operators.** Booking-screening for hazardous cargo, including off-specification or compromised-condition risk indicators, requires investment beyond UN-number / packing-group declarations. Pre-loading inspection authority and refusal protocols for visibly compromised containers are now standard.
- **For shippers, NVOCCs, and chemical-manufacturing booking parties.** Booking-time documentation discipline and accurate condition-of-packaging declarations are the primary loss-allocation surface. Internal compliance programmes are not a defence under Article IV.6 if carrier-facing declarations omit material condition information.
- **For P&I clubs and hull underwriters.** *X-Press Pearl* is now a standard reference in container-line P&I cover review, particularly on the boundary between Article IV.6 shipper-recovery economics and salvage / Bunkers / WRC limitation interactions.
- **For coastal-state environmental authorities.** MEPA's claim methodology — combining direct remediation, biodiversity damage, fisheries-loss, and tourism-loss components — provided a quantification template subsequently studied by other Indian Ocean and South-East Asian coastal-state authorities.
- **For salvors.** SMIT and successor salvors' operations on the burning and then submerged wreck contributed to the post-incident operational doctrine for confined-coastal-water container-fire response, particularly the trade-offs between firefighting at anchor and tow-to-deeper-water decisions.
- **For freight forwarders and IMDG-compliance consultancies.** Booking-screening tools (Hazcheck Detect, NCB systems) extended their adoption post-*X-Press Pearl*, and condition-of-packaging audit fields became commoner in shipper-warranty templates.

## Subsequent application

- **2022 IMO MEPC plastic-pellet-packaging guidelines.** The MEPC's intersessional consideration of plastic-pellet packaging and stowage, formalised through 2023 guidance, traces directly to *X-Press Pearl* damage-assessment findings.
- **Sri Lanka v EOS RO Pte Ltd / X-Press Feeders ongoing proceedings.** Quantum and limitation disputes continue to generate procedural rulings on the engagement of LLMC 1996 Article 4 cap-break gateway in the context of allegedly pre-loading-known cargo defects.
- **Subsequent container-fire casualties (2022–2024).** *X-Press Pearl* is now cited alongside *Flaminia* in advising on container-fire causation analysis, Article IV.6 shipper recovery routes, and multi-convention coordination.
- **Plastic-pollution-as-marine-pollution doctrinal trajectory.** UNEP and IUCN scholarship cite the casualty as the inflection point at which plastic-pellet pollution shifted from environmental-NGO advocacy concern to recognised IMO-regime category.
