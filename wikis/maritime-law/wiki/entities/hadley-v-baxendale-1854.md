# Hadley v Baxendale (1854) 9 Exch 341

## Case overview

**Facts.** The plaintiffs, Hadley and others, operated a flour mill at Gloucester. The crank shaft of the mill's steam engine fractured, halting all milling operations. The plaintiffs engaged the defendants, Baxendale and partners trading as Pickford & Co (a common carrier), to deliver the broken shaft to W. Joyce & Co engineers in Greenwich so that a replacement could be cast to the broken shaft's specifications. The carriers were told the article was a mill shaft and that the plaintiffs were millers, but were not told the mill was wholly stopped pending its return. Through the carrier's neglect the shaft was delivered several days late. The mill remained idle throughout the delay; the plaintiffs sued for lost profits over the additional days of stoppage.

**Issue.** What is the proper rule for determining the damages recoverable for breach of contract — specifically, when may a non-breaching party recover losses that do not flow inevitably from the breach but that are claimed as a consequence of it? Is reasonable foresight by the breaching party at the time of contract formation the operative test?

## Procedural history

- **Gloucester Assizes (jury)** — Awarded the plaintiffs £25 in damages including lost profits during the period of delay.
- **Court of Exchequer, 23 February 1854** — Baron Alderson, delivering the unanimous judgment of the Court (Pollock CB, Parke B, Martin B sitting), allowed the carrier's appeal, set aside the verdict, and ordered a new trial. Lost profits were not recoverable on the facts because the special circumstance — that the mill was wholly stopped pending the shaft — had not been communicated to the carrier at contract formation.

## Key legal holdings

- **Two-limb rule for contract damages.** Baron Alderson articulated the canonical formulation: damages recoverable for breach of contract are those that "may fairly and reasonably be considered either arising naturally, i.e., according to the usual course of things, from such breach of contract itself, or such as may reasonably be supposed to have been in the contemplation of both parties, at the time they made the contract, as the probable result of the breach of it."
- **First limb — objective foreseeability.** Damages "arising naturally" from the breach are recoverable without proof that the parties specifically considered them. The standard is what an ordinary person, with general knowledge of the kind of contract and trade involved, would expect to follow as the usual consequence of the breach.
- **Second limb — subjective contemplation.** Damages beyond the natural-consequence range are recoverable only if the special circumstances giving rise to them were communicated to, and reasonably contemplated by, both parties at contract formation. Knowledge by the breaching party of facts making the loss probable is required.
- **Application to the facts.** The carrier was not told the mill was idle; mills sometimes hold spare shafts or alternate equipment, so a reasonable carrier could not be taken to have contemplated profit loss from continuous stoppage. The lost-profits claim accordingly fell outside both limbs on the evidence.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Foundational rule of contract damages.** *Hadley v Baxendale* is the universally cited starting point for the law of contract damages in the common-law world. It supplies the conceptual scaffolding for every subsequent decision on remoteness, foreseeability, and consequential loss.
- **Bedrock for maritime and commercial damages-claim analysis.** Cargo claims, charterparty claims, demurrage analyses, and insurance-subrogation actions all run their quantum analysis through the two-limb framework before reaching contract-specific modifications such as Hague-Visby Article IV.5 package limits or LLMC tonnage-banded caps.
- **Pre-modern but doctrinally enduring.** The 1854 formulation has been refined — most notably by *The Heron II* [1969] 1 AC 350 (reformulating the first-limb objective standard as "not unlikely") and *The Achilleas* [2008] UKHL 48 (narrowing the second-limb assumption-of-responsibility filter) — but the two-limb structure itself has never been displaced.
- **Adopted across common-law jurisdictions.** Cited as authoritative in US (Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 351), Canadian, Australian, New Zealand, Singaporean, and Hong Kong damages jurisprudence; influential on civil-law foreseeability doctrines through Pothier, who Alderson B acknowledged.

## Practitioner impact

- **For commercial-contract drafters.** Special-circumstances notice is a load-bearing drafting consideration. Where a counterparty needs a particular outcome to avoid a particular loss, that fact must be communicated at contract formation — typically in recitals, scope clauses, or pre-contract correspondence retained on file — to bring the loss within the second limb.
- **For carriers and freight forwarders.** *Hadley v Baxendale* underwrites the standard practice of declaring high-value or time-critical cargo at booking. Without notice, downstream losses from delay or damage are limited to the natural-consequence range, which in carriage typically means market-rate cargo value rather than consequential business loss.
- **For shippers and cargo claimants.** Pre-shipment communications are evidence at trial. Booking emails, shipping instructions, and special-handling requests become the documentary basis for second-limb recoverability.
- **For arbitrators and trial judges.** The two-limb framework provides a structured quantum analysis: classify each head of loss as first-limb (natural) or second-limb (contemplated), then apply contract-specific limits. The analysis remains the standard approach 170 years after the decision.

## Subsequent application

- ***Victoria Laundry (Windsor) Ltd v Newman Industries Ltd* [1949] 2 KB 528** — The Court of Appeal restated the *Hadley* test in modern terms, distinguishing recoverable ordinary lost profits from non-recoverable extraordinary lucrative-contract losses.
- ***Koufos v C Czarnikow Ltd (The Heron II)* [1969] 1 AC 350** — The House of Lords reformulated the first-limb standard as loss "not unlikely" to result, refining Alderson B's "natural" formulation while preserving the two-limb structure.
- ***Transfield Shipping Inc v Mercator Shipping Inc (The Achilleas)* [2008] UKHL 48** — The House of Lords narrowed the second limb through an assumption-of-responsibility filter, but treated *Hadley v Baxendale* as the unbroken starting point.
- **US Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 351** — Codified the *Hadley* framework into US contract doctrine; cited in US cargo-claim litigation under COGSA and federal common law.
