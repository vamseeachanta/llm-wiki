# Front Altair (2019)

## Case overview

**Facts.** On the morning of 13 June 2019, the Norwegian-flagged Suezmax tanker *Front Altair* (IMO 9745410, 109,991 dwt, built 2016, operated by Frontline Management AS on behalf of the Frontline group) was transiting the Gulf of Oman approximately 25 nautical miles south of the Iranian coast en route from Ruwais, UAE, to Kaohsiung, Taiwan, with a cargo of approximately 75,000 tonnes of naphtha. *Front Altair* sustained two explosions on the starboard side, ignited a major fire, and was abandoned; the 23-crew complement (11 Russian, 11 Filipino, 1 Georgian) was rescued by the South Korean–flagged tanker *Hyundai Dubai* and subsequently transferred to Iranian authorities, who repatriated them. In a coordinated attack the same morning, the Panamanian-flagged but Japan-operated tanker *Kokuka Courageous* (IMO 9568860, 27,000 dwt, operated by Kokuka Sangyo) was struck by a limpet-mine device approximately 30 nautical miles east of *Front Altair*'s position; *Kokuka Courageous*'s 21 crew were rescued by the US Navy destroyer *USS Bainbridge*. The combined ship-and-cargo loss exceeded US$200 million.

**Issue.** Doctrinally novel on multiple axes: (i) state-actor-attribution attack on commercial tonnage in a non-war-zone, in a chokepoint critical to ~20% of global oil trade; (ii) Joint War Committee (JWC) listed-area pricing and war-risk-insurance engagement at scale, in a pattern that reshaped the post-2019 Strait of Hormuz commercial-shipping insurance landscape; (iii) Salvage Convention 1989 Article 14 environmental-protection compensation in an attack-driven casualty; (iv) Bunkers Convention 2001 strict-liability on a cargo-tanker incident where the cargo (naphtha) is itself a persistent-oil candidate distinct from bunker fuel oil.

## Procedural history

- **13 June 2019 — Attacks and rescue.** Both vessels were rescued by third-party tonnage and naval assets; *USS Bainbridge* documented and recovered an unexploded limpet-mine device from the *Kokuka Courageous* hull, which became central evidence in subsequent attribution.
- **June–July 2019 — US attribution.** The US government (Departments of State and Defense, US Central Command) publicly attributed the attacks to the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) Navy, citing surveillance imagery of an IRGC patrol-boat removing an unexploded device from *Kokuka Courageous*. Iran formally denied involvement.
- **2019 — JWC listed-area expansion.** The Lloyd's Market Association Joint War Committee (JWC), which maintains the Hull War, Strikes, Terrorism, and Related Perils Listed Areas guidance, expanded its listed-area scope around the Strait of Hormuz and Gulf of Oman within weeks of the attacks; war-risk additional-premiums spiked materially.
- **2019–2020 — Salvage of *Front Altair*.** The vessel burned for several days; salvage was conducted by Iranian-flag salvors with subsequent transfer to commercial salvors. Hull was assessed as constructive total loss and ultimately scrapped.
- **2019–2020 — Insurance recovery.** Frontline's hull-cover and war-risk-cover underwriters engaged on the constructive-total-loss valuation; Kokuka Sangyo's recovery was likewise routed through Japanese hull-and-war-risk markets. Both incidents engaged extensive war-risk-policy interpretation rather than standard hull-cover.
- **2019–2024 — Continued Strait of Hormuz incidents.** The June 2019 cluster initiated a multi-year sequence of comparable incidents (*Stena Impero* July 2019 detention, *Mercer Street* July 2021 drone attack, multiple 2023–2024 detentions and attacks) that extended the doctrinal and insurance-market context.

## Key legal holdings

- **War-risk-insurance engagement at scale.** *Front Altair* and *Kokuka Courageous* are the leading post-2019 references on Hull War, Strikes, Terrorism, and Related Perils ("Institute War and Strikes Clauses Hulls 1/11/95" or modern equivalents) policy interpretation in chokepoint-attack scenarios. Conventional hull-cover war-exclusions (commonly framed under hostilities, civil war, terrorism, malicious-act perils) operate to channel recovery into war-risk policies; the *Front Altair* recovery is the modern reference for that channelization.
- **JWC listed-area pricing dynamics.** The post-13-June-2019 listed-area expansion drove an order-of-magnitude additional-premium increase on Persian Gulf transits; this is a doctrinal reference on JWC-listed-area mechanics, the seven-day notice provision in standard war-risk wording, and the relationship between Lloyd's market guidance and underwriter quotation practice.
- **Salvage Convention 1989 Article 14 in attack-driven casualty.** The salvage operations on *Front Altair* engaged Article 14 special-compensation logic for environmental-protection efforts, in a context where the underlying casualty was an attack rather than a navigation-error or weather event. This extends Article 14 application beyond its core *Amoco Cadiz* / SCOPIC framing.
- **Bunkers Convention 2001 — limited application on cargo-tanker incident.** *Front Altair*'s naphtha cargo and bunker-fuel-oil release engaged Bunkers Convention 2001 strict-liability machinery for the bunker-fuel component; CLC 1992 application on naphtha as a marginal-persistence cargo paralleled the *Sanchi* (2018) doctrinal pattern.
- **State-actor-attack attribution and insurer rights.** Where a state-actor is identified as the source of damage to commercial tonnage, war-risk underwriters' rights of subrogation against the state-actor are theoretical but practically constrained by sovereign-immunity doctrine. *Front Altair* is the leading post-2019 reference on that theoretical-vs-practical asymmetry.

## Doctrinal significance

- **Founding case of the post-2019 Strait of Hormuz incident class.** *Front Altair* and *Kokuka Courageous* mark the start of a 2019–2024 sequence of geopolitically-tinged commercial-shipping incidents that extended through the Mercer Street drone attack (2021) and the post-2023 Red Sea Houthi-attack cluster.
- **JWC listed-area mechanism as commercial-impact transmission.** The post-13-June-2019 listed-area expansion is the modern reference for how Lloyd's market guidance translates geopolitical risk into commercial-shipping economics — premium impact, route-deviation incentives, and charter-party adjustment clauses.
- **War-risk vs hull-cover boundary.** The *Front Altair* and *Kokuka Courageous* recoveries clarified the operational boundary between standard hull-cover and war-risk policies in attack-driven casualties, a boundary that was less-tested in the post-1991 Gulf War period.
- **Geopolitical-attack-as-pollution-incident class.** The combined attack-and-pollution profile (cargo release, bunker release, fire-driven secondary atmospheric emissions) creates a hybrid geopolitical/environmental incident class that subsequent 2023–2024 Red Sea events extended further.
- **Article 14 environmental-protection logic in non-traditional contexts.** *Front Altair* extends the Salvage Convention 1989 Article 14 environmental-protection rationale into attack-driven casualties, complementing the pre-emptive-salvage extension demonstrated by *FSO Safer* (2023) — both push Article 14 beyond its *Amoco Cadiz* / SCOPIC origin context.

## Practitioner impact

- **For war-risk underwriters and JWC participants.** *Front Altair* is the canonical post-2019 reference on listed-area-mechanism dynamics, additional-premium-pricing precedent, and the seven-day notice provision in standard war-risk wording. JWC participants now treat chokepoint-attack risk as a discrete sub-class within war-risk underwriting practice.
- **For tanker operators and charterers.** Strait-of-Hormuz and Gulf-of-Oman transit risk-management practice was substantially reshaped post-13-June-2019, including charter-party amendments, Best Management Practices guidance updates, and routing/escort-coordination practice with US, UK, and other naval assets under the International Maritime Security Construct (IMSC) and EUNAVFOR EMASoH.
- **For salvors.** The *Front Altair* salvage operation under attack-driven-casualty conditions, with constrained access for non-Iranian salvors, is a reference on salvage practice in geopolitically-restricted incident zones.
- **For maritime-security practitioners.** *Front Altair* is studied as the modern reference on attack-attribution evidence-handling, the chain of custody for recovered limpet-mine devices, and the interaction between commercial-incident response and naval-evidence-collection practice.
- **For P&I clubs.** The crew-recovery and welfare-disbursement architecture across mixed-nationality crew (Russian, Filipino, Georgian) under attack-driven-casualty conditions is a reference on MLC 2006 Standard A4.2 financial-security disbursement in geopolitically-restricted contexts.
- **For flag-state administrations.** Norway and Panama's flag-state engagement on *Front Altair* and *Kokuka Courageous* respectively is a reference on flag-state casualty-investigation practice in attack-driven scenarios where coastal/littoral-state investigative access is constrained.

## Subsequent application

- **July 2019 *Stena Impero* detention.** The Iranian seizure of the UK-flag *Stena Impero* in July 2019 extended the post-13-June incident class into formal-detention scenarios; cross-references to the *Front Altair* JWC listed-area framework.
- **July 2021 *Mercer Street* drone attack.** The drone attack on the Liberian-flag *Mercer Street* off Oman extended the attack-class into stand-off-weapon scenarios, drawing on *Front Altair*-precedent insurance-and-response frameworks.
- **2023–2024 Red Sea Houthi-attack cluster.** The Bab-el-Mandeb tanker-attack sequence (notably *Tutor*, *Sounion*, *Galaxy Leader*) drew extensively on *Front Altair*-precedent JWC-listed-area pricing and war-risk-engagement doctrine.
- **2019–2024 IMSC and EUNAVFOR EMASoH coordination.** The post-*Front Altair* multinational naval coordination architecture informs subsequent maritime-security-construct development in adjacent regions (Red Sea, Gulf of Guinea).
- **Academic and Lloyd's List Intelligence reporting.** *Front Altair* is treated as the canonical case study in chokepoint-attack maritime-insurance practice; 2019–2024 Lloyd's, IUMI, and ICC International Maritime Bureau reporting forms the leading public-record corpus.
- **War-risk-policy wording evolution.** Post-2019 war-risk-policy wording on chokepoint coverage, additional-premium notice provisions, and attack-vs-detention coverage distinctions reflects *Front Altair*-driven market lessons.
